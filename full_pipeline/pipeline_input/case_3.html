Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: SWL-08112-17

File Number: SWL-08113-17

 

 

 


KC and JC (the 'Landlords') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict JS (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes and because the Tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or someone the Tenant permitted in the residential complex has substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment or lawful right, privilege or interest of the Landlords or another tenant.

 

This application was heard in Windsor on January 17, 2018.  The Landlords KC and JC, attended the hearing along with the Landlords’ Legal Representative, LM.  The Tenant attended the hearing and spoke with Duty Counsel prior to the hearing.     

 

Determinations:

 

L1 Application:

 

1.      The Tenant has not paid the total rent the Tenant was required to pay for the period from September 1, 2017 to November 30, 2017.  Because of the arrears, the Landlords served a Notice of Termination effective September 16, 2017.

2.      The Tenants are in possession of the rental unit.

3.      The monthly rent is $761.00

4.      The Landlord is not holding a last month's rent deposit.

5.      The Tenants have made no payments since the application was filed.

6.      The parties agree that the amount outstanding to January 31, 2018, inclusive of rent arrears ($3,783.00), and costs ($175.00), is $3,985.00.

Section 82:

7.      The Tenant raises maintenance issues under section 82 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006.  The Tenant testified she has lived in the rental unit for 17 years and in 2015 she told the Landlord the furnace was not working.  According to the Tenant the furnace sits on cinder blocks under the house and the gas company would not certify it for use.  The Tenant said the furnace needed to be installed on a cement pad. 

8.      The Tenant said she used her wood stove for heat, but in mid- December 2017 there was a fire in the chimney of the wood stove and the Landlord did not fix it until January 13, 2018.  The Tenant said she was without heat for a month.  The Tenant testified she used her oven and space heaters to heat the unit.

9.      The Tenant said she had her own contractor attend to the chimney stack, but it was still not working properly.

10.   The Landlords said they purchased the property in March 2016 and during their conversation with the Tenant, KC said the Tenant advised them she preferred to use the wood stove as her source of heat and not to bother fixing the furnace.

11.   The Landlord, KC, testified that the first time they knew about the heat situation was January 2, 2018 when they were contacted by the health inspector.  KC said the Tenant never contacted them about the problem. KC said the rental unit was equipped with baseboard heaters when they purchased it, but without their knowledge, the heaters have been removed.

12.   KC said that when they found out there was no heat from the health inspector they advised the Tenant they would attend the property on January 3, 2018, but when they attended they were denied access because they had not provided 24 hour written notice.  KC said they returned on January 5, 2018 with the police after providing notice.  According to KC they brought space heaters for the Tenant.

13.   KC said when they inspected the rental unit they found the furnace had never been turned on so they have made arrangements to have a new furnace installed.

14.   Because there had been a fire in the chimney stack, the Tenant told the Landlord she had the chimney cleaned but it was still a problem.  KC said they called chimney maintenance person to attend to the chimney but the weather was not conducive to climbing onto the roof, so the first time the chimney could be addressed was on January 13, 2018.  KC said the fire was caused by the Tenant burning garbage and soft wood which clogged up the chimney.   

15.   JC testified that when he spoke with the Tenant she advised him she preferred to use the stove for heat that is why they didn’t replace the furnace.  JC said that if he had known it was going to be an issue, he would have put a new furnace in right away.  In any event, the Landlords said they would install a new furnace right away and it is so ordered.

16.   I have considered all the evidence raised by the Tenant under section 82 and I find that the Tenant failed to advise the Landlords that there was no heat and the Landlords only found out about the heat situation on January 2, 2018 through the health unit.  The Landlords acted quickly, but the Tenant refused them entry when they attempted to attend the rental unit to determine what needed to be done.  Once the Landlords gained entry, the chimney stack was cleaned as soon as weather permitted. 

17.   Although the furnace was not functioning, the Tenant relied on the wood stove as a preferred source of heat.  Given the lack of notice to the Landlord about the heat issue, I do not find the Landlord is in breach of section 20 or the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006.  The Landlords acted promptly once notified and are prepared to install a new furnace now that they are aware that the Tenant needs one. In any event, the Tenant had heat once the Landlords were allowed access and able to get on the roof.  As of the date of the hearing, the Tenant said she had heat.  Therefore I find that under section 83 there are no serious outstanding maintenance issues in the rental unit and termination of the tenancy should not be denied.     

L2 Application:        

18.   The Landlord served a first N5 Notice to End Your Tenancy For Interfering with Others, Damages or Overcrowding (N5) on September 15, 2017 because the Tenant allegedly left garbage in the lane and the Tenant assaulted the Landlord, KC, when she went to pick up rent.

 

19.   The Landlords said the Tenant failed to clean up the lane, so didn’t void the first N5 Notice.  I am satisfied the Tenant didn’t void the first N5 Notice and the Landlord can proceed with the L2 Application on the first N5 Notice.

 

Landlords’ Evidence

 

20.   KC said she received a notice from the City of Windsor on August 31, 2017 because there was refuse in the lane of the property and the notice directed the Landlord to clean up the debris or they would be charged for the cleanup (Exhibit #1).

 

21.   On September 21, 2018 KC said they went back to the property and cleaned up the refuse because the Tenant had failed to adequately address the problem ( Exhibit #2).

 

22.   KC testified that when she went to collect the rent on September 1, 2017, she brought paint the Tenant had requested.  However, KC said the Tenant was not satisfied with the type of paint and refused to pay rent, then the Tenant pushed KC and KC stumbled backwards.  The Tenant then slammed the door in the Landlord’s face.  KC said she called police.

 

23.   KC stated she went home and spoke with police about 7 or 8 pm.  KC then said she attended the police station to fill out a report.  KC said she did not want the Tenant charged because she did not want the Tenant to know where she lived.

 

24.   JC testified that he went to the property on January 10, 2018 to discuss the furnace.  The Tenant had violation papers she said was from Union Gas about the furnace.  JC said that when he took the papers and attempted to leave to get copies, the Tenant blocked the exit, pushed the Landlord and took the papers from him. JC said the police were called by the Tenant and no charges were laid. JC said the police advised him that he should bring the police every time he attended the rental unit.

 

Tenant’s Evidence

 

25.   The Tenant didn’t deny that she failed to clean up the lane after receiving the N5 Notice.  The Tenant stated she thought she had an arrangement with the Landlords that they would clean up the refuse in the lane as she had no way of getting rid of all of the materials.  The Landlords denied they had any arrangement with the Tenant.  The Tenant said some of the items were hers, but some belonged to neighbours.  In any event, the Tenant said her belongings had been piled in the lane for about eight months and she tried to clean up what she could but wasn’t able to get rid of everything.

 

26.   On September 1, 2017, the Tenant said the Landlord, KC, brought paint but it was the wrong kind of paint.  The Tenant testified the Landlord was standing on the porch and she shut slammed the main door shut, but didn’t touch KC.  The Tenant said she heard nothing from the police.

 

27.   On January 10, 2018, the Tenant testified the Landlord came to her rental unit and tried to take the Union Gas violation notices.  The Tenant said she was afraid the Landlord would not bring the notices back to her, so she grabbed them from him.  In the process the Tenant said JC stepped on her foot which caused it to bruise.

 

28.   The Tenant said she called police and no charges were laid even though she requested a peace bond and assault charges. The Tenant said she suffers from anxiety and “flips out sometimes”.

 

29.   I find that the evidence surrounding the issues on September 1, 2017 and January 10, 2018 demonstrated that the relationship is strained and it would be prudent for the police to attend the unit with the Landlord in the future.  As I believe both the Landlords and the Tenant equally, I do not find the Landlords proved on a balance of probabilities the Tenant substantially interfered with their reasonable enjoyment in those two incidents.

 

30.   After considering all of the evidence with respect to the refuse in the lane, I find that the Tenant has failed to comply with the N5 Notice and did not clean up the materials and garbage in the lane putting the Landlord at risk of being billed by the city, thereby substantially interfering with the Landlord’s lawful right, interest or privilege. However, this was a onetime incident and the issue is now resolved.  I find it does not rise to the level to warrant termination of the tenancy and a conditional order would be appropriate in the circumstances. 

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.      Unless the Tenant voids the order as set out below, the tenancy between the Landlords and the Tenant is terminated.  The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before February 28, 2018.

2.      The Tenant shall pay to the Landlords $3,822.26*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to February 2, 2018.

3.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlords $24.66 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting February 3, 2018 to the date the Tenant moves out of the unit.

4.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlords $175.00 for the cost of filing the application.

5.      If the Tenant does not pay the Landlords the full amount owing* on or before February 28, 2018, the Tenant will start to owe interest.  This will be simple interest calculated from March 1, 2018 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

6.      If the unit is not vacated on or before February 28, 2018, then starting March 1, 2018, the Landlords may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

7.      Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlords, on or after March 1, 2018.

8.      If, on or before February 28, 2018, the Tenant pays the amount of $4,719.00** to the Landlords or to the Board in trust, this order for eviction will be void.  This means that the tenancy would not be terminated and the Tenant could remain in the unit.  If this payment is not made in full and on time, the Landlords may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

9.      The Tenant may make a motion to the Board under subsection 74(11) of the Act to set aside this order if they pay the amount required under that subsection on or after March 1, 2018 but before the Sheriff gives vacant possession to the Landlords. The Tenant is only entitled to make this motion once during the period of the tenancy agreement with the Landlords.

10.   If the Tenant complies with paragraph 8 of the order then;

11.   The Landlord’s application for eviction of the Tenant is denied on the condition that:

 

a)   The Tenant shall refrain from accumulating garbage in and around the property for a period of 12 months commencing February 2, 2018 and up to and including February 1, 2019.  

12.   If the Tenant fails to comply with the conditions set out in paragraph 11 above, then, within 30 days of the breach, the Landlord may apply under section 78 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’) for an order terminating the tenancy and evicting the Tenant.  The Landlord must make the application within 30 days of a breach of a condition set out in paragraph 11 of this order.

13.   The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $175.00 or the cost of filing the L2 application.
