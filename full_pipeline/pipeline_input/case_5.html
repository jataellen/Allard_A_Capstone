Order under Section 21.2 of the

Statutory Powers Procedure Act

and the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TSL-07666-19-RV

 

 

 


Review Order

 

M D and B K (the 'Landlords') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict O G and K D (the 'Tenants') because the Tenants did not pay the rent that the Tenants owe. (L1 Application)

 

The Landlords also applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict the Tenants because the Landlords require possession of the rental unit for the purpose of residential occupation; and because the Tenants have been persistently late in paying their rent. The Landlords also claimed compensation for each day the Tenants remained in the unit after the termination date. (L2 Application)

 

This L1/L2 Application was resolved by order TSL-07666-19 issued on August 30, 2019.

 

On September 3, 2019, the Tenants requested a review of the order and that the order be stayed until the request to review the order is resolved.

 

On September 4, 2019, interim order TSL-07666-19-IN was issued, staying the order issued on August 30, 2019.

 

The request was heard in Toronto on November 1, 2019.

 

The Landlords; the Landlords’ legal representative, M P; and the Tenants attended the hearing.

 

 

 

 

Determinations:

 

Request to review granted

1.      At the hearing, the Landlords consented to the Tenants’ request to review. I proceeded to hear the application de novo.

Merits hearing

L1 Application: Tenants owe the Landlords $3,854.79

2.      At the hearing, the Tenants did not dispute the amount the Landlords claim they owe. They acknowledge that they owe the Landlords approximately two months’ rent. One month represents the Tenants’ failure to pay rent in May 2019 because they expected the Landlords to apply their last month’s rent deposit to that month, improperly so. The other month represents the rent for November 2019, which was due on the day of the review hearing but had not been paid as of the hearing.  

3.      Based on the evidence before me, I am satisfied on a balance of probabilities that as of the review hearing, the Tenants owed the Landlords $3,854.79, including arrears of rent to November 30, 2019 and the application filing fee. If the Tenants have since made any payments to the Landlords, including for November’s rent, those amounts should be deducted from the amount owing in this order.

L2 Application: Persistent late payments and Landlords’ own use

4.      The parties do not dispute that the Tenants paid their rent late for eight of the 13 months between May 2018 and May 2019 as claimed in the N8 Notice of Termination. Therefore, I am satisfied on a balance of probabilities that the Tenants have persistently paid their rent late.

5.      I am satisfied on a balance of probabilities that the Landlords genuinely intend to occupy the rental unit for residential purposes for a period of at least one year.

6.      The Landlords testified that they recently sold their home in Mississauga and they intend to move into the rental unit so they can be closer to the hospital where the female Landlord has regular appointments and treatments and so they can downsize in preparation for the male Landlord’s retirement.

7.      The Tenants testified that they do not believe that the Landlords genuinely intend to move into the rental unit because the Landlords have repeatedly threatened to evict the Tenants in connection with various behaviour, including after the Landlords discovered that the Tenants were growing marijuana plants in the rental unit. The Landlords denied that they served the N12 Notice of Termination for any ulterior motive, including in response to the Tenants’ marijuana plants. The only evidence the Tenants produced to corroborate their position is a text message from the male Landlord from March 30, 2019, stating that if the Tenants denied the Landlords’ access to the rental unit to repair a faucet there would be an eviction notice. However, I do not find that this is sufficient evidence of an ulterior motive, especially compared to the evidence the Landlords submitted in support of their position that they are intending to move into the unit.

8.      The Landlords submitted the agreement of purchase and sale for their previous home. The Landlords also submitted booking confirmations for Airbnb units that they have been living in since the sale of their home closed. Together this evidence supports the Landlords’ testimony that they were intending to move into the rental unit after they sold their previous home, they are still waiting for this to happen and they are prepared to move into the rental unit as soon as it becomes vacant. The Landlords also submitted a letter from the female Landlord’s doctor confirming that the female Landlord receives weekly treatments at the Princess Margaret Hospital, which is closer to the rental unit than it was to the Landlords’ previous home.

9.      The parties do not dispute that the Landlords paid the one month’s compensation required by sections 48.1 and 55.1 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the “Act”) by waiving the Tenants’ rent for April 2019.

Section 83 considerations

10.   I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Act, and find that it would be unfair to grant relief from eviction pursuant to subsection 83(1) of the Act.

11.   The Tenants have had considerable time to find another place to live since they received the notices of termination months ago. The Tenants’ have a combined household income of over $5,000.00, which is not insignificant. Although the Tenants testified that they have had a difficult time finding another affordable place to move into, they did not bring proof of the results of their housing search in support of this claim.

12.   The Tenants raised a maintenance issue in the rental unit – a portion of the ceiling which is discoloured. The Tenants did not submit evidence to establish that this is a serious breach of the Landlords’ responsibilities under the Act to merit mandatory refusal of eviction under subsection 83(3) of the Act. On the other hand, the Landlords submitted an inspection report for the unit that was prepared in May 2019, which indicates that the discolouration is a stain that can be removed with a foot powder spray.

13.   Ultimately, I find there is no reason to delay eviction any further.

14.   Since the arrears have changed since the original order was issued, I find it appropriate to replace that order with an updated calculation of the amount owing.

It is ordered that:

 

1.      The request to review order TSL-07666-19 issued on August 30, 2019 is granted.

2.      Order TSL-07666-19 issued on August 30, 2019 is replaced with the following terms.

3.      The tenancy between the Landlords and the Tenants is terminated, as of December 1, 2019. The Tenants must move out of the rental unit on or before December 1, 2019.

4.      The Tenants shall pay to the Landlords $1,238.53*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to November 20, 2019, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlords owe on the rent deposit.

5.      The Tenants shall also pay to the Landlords $60.24 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting November 21, 2019 to the date the Tenants move out of the unit.

6.      The Tenants shall also pay to the Landlords $190.00 for the cost of filing the application.

7.      If the Tenants do not pay the Landlords the full amount owing* on or before December 1, 2019, the Tenants will start to owe interest.  This will be simple interest calculated from December 2, 2019 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

8.      If the unit is not vacated on or before December 1, 2019, then starting December 2, 2019, the Landlords may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

9.      Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlords on or after December 2, 2019.
