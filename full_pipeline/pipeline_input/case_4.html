Order under Sections 30, 31 and 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Numbers: SWL-13901-18

SWT-14627-18

 

 


Ontario Ltd. (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict KM (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes (the ‘L1 Application’).

 

The Tenant applied for an order determining that the Landlord, the Landlord’s superintendent or the Landlord’s agent withheld or deliberately interfered with the reasonable supply of a vital service, care service, or food that the Landlord is obligated to supply under the tenancy agreement (the ‘T2 Application’).

 

The Tenant also applied for an order determining that the Landlord failed to meet the Landlord’s maintenance obligations under the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act') or failed to comply with health, safety, housing or maintenance standards (the ‘T6 Application’).

 

These applications were heard in [CITY] on May 23, 2018.  The Landlord’s Agent, AA, the Tenant, the Tenant’s Agent, GL, and the witness, BB, attended the hearing.

 

Preliminary Issues:

 

1.     The Tenant’s applications are amended to remove A.A. as a party.

2.     The submitted tenancy agreement indicates that the Landlord is a second mortgagee.  The first mortgagee, P.B., was not served with Notice of the Hearing.  However, as A.A. advised that her corporation had purchased the entirety of P.B.’s interest in the rental unit, I find no prejudice to P.B. in proceeding with the hearing in his absence. 

Determinations and Reasons:

The L1 Application

 

3.     The Tenant has not paid the total rent she was required to pay for the period from February 1, 2018 to May 31, 2018.  Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination effective February 17, 2018.

4.     The Tenant is in possession of the rental unit.

5.     The monthly rent is $1,050.00.

6.     The Landlord collected a rent deposit of $1,050.00 from the Tenant and this deposit is still being held by the Landlord.

7.     Interest on the rent deposit is owing to the Tenant for the period from July 22, 2017 to February 17, 2018.

8.     The parties agree that the amount outstanding to May 31, 2018, inclusive of rent arrears ($4,200.00) and costs ($175.00), is $4,375.00.  At the hearing, the Tenant agreed that she was four months in arrears.  While she acknowledged that she wilfully withheld the rent in retaliation for the outstanding maintenance issues, she also testified that P.B. had attended at the rental unit and instructed her to pay the rent only to him, rather than A.A. in response to a financial conflict between the two mortgagees.  It is unclear if P.B.’s interference was a substantial factor in the Tenant’s failure to pay the arrears as she did not pay him any portion of the outstanding balance either.

9.     The Tenant supports three children on a rather limited income and has so far been unable to find alternate housing in her price range.  She requested that the eviction be postponed until the end of June, 2018 to allow her children to complete their school year.  On the balance of probabilities, I find that the Tenant will reasonably require additional time to either void the order for eviction or secure new accommodations.

10.  I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Act and find that it would not be unfair to postpone the eviction until June 30, 2018, pursuant to subsection 83(1)(b) of the Act.

The T2 Application

11.  According to the Tenant’s description of this issue in the T2 Application and the evidence submitted at the hearing, the problem related to a maintenance issue with the hot water heater rather than a situation in which the Landlord or its agent withheld hot water from the Tenant or wilfully interfered with its supply.

12.  As a result, I find that the Tenant should have included this issue under the T6 Application rather than under the present T2 Application.  As the Act provides different remedies with regard to a maintenance application compared to a T2 Application, the forms are not interchangeable.

13.  Since the Landlord received notice that the Tenant intended to raise this issue at the hearing, the matter of the hot water heater has been addressed below under the proper T6 Application.

14.  Therefore, I do not find that the Landlord, the Landlord's superintendent or the Landlord's agent withheld a reasonable supply of a vital service, care service, or food that the Landlord was obligated to supply under the tenancy agreement or deliberately interfered with the reasonable supply of vital service, care service, or food.

The T6 Application – Water Leaks and the Kitchen Sink

15.  The Tenant testified that she discovered that the kitchen sink was leaking when she moved into the rental unit on July 21, 2017.  The sink is located on a kitchen island with cabinets.  She notified the Landlord of these issues on August 20, 2017 and around the same time, she also discovered a maggot infestation that appeared to originate from under the kitchen island.  On or about September 14, 2017, A.A.’s husband, O.A., attended at the rental unit to investigate and conduct repairs.  She stated that he removed the sink and cupboards, but promised to return the next day to complete the work.  However, this did not occur.  Removal of the sink did not resolve the underlying leak issue.

16.  At the hearing, she submitted photographs of the flooring that had been under the moved kitchen island, showing a considerable amount of food and other debris accumulated in this area.  Other images show several maggots moving along what appears to be a baseboard or some other surface in the rental unit.  She recalled that these photographs were taken at some point in September, 2017.  She testified that in October, 2017, she discovered the remains of a one kilogram bag of dog food under the baseboards under the island.  This and other garbage wedged into the flooring apparently provided a food source for the maggots.

17.  She testified that O.A. did not return for five months and the issue was only resolved when she replaced the cabinetry at her own expense.  She submitted a receipt from B.B., her own contractor.  This document indicated that he installed kitchen cabinets, a new countertop, sink and faucet and completed all associated electrical plumbing and electrical work.  He also installed a new dishwasher.  The total that B.B. charged for this work was $600.00.  The receipt indicated that the Tenant had paid $125.00 of this amount as of February 22, 2018, leaving a balance of $475.00 outstanding.  The Tenant testified that she ultimately paid the full balance as A.A. refused to pay on the grounds that she had not authorized the work.

18.  B.B. has known the Tenant for approximately twenty years.  He has approximately twelve years’ experience in carpentry and related trades.  He testified that A.A. sent him to pick up a new kitchen cabinet at Habitat for Humanity (‘Habitat’) for the rental unit at some point in February, 2018 and advised him that she had a credit with that agency.  When he attended at Habitat, he discovered that no such credit was on file.  Habitat also had no cabinet set aside for the Landlord as she had claimed.  He testified that despite an agreement with A.A., A.A. only paid $125.00 of his $600.00 invoice for the work completed in the rental unit; he confirmed that the Tenant paid the balance.  He ultimately obtained the cabinets for the rental unit at Habitat, but emphasized that these were not owned or set aside by the Landlord.

19.  B.B. also confirmed that the state of the Tenant’s kitchen was “deplorable” and that she was using cutlery stored in boxes for meals as there was no usable kitchen in the residence.

20.  The Tenant testified that she was understandably anxious over the presence of maggots in the rental unit.

21.  While she waited on repairs to her kitchen and now removed sink, the Tenant was compelled to wash her dishes in the bathroom sink and tub, as confirmed by photographs submitted at the hearing.  She testified that the kitchen island and leak issues were eventually resolved in early January, 2018.

22.  A.A. did not dispute the Tenant’s evidence of the leak or the ensuing maggots in the kitchen, but emphasized that she opened up the part of the floor where they were discovered and waited one day to confirm that the problem had been resolved.  I agree with A.A. that there was no logic in replacing the cabinetry until the maggots had been eradicated.  However, on the evidence, she appears to have taken no further steps in September, 2017 to confirm that the problem was fully resolved when the Tenant did not advise her whether or not there were still maggots in the kitchen.

23.  With respect to a serious health issue such as vermin in the rental unit, I find that it was incumbent on the Landlord to take proactive measures to confirm that these pests have been fully exterminated.

24.  For this reason, I find that the Landlord should have entered the rental unit to resolve this issue as soon as possible.  While A.A.’s consideration of the Tenant’s privacy while her mother was hospitalized is laudable, the parties agreed that the Tenant was absent from the rental unit for much of October, 2017.  During that period, the Landlord could have easily completed the necessary repairs to the kitchen island in the Tenant’s absence with minimal disruption to her enjoyment of the residence.

25.  As a result, I do not find that the Landlord resolved this issue in an appropriately timely manner and therefore breached its duties under subsection 20(1) of the Act for providing and maintaining a residential complex, including the rental units in it, in a good state of repair and fit for habitation and for complying with health, safety, housing and maintenance standards.

26.  As I find that the Landlord’s breach caused substantial disruption to the Tenant’s ability to normally reside in the rental unit and significantly impinged on her use and enjoyment of the residence, I find that the Tenant is entitled to an abatement of rent structured as follows:

a.   Loss of use of the kitchen sink from August 20, 2017 to January 1, 2018 at 15% ($697.15);

b.   Loss of the kitchen island from September 14, 2017 to January 31, 2018 at 5% ($237.62);

c.   Maggot infestation in rental unit for one month at 50% ($525.00).

27.  Since neither the Tenant nor B.B. were particularly specific in their recollections of dates, I have selected the narrowest ranges of dates provided in their evidence at the hearing as the most reliable evidence.  This remedy is intended to include compensation for the stress suffered by the Tenant as a result of the Landlord’s delay in restoring her kitchen.

28.  While the Tenant also requested compensation for her out of pocket expenses incurred to pay B.B. for his renovation work, the evidence indicated that the Tenant was not authorized to replace the dishwasher in the rental unit and did not lead evidence to suggest that this work related to a maintenance breach properly brought to the Landlord’s attention, let alone included as a cause of action in either application.  As B.B.’s invoice failed to itemize the cost of the replacement cabinetry separate from the unauthorized upgrades, it is not possible to order compensation for the eligible items alone.  As a result, this compensation will not be ordered.

29.   Lastly, the Tenant requested an award of $500.00 in “non-pecuniary damages” based upon the stress she endured while the Landlord’s delayed repairing the issues in the rental unit.  The Tenant most likely seeks an award of general damages pursuant to paragraph (9) of subsection 30(1) of the Act.  As I noted above, the ordered abatement is intended to address the emotional consequences of the Landlord’s conduct. 

30.   As well, the threshold for imposing general damages would require extremely egregious conduct on the part of the Landlord.  The leading authority on this issue is the Ontario Divisional Court’s analysis in Mejia v. Cagini.[1]  In that case, an unquestionably toxic relationship between the parties culminated in an assault upon the tenant by three friends of the landlord, in the landlord’s presence.  The Ontario Rental Housing Tribunal found the assault took place in the presence of the tenant’s family and that its purpose was to put pressure upon the tenant to vacate the premises.  The tenant was required to attend hospital for treatment.  The Tribunal did not find that the landlord had himself committed the assault, but found that he did nothing to stop it and that he was responsible for the actions of his friends.  The Tribunal found that the landlord had thereby interfered with the reasonable use and enjoyment of the apartment by the tenant and his family and awarded general damages.

31.   In light of all of the circumstances, I do not find that the Landlord’s conduct in the present case rises to the level of seriousness that would warrant an order of general damages apart from the ordered abatement. 

The Hot Water Heater

32.  The Tenant testified that whenever it rains, the basement tends to flood.  She offered no other details on the amount of water in the basement or its effect on her use of the space.  However, between August 28 and August 29, 2017, the Windsor area received substantial rainfall, resulting in flooding of many homes, including this rental unit.  The Tenant stated that she “lost everything in the flood” and that her washer and dryer as well as the hot water heater were immersed in over one foot of water.

33.  The Landlord did not replace the heater for four weeks.  In the interim, the Tenant states that she was forced to wash her laundry elsewhere, taking cabs about the city.  While she described this out of pocket expense as “cabs were killer,” she did not explain why she was unable to use a more economical method of public transit.  Although A.A. did not contest the Tenant’s evidence that the hot water heater was not repaired for four weeks, the Tenant did not offer any explanation why her laundry could not have been washed with cold water as she did not suggest that the washing machine was disabled by the flood.  Although she estimated the total cost of commuting through Windsor to attend commercial laundries at $300.00, she was unable to substantiate this estimate with any receipts or other documentation at the hearing.

34.  However, the Tenant acknowledged that she waited for two or three days before notifying A.A. of the flood in her basement as she presumed that A.A. must know that the house was affected.  This necessarily delayed resolution of the issue and may have caused irreparable damage to the heater that could have been prevented had the Tenant contacted A.A. more promptly.

35.  While the Tenant testified about high hydro bills and the Landlord’s slow and allegedly inadequate cleaning of the basement, as these issues were not mentioned in the applications, the Tenant denied the Landlord reasonable notice that she intended to raise these issues at the hearing.  As a result, these issues will form no part of the order to follow other than as context for the deteriorating relationship between the parties.

36.  A.A. testified that as soon as she was notified of the water in the basement, she attended at the rental unit.  She attempted to contact a restoration company; however, as much of Windsor had been affected by severe flooding, none were available in short order.

37.  However, when she and O.A. attended at the rental unit to inspect the damage, the Tenant’s boyfriend told them not to worry about the basement and that he would take care of it.  While the Tenant was reluctant to allow A.A. to enter the rental unit or to assist her with cleaning the basement, A.A. insisted on addressing the issue as soon as possible to avoid further damage.  She testified that she was able to remove most of the Tenant’s property from the affected area and cleaned approximately half of the basement. 

38.  In her own testimony, the Tenant expressed dissatisfaction with the assistance offered by A.A. and her husband, more in relation to the speed of their efforts.  As she put it, they “moved like turtles.”

39.  Despite numerous hisses and gasps of derision, the Tenant did not substantively contest A.A.’s testimony that her boyfriend largely commandeered the repairs to the basement, insisting on various supplies and interfering with the efforts of the Landlord’s contractors to replace the tank.  This version of events was confirmed by a letter from E.P., A.A.’s regular contractor stating that when he initially attended at the rental unit to complete the work, the Tenant’s boyfriend interfered with his efforts and insisted that he complete the work himself.  To that end, the boyfriend gave A.A. a lengthy list of materials that a later electrician deemed unnecessary and serving only to waste time.

40.  Unlike the issues in the kitchen, the evidence with respect to the delay in replacing the water heater includes a significant element of interference by the Tenant and her boyfriend despite the Landlord’s concerted efforts to resolve the issue.  As well, particular circumstances related to the widespread flooding of basements throughout the city at the time hampered A.A.’s reasonable access to remediation that could have been more readily available at other times.

41.  Putting aside the Tenant’s failure to request a remedy for the items allegedly damaged in the basement by the flood itself, she also failed to demonstrate that the Landlord could have reasonably anticipated this event.  In McQuestion v. Schneider, the Ontario Court of Appeal held that liability for injuries or damages could not be imposed on a landlord who had no knowledge or could not reasonably be expected to have knowledge of a maintenance problem.[2]  The Court held that then subsection 96(1) of the Landlord and Tenant Act (now subsection 20(1) of the Act):

… does not impose an absolute liability upon a landlord for any injuries or damages that may be caused by a latent defect, of which the landlord has no knowledge, nor could reasonably be expected to have had such knowledge. To alter the law so drastically as to impose strict liability on a landlord, regardless of his knowledge or constructive knowledge, would require much more precise language.[3]

42.  In light of the all of evidence at the hearing, I find that the extreme flooding in August, 2017 was not reasonably foreseeable.  While the Tenant testified that the basement was subject to occasional wetness, on the evidence, the sheer volume of water entering the rental unit in the last days of August, 2017 was unexpected and unforeseeable.  There was also no evidence that the Tenant had even notified the Landlord of the more minor moisture issues in the rental unit.  As a result, I do not find that the Landlord failed to meet its maintenance obligations under subsection 20(1) of the Act with respect to the initial flooding.  As a result, the Landlord cannot be held liable for the Tenant’s initial losses caused by the flood itself. 

43.  In Onyskiw v. CJM Property Management Ltd. the Ontario Court of Appeal rejected a long-standing presumption that a landlord may be deemed immediately in breach of subsection 20(1) of the Act once a defect arises:

58.  If the legislature intended an automatic breach of the landlord’s duty to maintain and repair as soon as a service was interrupted, there would be no need for the Board to make a determination as to whether the landlord had breached its obligation. The legislation would instead simply provide that where a service has been interrupted the landlord is in breach of its duty to maintain and repair and provide that the Board “may” award a remedy under s. 30(1) which authorizes a range of remedies including an abatement of rent.

59.  The tenants’ position that the landlord is automatically in breach of its statutory obligation to maintain and repair even while something is being repaired also offends the legal maxim lex non cogit ad impossibilia: that the legislature does not intend compliance where, for all practical purposes, it is impossible: see Côté, The Interpretation of Legislation in Canada, at p. 479.

60.  Applying general principles of statutory interpretation, I would reject the tenants’ submission that, when the RTA is properly interpreted, a landlord is automatically in breach of its obligation to repair and maintain under s. 20(1) as soon as an interruption in service occurs.[4]

 

44.  The prior leading authority on this issue was the Supreme Court of Canada’s decision in Pajelle Investments Ltd. v. Herbold.[5]  However, as the Court in Onyskiw held, in light of the evolution in the law in the decades since that case was decided, Pajelle also stands for the principle that a contextual approach is necessary when considering whether the landlord responded appropriately to a maintenance defect.  Such a reading is appropriate having regard to the interpretive principle that legislation enacted to regulate an activity that is ongoing over an extended period of time invites a dynamic, as opposed to a fixed, interpretation.[6]

45.  This contextual approach is also consistent with the Board’s Interpretation Guideline 5, which states as follows:

If a landlord has done little maintenance for an extended period, and a serious condition results that takes some time to rectify, the landlord should be responsible for the tenants’ loss of use of their unit or the common facilities during the repairs.  However, if the landlord has a reasonable program of maintenance, including preventative maintenance, and is acting responsibly to rectify a problem that requires extensive repairs, an abatement of rent should not be ordered.

46.  The Tenant’s Agent relied upon an earlier decision of the Landlord and Tenant Board (the ‘Board’), CET-29301-14, issued on March 26, 2013.  While past decisions of the Board are not binding on me, it is worth noting that this order predates Onyskiw.  In CET-29301-13, the Member relied upon the now outdated contractual analysis of Offredi v. 751768 Ontario Ltd.[7]  As a result, I find that this case is of no assistance.

47.  Following the analysis in Onyskiw, I find that since the Landlord demonstrated a proactive program of maintenance and acted responsibly and in a very timely manner to resolve the Tenant’s concerns and was obstructed by the unreasonable actions of the Tenant’s boyfriend, it was not in breach of its responsibilities under subsection 20(1) of the Act with respect to the water heater.

Other Issues

48.  The tenancy agreement was signed by the Tenant and A.A. on July 20, 2017.  On the second page of the contract, the parties agreed to the following additional conditions:

Landlord agrees to supply the following:

Screens (2)

More Cupboards and Counter top for the kitchen.

1 window already ordered for the rear bedroom.

Storm door (longer)

49.  Both the Tenant and A.A. signed below this addendum.  The Tenant acknowledged that the Landlord has replaced one double pane window.

50.  However, none of these issues, or another problem with a door, were mentioned in either application.  As a result, the Tenant failed to provide the Landlord with reasonable notice that she intended to raise these issues at the hearing.  Consequently, these issues will form no part of the order to follow.

51.  This order contains all of the reasons in this matter and no further reasons will issue.

It is ordered that:

The L1 and the T6 Applications

 

1.      Unless the Tenant voids the order as set out below, the tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated.  The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before June 30, 2018.

2.      The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $1,897.87*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to June 8, 2018, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlord owes on the rent deposit and less the amount of the abatement the Landlord owes the Tenant.

3.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $34.52 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting June 9, 2018 to the date the Tenant moves out of the unit.

4.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $175.00 for the cost of filing the application.

5.      If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing* on or before June 30, 2018, the Tenant will start to owe interest.  This will be simple interest calculated from July 1, 2018 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

6.      If the unit is not vacated on or before June 30, 2018, then starting July 1, 2018, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

7.      Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord, on or after July 1, 2018.

8.      If, on or before June 30, 2018, the Tenant pays the amount of $3,965.23** to the Landlord or to the Board in trust, this order for eviction will be void.  This means that the tenancy would not be terminated and the Tenant could remain in the unit.  If this payment is not made in full and on time, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

9.      The Tenant may make a motion to the Board under subsection 74(11) of the Act to set aside this order if she pays the amount required under that subsection on or after July 1, 2018, but before the Sheriff gives vacant possession to the Landlord.  The Tenant is only entitled to make this motion once during the period of the tenancy agreement with the Landlord.

The T2 Application

10.  The Tenant’s application is dismissed.
