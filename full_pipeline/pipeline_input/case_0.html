Order under Sections 31, 69 and 89

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Numbers: TET-89650-18

TEL-90138-18

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 


A.C. (the 'Tenant') applied for an order determining that M.M. (the 'Landlord') or the Landlord's agent harassed, obstructed, coerced, threatened or interfered with the Tenant, entered the rental unit illegally, altered the locking system on a door giving entry to the rental unit or residential complex without giving the Tenant replacement keys, and substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or residential complex.

 

The Landlord applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict the Tenant because the Tenant did not pay the rent the Tenant owes, and because the Tenant has substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment or lawful right, privilege or interest of the Landlord, committed an illegal act, and wilfully or negligently caused undue damage to the premises. The Landlord has also applied for an order requiring the Tenant to compensate the Landlord for the damage. The Landlord also claimed compensation for each day the Tenant remained in the unit after the termination date.

 

This application was heard in Toronto on May 25, 2018.

 

The Tenant and the Landlord attended the hearing.

 

Determinations:

 

1.      These applications are about a friendly relationship that went very bad. So bad that criminal charges are pending against the Tenant.

2.      The parties used to be friends. The Tenant has lived in the Landlordâs one bedroom basement apartment more than once. He lived there about six years ago, moved out and then moved in again in July of 2015.


THE ARREARS OF RENT

3.      The parties agree that there was no written tenancy agreement between them but disagree as to the lawful monthly rent charged.

4.      Both the Landlord and the Tenant say that initially the monthly rent agreed to was $750.00. They also both agree that the Tenant subsequently told the Landlord he could not afford that amount.

5.      The Tenant says that they agreed the rent would be lowered to $650.00. He further says the Landlord agreed to âcook the booksâ and issue receipts for $550.00 even though the Tenant was paying $650.00. This was because the Tenant is in receipt of social assistance from Ontario Works (âOWâ) and his worker told him he had to have cheaper housing.

6.      The Landlord says that because the Tenant was financially struggling he agreed to accept payments towards the rent and the Tenant would pay any shortfall when his situation improved. They were friends so the Landlord just left the situation as it was.

7.      The only documentary evidence produced by either party about the lawful monthly rent is a series of receipts produced by the Landlord.

8.      Based on the receipts and the evidence of both parties I am satisfied that the tenancy commenced July 1, 2015 and the parties agreed to a monthly rent of $750.00.

9.      The receipts corroborate the evidence of both the Landlord and the Tenant in that they show for July and August of 2015 the Landlord collected $750.00 each month plus an additional $500.00 as a last monthâs rent deposit. The receipts also indicate that until February 28, 2016 the Landlord charged and collected $750.00 each month from the Tenant.

10.   The reason this is important is because s. 113 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the âActâ) says the lawful rent is the rent first charged to the Tenant subject to any discounts offered under s. 111.

11.   The scheme of the Act tightly regulates rent increases and decreases. None of what the Tenant describes constitutes a legal decrease in the rent under any of the relevant provisions of the Act nor was a new tenancy agreement entered into. (See for example, ss. 111, and 125.) Pursuant to s. 3 of the Act, the provisions of the Act apply despite any agreement or waiver to the contrary. So absent a legally recognizable agreement to decrease the rent or a new tenancy agreement, the lawful monthly rent here has always been $750.00.

12.   As explained further below, the Tenant was essentially illegally evicted effective April 21, 2018. The arrears of rent and daily compensation owing for the period ending April 21, 2018 total $5,443.84.

13.   The Landlord collected a last monthâs rent deposit from the Tenant of $500.00. Pursuant to subsection 106(10) of the Act the deposit must be applied to the rent due for the last month of the tenancy.

14.   Interest is owing on the deposit for the period July 1, 2015 to May 31, 2018 totalling $26.76.

15.   The Landlord incurred costs of $190.00 for filing the application and is entitled to reimbursement of those costs.

THE TENANTâS CLAIMS OF ILLEGAL ENTRY

16.   The relationship between the parties seems to have gone seriously wrong as a result of events that flowed from the rental unit having a problem with mice.

17.   The problem with the mice seems to date back to 2016 which is a period outside of the Boardâs one year limitation period (see section 29(2)). But what the Tenant is really complaining about is what happened on February 17, 2018.

18.   The Tenant complained to the Landlord about the mice in his unit so the Landlord or his spouse contacted a contractor and the Landlord told the Tenant the contractor would come in to address the issue within 48 hours. The Tenant wanted prompter action and told the Landlord that was unreasonable. According to the Tenant, the Landlord called him a âbitchâ and a âlittle wifeâ and the Tenant went back downstairs for a nap.

19.   The Landlord does not specifically deny saying these things to the Tenant. He just says the Tenant was very arrogant and being unreasonable.

20.   A couple of hours later the Landlordâs spouse texted the Tenant to say the contractor would be there in 10 minutes. According to the Tenant, the contractor then barged into his unit. The Tenant told the contractor he had not received proper notice of entry, this was a bad time, and asked him to leave. When the contractor expressed reluctance, the Tenant called 911 and the contractor left immediately. That was about 7 p.m. on February 17, 2018.

21.   Section 25 of the Act says a landlord can only enter a rental unit in accordance with the Act. Section 26 details examples of when notice is not required. In this situation none of the provisions of s. 26 apply. The mice were not an emergency despite the Tenantâs insistence that 48 hours was too long to wait, and the Tenant did not let the contractor into the unit at the time of entry. As notice of entry was not served in accordance with s. 27 of the Act, the entry was not in compliance with the Act. As the Landlord is responsible for the acts of his contractors and agents, this means I am satisfied that on February 17, 2018 the Landlord breached s. 25 of the Act.

22.   With respect to the Landlord calling the Tenant a âbitchâ and a âlittle wifeâ, the Landlord does not deny that occurred so I am satisfied that it did. Name calling like that is unprofessional behaviour. A landlord is a business professional selling a vital service to a customer so the Landlord here ought to have known that such remarks were inappropriate and would be most unwelcome to the average tenant. In other words, the Landlordâs remarks constitute a breach of s. 23 of the Act.

23.   During this series of events the Landlord actually left the City to go to the United States. He left February 17, 2018 and was away until February 23, 2018.

24.   About three hours after the Tenant got the contractor to leave, the Tenant went outside to smoke and found the tires of his car slashed. He called the police and told them he believed the contractor was responsible but the police told them there was insufficient evidence to establish that.

25.   That is also true for my purposes here. The evidence is insufficient to establish it is more likely than not the contractor slashed the Tenantâs tires. Even if I was prepared to make a finding the contractor did it, that behaviour was clearly outside of the scope of his authority from the Landlord. The Landlord hired the contractor to deal with the mice issue not to slash the Tenantâs tires, so even if the contractor did do it the evidence is insufficient to establish the Landlord should be held liable for the contractorâs behaviour.

26.   With respect to the Landlordâs breaches of the Act as found above, remedy is discussed below.

THE LANDLORDâS NOTICES OF TERMINATION

27.   After the Tenant discovered his tires slashed he became very angry. He blamed the Landlord for what happened. He broke into the Landlordâs private space upstairs. He broke the bedroom door, damaged the ceiling, and made a mess throwing plants around.

28.   The Tenant attempted to clean up and apologised to the Landlord. He offered to pay $100.00 for the damage. But when the Landlord returned from the United States, the Landlord called the police.

29.   The police came to the residential complex on or about February 25, 2018 and interviewed the parties. By that point in time the Landlord just wanted the Tenant to move out. He says his wife did not want to stay there as long as the Tenant remained in the rental unit below. So the police suggested that the Tenant could avoid criminal charges if he agreed to move out.

30.   As far as I can tell from the Tenantâs testimony, he thought better of his initial agreement to this offer and called the police station. The officers present at the house returned the Tenantâs keys to him and he was arrested and charged with mischief.

31.   On March 9, 2018 the Landlord served two notices of termination on the Tenant, an N6 for illegal act pursuant to s. 61(2)(b) and a voidable N5 pursuant to both ss. 62 and 64.

32.   At the hearing before the Board I pointed out to the parties that the Landlordâs application for an eviction order was essentially moot because the parties agree that the Landlord changed the code for the front door lock on April 21, 2018, and refused to give the Tenant the replacement code. In other words, the Tenant was for all intents and purposes illegally evicted on April 21, 2018. As the Tenantâs amended application seeks an order terminating the tenancy effective April 21, 2018, an order is going to issue terminating the tenancy on the Tenantâs application effective April 21, 2018.

33.   In other words, it was unnecessary to hear evidence in support of the allegations contained in the Landlordâs notices of termination.

THE LANDLORDâS DAMAGE CLAIM

34.   The Landlordâs application as filed seeks compensation of $1,300.00 for negligent or wilful damage to the residential complex pursuant to s. 89(1) of the Act. However, the application contains no particulars.

35.   On the day of the hearing before the Board the Landlord filed a request to amend his damage claim and served it on the Tenant. The Tenant consented to the Board amending the application as requested by the Landlord so I proceeded to hear the amended damage claim.

36.   The Landlordâs evidence establishes that he paid his contractor $1,469.00 to repair the stucco on his bedroom ceiling, prime and paint the room, and replace the door and lock.

37.   The Tenant questions the reasonableness of this amount, but led no evidence in support of the proposition that another contractor would have charged less, or in support of the possibility the Landlord did not in fact pay that amount to his contractor.

38.   According to the Landlord he received a second quote that was similar.

39.   In the end result the Landlordâs evidence is sufficient to establish that it is more likely than not that he paid $1,469.00 to repair the wilful damage the Tenant did to the Landlordâs bedroom and that amount represents the reasonable cost of repair.

40.   The second part of the Landlordâs claim is more problematic. It claims $226.00 for cleaning out the basement and disposing of the contents. It also claims $1,356.00 for âsanitizingâ the basement, cleaning the graffiti and priming and painting the rental unit.

41.   Damage claims under the Act are brought pursuant to s. 89(1) which says:

A landlord may apply to the Board for an order requiring a tenant to pay reasonable costs that the landlord has incurred or will incur for the repair of or, where repairing is not reasonable, the replacement of damaged property, if the tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or a person whom the tenant permits in the residential complex wilfully or negligently causes undue damage to the rental unit or the residential complex and the tenant is in possession of the rental unit. 

42.   As can be seen from this provision it is about physical damage to the property and not cleaning or sanitizing.

43.   More importantly perhaps, the Landlordâs behaviour in removing the Tenantâs possessions from the rental unit was actually an illegal act for the reasons stated below in the context of the Tenant application. The Landlord cannot be entitled to claim against the Tenant costs he incurred as a result of the Landlordâs own illegal behaviour.

44.   Given the above, the $226.00 claimed by the Landlord for cleaning out the basement and disposing of the contents is denied.

45.   The Tenant does not deny that what the Landlord calls graffiti on the walls of the rental unit is decoration the Tenant added to the rental unit. Nor does he deny the unit needed to be painted as a result.

46.   But the Tenant says that he offered to paint the unit himself once he removed his possessions. That was always the plan for when the Tenant moved out prior to the disintegration of the relationship. At that point the Landlord refused to let the Tenant do the work himself and the Tenant never had a chance to do it anyway because of the illegal eviction. The Landlord does not dispute this.

47.   The Tenant and the Landlord agree that before the Tenant moved in the Landlord paid the Tenant to paint the unit himself. The Landlord says he paid the Tenant $1,000.00. The Tenant says he was paid $500.00.

48.   The reason this is important is because s. 16 of the Act says:

When a landlord or a tenant becomes liable to pay any amount as a result of a breach of a tenancy agreement, the person entitled to claim the amount has a duty to take reasonable steps to minimize the personâs losses.

49.   In this context s. 16 means the Landlord was obligated to take reasonable steps to minimize the cost of repainting the unit. As the Tenant had apparently done an acceptable job in exchange for good consideration before, it is not clear to me that it was particularly reasonable for the Landlord to refuse to let him paint it again. The is particularly true here because if the Landlord had simply followed the process in the Act to obtain a legal eviction order from the Board, the undisputed evidence of the Tenant suggests the Tenant would have returned the unit to its original condition.

50.   As a result, I am not prepared to find that the Landlord reasonably mitigated the Tenantâs losses or that the costs the Landlord incurred in hiring a contractor to paint the unit were reasonable. So that part of the Landlordâs damage claim is also denied.

MISCELLANEOUS OTHER CLAIMS BY THE TENANT

51.   After the police became involved in February and the Landlord started serving notices of termination in March, the Tenant filed this application with the Board on March 23, 2018. He subsequently filed and served a request to amend the application on April 25, 2018. As the Landlord was properly served I granted the Tenantâs request to amend his application.

52.   What appears to have happened after the police became involved and notices of termination served is that the partiesâ relationship and behaviour deteriorated further.

53.   The Tenant says that on March 28, 2018, the Landlord turned on the music in his part of the residential complex, turned it up very loud, and went out. The Landlord says this was not true; he was home, it did not happen.

54.   As the Tenant bears the burden of proof with respect to this allegation and the evidence of the parties is essentially equal but opposite I find that the Tenant has led insufficient evidence to establish the Landlord harassed him on March 28, 2018 by playing music loudly and leaving it on while he went out.

55.   The Tenant says the Landlord did this a second time on March 29, 2018. So the Tenant turned off the electricity to stop the noise. That caused a malfunction in an alarm in the basement triggering an annoying intermittent beeping. When the beeping did not stop the Tenant told the Landlord he could not live like that. The Tenant says the Landlord told him it was his own fault and flipped him the middle finger.

56.   The Landlord acknowledges the Tenant turned off the electricity because the Landlord was playing music. He did not dispute the Tenantâs evidence that the Landlord told him it was his fault nor does the Landlord deny flipping him the middle finger.

57.   There can be no excuse for a landlord âflipping the fingerâ at a tenant. Any reasonable landlord knows or ought to know that such behaviour is unprofessional and would be unwelcome to any reasonable tenant. So I am satisfied that in doing so the Landlord breached s. 23 of the Act.

58.   Because of the beeping the Tenant rented alternative accommodation and stayed there from March 29, 2018 to April 22, 2018. He paid $685.00 for that alternative accommodation.

59.   Although I would agree with the Landlord that the Tenant is partially responsible for the annoying beeping because he turned off the electricity, I am satisfied that the Tenant did so because of the Landlordâs behaviour in playing loud music. That being said, the reasonable thing the Tenant should have done, but did not, was ask the Landlord to turn the music down.

60.   Given all of the above, I do not believe the Landlord can be held financially responsible for the disturbance caused by the beeping. The Tenant is not entitled to an order for $685.00 for the place he stayed in from March 29, 2018 to April 22, 2018.

61.   During this period the Tenant went back and forth to the rental unit.

62.   He says that on April 3, 2018 he noticed $150.00 was missing from his coffee table indicating someone was in his unit. The Landlord says âwhy would I take the Tenantâs money?â which I took to mean the Landlord denies entering the rental unit or stealing money from the Tenant.

63.   The evidence with respect to this money and the alleged illegal entry is simply insufficient to meet the Tenantâs burden of proof. He may have had money go missing; but he may not have. So that part of the Tenantâs application must also be dismissed.

64.   On April 17, 2018 the beeping noise was repaired.

THE CHANGING OF THE LOCKS

65.   On April 21, 2018 the Tenant discovered the key pad code to the front door had been changed but he was able to get into the residential complex because the cleaner was there. The Tenant says when he asked the Landlord for the new code, the Landlord refused so the Tenant threatened to turn the power off again. The Tenant says the Landlord was in his face yelling at that point so the Tenant called 911 again.

66.   The Landlord confirms he refused to give the Tenant the new code because the Tenant had not paid rent for the month of April, 2018.

67.   Section 24 of the Act says:

A landlord shall not alter the locking system on a door giving entry to a rental unit or residential complex or cause the locking system to be altered during the tenantâs occupancy of the rental unit without giving the tenant replacement keys. 

68.   By changing the key pad code and refusing to give him the new one the Landlord breached s. 24 of the Act. Remedy with respect to this breach is discussed below.

69.   According to the Tenant, when the police arrived they told him that nothing was in writing so he had to leave. The Tenant was given four hours to remove all of his possessions which was not sufficient time. He also says the Landlord refused to let him access his stored possessions in the garage.

70.   The Landlord confirms the police gave the Tenant four hours to remove his possessions but says the Landlord offered him more time. According to the Landlord the Tenant returned on April 30, 2018 to remove more of his possessions but the stuff left in the unit downstairs after that was garbage. So the Landlord put it out in bags and the Tenant did not come to retrieve it. However, the Landlord admits that he told the Tenant he would not let the Tenant access possessions in the garage unless the Tenant cleaned out the rental unit first.

71.   I would note at this point that the police had no right to tell the Tenant he had four hours to collect his possessions and leave. Subsection 168(2) of the Act says:

The Board has exclusive jurisdiction to determine all applications under this Act and with respect to all matters in which jurisdiction is conferred on it by this Act.  

72.   Moreover, s. 37(1) says:

A tenancy may be terminated only in accordance with this Act. 

73.   This means that only the Board had the authority to tell the Tenant when and if he had to move out.

74.   At that point in time the Landlord had filed an application with the Board for eviction but that application had not been heard. Even if the Landlord had obtained an eviction order, the Boardâs orders cannot be enforced by a landlord or by the police; they are enforceable only through the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) as they are the legal equivalent of writs of possession. The Sheriff gives tenants notice as to when they are coming and a tenant has 72 hours after a legal eviction to recover his or her possessions.

75.   Further, the Tenant here had clearly not abandoned the unit. His possessions were there. He was returning to the unit on a regular basis.

76.   So what happened to the Tenant on April 21, 2018 was also an illegal eviction. By putting the Tenant out of possession, clearing out the unit of the Tenantâs remaining items, and refusing the Tenantâs request to recover his possessions in the garage, the Landlord breached sections 22 and 23 of the Act. Again, remedy is discussed further below.  

77.   On May 3, 2018 the Landlord put the Tenantâs remaining possessions out on the curb and sent the Tenant a text showing the Tenant the bags sitting outside.

78.   The Tenant says he found a new place to live and was able to get possession of his new rental unit on April 22, 2018.

REMEDIES FOR THE LANDLORDâS BREACHES

79.   The Tenantâs application seeks: abatement of the rent; a cease and desist order; compensation for out of pocket expenses; an administrative fine; and termination of the tenancy.

80.   The request for a cease and desist order is with respect to the illegal entry and is no longer relevant as the Tenant no longer lives in the rental unit.

81.   However, the amended application also seeks an order requiring the Landlord to return to the Tenant any of his possessions still in the garage. The Tenant is entitled to such an order.

82.   The request for compensation for out of pocket expenses is for rat poison, traps and door âscrapesâ. No evidence was led in support of these claims but even if it had been they would have been denied. As I stated at the hearing, this application is not based on the rights and obligations set out in s. 20(1) of the Act. The breaches by the Landlord of the Act as set out above, are not connected to these claims so they are denied accordingly.

83.   In the amended application the Tenant seeks $500.00 for moving costs plus the cost of staying elsewhere for the period March 29, 2018 and April 22, 2018. No evidence was led that the Tenant incurred moving expenses and for the reasons already stated above, the Tenant is not entitled to a remedy for the cost of staying elsewhere due to the beeping.

84.   As stated above, the tenancy shall be terminated effective April 21, 2018. The Landlordâs behaviour in changing the locks and illegally evicting the Tenant justifies such an order.

85.   That leaves the claims for abatement and an administrative fine.

86.   Abatement of the rent is a contractual remedy entitled to reflect the idea that the Tenant is paying for a bundle of goods and services and if the Tenant is not receiving those goods and services the rent should be abated proportional to the difference between what is being paid for and what is being received.

87.   The Tenant here is clearly entitled to abatement of the rent.  

88.   With respect to the illegal entry by the Landlordâs contractor on February 17, 2018, although the entry was illegal and the Tenant was entitled to notice of a specific date and time at least 24 hours in advance, one of the reasons the Tenant did not receive proper notice is because he insisted the mice problem be addressed quickly. The 48 hoursâ notice he was given was not quick enough. So it seems to me to be somewhat unreasonable to award the Tenant any significant abatement for the entry as the Landlord was simply trying to acquiesce to the Tenantâs demands. As a result, only a minimal abatement is warranted which I fix at $50.00.

89.   With respect to the name calling on February 17, 2018, and the Landlord flipping the Tenant the finger on March 29, 2018, abatement of the rent is justified. The Tenant led little evidence as to how these events impacted him but he does say he felt belittled and depressed. Given all of the evidence before me and my knowledge of similar like cases before the Board, it seems to me that a reasonable abatement of the rent for these two breaches would be $200.00 each for a total of $400.00.

90.   With respect to the changing of the locks, the disposal of the Tenantâs possessions, the refusal to let the Tenant access the garage, and the illegal eviction, I believe a reasonable abatement of the rent would be two monthâs rent or $1,600.00. I say this based on similar like cases before the Board and because even though the Tenant found another place to live the day following the illegal eviction, the sequence of events described above was clearly distressing to the Tenant.

91.   The final request in the amended application is for an administrative fine.

92.   With respect to the request for an administrative fine the Boardâs Interpretation Guideline # 16 on Administrative Fines says:

An administrative fine is a remedy to be used by the Board to encourage compliance with the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the âRTAâ), and to deter landlords from engaging in similar activity in the future. This remedy is not normally imposed unless a landlord has shown a blatant disregard for the RTA and other remedies will not provide adequate deterrence and compliance.

93.   The Landlord here has clearly shown a blatant disregard for the Act. Changing the locks, illegal evicting the Tenant, and refusing to give him access to his possessions all demonstrate an utter disregard for the Tenantâs rights and the law.

94.   In terms of quantum for the fine, the remedies granted above to the Tenant are relatively minor. Further, the nature of the behaviour in question is such that a substantial fine is warranted. Given all of the above, and my knowledge of similar like circumstances I am satisfied an administrative fine of $5,000.00 is warranted. An order shall issue accordingly.

95.   This order contains all of the reasons for the decision within it. No further reasons shall be issued.

It is ordered that:

1.      On or before August 15, 2018, the Landlord shall deliver to the Tenant any of the Tenantâs possessions still in the Landlordâs control and give the Tenant access to the garage at the residential complex on a date and time of the Tenantâs choosing.

2.      The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $4,336.08. This amount represents rent arrears and daily compensation owing up to April 21, 2018, plus compensation for undue damage to the residential complex caused by the Tenantâs wilful or negligent behaviour, less the Tenantâs last monthâs rent deposit and interest owing on the deposit and the amount the Landlord owes the Tenant for abatements of the rent.

3.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $190.00 for the cost of filing the application.

4.      The total amount the Tenant owes the Landlord under this order is $4,526.08.

5.      If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing by August 3, 2018, the Tenant will owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from August 4, 2018 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

6.      The Landlord shall pay $5,000.00 to the Board as an administrative fine on or before August 31, 2018.
