Order under Section 21.2 of the

Statutory Powers Procedure Act

and the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TNL-28887-20-RV

 

 

In the matter of:      504, 1309 WILSON AVENUE

TORONTO ON M3M1J4

 

Between:                  Metcap Living Management Inc.                                                                 Landlord

 

 

and

 

Jhon Palacio

Luz Ofelia Giraldo

Tenants

 

 

Review Order

 

Metcap Living Management Inc. (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict Luz Ofelia Giraldo and Jhon Palacio (the 'Tenants') because the Tenants did not pay the rent that the Tenants owe.

 

This application was resolved by order TNL-28887-20 issued on August 16, 2021.

 

On November 3, 2021, the Tenants requested a review of the order because the Tenants’ Representative (who is an occupant of the rental unit) was not able to participate in the proceedings as the Representative was out of the country on the date of the hearing.

 

On November 5, 2021, the Board issued interim order TNL-28887-20 staying the order issued on August 16, 2021.

 

The Tenants’ request was heard by way of video conference on January 25, 2022. The Tenants’ Representative (spouse), Israel Sosa, the Tenants’ Agent, Ricardo Colonnese and the Landlord’s Legal Representative, Sara Matthew attended the hearing.

 

Preliminary Issues

 

The Tenants submitted in writing to the Board that Israel Sosa (‘IS’) is authorized to represent their interest at the hearing. IS is the spouse of the Tenant, Luz Ofelia Giraldo. IS also lives in the rental unit.

 

Determinations:

 

Tenants’ Request to Review

 

1.        IS testified that on the date of hearing, May 10, 2021, he did not attend because he was out of the country tending to his mother who was ill. IS testified that he had difficulty returning to Canada due to the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic. He only returned to Canada on October 16, 2021.

 

2.        IS testified that he represents his spouse in most matters as she has difficulty speaking English. Further, his spouse was also out of the country at the time of the hearing and had difficulty returning due to the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic.

 

3.        IS testified that because both he and his spouse were out of the country when the Notice of Hearing was mailed, they did not receive it until their return to Canada in October 2021.

 

4.        The Tenants’ Agent submitted that IS’ evidence is clear that the Tenants were not reasonably able to participate in the hearing.

 

5.        The Landlord’s Representative submitted that IS’ evidence is reasonable as to why the Tenants could not participate in the hearing.

 

Findings

 

6.        Based on the uncontested evidence before me, I am satisfied that the Tenants were not reasonably able to participate in the hearing. I say this because the Tenants and IS were out of country when the Notice of Hearing was mailed to their rental unit and on the date of the hearing. The Tenants had no idea a hearing was scheduled.

 

7.        The Tenants’ request to review the order is granted.

 

New Hearing of Landlord’s L1 Application

 

8.        The Tenants have not paid the total rent the Tenants were required to pay for the period from February 1, 2020 to January 31, 2022. Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination effective October 26, 2020.

 

9.        The Tenants are in possession of the rental unit.

 

10.   The lawful monthly rent is $$1,077.10.

 

11.   The Landlord collected a rent deposit of $894.87 from the Tenants and this deposit is still being held by the Landlord.

 

12.   Interest on the rent deposit is owing to the Tenants for the period from January 1, 2020 to October 26, 2020.

 

13.   The Tenants paid $7,178.87 after the application was filed.

 

14.   The parties agreed the amount owed to the Landlord is $11,132.93 which is inclusive of arrears of rent ($10,946.93) and the cost ($186.00) for filing the application.

 

Landlord’s Evidence

 

15.   The Landlord’s Representative submitted that the Landlord has reached out to the Tenants by way of letter on October 7, 2020 and October 27, 2020 requesting a repayment plan for the arrears of rent. The Tenants never responded to the Landlord’s request.

 

16.   The Landlord’s Representative submitted that the Landlord cannot accept a payment plan longer than 6 months. Later in the hearing, during closing submissions, the Landlord’s Representative submitted that if the Board grants relief by way of payment plan, it is the Landlord’s request that the plan be less than 2 years in length.

 

17.   The Landlord’s Representative further submitted that since early 2020 the Tenants have consistently been in arrears of rent. As such, the Landlord requires assurance that the Tenants will pay the arrears of rent and the monthly rent.

 

Tenants’ Evidence

 

18.   IS testified that because of the impact of COVID-19, he lost his job in 2020. His income was insufficient to pay the monthly rent.

 

19.   IS testified that since November 2021, he has been gainfully employed and can afford to pay the monthly rent plus $600.00 a month for the arrears of rent. This translates to an 18-month payment plan.

 

20.   IS testified that he and his family would have no place to go if they were evicted. They want to stay in the rental unit. IS advised that the household monthly income is approximately $4,100.00 to $4,600.00.

 

21.   The Tenant’s Agent submitted that relief from eviction by way of payment plan has minimal prejudice to the Landlord as they would receive repayment of the arrears of rent and the monthly rent in full. Further, if the Tenants default on the payments, the Landlord could rely on section 78 of the Act to evict the Tenants.

 

Relief from Eviction

 

22.   I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would not be unfair to grant relief from eviction subject to the condition(s) set out in this order pursuant to subsection 83(1)(a) and 204(1) of the Act. In making my finding, I considered the fact that the Tenants’ proposed repayment plan is reasonable and is less than two years as requested by the Landlord’s Representative. Further, should the Tenants default in making any of the payments, including the monthly rent, the Landlord can apply to the Board under section 78 to evict the Tenants. I am satisfied in this matter that relief from eviction by way of a payment plan is the most just and fair decision.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.        The Tenants’ request to review the order is granted.

 

2.        Order TNL-28887-20 issued on August 16, 2021 is cancelled and replaced by the following:

 

3.        The Tenants owe the Landlord the amount of $11,132.93 which represents rent owing to January 31, 2022 and the cost to the Landlord to file this application with the Board.

 

4.        The Tenants shall pay to the Landlord the total amount owing as follows:

 

a)     Starting February 1, 2022, through to and including August 1, 2023, the Tenants shall pay $600.00 each month on or before the 1st day of the month.

 

b)     On September 1, 2023, the Tenants shall pay to the Landlord the final arrears of rent payment in the amount of $332.93.

 

c)      The Tenants shall also pay the Landlord the rent for the months February 2022 up to and including September 2023 in full, on or before the 1st day of each corresponding month.

 

5.        If the Tenants fails to make any of the payments in accordance with paragraph 4, and by the dates required, then:

 

(a) The Landlord may apply under section 78 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’) for an order terminating the tenancy and evicting the Tenants, and for the payment of any new arrears of rent and NSF charges not already ordered under paragraph 2 of this order. The Landlord must make the application within 30 days of a breach of a condition set out in paragraph 4 of this order.
