Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: EAL-93117-20

 

 

In the matter of:

116, 31 BAYSWATER PLACE KINGSTON ON K7M2B8

 

Between:

Osgoode Properties

Landlord

 

 

and

 

 

Peter Johnson

Tenant

 

 

Osgoode Properties (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict Peter Johnson (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or someone the Tenant permitted in the residential complex has substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment or lawful right, privilege or interest of the Landlord or another tenant. The Landlord also claimed compensation for each day the Tenant remained in the unit after the termination date.

 

This application was heard by videoconference on November 24, 2021 at 10:17am. Only the Landlord attended the hearing. The Landlord was represented by Anne Skelly. Also in attendance was the Landlord’s witness, Megan Wheatley.

Determinations:

 

PRELIMINARY ISSUE

 

1.     At the hearing on November 24, 2021, William Florence, a lawyer with the Kingston Community Legal Clinic, interrupted the proceedings on at least two occasions in attempts to advocate for the Tenant who was not present at the hearing; he advised that he had been working with the Tenant on other matters against the Landlord.

 

2.     WL indicated that on August 19, 2021, the legal clinic wrote to the Board requesting access to all active applications with respect to this tenancy; WL admitted there was no reference on this request to specific file numbers. The Board’s records did not then, and still do not, show a record of this request.

 

3.     After I inquired with WL more than once regarding his present status on this file, he confirmed that he had not been retained to represent the Tenant on this application before the Board on November 24, 2021 nor did he receive any instructions from the Tenant in regard to this file. This is confirmed by the agency authorization form submitted

 


to the Board on November 25, 2021 (the day after the hearing) where it appears the Tenant has signed an authorization form allowing the legal clinic access to his file.

 

4.     I proceeded to hear the matter uncontested and directed WL to cease from interrupting the proceedings as his own evidence confirmed that he was not a party to the application.

 

L2 APPLICATION

 

5.     By way of background this is a month-to-month tenancy in which rent is due on the first of the month in the amount of $879.06. The tenancy commenced four years ago and the Tenant is the sole occupant of the rental unit.

 

6.     The Landlord’s L2 application is based on a first N5 notice of termination served to the Tenant on December 7, 2020 with a termination date of December 27, 2020 pursuant to subsection 64(1) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006.

 

7.     As this was a first N5 notice of termination, the Tenant had seven days to void the notice by correcting the behaviour complained of on the notice of termination. This remedy period was between December 7 -15, 2020.

 

8.     The Landlord testified that the Tenant did not void the N5 notice of termination as the Tenant failed to provide proof of insurance by the end of the remedy period. Thus, I find the Tenant did not void the N5 notice of termination and I proceeded to hear the Landlord’s claim for termination of the tenancy based on substantial interference.

 

9.     The Landlord’s witness, Megan Wheatley (MW), who is an employee of the Landlord testified that part of her job includes going through tenants’ paperwork to ensure their insurance information is current in the Landlord’s files. MW conducts this task on a monthly basis and has been trying to obtain this information from the Tenant for the past

1.5 years without success.

 

10.  MW testified that on October 17, 2020, she wrote to the Tenant requesting proof of insurance but did not receive a response from the Tenant; a second letter was sent on November 3, 2020 seeking an update on the request made on October 17, 2020 but once again, no response was received from the Tenant. A final letter was sent on November 18, 2020 requesting a copy of the policy or a letter from the insurance agent with specific particulars – MW testified that the Tenant failed to response to this letter as well. As such, the Landlord resorted to the issuance of the N5 notice of termination. A copy of these letters was submitted into evidence.

 

11.  MW testified that she has also had many face-to-face conversations with the Tenant and that he is aware h needs to provide one but has failed to follow-through.

 

12.  The Landlord submitted a copy of the Tenant’s lease agreement which commenced on June 1, 2017, and which states, at section 36, the following and is initialled by the Tenant:

 

 

 

 

13.  The Landlord also submitted a copy of the Tenant’s insurance policy that took effect on June 1, 2017 to May 31, 2018 confirming coverage during this period.

 

14.  The Landlord seeks a termination of the tenancy and submits that by failing to keep an updated insurance policy and by failing to provide this information to the Landlord when requested, puts the Landlord’s insurance policy at risk and makes the Landlord liable in any adverse incidents. The Landlord submits that they have provided ample opportunity to the Tenant to provide this information prior to commencing legal proceedings.

 

15.  Based on the uncontested evidence before me, I find that the Tenant has substantially interfered with the Landlord's lawful rights, privileges or interests of the Landlord; I say this as the Landlord had a lawful right to require the Tenant to provide proof that he has the requisite tenant insurance in full force and effect upon request of the Landlord at any time. The Landlord also demonstrated that it requested the required proof on multiple occasions: once in October 2020 and twice in November 2020. The Tenant’s failure or refusal to provide requested proof of insurance coverage served as a complete interference with that lawful right.

 

16.  I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would be unfair to grant relief from eviction pursuant to subsection 83(1) of the Act. The Landlord is unaware of any circumstances that would justify relief from eviction. The Landlord attempted to work with the Tenant to help him improve the cleanliness of the rental unit and remove his unwanted guests, but the Tenant stopped cooperating.

 

17.  This order contains all of the reasons for my decision within it. No further reasons shall be issued.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     The tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated, as of December 27, 2020. The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before February 19, 2022.

 

2.     The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $10,887.30 (less any amount paid by the Tenants to the Landlord after the application was filed with the Board), which represents compensation for the use of the unit from December 28, 2020 to February 8, 2022, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlord owes on the rent deposit.

 

3.     The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $28.90 per day for compensation for the use of the unit from February 9, 2022 to the date the Tenant moves out of the unit.

 

4.     The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $186.00 for the cost of filing the application.

 

5.     If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing on or before February 19, 2022, the Tenant will start to owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from February 20, 2022 at 2.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

 

6.     If the unit is not vacated on or before February 19, 2022, then starting February 20, 2022, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

 

7.     Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord on or after February 20, 2022.
