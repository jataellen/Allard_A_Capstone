Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: HOL-08509-20

 

 

In the matter of:

37 RITCHIE DRIVE

GRAND VALLEY ON L9W6W4

 

Between:

Harsha Perera Shanika Perera

Landlords

 

 

and

 

 

Daniel Heenan Lauren Tilly

Tenants

 

 

Harsha Perera and Shanika Perera (the 'Landlords') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict Daniel Heenan and Lauren Tilly (the 'Tenants') because they, another occupant of the rental unit or someone they permitted in the residential complex have wilfully or negligently caused undue damage to the premises. The Landlords have also applied for an order requiring the Tenants to compensate the Landlords for the damage.

 

This hearing was held by videoconference on January 6, 2022. The Landlords and the Landlords’ representative, Goby Arumai, attended the hearing. One Tenant, Daniel Heenan, and the Tenants’ representative, Carrie Aylwin, also attended the hearing.

 

Determinations:

 

1.     On October 1, 2020 the Landlords submitted an L2 application to the Board applying to evict the Tenants as a result of damage to the hardwood flooring and hardwood staircase of the rental unit. The Landlords also requested $7702.34 in compensation for this damage.

 

2.     Board Order HOL-08509-20, issued on January 28, 2022, terminated this tenancy on October 31, 2020 on the basis that the Tenants had vacated the rental unit on that date. Therefore, the Landlords application for eviction is moot, and the only issue for the Board to consider is the Landlord’s claim for compensation for damage.

 

3.     Section 89(1)(a) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act') states:

 

A Landlord may apply to the Board for an order requiring a tenant or former tenant to pay reasonable costs that the landlord has incurred or will incur for the repair of or, where repairing is not reasonable, the replacement of damaged property if,

 

(a)      While the tenant or former tenant is or was in possession of the rental unit, the tenant or former tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or a person

 

permitted in the residential complex by the tenant or former tenant wilfully or negligently causes or caused undue damage to the rental unit or the residential complex.

 

4.     The Tenants were in possession of the rental unit on the date of the Landlords’ L2 application for damages to the Board. The Landlords submitted this application for damages on October 1, 2020 and the Tenants vacated the rental unit on October 31, 2020. Therefore, the Landlords’ L2 application for damages will be considered pursuant to s. 89(1)(a) of the Act.

 

Landlords’ Evidence

 

5.     The Landlords testified that they purchased the rental unit, a brand new single detached house, on December 1, 2017. They stated that it was their intent to immediately rent the unit; however, they did not have enough money to purchase appliances for the unit until 2019. The Landlords stated that the Tenants took possession of the unit on July 1, 2019, and at that time the hardwood flooring in the house was brand new.

 

6.     The Landlords submitted a letter from their Real Estate sales representative, Ritu Jain, signed and dated December 17, 2021. In the letter, Ms. Jain states that she was hired by the Landlords on April 20, 2019 to list their property for lease. She notes in her letter that the rental unit was new, never lived in, and that there were no scratches on the hardwood flooring during her two visits to the property after listing it. The Landlords did not submit any of the listing photos of the rental unit to the Board.

 

7.     The Landlords testified further that on August 8, 2020 they accompanied Leighton Brown, a professional home inspector, during a maintenance inspection of the rental unit. The Landlords contend that this inspection was conducted for Tarion Home Warranty requirements; however, the inspection was halted early at the request of the Tenants.

 

8.     The Landlords submitted the inspection report of the unit from Mr. Brown dated August 10, 2020 that included two photos of hardwood flooring with scratches. The Landlords also submitted an additional three photos of hardwood flooring with scratches photographed on August 8, 2020, the day of the inspection. In the report Mr. Brown states:

 

a)      The hardwood flooring on the staircase will require refinishing as a result of adhesives placed on each tread. Estimated cost of $3000.00.

 

b)      The hardwood flooring on the landing of the staircase will require replacement as a result of dog scratches. Estimated cost of $460.40 plus HST.

 

c)        The hardwood flooring in the Great Room will require replacement as a result of dog scratches. Estimated cost of $4762.40 plus HST. Mr. Brown noted that the inspection of this room was curtailed as a result of escalating hostilities.

 

9.     The Landlords asserted that the Tenants’ dog caused the damage to the hardwood flooring and the hardwood staircase. The Landlords stated that Mr. Brown received his

 

hardwood repair and replacement estimates from hardwood flooring specialists. They noted that as of the date of the hearing, the hardwood has not been repaired.

 

Tenants’ Evidence

 

10.  The Tenant testified that when he moved into the rental unit on July 1, 2019 the flooring was in good condition, but not pristine, and on July 1, 2019 he did not have an opportunity to observe whether there were any scratches on the hardwood floor or hardwood staircase. The Tenant noted that on July 1, 2019 the unit seemed like it had been lightly lived in before the start of his tenancy. The Tenant stated that during the course of his tenancy, in conversations with neighbours, the neighbours remarked that the house was used sometimes on the weekends before the Tenants moved in.

 

11.  Regarding the unit inspection on August 8, 2020, the Tenant explained that the Landlords and the home inspector were starting to move furniture in the living room, and that is the reason he requested that the inspection be stopped. He asserted that the inspection was being accomplished to support the Landlords’ L2 application.

 

12.  The Tenant testified further that at the end of his tenancy there were light surface scratches on the hardwood flooring and staircase caused by normal wear and tear from his own tenancy, and from whoever used the unit before his tenancy. The Tenant added that the adhesive strips placed on the staircase treads were installed at the request of the Landlords. The Tenant advised that his elderly 14-year old 80-pound dog resided in the rental unit during his tenancy, but his dog did not cause the scratches on the hardwood.

 

Analysis

 

Hardwood Flooring on the Staircase

 

13.  The Landlords applied for compensation for damage to the hardwood flooring as a result of scratches; however, the inspection report dated August 10, 2020 notes that the damage on the staircase is the result of an adhesive that was applied to each tread – not scratches. The Tenant asserted that this adhesive was applied at the request of the Landlords, and the Landlords did not refute this assertion.

 

14.  Although the Landlords did not provide photos of the condition of the hardwood staircase before the tenancy, on the basis of the evidence provided, I am satisfied that the hardwood staircase was in excellent condition at the start of this tenancy. However, with only one photo of one staircase tread provided from August 8, 2020, I am unable to determine, on a balance of probabilities, whether the damage exceeds normal wear and tear that might be reasonably expected during a tenancy, or the result of the Tenants complying with the Landlords’ request to apply adhesive strips to the staircase treads. I find that the home inspector’s estimate to repair the staircase damage, in the amount of

$3000.00, provides insufficient details regarding how this figure is determined.

 

15.  For the above reasons, on the basis of the evidence provided, I find that the Landlords have not established that the Tenants willfully or negligently caused undue damage to the hardwood flooring on the staircase of the rental unit.

 

Hardwood Flooring in the Great Room

 

16.  The Landlords provided no photos of the condition of the hardwood flooring in the Great Room before the tenancy; however, on the basis of the evidence provided, I am satisfied that the hardwood flooring in the Great Room was in excellent condition at the start of this tenancy.

 

17.  The Landlords provided a couple of photos of the Great Room from the inspection on August 8, 2020, and these photos illustrate some scratches in two areas of the room. The inspection report states that the scratches were caused by a pet and recommends the replacement of the entire floor at a cost of $4762.40 plus HST. I do not accept, on the basis of these few photos, that to remedy the identified scratches requires a complete replacement of the entire floor, or is the result of activity of the Tenants beyond normal wear and tear.

 

18.  For the above reasons, on the basis of the evidence provided, I find that the Landlords have not established that the Tenants willfully or negligently caused undue damage to the hardwood flooring in the Great Room of the rental unit.

 

Hardwood Flooring on the Staircase Landing

 

19.  Although the Landlords provided no photos of the condition of the hardwood flooring on the staircase landing before the tenancy, on the basis of the evidence provided, I am satisfied that the hardwood flooring on the staircase landing was in excellent condition at the start of this tenancy.

 

20.  The home inspector’s photo of the landing from the August 8, 2020 inspection displays a significant number of scratches on the hardwood floor. In his report dated August 10, 2020, the home inspector describes the landing as “severely scratched”, and provides a detailed breakdown of the costs to replace the flooring, totaling $460.40 plus HST. I am satisfied, on the basis of this photo, and the home inspector’s report, that these scratches require a replacement of the floor at a cost to the Landlords of $520.25 inclusive of HST.

 

21.  On the basis of the evidence provided, I am satisfied that the damage to the hardwood floor on the staircase landing, during this tenancy, was undue and not likely the result of normal wear and tear. I find that the Tenants, on a balance of probabilities, negligently caused undue damage to the hardwood flooring on the staircase landing.

 

22.  Given that the hardwood flooring on the staircase landing is approximately four years old, and using a depreciation rate of 3.0% per year, I am granting the Landlords’ request for an order requiring the Tenants to pay the Landlords $457.82. This represents the reasonable costs of replacing the damaged hardwood flooring on the staircase landing.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     The Tenants shall pay to the Landlords $457.82, which represents the reasonable costs of replacing the damaged property.

 

2.     The Tenants shall also pay to the Landlords $186.00 for the cost of filing the application.

 

 

 

3.     If the Tenants do not pay the Landlords the full amount owing on or before February 8, 2022, the Tenants will start to owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from February 9, 2022 at 2.00% annually on the balance outstanding.
