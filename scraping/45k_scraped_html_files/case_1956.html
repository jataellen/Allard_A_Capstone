Order under Section 135

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TST-15704-20

 

 

In the matter of:

1709, 51 EAST LIBERTY STREET TORONTO ON M6K3P8

 

Between:

Steven Sean Dagenais Kleef Kaitlyn Elizabeth Pauly

Tenants

 

 

and

 

 

Rebecca Chu

Landlord

 

 

 

Steven Sean Dagenais Kleef and Kaitlyn Elizabeth Pauly (the 'Tenants') applied for an order determining that Rebecca Chu (the 'Landlord') has collected or retained money illegally.

 

Steven Sean Dagenais Kleef and Kaitlyn Elizabeth Pauly (the 'Tenants') also applied for an order determining that Rebecca Chu (the 'Landlord') harassed, obstructed, coerced, threatened or interfered with them, entered the rental unit illegally and substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or residential complex by the Tenants or by a member of their household.

 

Steven Sean Dagenais Kleef and Kaitlyn Elizabeth Pauly (the 'Tenants') also applied for an order determining that Rebecca Chu (the 'Landlord') failed to meet the Landlord's maintenance obligations under the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act') or failed to comply with health, safety, housing or maintenance standards.

 

This application was heard via video conference on October 26, 2021. The Tenants and the Landlord attended the hearing.

Determinations:

 

T1 Application:

 

1.     In their T1 application, the Tenants made the following claims:

 

•         The Landlord overcharged the Tenants illegal rent in the amount of $2.04;

•         The Landlord was paid $175.00 as an illegal charge which was not returned to the Tenants;

 

•         The Landlord did not use the Tenant’s last month’s rent deposit to pay for the last rental period of the tenancy and did not return the deposit to the Tenants in the amount of $2,196.00;

•         The Landlord owes the Tenants interest on the last month’s rent deposit in the amount of $77.43.

 

2.     The Tenants moved into the rental unit on May 1, 2018 and vacated on April 21, 2020. Prior to the tenancy commencing, the Tenants paid a last month’s rent deposit in the amount of $2,157.00. Rent then increased to $2,196.00 on May 1, 2019.

 

Overcharge of rent:

 

3.     The Tenants state that the Landlord rounded up a rent increase by $.17 per month so that $2.04 was overcharged to the Tenants during a twelve month period.

 

4.     Notwithstanding that the Tenants agreed at the hearing that they are abandoning this claim, pursuant to section 207(4) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006, (the ‘Act’): “The Board shall not make an order for the payment of an amount of money if the amount is less than the prescribed amount”. As the prescribed amount set out in Ontario Regulation 516/06 section 58 is $5.00, no order will be made with respect to the Tenant’s claim that the Landlord overcharged the Tenants.

 

 

Illegal charge (Key Deposit):

 

5.     The Tenants testified that they provided a key deposit in the amount of $175.00 to the Landlord at the commencement of the tenancy and it has not been returned to them.

 

6.     The Landlord confirmed that she was holding onto a key deposit of $175.00 which she collected from the Tenants when the tenancy commenced and has not returned it to the Tenants. The Landlord shall pay $175.00 to the Tenants representing the key deposit.

 

Last month’s rent deposit:

 

7.     The Tenants testified that they paid a last month rent deposit when they commenced the tenancy on May 1, 2018 in the amount of $2,157.00 which was applied for the month of April, 2019. The Tenants then provided the Landlord with a new last month’s rent deposit in the amount of $2,196.00 on April 8, 2019 (taking into account a rent increase) which was used for the month of April, 2020.

 

8.     The Tenants confirmed that they pre-paid rent for March, 2020 via post-dated cheque, which the Landlord applied towards the March, 2020 rent.

 

9.     The Tenants told the Landlord by email communication that they were vacating the rental unit as at April 30, 2020 on March 17, 2020. The Tenants confirmed that although they were not residing in the rental unit, they had possession of the unit until April 21, 2020 when the keys to the rental unit were returned.

 

10.  The Tenants stated that the last month’s rent deposit of $2,196.00 was applied by the Landlord for the month of April, 2020 but because they did not reside in the rental unit as at February 28, 2020 due to a flooding event, the Landlord owes the return of last

month’s rent as they claim it should not have been applied and should have been returned to them.

 

11.  The Tenants provided just over one month’s notice to the Landlord that they were vacating the rental unit. Pursuant to the Act and as the lease was initially a yearly tenancy and then reverted to a monthly tenancy, the Tenants were to provide 60 days notice to the Landlord that they were vacating the rental unit and that date shall be on the last day of a rental period.

 

12.  The Landlord testified that she did not receive proper notice from the Tenants stating that they were vacating the rental unit but that the Tenants had already paid rent for March, 2020 when the Tenants provided their notice to vacate on March 17, 2020. As the Tenants advised they were vacating the rental unit as at the end of April, 2020, the Landlord applied the Tenants’ last month rent deposit for April, 2020.

 

13.  Based on the evidence and submissions that were introduced at the hearing, I do not find it appropriate to state that the tenancy was terminated effective February 28, 2020, as the Tenants requested. I say this because the Tenants had possession of the rental unit, they still accessed the rental unit and did not return the keys to the rental unit until April 21, 2020. The Tenants are required to pay (and in fact, they did pay) the Landlord rent for March, 2020. The Tenants are therefore entitled to rely on the last month rent deposit to pay for the rent owing for April, 2020.

 

14.  Based on the evidence and submissions from both parties and on a balance of probabilities, I find that the last month rent deposit was properly applied for the last month of tenancy for April, 2020.

 

Interest:

 

15.  The Tenants state that the Landlord owes interest from the last month’s rent deposits.

 

16.  The Tenants state that the Landlord did not pay them interest on their last month’s rent deposit for the period of May 1, 2018 to April 1, 2019 on the amount of $2,157.00 and also interest was not paid for the period of April 8, 2019 to April 21, 2020 on the amount of $2,196.00.

 

17.  The Landlord states that as she applied the last month rent deposit within a year period, no interest should be paid.

 

18.  Section 106(6) of the Act states: “a Landlord of a rental unit shall pay interest to the tenant annually on the amount of the rent deposit at a rate equal to the guideline determined under section 120 that is in effect at the time payment becomes due”.

 

19.  Landlords are required to pay interest on a last month’s rent deposit to a tenant annually pursuant to s. 106(6) of the Act so if the deadline for payment arrives and the Landlord

 

does not pay the interest or use it to top up the deposit then at that point in time the Landlord starts to illegally retain the interest.

 

20.  Technically, the Landlord should not have applied the last month’s rent at the end of each rental term as the lease agreement reverted to a monthly tenancy but there is no dispute that the rent was used to cover certain months during the tenancy. The point of interest is that the Landlord is holding the last month’s rent for the year but in this case, the last month’s rent deposit was applied before the end of the one year period.

 

21.  Based on the evidence and submissions at the hearing, the Tenants paid $2,157.00 to

the Landlord representing a last month’s rent deposit on May 1, 2018 which was applied on April 1, 2019. Interest at 1.8% for that period totals $35.63. The Tenants next paid

$2,196.00 to the Landlord on April 8, 2019 which was applied on April 1, 2020. Interest at 1.8% for that second period totals $38.88. The Tenants are entitled to interest on the last month’s rent deposits they provided to the Landlord. The Landlord shall pay to the Tenants the total interest owing in the amount of $74.51.

 

T2 Application

 

22.  The issue to be determined by the Board is whether the Landlord illegally entered the rental unit, substantially interfered with their reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or complex or with the reasonable enjoyment of a member of their household and whether the Landlord harassed, coerced, obstructed, threatened or interfered with them.

 

23.  For the reasons below, the Tenants’ application will be dismissed.

 

Illegal Entry

 

24.  The rental unit had a flood on February 27, 2020 after a toilet overflowed when the toilet got clogged with toilet paper. The exact cause of the flood is hotly contested as the Tenants claim it was due to a faulty valve in the toilet but the Landlord claims it was due to the negligence of the Tenants.

 

25.  The Landlord emailed the Tenants on March 2, 2020 providing notice that she would be attending the rental unit on March 3, 2020 to view the damage to the rental unit and that an insurance adjuster would visit the rental unit on March 4, 2020. The Tenants had no evidence that any illegal entry actually took place on the alleged dates other than an email communication from the Landlord dated March 6, 2020 which states “I visited the unit a few days ago. My insurance adjuster has done her work there too”.

 

26.  The Tenants further state that the Landlord attended the rental unit illegally on March 7, 2020 possibly with a plumber at 8:45pm. The Landlord’s notice stated that she may or may not attend at the rental unit as it depended on when the plumber was available to affect repairs. Although there was notice provided by the Landlord for entry, the Tenants explained that the Landlord illegally entered the rental unit as no specific time was stated of when the contractor/plumber was to attend the rental unit.

 

27.  Finally, the Tenants state that a notice of entry received from the Landlord on March 11, 2020 stated that the Landlord would attend the rental unit between the hours of 3:00pm to 9:00pm. The Tenants state that a 6 hour time period is too long and is inappropriate thus it was an illegal entry.

 

28.  Section 25 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 says a Landlord may enter a rental unit only in accordance with section 26 or 27. Section 26 relates to entries where written notice is not required; section 27 is about entry on notice.

 

29.  While the Landlord is required to provide notices of entry in compliance with the Act, the Tenants are also obligated to mitigate their losses by co-operating with the Landlord’s attempts to resolve the maintenance issues. On the occasions complained of, I find that the Landlord attempted to determine damage to the rental unit due to an emergency flooding event and to conduct repairs in a timely manner. The Tenants’ complaints of entries that the Landlord was not following the letter of the law would have only served to further delay any outstanding repairs in the rental unit. As a result, the remedy pertaining to illegal entry will not be ordered and this portion of the application is dismissed.

 

 

Substantial Interference and Harassment:

 

30.  Section 22 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’) states:

 

A landlord shall not at any time during a tenant’s occupancy of a rental unit and before the day on which an order evicting the tenant is executed substantially interfere with the reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or the residential complex in which it is located for all usual purposes by a tenant or members of his or her household.

 

31.  Section 23 of the Act states: “a landlord shall not harass, obstruct, coerce, threaten or interfere with a tenant”.

 

32.  There is no definition of “harassment” under the Act but generally speaking harassment is usually considered to be a course of conduct that a reasonable person knows or ought to know would be unwelcome.

 

33.  The conduct need not be more than one instance and a tenant is not required to demonstrate a pattern of behavior in order to show that a landlord has breached the Act.

 

Inspections:

 

34.  In the Tenants’ T2 application, the Tenants state that the Landlord did not deliver any notices of entry for inspections. For the period between September 27 to December 4, 2019, the evidence was that there was no definitive response from the Landlord that she would be attending the rental unit on a specific date or time which the Landlord then subsequently cancelled:

 

•         September 27, 2019: Landlord stated would come by in October during an inspection but date was not specified;

•         October 28, 2019: Landlord emailed Tenants stating could not come by in the next two weeks but “most probably” would come November 14.

•         November 12, 2019: Tenants emailed Landlord seeking clarification if the Landlord was coming by for an inspection. The Landlord replied “most probably, I will come either next Monday or Thursday”.

•         November 18, 2019: Landlord emailed Tenants “will be coming some time next week….with an advance notice”.

•         December 4, 2019 – notice of entry for December 5, 2019 was provided by the Landlord.

 

35.  With respect to periods as set out above, there were actually no inspections that took place but for an entry where notice was provided on December 4, 2019. For the period between September 27 to December 4, 2019, the Tenants could have gone about their daily life activities as no date was confirmed between the Landlord and the Tenants. The Landlord proposed dates for attendance at the rental unit but stated that advance notice would be provided to the Tenants.

 

36.  Based on the evidence and submissions at the hearing and on a balance of probabilities, the Board finds that as there was not a specific date arranged for an inspection, there was no substantial interference to the Tenants.   The Tenants have failed to prove, on a balance of probabilities, that the Landlord substantially interfered with their enjoyment of the rental unit with respect to proposed scheduling of inspections and attendances at the rental unit between September 27 to December 4, 2019.

 

Air filters:

 

37.  The Tenants testified that emails sent by the Landlord were threatening, interfered with the enjoyment of the rental unit and created negativity on the quality of their life The Tenants found the Landlord’s emails regarding the changing of the air filters on the furnace to be consistently nagging and the Landlord falsely accused the Tenants of not looking after the rental unit.

 

38.  The Tenants testified that the Landlord required the Tenants to change the air filters on the furnace on a very frequent basis which substantially interfered with their reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit. At times, the Tenants were changing the air filters monthly due to construction in the adjacent unit. The Tenants stated that to change the air filter they would open the door to the furnace, remove the old filter and put in a new one. There were some occasions where the Landlord supplied the air filters and other occasions where the Tenants provided them. The Tenants stated that it was not their responsibility to change the air filters on the furnace but that it was the Landlord’s responsibility.

 

39.  Based on the evidence and submissions before the Board, the Landlord sent emails to the Tenants regarding the changing of air filters on April 8, 9, 11, September 27, November 18, December 4, 2019 and on March 17 and 20, 2020. I find that these emails

 

were reminders to the Tenants to change the air filters and sought confirmation that the air filters had in fact been replaced.

 

40.  While the Landlord was ensuring the air filters were changed and was attempting to mitigate any possible furnace issues due to the Tenants having a dog and due to increased dust from construction taking place in nearby units, I am satisfied, on a balance of probabilities, that any interference or harassment complained of by the Tenants regarding the replacement of air filters in the furnace and reminder emails from the Landlord were minimal, does not rise to the level of it being substantial as is the intent of the Act and I do not believe these communications would be considered to be harassing, threatening or interfering.

 

Threats and Coercion:

 

41.  The Tenants state that they felt they were coerced by the Landlord to stay in the rental unit and abide by the lease following a flood event because the Landlord told them they would then be sued for ending the lease and for damages from the flood. The Landlord testified that she felt the Tenants should not be allowed to end the lease as she was working with others to fix the outstanding repairs and the damage from the flood that was caused by the Tenants negligence.

 

42.  The Tenants state that the Landlord stated in email communications that if the Tenants chose to leave the tenancy without fulfilling their responsibilities, the Landlord has to go through formal legal procedures without a choice.

 

43.  A definition of coercion found in the dictionary states that coercion generally means to impose ones will on another by means of force or threats.

 

44.  I am persuaded that the Landlord made the statements as described by the Tenants. However, I am not persuaded that these statements constituted a significant threat or some form of interference, coercion, or obstruction. I do not find the circumstances described amounts to coercion as meant by the Act, in particular, that the Landlord coerced the Tenants into doing something against their wishes. With regards to the threat of legal action, the Landlord would have a right to commence an action if she chose to. I do not find that to be a threat but more of a warning. I am therefore satisfied that the email communications do not rise to the level of threats or coercion.

 

T6 Application:

 

45.  The issue in this application is whether the Landlord has maintained the rental unit in a good state of repair and fit for habitation in accordance with section 20 of the Act.

 

46.  Section 20 of the Act states:

 

A landlord is responsible for providing and maintaining a residential complex, including the rental units in it, in a good state of repair and fit for habitation and for complying with health, safety, housing and maintenance standards.

 

47.  The Tenants seek relief for out-of-pocket expenses they incurred for private parking (parking was not included with the rental unit), hydro costs and internet costs as they were not residing in the rental unit and able to enjoy those services during the months of March and April, 2020. The Tenants also seek reimbursement of the time they spent on phone calls to their insurance company, to the Board, to legal personnel, to municipal by- law authorities and their time in responding to the Landlord’s claims.

 

48.  In the case Onyskiw v. CJM Property Management, 2016 ONCA 477 (Onyskiw) (CanLII), the Court of Appeal for Ontario determined that a contextual approach should be adopted when considering a landlord’s potential breach of subsection 20(1) of the Act and a breach will not be found if the landlord’s response to a maintenance issue was reasonable in the circumstances.

 

49.  The Tenants state that a flood occurred on February 27, 2020 at approximately 10:30 pm as a result of a faulty fill valve which did not shut off at the proper water level while the toilet was clogged with toilet paper.     Unfortunately, no-one was home when this occurred which resulted in the toilet overflowing and water spreading throughout portions of the rental unit. The Tenants state that they attempted to notify the Landlord of the flood that night but were unable to reach her. However, property management did attend and emergency plumbing services arrived at approximately 12:30 in the morning to perform emergency services. The Tenants advised that they were finally able to reach the Landlord in the late morning hours the next day, February 28, 2020, who authorized the emergency restoration company to perform the necessary work and also contacted her insurance company.

 

50.  The Landlord submits that she was not aware of any alleged issue with the fill valve on the toilet and denies the valve was the cause of the toilet overflowing, rather it was due to the neglect of the Tenants. The Landlord attended the rental unit until March 3, 2020 as she received photographs of the damage, contracted her insurer and also arranged for emergency services to be performed by contractors.

 

51.  The Tenants claim that as a result of the flood, it rendered the rental unit unhabitable for two months at which time, they then vacated the rental unit. They claim that due to the drywall being cut 12” from the floor exposing the inside plumbing and electrical components with screws and nails inside the walls, it made the unit unlivable and was a hazard to their dog as the exposed area was at his height. The Tenants also claim that the floorboards were allegedly warped and drywall dust was still present in the rental unit. However, the Tenants confirmed that the kitchen, en-suite bathroom and living room were functional but that their living room items had been packed up and stored off site while their couch, desk and other living room furniture were disposed of.

 

52.  The Tenants testified that their insurance company paid for hotel accommodations for 4-5 days immediately after the flood and then covered approximately two months of expenses associated with accommodations in another condominium until mid April.

 

53.  The Landlord denies that the rental unit was unlivable, the flood affected only a portion of the unit, the floors were cleaned, sanitized and safe for people to live in. The Landlord

 

stated that the flooring did not have to be replaced and that the damage was from clean water.

 

54.  The Landlord stated that she did everything she could to arrange for contractors to attend the property to repair the unit in a timely manner. The Landlord testified that she had a very difficult time in securing contractors to perform the necessary repairs to the rental unit due to the COVID pandemic. The Landlord stated that she contacted numerous contractors but that they were already booked, some did not attend the property although they initially claimed they would and others did not reply to her inquiries. The Landlord admitted to being very cautious with her choice in contractors and found it very difficult to find contractors to attend the rental property.

 

55.  The Landlord stated that the delay in repairing the rental unit was due to the COVID pandemic and government imposed lockdowns made it very difficult to retain qualified contractors.

 

56.  Whether or not the Tenants actually contributed or caused the flood, subsection 20(1) of the Act does not permit a Landlord to refuse to maintain the rental unit on the grounds that the Tenants may have caused the damage. In any event, the Landlord was obliged to conduct repairs in a timely manner regardless of the cause of the disrepair and was free to pursue those costs of the repairs through her own application to the Board should she believe that the Tenants wilfully or negligently caused undue damage to the premises.

 

57.  In accordance with Onyskiw, a breach may not be found if the Landlord’s response was reasonable in the circumstances

 

58.  The evidence before the Board was that emergency contractors attended the rental unit within hours of the flood being reported and that remedial work was commenced. There was insufficient evidence regarding the condition of the floors after the flood and the final work to repair the remaining items in the rental unit proved quite difficult to complete largely due to the COVID pandemic and the government imposed lockdowns. Further, while the rental unit had portions of components exposed due to the removal of the drywall along the bottom of the wall, any drywall dust could have been swept or vacuumed and the unit was cleaned, it would not have rendered the rental unit uninhabitable.

 

59.  I am satisfied that the Landlord’s response was reasonable in the circumstances considering that this event took place right at the beginning of the COVID pandemic when numerous government imposed lockdowns were initiated and construction was quite limited as a result. I am further satisfied that due to the COVID pandemic, the Landlord would have experienced significant difficulty in retaining contractors to attend the rental unit to complete the remedial repairs from the flood.

 

60.  In the Tenant’s opinion, they felt the rental unit uninhabitable due to their belief that the rental unit was not cleaned to their standards and that the exposed bottom portion on some of the walls from the removed drywall posed a danger to themselves and to their

 

dog.

 

61.  Ultimately, I find that the Tenants failed to demonstrate that the Landlord’s breach of duties to maintain the rental unit caused more than minimal disruption to their ability to reside in the rental unit, let alone render the rental unit uninhabitable for two months as they have suggested. The Tenants sought reimbursement of parking, internet and hydro expenses and also sought payment representing their time to remedy this issue. I find

that the Landlord’s response to affect the repairs was appropriate in light of the extenuating circumstances due to the pandemic and therefore the Landlord did not breach section 20(1) of the Act. Based on the evidence, submissions and on a balance of probabilities, no abatement or other remedy should be ordered. As a result, the

Tenants’ T6 application is dismissed.

 

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     With respect to the Tenants’ T1 application, the Landlord shall pay to the Tenants the sum of $74.51. This amount represents interest on the last month's rent deposit.

 

2.     With respect to the Tenant’s T1 application, the Landlord shall pay to the Tenants the sum of $175.00 which represents the return of the Tenants’ key deposit.

 

3.     The total amount the Landlord shall pay to the Tenants is $248.51.

 

4.     If the Landlord does not pay the Tenants the full amount owing by February 16, 2022 the Landlord will owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from February 17, 2022 at 2.00% annually on the outstanding balance.

 

5.     The Tenants have the right, at any time, to collect the full amount owing or any balance outstanding under this order.

 

6.     The Tenant’s T2 and T6 applications are dismissed.
