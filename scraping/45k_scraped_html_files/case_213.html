Order under Section 78(11)

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TEL-21253-21-SA

 

 

In the matter of:

52 JUDY SGRO AVENUE NORTH YORK ON M3L0E4

 

Between:

Rajwinder Singh Dhaliwal

Landlord

 

 

and

 

 

Camille Lewis Glen Albert Simon

Tenants

 

 

 

Rajwinder Singh Dhaliwal (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict Glen Albert Simon and Camille Lewis (the 'Tenants') because the Tenants failed to meet a condition specified in the order issued by the Board on July 9, 2021 with respect to application HOL-09199-20.

 

The Landlord's application was resolved by order TEL-21253-21, issued on January 17, 2022. The Tenants filed a motion to set aside order TEL-21253-21.

 

This motion was heard by videoconference on February 15, 2022.

 

The Landlord and the Tenants attended the hearing. The Landlord was represented by Shalini Puri. The Tenants were represented by Noland Merrick.

 

Determinations:

 

1.   The first issue on a motion to set aside such as this one is whether or not the Tenants breached the order as alleged.

 

2.   The Tenants disputed the breach alleged by the Landlord; the first-named Tenant explained that because she had paid the rent for December 2021 from two separate accounts, she thought she had paid the rent in full. When she received the ex parte order on January 25, 2022, she realized she had short-paid the rent by $106.00 and immediately corrected the error by paying the Landlord this amount the very same day.

 

3.   Based on the evidence before me, I find that the Tenants breached the order by not paying the rent for December 2021 in full and on time.

 

4.   Pursuant to subsection 78(11) of the Act, when there has been a breach to an order, the next issue to determine is whether or not I am “satisfied, having regard to all the circumstances, that it would not be unfair to set aside the order”.

 

5.   This tenancy began in September 2017. The Tenant resides in the rental unit with her five children ages 22, 16, 16, 12 and 10.

 

6.   Since the breach in December’s rent payment, the Tenants have paid the rent for January 2022 on January 4, 2022 and the rent for February 2022 on January 30, 2022. The Tenant explains that because January 1, 2022 was a statutory holiday, she paid the rent the following business day; she remembers having inquired about this prior to entering into the consent order.

 

7.   The Tenants seek a final opportunity to preserve their tenancy.

 

8.   The Landlord opposes the Tenants’ motion as the Landlord submits they have already provided the Tenants with the opportunity to preserve their tenancy by entering into a consent order for prompt payment – as opposed to seeking termination in July 2021. The Landlord maintains the position that the behaviour alleged in the N8 notice of termination has not been corrected by the Tenants as the Tenants have been late in August 2021 and January 2022 on top of December 2021.

 

9.   The Landlord is agreeable to an extension in the lifting of the stay of 60 days for the Tenants to make alternative housing arrangements.

 

ANALYSIS

 

10. The obligation to pay the rent on time is a basic term of the tenancy agreement. The Landlord should not have to obtain an order from the Board each time to ensure the Tenants keep to this term of their contract.

 

11. At the hearing on June 28, 2021, it was the parties’ decision to continue this tenancy and it would appear, based on the terms of the consent order, that it was with the intention to rehabilitate this tenancy. In hearing this motion, I may only consider circumstances that have arisen since the issuance of the order on July 9, 2021.

 

12. In this case, there is no dispute that the Tenants breached the order whether it was due to an oversight on their part or a lack of due diligence; however, I am satisfied that the Tenants made a genuine and reasonable effort to ensure that the shortfall was rectified as soon as it came to their attention, demonstrating a desire and ability to pay the rent owing so that they may rehabilitate this tenancy.

 

13. Given that the Tenants have been abiding by the order and having regard to all the circumstances, I find that it would not be unfair to set aside order HOL-09199-20, issued on July 9, 2021 pursuant to clause 78(11)(b) of the Act and replace it with a new conditional order requiring prompt payment for the next twelve months.

 

14. This order contains all of the reasons for the decision within it. No further reasons shall be issued.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     Order TEL-21253-21, issued on January 17, 2022 is set aside.

 

2.     Order HOL-09199-20 issued on July 9, 2021 cancelled and replaced with the following order.

 

3.     The Tenants shall pay to the Landlord the lawful monthly rent as it comes due in full and no later than the first day of each month commencing March 2022 through to February 2023.

 

4.     If the Tenants fail to comply with paragraph 2 of this order, the Landlord may, without notice to the Tenants and within 30 days of the breach, apply to the Board pursuant to section 78 of the Act for an order terminating the tenancy and evicting the Tenants.
