Order under Section 68

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: SWL-41295-20

 

 

In the matter of:

2, 50 GORDON AVENUE KITCHENER ON N2H1N8

 

Between:

Acso P. M. Inc

Landlord

 

 

and

 

 

Selene Macleod

Tenant

 

 

 

Acso P. M. Inc (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict Selene Macleod (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant has substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment or lawful right, privilege or interest of the Landlord or another tenant.

 

The Landlord also claimed compensation for each day the Tenant remained in the unit after the termination date.

 

This application was heard via videoconference on March 1, 2021 at 11:00 a.m.

 

The Landlord and their representative, Terri Van Huisstede, a licensed Paralegal, attended the hearing. The Tenant, Selene MacLeod, who was self-represented, also attended the hearing.

 

The Tenant declined to speak with Tenant Duty Counsel (TDC) before the hearing.

 

 

Determinations:

 

1.     This is a Landlord application based on an N5 notice of termination served on September 24, 2019 under section 64 of the Residential Tenancies Act 2006 (the “Act) alleging that

the Tenant has substantially interfered with the Landlord’s and or another tenants reasonable enjoyment of the residential complex and/or the lawful rights, privileges or interests. The details set out in the Notice states that the Tenant is not maintaining the unit in a state of ordinary cleanliness as required by section 33 of the Ac”. The first notice specified that:

 

a)     “inside the unit there was mud and dirt and food all over the floors throughout”

 

b)     “The bathroom sink had watermark staining and debris

 

c)      “There were pudding containers in water in the bathtub”

 

d)     “The kitchen floor had recyclable material like plastic bags and cartons and boxes littered with excessive dirt:

 

e)     “There were recyclable materials throughout the unit”

 

2.     Pursuant to section 64(3) if the Act, the Tenant had 7 days from the date the first N5 notices was served (September 25-October 1, 2019) to correct the behaviour described in order to void the order and continue the tenancy.

 

3.     This notice was void because the Tenant corrected the problem within the time period set out in the notice.

 

4.     On January 24, 2020, the Landlord served the Tenant with a second, non-voidable N5 notice of termination pursuant to section 68 of the Act which also alleges that the Tenant has substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment or lawful right, privilege or interest of the Landlord or another tenant. The details set out in the second N5 Notice state:

 

a)     “It was noted that upon today’s inspections that the closet door in the hallway was missing its door which is currently being stored on the balcony. The items on the balcony make the building unsightly & is not meant to be used as a storage area. Belongings must be removed as we’ve repeatedly asked on the past for”

 

b)     “The Balcony to be cleaned & for the unit to be maintained at an ordinarily level of cleanliness (s.33 of the Residential Tenancies Act)”

 

c)      “The Kitchen is a potential fire hazard due to both stive tops being covered with items & containers with the possibility of being plugged in (Agent unable to inspect due to level of cleanliness)”

 

d)     “The unit is extremely dirty and although there’s been some improvement our contractors are not fully able to access repairs due to the cleanliness of the unit”

 

e)     “We’ve issued legal in the past numerous times & the tenants comply temporarily.”

 

f)      “We need a permanent solution.”

 

5.     The January 24, 2020 notice also informs the Tenant that this is their second notice within the past 6 months, that the Tenant cannot void the notice, and that the Landlord can apply to the Board for an order to evict the Tenant.

 

6.     On January 24, 2020 the Landlord filed this application with the Board.

 

Landlord’s evidence and submissions

 

 

7.     The Landlord, Diane Digiandomenico, President and Property Manager for ASCO P.M. Inc., testified that numerous N5 notices have been issued to the Tenant since 2016. Ms. Digiandomenico further stated that;

 

a)     there have been continuous issues with the Tenant

 

b)     that the Tenant always wants more time to comply with requests to enter the unit for repairs, for the Landlord to enter in order to perform inspections, and or to clean up the unit

 

c)      that the unit has debris

 

d)     that the Tenant is hoarding dirty containers

 

e)     the unit is cluttered

 

f)      that during an inspection her feet had stuck to the floor

 

 

8.     While the Landlord testified about a fire in the rental unit, this allegation was not contained in the second N5 notice and therefore cannot be considered.

 

9.     The Landlord representative submitted a series of pictures taken on September 22, 2019, November 5, 2019, January 24, 2020 and February 1, 2021 by an employee of the Landlord who was not at the hearing to testify. However Diane Digiandomenico was able to describe the pictures as follows:

 

a)     Exhibit 01 - is A picture of the kitchen showing various containers, cans, paper and various other items strewn across the kitchen floor

 

b)     Exhibit 2 – A picture of the living room showing various clothing items on the furniture, bags, boxes and other items strewn across the floor

 

c)      Exhibit 03 - A picture (November 5, 2019 9:21 AM) of the bedroom showing various items on the floor, clothing covering the bed, and a shelving unit laden with various items

 

d)     Exhibit 04 – pictures taken January 24, 2020 ( picture on 2nd row on the right) shows one of the 2 stoves with various food items, a bag of bread, a can, a juice bottle, along with various sundry items on top of the stove

 

e)     Exhibit 05 - is a series of 8 pictures taken February 21, 2021, a year after the 2nd Notice was issued. The pictures were submitted to establish the current state of the unit:

 

Tenant’s evidence and submissions

 

 

10.  The Tenant testified that the unit was not as cluttered as claimed by the Landlord and asserted that on the Clutter Imaging Rating Scale, her unit at the worst could be rated as a “3” which does not warrant eviction.

 

11.  The Clutter Image Rating Scale was developed by the International OCD Foundation. It is a series of photographs showing varying degrees of clutter that help qualify the severity of a cluttering problem. The photographs are readily available on-line. There are different sets of photographs for a kitchen, bedroom, and living room and they are ranked on a numerical scale. Generally speaking a 3 or lower on the clutter scale is considered to be acceptable; a 4 or higher indicates something needs to be done.

 

12.  The Tenant also testified that since 2019 no agencies were called to provide any assistance or support her, nor have there been any violations or complaints reported other than by the Landlord. The Tenant has also testified that she has mobility issues and suffers from Arthritis in the knees.

 

13.  The Tenant stated that her unit is free of bedbugs, cockroaches, mice and other pests. This statement was not challenged by the Landlord nor did any of the photographs presented show evidence to the contrary.

 

14.  The Tenant stated that her unit does not have any pets or animals in the unit. The Landlord confirmed that her unit does not have any pets or animals in the unit.

 

15.  The Tenant submitted that her rent is always paid on time and that the only reason this situation is due to the Landlord not liking her housekeeping.

 

16.  The Tenant agrees with the Landlord that some aspects of her housekeeping may not be to the Landlord’s approval, however that they do not rise to the level of warranting eviction from her home.

 

17.  The Tenant has filed a Tenant’s Rights (T2) application against the Landlord and believes that there is a campaign against her by other tenants and that she has received death

threats. No evidence has been supplied to support the Tenant’s claim that other tenants have issues with her nor has any evidence been received confirming that the Tenant has received death threats.

 

18.  The Tenant further submits that she has severe mobility issues, that there is a pandemic, that local shelters are full, and that due to her medical conditions she would be at risk of catching COVID if she were placed on a shelter. In essence she stated that she would be reduced to sleeping in her car.

 

Analysis

 

1.     The burden of proof rests with the Landlord who must establish that the condition of the unit substantially interferes with the Landlord’s and or another tenant’s reasonable enjoyment of the residential complex and/or the lawful rights, privileges or interests.

 

2.     In this case, the photographic evidence and the Tenant’s own testimony establish that the condition of the unit may potentially threaten the lawful rights, privileges and interests of the Landlord.

 

3.     The Tenant has, by her own admission, agreed that there is “cluttering” and that it may on the face of it appear unsightly to the Landlord, but it does not spill over to the surrounding units and areas.

 

4.     Although the Tenant submitted that the Clutter Image Rating Scale would support her claim that the cluttering is not to an unreasonable degree, a subject expert was not provided to give testimony as to the Clutter Image Rating Scale in relation to the unit.

 

5.     I have also received submissions from both parties that the condition of the unit has varied since 2016. For a period of time the condition of the unit is acceptable to the Landlord, shortly thereafter the condition of the unit deteriorates requiring intervention and action by the Landlord.

 

Relief from eviction:

 

 

6.     I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would not be unfair to grant relief from eviction subject to the condition(s) set out in this order pursuant to subsection 83(1)(a) and 204(1) of the Act.

 

7.     Although the Landlord has established substantial interference, I am of the opinion that that the circumstances reflected in the evidence and testimony do not rise to the level where it is necessary to evict the Tenant.

 

8.     I have also considered the Tenant’s circumstances. The Tenant has in the past received assistance with keeping her unit clean but has not had any assistance from outside agencies since 2019. The Tenant has mobility issues and suffers from Arthritis in the knees. This may reflect in her ability to maintain her unit without outside assistance. I also recognize that the evicting the Tenant in the midst of the COVID-19 pandemic could have serious effects on her health and well-being

 

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     The Landlord’s application is denied on the following conditions:

 

 

 

a.   By February 28, 2022, the Tenant shall take all reasonable steps to seek out services that are available to assist her with her personal and home care

 

b.   By March 31, 2022, the Tenant shall declutter her entire unit bringing it to a level 3 or less on the Clutter Image Rating Scale

 

c.   The Tenant shall maintain her unit in a state fit for habitation, to a level 3 or less on the Clutter Image Rating Scale during the period March 31, 2022 to March 31, 2023

 

2.     If the Tenant fails to comply with the conditions set out in paragraph 1 above, them within thirty days of the breach, the Landlord may apply under section 78 of the Act for an order terminating the tenancy and evicting the Tenant. The Landlord mush make the application within 30 days of a breach of a condition set out in paragraph 1 of this order.

 

3.     The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $190.00 for the cost of filing the application by January 31, 2022

 

4.     If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing on or before January 31, 2022, the Tenant will start to owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from February 1, 2022 at 2.00% annually on the balance outstanding.
