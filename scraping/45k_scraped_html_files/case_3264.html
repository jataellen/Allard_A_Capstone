Order under Section 30, 31 AND 135

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: CET-91318-19

 

 

 

 

 

SSN and BSM (the 'Tenants') applied for an order determining that SKB (the 'Landlord') has collected or retained money illegally (the T1 application).

 

SSN and BSM (the 'Tenants') also applied for an order determining that SKB (the 'Landlord') failed to meet the Landlord's maintenance obligations under the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act') or failed to comply with health, safety, housing or maintenance standards (the T2 Application)

 

SSN and BSM (the 'Tenants') also applied for an order determining that SKB (the 'Landlord') substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or residential complex by the Tenants or by a member of their household (the T6 Application).

 

This application was heard via video conference on October 28, 2021.

 

The Tenant, SSN (“S.N.”), the Landlord and the Landlord’s representative JB attended the hearing.

 

 

Determinations:

 

T1 application

 

1.     S.N. states that the Landlord did not use last month’s rent deposit of $1,000.00 to pay for the last rental period of the tenancy and did not return the deposit to the Tenants.

 

2.     S.N. testified that he and his family came to Canada in February, 2018 and began their tenancy on March 12, 2018. S.N. and the Landlord did not enter into a formal lease

 

agreement but it was agreed that the Tenants would pay $1,000.00 per month to the Landlord in cash.

 

3.     S.N. states that he paid separate payments totalling $2,000.00 in cash to the Landlord on March 12, 2018 representing first and last month’s rent. Bank statements produced at the hearing show that on March 12, 2018, there were three withdrawals in the amount of

$500.00, one withdrawal in the amount of $450.00 and one withdrawal in the amount of

$50.00. S.N. states that their bank had a transaction limit of $500.00 so he had to do separate withdrawals in order to pay the $2,000.00 representing first and last month’s rent.

 

4.     S.N. states that the Landlord told him in August, 2019 that he had to leave the rental unit as they wanted to do renovations and gave the Tenants two months notice. No formal N12 notice was provided by the Landlord nor was there sufficient evidence led at the hearing to conclude that sufficient details were provided that could be considered a formal notice under the Act. However, the Tenants believed they had to vacate the rental unit in October, 2019.

 

5.     On October 15, 2019, S.N. told the Landlord he and his family were vacating the rental unit at the end of the month as per their notice they had received from the Landlord.

 

6.     When S.N. was preparing to move out of the rental unit on October 30, 2019, text communications between S.N. and the Landlord state the following:

 

Tenant: “Hi S. We are moving tomorrow. Please refund us the advance I paid.”

 

Landlord: Hi S. When you hand over your keys, gets your money back. Thanks”.

 

7.     The Landlord acknowledged that he sent this text communication as stated above but

then realized afterwards that the Tenants did not provide last month’s rent deposit to him when the tenancy began so he changed his mind and stated that the text communication was sent in error.

 

8.     S.N. testified that rent for the month of October, 2019 was paid by the Tenants on

October 5, 2019 and that the last months’ rent deposit was not applied or returned to the Tenants upon vacating the rental unit.

 

9.     The Tenants delivered their keys to the rental unit to the Landlord on October 31, 2019. It was at this point that the Landlord told S.N. that no monies would be returned to him because he had not provided the Landlord with a last month’s rent deposit. The Tenants vacated the rental unit on October 31, 2019.

 

10.  The Landlord acknowledged that he told the Tenants he wanted to do renovations in the rental unit but denied forcing the Tenants to leave the rental unit.

 

11.  The Landlord agreed that the Tenants paid $1,000.00 per month in cash as rent but stated that the Tenants only paid one month’s rent and did not pay a last month’s rent

 

deposit. The Landlord stated that he told the Tenants that because they did not pay last month’s rent deposit, none would be returned.

 

 

T2 and T6 Application:

 

12.  The Tenants claim that the Landlord substantially interfered with their reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit and are seeking only the following relief in their T2 application:

 

•         Rent differential in the amount of $6,400.00 representing an additional $450.00 per month the Tenants must pay to their new landlord over a twelve month period.

 

13.  The Tenants’ T6 application is essentially with respect to the same allegations as set out in the T2 application but seeks compensation for the suffering the Tenants have gone through. The incidents as set out in the two applications pertain to cockroaches, a leak in the bathroom ceiling and kitchen flooding. At the hearing, the Tenants referred to issues pertaining to laundry facilities but it was not detailed in the applications nor were their applications amended to include these allegations, therefore it will not be considered by the Board.

 

Cockroaches:

 

14.  The Tenants resided in a basement unit which had a kitchen, bathroom and separate entrance but shared laundry facilities with the Landlord. S.N. testified that he observed cockroaches in the unit just two months after moving into the rental unit.

 

15.  S.N. stated that he immediately advised the Landlord of the cockroaches to which the Landlord said it was normal but they could use a spray pesticide to eradicate the cockroaches. The Tenants stated that they could not use a spray pesticide as a treatment option due to having young children and asked for cockroach gel treatment. The Tenants purchased the gel treatment which was applied the cockroach gel in the room where their young child would not have access to but it was ineffective and the cockroaches remained.

 

16.  S.N. testified that because the Landlord felt cockroaches were normal and that one just had to get used to them, there was nothing further done to get rid of the cockroaches.

 

17.  The Landlord testified that they were very surprised to learn that there were cockroaches in the rental unit. Upon inspection, the Landlord observed remnants of cockroach feces in the cabinets. The Landlord stated that there were no cockroaches prior to the Tenants moving into the rental unit and felt that it was the Tenants who brought the cockroaches into the home.

 

18.  When told about cockroaches in the rental unit, the Landlord testified that they bought spray cans in June, 2018 and that the cockroaches were under control for a period of time however, they also contacted a pesticide company who indicated three weeks would

 

be required for a proper treatment regime and that the rental unit would need to be vacate for four to five hours after each treatment. As the Tenants had small children and the chemicals were strong, the Tenants refused the professional chemical treatment that the Landlord offered and instead opted to use spray cans.

 

19.  The Landlord stated that as there was no effective cockroach treatment, the cockroach infestation increased. The Landlord also stated that they were frustrated as the Tenants had food scraps and their garbage bins were inside the rental unit which would have increased the numbers of cockroaches.

 

Flooding in Kitchen:

 

20.  S.N. testified that there was frequent flooding in the kitchen of the rental unit however, he was unable to recall specifics including when these flooding incidents occurred, how often they occurred and how it impacted them other than it made the rental unit damp and moist.         S.N. stated that whenever it rained, rain would enter the rental unit. No details were provided as to where the rental unit had water enter it. The Tenants testified that they do not know when they would have told the Landlord but stated that the Landlord never repaired or checked the defect which made the rental unit damp and moist.

 

21.  The Landlord did not provide any comments at the hearing regarding any flooding in the kitchen or that it was damp and moist.

 

Bathroom ceiling leak:

 

22.  S.N. testified that he told the Landlord there was water leaking from the bathroom ceiling in April of 2019. The Tenant stated that there was water dripping down from the ceiling and that it gradually increased over time so that the ceiling completely broke, leaving a damaged and collapsed ceiling of approximately 15 to 20cm in length. S.N. stated that when he told the Landlord about the leak from the ceiling, they were told to put a bucket down to catch the water and did nothing further until it was finally repaired in June of 2019.

 

23.  The Tenants stated that the bathroom ceiling leak was not repaired for two months.

 

24.  The Landlord stated that he contacted a plumber to fix the bathroom ceiling. That plumber failed to attend the rental unit so another plumber was contacted by the Landlord. The Landlord states that it took 2 weeks to fix the bathroom ceiling because their first plumber failed to attend.

 

The Law/Analysis:

 

T1 Application:

 

25.  Pursuant to section 106 of the Act,

 

A landlord may require a tenant to pay a rent deposit with respect to a tenancy if the landlord does so on or before entering into the tenancy agreement.

…

 

A landlord shall apply aren’t deposit that a tenant has paid to the landlord or to a former landlord in payment of the rent for the last rent period before the tenancy terminates.

 

26.  Based on the evidence and submissions and on a balance of probabilities, I am satisfied that the Tenants did provide a last month’s rent deposit to the Landlord at the commencement of the tenancy and upon vacating the unit, it was not applied for the last month’s rent nor returned to the Tenants. I say this because at the hearing, S.N. provided statements from his bank that showed on the date he and his family moved into the rental unit, the total sum of $2,000.00 was withdrawn from his bank account and that this sum totalled first and last month’s rent deposit.

 

27.  Further, the Landlord acknowledged that the monies would be returned to the Tenant in text communications just prior to the Tenant vacating the rental unit but then stated at the hearing that the comment was made in error.

 

28.  The Landlord shall pay to the Tenants $1,000.00 representing the last month’s rent that was not applied or returned to the Tenants.

 

T2 Application:

 

29.  Section 22 of the Act states:

 

A landlord shall not at any time during a tenant’s occupancy of a rental unit and before the day on which an order evicting the tenant is executed substantially interfere with the reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or the residential complex in which it is located for all usual purposes by a tenant or members of his or her household.

 

30.  The T2 application does not particularize what the Tenants’ are claiming other than setting out what would properly be related to section 20 of the Act. In my view, it is more appropriate for the Tenants to rely on section 20 of the Act, in these circumstances, rather than section 22. However, even if I was prepared to make a finding that the Landlord breached s. 22 of the Act, there would be no point in doing so as the Tenants would not be entitled to double recovery; no additional remedy would flow.

 

31.  Further, the Tenants’ only remedy for their allegation of substantial interference is that they were seeking a rent differential and I am not satisfied, based on the evidence and

 

submissions at the hearing and on a balance of probabilities, that the Tenants were forced to secure new accommodations due to substantial interference by the Landlord, particularly due to the allegations of the Landlord’s lack of dealing with cockroaches, a bathroom ceiling that leaked but was repaired and flooding in the kitchen which was not substantiated at the hearing. I say this because the Tenants did nothing more than a gel treatment to deal with the cockroaches and refused other possible treatments, the bathroom ceiling was repaired in a reasonably timely manner and there was insufficient particulars for the Board to consider the allegations of flooding in the kitchen. There was very little, if any, evidence on how these issues substantially interfered with the Tenants. As a result, that application shall be dismissed.

 

T6 Application:

 

32.  Subsection 20(1) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the “Act’) says:

 

A landlord is responsible for providing and maintaining a residential complex, including the rent units in it, in a good state of repair and fit for habitation and for complying with health, safety, housing and maintenance standards.

 

33.  Here, there is no dispute that the Landlord responded to the Tenants’ claims about cockroaches promptly. Rather, the issue is that the treatment option selected by the Tenants did not eradicate the infestation and was probably not as effective as a commercially available treatment as the infestation continued. The Landlord investigated treatment options and put to the Tenants the available options including having a professional chemical treatment performed by a pest control technician but the Tenants refused and preferred to use alternative methods.

 

34.  Section 16 of the Act says:

 

When a landlord or a tenant becomes liable to pay any amount as a result of a breach of a tenancy agreement, the person entitled to claim the amount has a duty to take reasonable steps to minimize the person’s losses.

 

35.  This concept is frequently referred to as the duty to mitigate. Put simply, in disrepair situations tenants have a positive obligation to report the disrepair and to co-operate reasonably with the landlord’s attempts to remedy it. Where a tenant interferes with or hampers a Landlord trying to do repairs that behaviour can disentitle the tenant to a remedy or otherwise reduce the remedy awarded. In this case, the Tenants chose to utilize a gel rather than have a professional fumigation and it was not effective. They refused to attempt other remedies and/or solutions (ie: removing garbage from inside the rental unit) to eradicate the cockroach infestation once it was determined that the cockroach infestation continued.

 

36.  There was no dispute between the parties with respect to there being a bathroom ceiling leak however, it was not agreed as to how long the repair took to complete. I accept the Landlord’s evidence that once they became aware of the issue, they took steps to remedy the leak and repair the ceiling in a reasonable period of time.

 

 

 

37.  It would be a reasonable expectation that at some point in a tenancy, some disrepair will probably occur and because it would be expected, no remedy would usually flow where the landlord successfully resolved the problem promptly.

 

38.  The only remedy the Tenants’ T6 application seeks is an unspecified amount for compensation. As for the request for compensation, the application did not contain a figure claimed and stated “for the suffering” the Tenants have gone through. At the hearing, the Tenants stated that as new immigrants to Canada, the suffering they experienced at the Landlord’s hands was inappropriate and that they felt taken advantage of. Given the lack of an amount and absent an explanation in the T6 application with respect to the compensation sought, the request shall be denied.

 

39.  Therefore I find that the Tenants’ T2 and T6 applications are dismissed.

 

40.  This order contains all of the reasons in this matter and no further reasons will be issued.

 

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     The Tenants’ T2 and T6 applications are dismissed.

 

2.     With respect to the Tenants’ T1 application, the Landlord shall pay to the Tenants the sum of $1,000.00. This amount represents $1,000.00 for the last month's rent deposit that was not applied or returned to the Tenants.

 

3.     If the Landlord does not pay the Tenants the full amount owing by February 10, 2022, the Landlord will owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from February 11, 2022 at 2.00% annually on the outstanding balance.

 

4.     The Tenants have the right, at any time, to collect the full amount owing or any balance outstanding under this order.
