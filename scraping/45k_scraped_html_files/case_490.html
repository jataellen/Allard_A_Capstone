Order under Section 30

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TNT-25457-20

 

 

In the matter of:

BASEMENT, 448 LYNETT CRESCENT RICHMOND HILL ON L4C2V7

 

Between:

Linda Tieu Phat Tieu

Tenants

 

 

and

 

 

Peng Li

Landlord

 

 

 

Linda Tieu and Phat Tieu (the 'Tenants') applied for an order determining that Peng Li (the 'Landlord') failed to meet the Landlord's maintenance obligations under the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act') or failed to comply with health, safety, housing or maintenance standards.

 

This application was heard by videoconference on October 1, 2021, with two subsequent interim orders issued. Interim order dated December 6, 2021 requested the parties to provide final written submission on or before December 31, 2021.

 

The Tenant Phat Tieu, the Tenants’ interpreter T. Lai, the Landlord and the Landlord’s representative R. Yu attended the hearing.

 

Determinations:

 

1.   The Tenant brought a T6 Application, pursuant to s.29(1) Residential Tenancies

Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’), which alleges the Landlord has breached an obligation under s.20(1):

 

A landlord is responsible for providing and maintaining a residential complex, including the rental units in it, in a good state of repair and fit for habitation and for complying with health, safety, housing and maintenance standards

 

2.   The monthly rent was $800.00. The Tenants moved into the rental unit in July 2019. The Tenants were still in possession of the rental unit at the time the application was heard.

 

Preliminary Issue

 

3.   Pursuant to my interim order dated October 25, 2021, I requested that the Tenants advise the Board on or before November 9, 2021 as to whether or not they intended to proceed with allegation #10 in their application, namely that the driveway was poorly maintained and that one of the Tenants had suffered an injury as a result (the “Driveway Ice

 

Allegation”). On November 1, 2021, the Tenants advised that they wished to withdraw the Driveway Ice Allegation. Accordingly, by interim order dated December 6, 2021, the Tenants’ Driveway Ice Allegation was withdrawn.

 

4.   The Tenants thus make the following allegations:

 

1   the Dryer was not working for over 1 month;

2   the alarm system is not interconnected;

3   there are no fire extinguishers in the house;

4   there is no heat in the rental unit;

5   the sewer backup causes a foul odor in the rental unit;

6   the kitchen and toilet sinks are clogged;

7   there is black mould in the house;

8   there are centipedes in the basement; and,

9   the circuit breakers keep tripping.

 

Dryer

 

1.   The Tenants allege that the dryer was non-functional for 1 month. The Tenant Phat Lieu (“PL”) states the dryer stopped working at the beginning of February and was not repaired until February 29, 2020.  The Landlord submitted he acted reasonably upon being advised of this issue, stating at the request of the Tenants he was delayed in performing repairs since he had just returned from China and was requested to wait 14 days prior to entering the rental unit.

 

2.   While it is reasonable for the Landlord to take the precautions he took, the Landlord could have arranged for another individual or some other alternative course of action to ensure the timely repair of the dryer. I find the lack of a functioning dryer during the month of February caused a minimal disruption to the Tenants’ ability to normally reside in the unit. I find an abatement of $40, being approximately 5% of rent for the month of February to be appropriate in the circumstances.

 

Alarm system & Fire extinguishers

 

3.   The Tenants allege the smoke alarm system is not interconnected in the house and that the Landlord failed to install a fire extinguisher in the house. The Landlord admits the fire alarm is not “interconnected”, but states there is a functional battery-operated fire alarm system at each level, which PL admitted on cross-examination. The Tenant did not allege that the current set-up was a breach of the Ontario Fire Code, or any other municipal standard or regulation. Based upon the evidence presented, I do not find that the Tenant has proven on a balance of probabilities that the Landlord breached his obligations under s.20(1) as a result of this issue.

 

4.   The Landlord tendered evidence that a fire extinguisher was installed at the residential complex shortly after being required to do so, following a municipal inspection. The Tenant admitted that he did not raise the issue of the fire extinguisher with the Landlord

 

prior to the municipal inspection. While it is regrettable that the fire extinguisher was not installed sooner, the Tenants did not show there was any disruption to their ability to normally reside in the unit resulting from this infraction, and thus, I am not awarding any abatement as a result of this allegation.

 

Heat

 

5.   The Tenants allege there was a “lack of heat” in the basement, causing the Tenants to cough and get sick. While PL admits there was heat in the upstairs unit, he states there was no heat coming out of the ductwork to his unit. For this reason, he had to wear a jacket. He alleges he advised the Landlord of this concern prior to the winter of 2019 and he was forced to purchase 3 personal heaters which, ultimately, resolved this issue.

 

6.   The Landlord denies there was a lack of heat in the basement unit and tendered into evidence pictures showing heat vents in various rooms within the Tenant’s unit, as well as a fireplace.  The onus in this type of application rests with the Tenants. There were no temperature readings produced, evidence as to the frequency of the lack of heat, or any other independent, corroborating evidence to prove the actual temperature in the Tenants’ unit. I do not find that the Tenants have proven on a balance of probabilities that the Landlord failed to provide adequate heat to the rental unit.

 

Sewer-backup

 

7.   The Tenants allege the sewer backed up in October 2019 and created a foul odor in the house. The Tenants also produced pictures showing black markings on the tile besides the drain, suggesting the black markings were feces requiring clean up.

 

8.   The Landlord testified that the backup was caused by heavy rain and the Landlord authorized the Tenant to call a plumbing service and reimbursed the Tenant for the expenses incurred. Further, the Landlord advised that a subsequent back-up two days later was immediately resolved after the Landlord arranged for a plumber.                                                                                                                                 PL acknowledged that the Landlord arranged for a plumber to unplug the drain, but stated the plumber indicated the pipe was clogged “25 feet away” and needed to be changed, which was not done.

 

9.   In Onyskiw v. CJM Property Management (“Oniskew”), the Ontario Court of Appeal 2016 ONCA 477 (CanLII) provided a guiding framework for the analysis of alleged breaches of section 20 of the Act. The Court of Appeal urged a “contextual approach” to the analysis of maintenance and repair issues. The court also rejected that the mere occurrence of a problem constitutes an automatic breach of the Act. While it is unfortunate that the sewer backup occurred, it does not appear as though the Landlord could have reasonably anticipated the backup, and I find the Landlord acted quickly upon becoming aware of this issue. This portion of the Tenants’ application is dismissed.

 

Sinks clogged

 

10. The Tenants allege the kitchen/bathroom sinks would get clogged from time to time, making it difficult to use and wash dishes. PL testified that this issue was brought to the

 

Landlord’s attention orally, as it only happened “once in a while”. The Tenants did not provide particulars as to the dates that the clogging occurred, whether the clogging occurred at anytime after the plumber came, and/or how much it impacted their living situation.  I find the Tenants have not proven on a balance of probabilities that the Landlord breached his obligations under s.20(1) in respect of this allegation.

 

Black Mould

 

11. The Tenants allege that there was black mould in the laundry room, caused by water leaking around the windows in the upstairs unit.  PL states he verbally notified the Landlord “a few weeks” after move-in, but the Landlord did not address this concern. While the Tenant sent one picture in support of the allegation, it could not be ascertained from the picture that (i) there was black mould, and if present, (ii) how much was present. The Tenant indicated the mould makes it difficult to breathe after he uses the laundry room. The Tenant, however, did not produce any medical documentation or any other diagnosis confirming or linking any health symptoms to the alleged presence of mould. On cross-examination, PL admitted he has not seen a doctor in respect of this issue.

 

12. The Landlord submitted that there was an inspection of the rental unit prior to renting the unit and that no mould was found. He states the Tenants did not advise the Landlord of this issue. The burden of proof in this application rests upon the Tenants, and I find the Tenants have not proven on a balance of probabilities that there was black mould. This allegation is dismissed.

 

Centipedes

 

13. The Tenants allege that centipedes were discovered a few days after moving into the rental unit, and produced a picture of one centipede. While PL feared the centipedes would bite, he conceded on cross-examination that he has never been bit. PL also testified that the Landlord was verbally notified about this issue 3 weeks after move-in, but nothing was done. The Landlord denies both the presence of centipedes and being advised by the Tenants of their alleged existence.

 

14. The burden of proof in this application rests upon the Tenants. Given the lack of communication between the Tenant and the Landlord on this issue and the lack of evidence produced that there was a pest problem in the rental unit, I am not convinced that the landlord breached his obligations under s.20(1) as a result of this particular allegation This portion of the Tenant’s claim is dismissed.

 

Circuit breakers

 

7        The Tenants alleged that circuit breakers keep tripping, causing lights to go offer every 2-3 weeks. When this occurred, the breaker had to be reset, and the lights would go back on. The Tenant indicated he told the Landlord shortly after move-in. The Landlord submits he acted reasonably since the home was powered as per industry standard, and that the Tenants usage caused the circuit breakers to trip.

 

8        The Tenant’s evidence as to the duration and impact of the circuit breaker was vague and it is unclear upon what dates this occurred, or how it could have been prevented. Based upon the limited evidence before me, I do not find that the Tenants have proven on a balance of probabilities that the Landlord breached his obligations under s.20(1).

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     The Landlord shall pay to the Tenants a rent abatement of $40.00.

 

2.     The Landlord shall pay the Tenants the full amount owing by February 28, 2022.

 

3.     If the Landlord does not pay the Tenants the full amount owing by February 28, 2022 the Landlord will owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from March 1, 2022 at 2.00% annually on the outstanding balance.

 

4.     The Tenant has the right, at any time, to collect the full amount owing or any balance outstanding under this order.
