Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: SWL-52923-21

 

 

In the matter of:

1, 43 BLANDFORD STREET WOODSTOCK ON N4S7H7

 

Between:

5008767 Ontario Ltd

Landlord

 

 

and

 

 

John Roloson Trista Clunas

Tenants

 

 

 

5008767 Ontario Ltd (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict John Roloson and Trista Clunas (the 'Tenants') because they, another occupant of the rental unit or someone the Tenants permitted in the residential complex has substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment or lawful right, privilege or interest of the Landlord or another tenant; and because they another occupant of the rental unit or someone they permitted in the residential complex have wilfully or negligently caused undue damage to the premises. The Landlord has also applied for an order requiring the Tenants to compensate the Landlord for the damage.

 

This application was heard via videoconference on January 10, 2022. Only the Landlord’s legal agent, H. Caron, attended the hearing. As if 10:22 a.m., the Tenants were not present or represented although properly served with notice of this hearing by the Board.

 

Determinations:

 

1.     The Landlord applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict the Tenants because they, another occupant of the rental unit or someone the Tenants permitted in the residential complex has substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment or lawful right, privilege or interest of the Landlord or another tenant; and because they another occupant of the rental unit or someone they permitted in the residential complex have wilfully or negligently caused undue damage to the premises. The Landlord had served on the Tenants with two N5 notices. The first N5 had a termination date was July 20, 2021 and was served on June 30, 2021. The second had a termination date of July 24, 2021 and was served on July 9, 2021.

 

2.     The Landlord’s legal agent testified she is the owner of the unit. The first N5 was pertaining to the Tenants’ substantial interference and damages. On June 24, 2021, the Landlord’s property manager attended the Tenants’ unit and noted the Tenants erected a tent in the common area, damaged the exterior walls, and stored various refuse in the common areas. The Tenants were requested to clean up the area. An inspection of the interior of the unit

 

was scheduled for June 28, 2021. On this date, the erected tent was not removed, and the Tenants refused the Landlord’s access for inspection. On June 29, 2021, the Landlords scheduled an inspection of the interior of the Tenants’ unit on July 1, 2021, for which the Tenants refused.

 

3.     The Landlord served a second N5 notice on the Tenants, seeking termination of the tenancy for the Tenants’ wilful or negligent damage. The notice repeats the events of June 28, 2021 and July 1, 2021 wherein the Tenants denied entry into the unit for inspection. The new event took place on July 3, 2021 wherein the Landlord attended the unit to inspect the unit for which the Tenants again denied access. The police were called to the unit to provide the Landlord access. While inside, the Landlord noted that damages to the interior were substantial. A. Masri from Master Homes Renovations attended with her and estimated the damages at $44,904.90.

 

Law and Analysis

 

4.     The Landlord’s claim for damages in the first N5 is invalid. Subsection 62(2) of the Act sets out the required elements for a valid N5 Notice for damages:

 

62.   (2) A notice of termination under this section shall,

 

(a)  provide a termination date not earlier than the 20th day after the notice is given;

(b)  set out the grounds for termination; and

(c)  require the tenant, within seven days,

(i)  to repair the damaged property or pay to the landlord the reasonable costs of repairing the damaged property, or

(ii) to replace the damaged property or pay to the landlord the reasonable costs of replacing the damaged property, if it is not reasonable to repair the damaged property.

 

 

5.     While the Landlord included an option to void, she did not indicate what the reasonable costs were to repair or replace the damaged property as required by paragraph (c) above. As such, this portion of the Landlord’s first N5 notice is dismissed.

 

6.     Subsection 64 (1) of the Act states:

 

A landlord may give a tenant notice of termination of the tenancy if the conduct of the tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or a person permitted in the residential complex by the tenant is such that it substantially interferes with the reasonable enjoyment of the residential complex for all usual purposes by the landlord or another tenant or substantially interferes with another lawful right, privilege or interest of the landlord or another tenant. 2006, c. 17, s. 64 (1).

 

7.     Based on the uncontested evidence of the Landlord, I find that the Tenants substantially interfered with the Landlord’s reasonable enjoyment by failing to address the substantial amount of refuse and refusing entry into the unit for inspections.

 

8.     Section 68(1) of the Act states that a Landlord may give a tenant a second notice of termination of the tenancy if a notice of termination was given to the tenant under section 62, 64 or 67 of the Act and if more than seven days but less than six months after the first notice was given, conduct occurs that constitutes grounds for a notice of termination under section 60, 61, 62, 64 or 67. The conduct that occurred in the second N5 constitutes grounds for a notice of termination under section 62 of the Act which allows the Landlord to serve a notice of termination for wilful or negligent damage and section 64 pertaining to substantial interference. However, the conduct alleged in the second N5 notice took place within seven days after the first notice was served. As such, the Landlord cannot terminate the tenancy based on the second N5.

 

9.     However, subsection 64(3) states that the notice of termination under subsection (1) is void if the tenant, within seven days after receiving the notice, stops the conduct or activity or corrects the omission. The evidence at the hearing and the second N5 notice describes how the Tenants denied the Landlord’s access for inspection on July 1, 2021 and July 3, 2021. As such, I find that the Tenants did not void the first N5 notice.

 

DAMAGES

 

10.  The Landlord also applied for compensation for damages. Photographs of the damages taken on September 22, 2021 were submitted into evidence. The photographs showed garbage in the front and back of the unit, a torn screen in the master bedroom window, stucco that had been scraped and damaged, torn wallpaper, severely damaged carpet, damaged smoke detector and carbon monoxide detector, substantial water damage and mold was found in the bathroom, the master bedroom closet door was off it’s hook, the light fixture in the master bedroom was damaged, the light switches in multiple rooms were broken, large holes in the bedroom and kitchen wall, damaged stove, scraped baseboards, kitchen counters had dents and chips, among other things.

 

11.  The Landlord also submitted into evidence the quote for damages from A. Masri from Master Homes renovations dated July 6, 2021. The email detailed the damages and provided the estimate for repair at $44,904.90. The Landlord testified that the quote was just for repair and not for any improvements or renovations.

 

Law and Analysis

 

12.  Subsection 89(1) says:

 

A landlord may apply to the Board for an order requiring a tenant to pay reasonable costs that the landlord has incurred or will incur for the repair of or, where repairing is not reasonable, the replacement of damaged property, if the tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or a person whom the tenant permits in the residential complex wilfully or negligently causes undue damage to the rental unit or the residential complex and the tenant is in possession of the rental unit.

 

13.  The language of section 89 requires damage to be undue. It is recognized that some damage will occur over the regular course of time, due to regular wear and tear in a

 

residential complex. Reasonable wear and tear would not normally constitute “undue damage” (Kamoo v. Brampton Caledon Housing Corp. 2005 O.J. No. 3911).

 

14.  Based on the uncontested evidence of the Landlord, I find that the Tenants caused this damage and the damage was undue. I base this on the photographs submitted into evidence, showing substantial damage throughout the unit and on the exterior of the unit.

 

15.  The remaining issue is whether the Landlord’s claim for $35,000.00 to repair or replace the damage is reasonable. I find that it is. The Landlord’s uncontested evidence from A. Masri clearly identified the damage in the unit and the estimated cost to repair. The amount claimed was well above the Board’s monetary jurisdiction of $35,000.00. I am also satisfied that the quote was only for repairs to the unit and did not include any claims for improvements or renovations.

 

Relief from Eviction

 

16.  I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would be unfair to grant relief from eviction pursuant to subsection 83(1) of the Act. The Landlord was not aware of any of the Tenants’ circumstances for me to consider denying or delaying eviction. The Tenants did not attend the hearing to testify to their circumstances. As such, relief will not be granted.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     The tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenants is terminated, as of February 7, 2022. The Tenants must move out of the rental unit on or before February 7, 2022.

 

2.     The Tenants shall pay to the Landlord $35,000.00, which represents the reasonable costs of repairing the damage and replacing the damaged property.

 

3.     If the Tenants do not pay the Landlord the full amount owing on or before February 7, 2022, they will start to owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from February 8, 2022 at 2.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

 

4.     If the unit is not vacated on or before February 7, 2022, then starting February 8, 2022, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

 

5.     Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord on or after February 8, 2022.
