Order under Section 31

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TET-13996-20

 

 

In the matter of:

A, 1787 BRONTE SQUARE PICKERING ON L1V3C1

 

Between:

Mary Pat Nash

Tenant

 

 

and

 

 

Arham Shakoor

Landlord

 

 

 

Mary Pat Nash (the 'Tenant') applied for an order determining that Arham Shakoor (the 'Landlord') harassed, obstructed, coerced, threatened or interfered with the Tenant.

 

This application was heard by videoconference on December 14, 2021.

 

The Tenant and the Landlord attended the hearing. The Tenant spoke with Tenant Duty Counsel before the hearing.

 

Preliminary Issues

 

1.     Prior to the start of the hearing, I raised two preliminary issues with the Tenant.

 

2.     Having reviewed the Tenants application, I had noted that a number of the allegations and remedies sought by the Tenant were outside of the one-year time limit as outlined under s. 29(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act (the “Act”).

 

3.     As such, I had asked the Tenant to keep her testimony within one-year of the filing date of September 14th, 2020. Taking into consideration s. 1 of Ontario Regulation 73/20, which suspended limitation periods from March 16th, 2020, to September 14th, 2020 due to the COVID -19 pandemic and declared emergency, this would take the limitation period for the claim to March 16th, 2019.

 

4.     I further noted that the Tenant filed a T2 application alleging that the Landlord harassed, obstructed, coerced, threatened, or interfered with the Tenant in violation of s. 23 of the Act and had only noted this allegation in her application.

 

5.     If an allegation under s. 23 is proven by the Tenant, they may seek a remedy under s. 29 of the Act.

 

6.     Noting the Tenants application, some of the allegations contained issues with maintenance which would normally be included as part of a T6 application alleging a violation under s. 20 of the Act, in which a remedy under s. 29 may also be sought.

 

7.     Section 185 of the Act states:

 

(1) An application shall be filed with the Board in the form approved by the Board, shall be accompanied by the prescribed information and shall be signed by the applicant.

 

8.     Despite this section I find that T2 and T6 applications, while being different Board approved forms both contain remedies under s. 29 of the Act and can be applied to one another within this application.

 

9.     As such, I was prepared to accept evidence on this allegation.

 

10.  However, the Tenants application contained further allegations with respect to an illegal charge and an allegation that the Tenant was not paid interest on last month’s rent.

 

11.  These allegations would normally be included in a T1 application, and remedies would be sought under ss. 135 and 106(10) respectively.

 

12.  As such, I find that the Tenants T2 application could not consider these items. I note that the Tenant spoke with Tenant Duty Counsel ahead of the hearing and had also received advice from the Board before filing her claim. Presumably the Tenant would have been advised of these issues ahead of both completing her claim and conducting the hearing.

 

Maintenance

 

13.  The Tenant moved into the unit with her ex-husband on July 1st, 2016. The Tenant has since moved out of the unit as of July 31st, 2020.

 

14.  When the Tenants moved in, the lease that they had signed stipulated that the Tenants would be responsible for lawn care and snow removal. The Tenant’s ex-husband completed this work until he moved out in 2019, and then the Tenant would hire outside help.

 

15.  While the lease stipulates these terms, the Tenant is correct in her assertion that snow removal is the responsibility of the Landlord pursuant to s. 26(1)(5) of Ontario Regulation 517/06.

 

16.  That said, the Tenant is seeking $896 for snow removal, estimating 16 removals a year for 4 years. The issue is here is that I can only consider snow removal going back to March 16th, 2019. While the Tenant never provided any receipts nor could provide me any dates or the exact costs, this issue was uncontested by the Landlord. As such, an abatement will be awarded as a result.

 

17.  The next issue that the Tenant alleged was that the Landlord never secured the premises properly which caused the Tenant to never feel safe after a break-in however, the Tenant submitted that the Landlord had secured the wooden door with a locking mechanism and had mounted security cameras around the premises. The Tenant further admitted under cross-examination that she never advised the Landlord of this issue because she didn’t want to “stir the pot”.

 

18.  Other than the issue with snow removal, I find that there is no evidence to support the other maintenance issue the Tenant has alleged, and that the Tenant never properly advised the Landlord of this issue in a timely manner as required under s. 30(2) of the Act. As such, this portion of the application is dismissed.

 

Harassment

 

19.  The Tenant alleges that the Landlord contacted her ex-husband after he had moved out to harass, threaten or coerce her.

 

20.  The Tenant submitted that she had asked the Landlord to use a new e-mail address as her ex-husband had access to the old one, and the end of the relationship had been acrimonious.

 

21.  The Tenant submits that the Landlord tried to get the Tenants ex-husband involved in this proceeding to harass her.

 

22.  The Landlord submits that the Tenant never advised him that her ex-husband had moved out of the unit in March of 2019, and that she was still talking about him in May of that year.

 

23.  The Landlord submitted e-mails from that time-period showing that the Tenant had never mentioned to the Landlord that her ex-husband had moved out and that she did not want him to be contacted with respect to tenancy issues.

 

24.  As such, I don’t believe that at any point when the Tenant’s ex-husband moved out of the unit the Landlord was ever formally notified of same and was never told not to contact him as he was still a tenant on the lease. This allegation is dismissed.

 

Reference Letter

 

25.  The Landlord submitted a text message thread dated August 9th, 2020 into evidence between himself and the Tenant detailing a request from the Tenant for a reference letter to assist her in obtaining a new tenancy.

 

26.  Because of some maintenance issues that came to the Landlord’s attention after the Tenant’s moved out, the Landlord refused.

 

27.  The Tenant refuted this allegation and then goes on to say:

 

“…If you don’t want to provide me a reference so that I can access housing, then I will take you to the tenant board for the difference…If you would prefer to not go the legal route and provide us with a reference we can do that”

 

28.  I find this narrative used by the Tenant could be interpreted as an abuse of process under

s. A8.2 of the Landlord and Tenant Board Rules of Procedure however, I will not go as far as to make a finding in that regard considering the tenancy has ended and this issue has now been completed.

 

Remedies:

 

29.  With respect to the issue of snow removal, parties to a tenancy cannot contract themselves out of the Act and as such the Landlord was responsible for snow removal from March 16th, 2019 until July 31st, 2020 when the Tenant vacated the unit. That period roughly covers the winter of 2019-20.

 

30.  While the Tenant does not possess any receipts, she estimates having paid $14 per removal at 16 removals per year. The total then would be $224.00. This is a reasonable out of pocket expense.

 

It is ordered that:

 

31.  The total amount the Landlord owes is $224.00.

 

32.  The Landlord shall pay the Tenant the full amount owing by January 30th, 2022.

 

33.  If the Landlord does not pay the Tenant the full amount owing of $224.00 on or before January 30th, 2022, then the Landlords will start to owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from January 31st, 2022 annually on the balance outstanding

 

34.  The Tenant's remaining application is dismissed.
