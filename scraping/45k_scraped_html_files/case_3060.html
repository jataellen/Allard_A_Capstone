Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TEL-18240-21

 

 

In the matter of:

UNIT 1, 399 PINE AVENUE OSHAWA ON L1J2J1

 

Between:

Leila Noreen Khan Leonardo Dino Biondo

Landlords

 

 

and

 

 

Aysha (Elenore) Jones

Tenant

 

 

 

 

Leila Noreen Khan and Leonardo Dino Biondo (the 'Landlords') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict Aysha (Elenore) Jones (the 'Tenant') because the Landlord requires possession of the rental unit for the purpose of residential occupation.

 

This application was heard by video conference on December 10, 2021.

 

The Landlords, the Landlords’ legal representative, B. Di Lisi, and the Tenant attended the hearing. The Tenant spoke to Tenant Duty Counsel prior to the hearing.

 

Determinations:

 

1.     The Landlords are business partners. Both Landlords submitted to the Board Declarations that stated that L. Biondo (LB) was moving into the rental unit for a minimum of one year. Both Landlords also stated that they had not served a previous N12 notice of termination on the Tenant in the past two years.

 

2.     The termination date on the N12 notice of termination was September 30, 2021, and the Tenant did not move out on that date. The Tenant was still in possession of the rental unit on the date of the hearing.

 

3.     The monthly rent is $850.00.

 

Preliminary Issues:

4.     The Tenant raised two preliminary issues. She said that the address on the N12 notice of termination was not correct, and she was not given compensation equal to one month’s rent, in contravention of sections 48.1 and 55.1 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’).

 

 

 

5.     The Tenant said that the postal code listed on the N12 notice of termination was not correct.

 

6.     The Tenant also said that the Landlords did not give her compensation equal to one month’s rent. Instead, she said they refused to accept the monthly rent for August, 2021.

 

7.     The Landlords said that the postal code listed on the N12 notice of termination was the postal code from google maps, and it is the postal code listed on all the paperwork related to the property.

 

8.     I find that the Tenant failed to prove, on a balance of probabilities, that the postal code on the N12 notice of termination was not correct. The postal code on the N12 notice of termination matched google maps. In addition, the Tenant did not allege that the notice or the application had gone astray, or that she had not received it because of the incorrect postal code. Consequently, I find that the N12 notice of termination was in substantial compliance with the requirements of the Act for notices of termination.

 

9.     Sections 48.1 and 55.1 of the Act oblige a Landlord to compensate a Tenant in the amount equal to one month’s rent when a Landlord serves a Tenant a notice of termination of the tenancy under section 48. The Act is silent about the form or means of paying the required compensation. Refusing to accept a month’s rent is equivalent to compensating the Tenant a month’s rent. The Landlords refused a month’s rent prior to the termination date of September 30, 2021. Consequently, I find that the Landlords complied with sections 48.1 and 55.1 of the Act.

 

10.  The rental unit is the basement apartment in a duplex.

 

11.  The Landlord, LB, said that when the Landlords purchased the residential complex on November 6, 2020, it was an illegal triplex. He said that the Landlords have now converted the residential complex into a legal duplex.

 

12.  LB said that he is presently separated from his wife, and he is staying temporarily with his business partner, the other Landlord, L. Khan (LK).

 

13.  LB said that he vacated his marital home on November 1, 2021, and he wants to move into the rental unit as soon as possible. He said that he intends to stay there for the foreseeable future.

 

14.  LB submitted into evidence a letter from Jason Lane of Woitzik Polsinelli Lawyers and Mediators, dated December 3, 2021, that stated that LB had consulted with him “regarding the matrimonial matters between Dino and Irene Biondo, specifically with regard to Dino vacating the matrimonial home….”

 

15.  LB said that his matrimonial home is in Stouffville. He said that even though the rental unit is in Oshawa, it does not matter where he is based since he is a home inspector who travels all over for his profession. He said that besides the matrimonial home, he has a cottage way up north to which it is impractical to move. He said that his home

 

inspections are carried out from Hamilton to Port Hope, but he mostly works in the Durham area.

 

16.  The Tenant challenged the LB’s good faith intention to move into the rental unit.

 

17.  The Tenant said that LB has harassed her and tried to evict her since before he owned the residential complex. She said that LB was “involved” with the previous Landlord, and he told her that her rent is too low, and she said that he has threatened to serve her with an N12 notice of termination on multiple occasions.

 

18.  The Tenant submitted into evidence multiple pages of notes that recounted her recollections of events that occurred over the previous year. She referred to one of the pages of notes that were recollections from September 23, 2020, the day she observed a “sold” sign on the lawn of the residential complex. The Tenant said that earlier in the day there was severe pounding and banging on her front door, and someone yelled that she, the Tenant, was insane. The Tenant said that this was “later revealed to be Khan”, i.e. one of the Landlords.

 

19.  The Tenant said that on June 16, 2021, LB called her on the phone and said that her rental unit was overcrowded because the father of her child was living with her. She said that LB threatened to serve her with an N12 notice of termination at that time.

 

20.  The Tenant said that there were a number of times in September and October, 2020, when the Landlords told her they were the owners of the residential complex, and acted as Landlords, even though they had not yet purchased the building. She said that they treated her with “complete negativity and unnecessary harshness”. She said that they kept telling the father of her child that he had to remove his dog from the premises, they called him her “boyfriend” and they laughed at her and the father of her child.

 

21.  The Tenant said that the Landlords came to her window, which faces the backyard, on many occasions. She said that they peered in her windows. She also said that they had tricked her into clearning out all her belongings from the backyard.

 

22.  The Tenant said that she had informed LB that she was very allergic to cats before he rented the upstairs unit to people who had two cats. She submitted into evidence a letter from her doctor, dated July 25, 2021, that is addressed to the Tenant. The letter states that the Tenant suffers from severe allergies and asthma. The Tenant said that she gave the letter to LB by hand, but he refused to look at it.

 

23.  The Tenant said that she has lived in the rental unit since May 2019, and she has been looking for an alternative place to live for a long time. She said that it is impossible to find anything within her budget. She said that her income is currently $1,100.00 social assistance and $1,800.00 child tax credit. The monthly rent is $850.00.

 

24.  The Landlords submit that LB intends to move into the rental unit. They submit that LB chose the basement rental unit, because the upstairs unit is bigger, and it is not needed for LB on his own. The Landlords submit that LB’s marriage broke down over the last year, and he is currently homeless. He requires the rental unit as his residence.

 

 

 

25.  The Tenant submits that LB does not, in good faith, intend to move into the rental unit. She submits that the Landlords intend to raise the rent and get more income from her unit after she moves out. The Tenant submits that it makes no sense for LB to move into a 1 bedroom basement apartment with all his other properties.

 

Reasons and Analysis:

26.  The issue to be determined by the Board is whether the Landlord has satisfied the “good faith” requirement set out in subsection 48(1) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006, (the 'Act') which provides: a landlord may, by notice, terminate a tenancy if the landlord in good faith requires possession of the rental unit for the purpose of residential occupation by,

 

(a)   the landlord

 

(b)   the landlord’s spouse

 

(c)   a child or parent of the landlord or the landlord’s spouse; or

 

(d)   a person who provides or will provide care services………….

 

27.  In the leading case law involving a landlord’s own use application, Salter v. Beljinac, 2001 CanLII 40231 (ON SCDC), [2001], O.J. No. 2792 (Div. Ct.), a case in which the Landlord filed the application on the basis that he required possession of the rental unit for purpose of residential occupation by his adult son and the son’s family, the Divisional Court held that:

 

the test of good faith is genuine intention to occupy the premises and not the reasonableness of the landlord’s proposal…

 

28.  The case law in this area establishes that the test is determined by considering the intention of the person named in the application. If that person genuinely intends to reside in the unit then the notice is given in good faith.

 

29.  The Landlord in this case, LB, lived with his wife in a large home in Stouffville. He also owns a country property up north. The rental unit is a 1 bedroom basement unit in Oshawa, about 50 km away from Stouffville.

 

30.  Although it makes sense for LB to live in a property he owns, rather than to rent a new one, his proposal to move that far away from his family to live in a basement does not appear to be that reasonable.

 

31.  The Tenant gave vague testimony about the Landlords negativity and harshness towards her. She said they harassed her, and they tried to evict her. She had no documents and no prior notices of termination to support her allegations.

 

32.  It is undisputed that the Landlords began acting as Landlords before they, in fact, owned the residential complex. However, that alone does not support the Tenant’s allegation

 

that the Landlords harassed her, nor that LB does not intend, in good faith, to move into the rental unit.

 

33.  The Tenant also has the option of filing a Tenant application claiming that the Landlords harassed her.

 

34.  LB had documentary evidence of consultation with a lawyer about his marital issues. The Tenant pointed out that the date of the letter, December 3, 2021, was just prior to the hearing date, and it was after the Landlords had received her disclosure for the hearing. The Tenant said that LB would have submitted such a letter long before December 3, 2021, in order to support the application, if he were genuinely intending to move into the rental unit because he was separating from his wife.

 

35.  The Tenant’s evidence primarily focused on harassment by the Landlords, and it was very vague and ungrounded by documentary evidence. Tenants frequently do not have a good relationship with their Landlord. However, even if there was harassment by the Landlords, it does not mean that LB does not intend to move into the rental unit.

 

36.  I find that the Landlords have proved, on balance of probabilities, that LB intends, in good faith, to move into the rental unit for the following reasons. It is LB’s testimony that he separated from his wife and left the marital home, and he can work anywhere in south western Ontario. LB said that he has a lot of work in the Durham region where the rental unit is located. A basement apartment may not be the type of residence he is used to, but it provides him a base for which he does not have to pay extra rent.

 

37.  Therefore, I find that the Landlords in good faith require possession of the rental unit for the purpose of residential occupation for LB.

 

38.  The Landlords paid the Tenant compensation equal to one month's rent on August 1, 2021.

 

39.  The Tenant said that she has been unable to find a rental unit on her budget. She has a very young child, and she lives on social assistance. Although LB said that he is homeless, he has been living with his business partner. I find that it is not unfair to provide the Tenant longer to find an alternative place to live.

 

40.  I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would not be unfair to postpone the eviction until February 28, 2022, pursuant to subsection 83(1)(b) of the Act.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     The tenancy between the Landlords and the Tenant is terminated, as of February 28, 2022. The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before February 28, 2022.

 

2.     If the unit is not vacated on or before February 28, 2022, then starting March 1, 2022, the Landlords may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

 

3.     Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlords on or after March 1, 2022.
