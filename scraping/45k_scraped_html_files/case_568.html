Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: SOL-24914-21

 

 

In the matter of:

574 NORTH MIDDLETON TOWNLINE ROAD COURTLAND ON N0J1E0

 

Between:

Vanmeer Farms Inc

Landlord

 

 

and

 

 

Brian Robertson Laura Robinson

Tenants

 

 

 

Vanmeer Farms Inc (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict Brian Robertson and Laura Robinson (the 'Tenants')

 

This application was heard via video conference on January 5, 2022.

 

The Landlord’s Representative Wilhelmina Vermeersch, the Landlord’s Legal Representative Genevieve Good, the Tenants and the Tenants’ Legal Representative Ian Aitken attended the hearing.

 

 

Determinations:

 

 

1.      For the reasons that follow, I find that the Landlord, on behalf of the purchasers, in good faith requires possession of the rental unit for the purpose of residential occupation. The tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenants will be terminated.

 

2.      The Tenants are in possession of the rental unit which is a rural property. This is a month- to-month tenancy, with rent of $730.00 due on the first of the month.

 

3.      Along with the Landlord’s L2 application, an N12 notice of termination, certificates of service, declarations and Schedule B were completed and attached.

 

4.      On July 20, 2021, the Landlord entered into an agreement of purchase of sale of the rental unit with the purchasers intending to move into the rental unit. The closing date as stipulated on the Agreement of Purchase and Sale was November 1, 2021.

 

5.      On July 28, 2021 the Landlord served an N12 notice of termination on the Tenants. The N12 notice states that the Landlord, on behalf of the purchasers, intend to move into the

 

rental unit and occupy it for at least one year. The N12 notice sought possession of the rental unit for September 30, 2021.

 

6.      The Landlord testified that the closing date of the purchase of the property was extended to March 1, 2022 due to the Landlord not being able to deliver vacant possession of the rental unit.

 

7.      A preliminary issue was raised at the outset of the hearing wherein the Tenants alleged that the address on the N12 notice of termination is incorrect and is therefore invalid. The Landlord states that there should not be a strict reading of the address as it is a rural address and are at times inconsistent with short forms being utilized and names being reversed. The Landlord also states that the Tenants themselves have used the address as set out on the N12 notice of termination.

 

8.      There was no dispute that the Tenants received mail correspondence and cheques by courier at the address as stipulated on the N12 notice of termination. Documentation provided to the Board relating to this matter reference various forms of an address for the rural rental property.

 

9.      Based on the evidence before the Board, it appears that this rural property may indeed have different but correct addresses. I am satisfied that the address as stipulated on the N12 notice of termination holds no confusion as to the address of the property and is not prejudicial to the Tenants.

 

10.   Section 48.1 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’) requires the Landlord to provide compensation to the Tenants in an amount equal to one month’s rent where an N12 notice is served. The Landlord testified that they provided the Tenants with a compensation cheque on September 22, 2021 prior to the termination date specified in the N12 notice of termination and that it was cashed on October 1 2021.

 

11.   Pursuant to section 72(1) of the Act, the Landlord provided two signed declarations from the purchasers dated September 28, 2021 and another set dated October 12, 2021 stating that they require the rental unit for their own personal residential use for a period of at least one year.

 

12.   The issue to be determined by the Board is whether the Landlord has satisfied the “good faith” requirement set out in subsection 48(1) of the Act which provides:

 

A landlord may, by notice, terminate a tenancy if the landlord in good faith requires possession of the rental unit for the purpose of residential occupation for a period of at least one year by,

 

(a)   the landlord;

….

 

13.   The onus is on the Landlord to establish that the Landlord in good faith requires the rental unit for the purpose of residential occupation.

 

14.   In Salter v. Beljinac, 2001 CanLii 40231 (ON SCDC), the Divisional Court determined that a Landlord satisfied the “good faith” requirement to terminate a tenancy where the person identified in the notice of termination genuinely intends to occupy the rental unit for residential purposes.

 

15.   The Tenants did not provide any evidence challenging the Landlord’s evidence respecting the purchasers’ requirement to use the rental unit for residential purposes.

 

16.   I find that, based on the evidence and submissions and on a balance of probabilities, that the purchasers genuinely intend to use the rental unit for their residence and that the purchasers in good faith, require possession of the rental unit for their residence.

 

17.   Pursuant to section 83 of the Act, I am required to consider all of the circumstances in the case including both parties’ situations to determine if it would be appropriate to grant relief.

 

18.   The Tenants testified that they have resided at the rental until since October, 2010. Their source of income is a disability pension, income from part-time employment and a settlement from a personal injury claim. The Tenants state that they have attempted to secure alternative housing but have not been successful to date. They testified that they purchased a trailer in the fall of 2021 but have nowhere to put the trailer for them to reside in. The Tenants are hoping to stay in the rental unit until at least the end of April, 2022.

 

19.   The Landlord’s position is that the Tenants received notice to vacate in July, 2021 and have had six months to find alternative housing. They submit that the Tenants have had plenty of time and have not taken sufficient steps to locate somewhere else to live. The Purchasers have had to extend their closing date for the sale of the rental unit and are themselves residing in a trailer. The Landlords seek an immediate eviction.

 

20.   I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act (the “Act”) and find that it would not be unfair to postpone the eviction until March 31, 2022 pursuant to subsection 83(1)(b) of the Act. Postponing

the Tenants’ eviction until March 31, 2022 will provide the Tenants with more time to secure another rental unit given their financial and medical status or secure a location for their trailer.

 

 

It is ordered that:

 

 

1.     The tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenants is terminated, as of September 30 2021. The Tenants must move out of the rental unit on or before March 31, 2022.

 

2.     If the unit is not vacated on or before March 31, 2022, then starting April 1, 2022 the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

 

3.     Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord on or after April 1, 2022. The Sheriff is requested to expedite the enforcement of this order.
