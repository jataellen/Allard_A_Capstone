Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: SWL-46810-20

 

 

In the matter of:

318, 263 ST. ANDREWS STREET CAMBRIDGE ON N1S4Z5

 

Between:

Cambridge Non-profit Housing Corp.

Landlord

 

 

and

 

 

John (a.k.a. Jack) Keith

Tenant

 

 

 

Cambridge Non-profit Housing Corp. (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict John (a.k.a. Jack) Keith (the 'Tenant') The Landlord also claimed compensation for each day the Tenant remained in the unit after the termination date.

 

This application was heard by video conference on November 1, 2021.

 

At 11:00 am, Cathy Shafe the Landlord, Peter Schroeder, the Landlord’s Representative and Laura Shewerl the Landlord’s Witness attended the hearing. The Tenants were neither present nor represented at the hearing.

 

Determinations and Reasons:

 

1.     The Tenant is in possession of the rental unit.

 

2.     The rental unit is a 1-bedroom apartment in a mulit-unit complex.

 

3.     The tenancy began on or about July 2017.

 

First and Second N5 Notices

 

4.     On August 11, 2020, the Landlord served the Tenant with a N5 Notice alleging that between April 2, 2020 and August 9,2020, the Tenant substantially interfered with another tenant’s or the Landlord’s reasonable enjoyment of the residential complex and/or lawful rights, privileges, or interests by repeatedly throwing peanuts, cigarette butts and dirty water on the patio below. There is also an allegation that the Tenant “rages” at the Tenants in the lower unit.

5.     Section 68(1) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’) states: A landlord may give a tenant notice of termination of the tenancy if,

 

 

 

(a)   a notice of termination was given to the tenant under section 62, 64 or 67, and

 

(b)   more than seven days but less than six months after the notice mentioned in clause

(a)   was given to the tenant, an activity takes place, conduct occurs or a situation arises that constitutes grounds for a notice of termination under section 60, 61, 62, 64 or 67, other than an activity, conduct or a situation that is described in subsection 61(1) and that involves an illegal act, trade, business or occupation described in clause 61(2) (a).

 

 

6.     Pursuant to section 68(1) of the Act, on October 28, 2020, the Tenant was served with a second N5 Notice. The Landlord’s application is based on the second N5 Notice. Accordingly, the Landlord need not prove the truthfulness of the allegations in the first N5 Notice, only that it meets the statutory requirements contained in section 64 of the Act. I am satisfied that it does. The allegations in this notice include the throwing peanuts off the balcony to the patio below, threatening notes to other Tenants and verbal abuse.

 

7.     I acknowledge it would be prejudicial to a tenant if the allegations in a first N5 Notice were without any foundation and made simply for the purpose of allowing a landlord to serve a second, non-voidable notice pursuant to section 68(1). I am satisfied that is not the case in the matter before me.

 

Testimony and Evidence

 

8.     The Landlord presented evidence supporting the allegations contained in the first N5 notice in the form of handwritten notes, emails and photos from other Tenants and office staff.

 

It is well-established law that the Board, as a tribunal governed by the Statutory Powers Procedure Act (“SPPA”), may accept and consider hearsay evidence at hearings. The traditional approach to accepting such evidence is to assign it the weight that it merits.

 

9.     As the evidence was uncontested, I find that the submission supports the allegations outlined in the first N5 Notice. I am, therefore, satisfied that there was a reasonable basis for the Landlord to serve the first N5 Notice on August 11, 2020. As a result, the Landlord was entitled to serve the second, non-voidable N5 Notice on October 28, 2020 in accordance with subsection 68(1)(a).

 

10.  In the second N5 Notice, the Landlord provides details in Appendix A regarding an incident that occurred on or about October 16, 2020.

 

11.  It was the testimony and evidence of the Landlord that on or abut October 16, 2020 the Tenant slid a “disturbing handwritten note” under the door of another tenant in the residential complex which states:

 

“JUST A NOTE KEEP YOU’RE NOSE OUT OF MY AFFAIRS SPOKEN WITH

HOUSING, you will be OUT OF HERE LIAR – Love Jack.”

 

Later that evening, the Landlord alleges that the Tenant knocked on the same tenant’s door in a threatening manner. The Tenant then returned to his rental unit and threw peanuts off the balcony onto the patio below which is the same patio belonging to the tenant as described above.

 

The Landlord said that Police were called and following that visit, the Tenant was verbally abusive to the tenant in the lower unit, yelling from his balcony.

 

The Landlord testified and provided evidence that a second handwritten note was slid under the Tenant’s door. The note contained expletive and offensive language.

 

12.  Laura Shewerl (LS) appeared as a witness for the Landlord. She testified that she lives at the residential complex two units below the Tenant. LS provided testimony on the issues regarding the Tenant repeatedly throwing peanuts to her patio below and also threw water on them while they were eating dinner. She stated that she received the 2 handwritten notes as detailed above that felt threatened by the Tenant. LS testified that the Tenant is often intoxicated and disruptive to be around.

 

13.  Based on the uncontested evidence before me, I am satisfied that the Tenant has substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment or lawful right, privilege or interest of the Landlord or another tenant. I am satisfied that the allegations that formed the basis of the application have been made out on a balance of probabilities.

 

14.  The evidence before me establishes that the Tenant frequently threw peanuts down onto the patio below in an effort to disrupt the neighbouring tenant. The Tenant has also acted verbally aggressive towards the tenant and others in the residential complex. The Tenant has been publicly intoxicated and disruptive. As a result of the Tenant’s conduct, police have attended the residential complex.

 

15.  The Landlord and witness testified that the Tenant’s conduct is disturbing, interfering with their quiet enjoyment of their homes, and makes them feel unsafe.

 

16.  The Landlord testified that several attempts at communication and resolution have been made with the Tenant including written correspondence, phone calls and in person. The Landlord also said she tried to set up a meeting between the parties with a mediator on or around spring of 2021. The Tenant did not participate.

 

17.  I asked the parties if a conditional order would be a possible consideration. The Landlord and Landlord’s Representative indicated that due to the efforts made and the lack of willingness for the Tenant to participate in the hearing, an order such as this would not be effective.

 

18.  The Landlord was not aware of any reason to delay or deny the eviction.

 

19.  I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would not be unfair to postpone the eviction until January 31, 2022 pursuant to subsection 83(1)(b) of the Act.

 

20.  Although no submissions were made at the hearing regarding the Tenant’s personal circumstances, based on the timing of this order, I have delayed eviction until January 31, 2022.

 

21.  This order contains all of the reasons for the decision within it. No further reasons shall be issued.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     The tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated, as of January 31, 2022. The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before January 31, 2022.

 

2.     The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $1,191.33, which represents compensation for the use of the unit from December 1, 2020 to January 31, 2022.

 

3.     The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $2.79 per day for compensation for the use of the unit from February 1, 2022 to the date the Tenant moves out of the unit.

 

4.     If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing on or before February 11, 2022, the Tenant will start to owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from February 12, 2022 at 2.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

 

5.     If the unit is not vacated on or before January 31, 2022, then starting February 1, 2022, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

 

6.     Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord on or after February 1, 2022.
