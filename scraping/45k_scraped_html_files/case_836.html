Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: CEL-93679-20 &

CEL-01924-21

 

 

 

 

In the matter of:

12 SILVERSTREAM ROAD BRAMPTON ON L6Z4L2

 

Between:

Roland Akwensivie

Landlord

 

 

and

 

 

Florence Mbah-segbaya

Tenant

 

 

 

Roland Akwensivie (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict Florence Mbah-segbaya (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes (the ‘L1 application - CEL-93679-20).

 

The Landlord also applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict the Tenant because the Tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or someone the Tenant permitted in the residential complex has wilfully or negligently caused undue damage to the premises.

 

The Landlord has also applied for an order requiring the Tenant to compensate the Landlord for the damage; because the Tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or someone the Tenant permitted in the residential complex has wilfully caused undue damage to the premises and used the rental unit or the residential complex in a manner that is inconsistent with use as a residential premises and that has caused or can be expected to cause significant damage.

 

The Landlord has also applied for an order requiring the Tenant to compensate the Landlord for the damage; and because the Tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or someone the Tenant permitted in the residential complex has substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment or lawful right, privilege or interest of the Landlord or another tenant. The Landlord also claimed compensation for each day the Tenant remained in the unit after the termination date (the ‘L2 application - CEL-01924-21).

 

These applications were heard by video hearing on October 1, 2021 and continued on November 30, 2021.

 

The Landlord, his Legal Representative John Lambe, the Tenant, and her Legal Representative Hamid Durrani attended the hearings. The parties were assisted by French-English Interpreter Manuel Costa.

 

Determinations:

 

Preliminary Issues:

 

1.      After some discussion with the legal representatives, it was determined that since the Tenant has already filed her own applications related to the rental unit, namely an application about tenant rights (a ‘T2 application’) and a tenant application about maintenance (a ‘T6 application’), the Tenant's Legal Representative agreed that any arguments related to section 82 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’) would be reserved for said hearing to avoid the duplication of process.

 

2.      I also confirmed with the Landlord's Legal Representative that the Landlord understood that the Act stated that maximum amount the Landlord Tenant Board (the ‘Board’) could award was $35,000.00. The Landlord's Legal Representative confirmed that this was understood.

 

L1 application - CEL-93679-20

 

3.      The Tenant has not paid the total rent the Tenant was required to pay for the period from January 1, 2020 to November 30, 2021. Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination effective January 31, 2020.

 

4.      The Landlord collected a rent deposit of $1,800.00 from the Tenant and this deposit is still being held by the Landlord.

 

5.      Interest on the rent deposit is owing to the Tenant for the period from February 7, 2020 to January 31, 2021.

 

6.      The Landlord testified about his issues with the Tenant and her failure to pay her rent. He provided testimony explaining the rental arrear calculations provided to the Board, which shared that the Tenant owed the Landlord $17,600.00 as of November 1, 2021. He submitted that the Tenant stopped paying her rent on time in January of 2020 and continued to accrue arrears from then on.

 

7.      The Tenant's Legal Representative submitted that the Tenant had paid her lawful, monthly rent until May 2021. However, he predicated this with explanation that there was confusion between the parties related to the Landlord’s undertaking to repair the rental unit’s leaking roof and to clean the rental unit’s garage and basement. The Tenant's Legal Representative submitted that since the Landlord did not address these issues until today, the Tenant withheld rent for June, July, August and September 2021.

 

8.      The Tenant testified that due to her personal circumstances and the current, on-going pandemic, she could not pay her rent on time. She didn’t pay her rent for January 2020 on time due to being outside of Canada at that time. She submitted that she received a letter in December 2019, asking her to move out of the rental unit.

 

9.      The Tenant did admit that she stopped paying rent in May of 2021 and owes arrears from then on, estimating she owes approximately $12,200.00 and not the $17,600.00 as alleged by the Landlord. She did not provide rental receipts to substantiate this.

 

10.   However, the Tenant intends to pay the Landlord the rent arrears with the assistance of a Board ordered payment plan.

 

11.   The Landlord refused to accept this solution, as he testified that she had previously engaged in a payment plan with him but refused to comply with it and has not made any payments since May 1, 2021. As such, he requested that the Tenant be ordered to pay all her arrears within the standard, eleven-day period ordered by the Board.

 

12.   After considering the submissions and evidence of the parties, based on a balance of probabilities, I find that the Tenant owes the Landlord $17,600.00 in rental arrears from failure to pay her full rent from January 2020 until November 1, 2021, despite making some payments on March 1, 2020, August 1, 2020, November 1, 2020, January 1, 2021,

February 1, 2021, March 1, 2021, and May 1, 2021.

 

13.   The Landlord was able to support his position with documentary evidence and testimony, while the Tenant only relied on her testimony. Had the Tenant made the payments she alleged, I would have expected her to substantiate this with proof of such, either via bank statements, etransfer receipts or other documents.

 

L2 application - CEL-01924-21

 

Law

 

13.   Section 62(1) of the Act states that a landlord may give a tenant notice of termination of the tenancy if the tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or a person the tenant permits into the residential complex wilfully or negligently causes undue damage to the rental unit or residential complex.

 

14.   Section 63(1)(a) of the Act states that a landlord may give a notice of termination of the tenancy that provides a termination date not earlier than the 10th day after the notice is given if the tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or a person whom the tenant permits in the residential complex wilfully causes undue damage to the rental unit or the residential complex.

 

15.   Section 64(1) of the Act states that a landlord may give a tenant notice of termination of the tenancy if the conduct of the tenant, another occupation of the rental unit or a personal allowed in the residential complex by the tenant is such that is substantially interferes with the reasonable enjoyment of the residential complex for all usual purposes by the landlord or another tenant, or substantially interferes with another lawful right, privilege or interest of the landlord or another tenant.

 

16.   Section 207(1) of the Act states that the Board may order payment to a party up to the limits of the jurisdiction of Small Claims Court, which is currently $35,000.00.

 

N5 Notice & N7 Notice for damage and substantial interference

 

17.   The Landlord served the Tenant with a Notice to End your Tenancy for Interfering with Others, Damage or Overcrowding (the first ‘N5 Notice’) on April 13, 2021. This notice alleged that the Tenant’s behavior, or that of someone visiting or living with her, has substantially interfered with another tenant or the Landlord. This notice gave the Tenant 7 days to correct the problem.

 

18.   The N5 Notice also alleged that the Tenant had wilfully or negligently damaged the rental unit and gave her 7 days to either repair the damaged property or pay the Landlord

$35,000.00 to address said damage. The Landlord confirmed that as of the day of the hearing, the Tenant had not fully repaired the damage or paid the request sum for repairs. Based on this, the Landlord's Legal Representative submitted that the Tenant did not void the N5 Notice.

 

19.   The N5 alleged that on June 2, 2020, the Landlord inspected the rental unit and found damage to its door frame, door and door casing. It continued by stating that on March 19, 2021, the rental unit’s toilet was overflowing due to clogging and that the Tenant did not notify the Landlord of such but continued using the clogged toilet. The Tenant waited until a week later, on March 26, 2021, to contact the Landlord, where she requested a plumber.

 

20.   When the plumber arrived at the rental unit, the Tenant informed the plumber that the toilet was unclogged. By not informing the Landlord of such, the Tenant obliged the Landlord to pay for the plumber’s services in the amount of $219.00.

 

21.   The N5 also alleges that on April 9, 2021, the Landlord’s contractor attended the rental unit and observed water damage to the rental unit and its garage. Said contractor provided a verbal estimate for the repairs in the amount of $35,000.00.

 

22.   The Landlord served the Tenant with a second N5 Notice on May 18, 2021, which alleged that the Tenant’s behavior again, was substantially interfering with another tenant or the Landlord. This time, the Tenant was given 7 days to rectify the behavior and was notified that since this was the Landlord’s second N5 related to behavior, the Landlord could apply to the Board to evict the Tenant.

 

23.   The second N5 alleged that the Tenant had moved her adult children into the rental unit as well as a family of four, without informing or asking permission from the Landlord, which the Landlord allegedly caused substantial interference to the Landlord.

 

24.   The Landlord also served the Tenant with a Notice to End your Tenancy for causing Serious Problems in the Rental Unit or Residential Complex (the ‘N7 Notice’) on May 18, 2021. The N7 notice alleges the events described in both N5 Notices, namely the alleged

$35,000.00 in damage to the rental unit, and the unauthorized Tenants.   The N7 also included information regarding the dispute related to the rental unit’s garage, which shall be addressed in the Tenant’s applications to the Board

 

25.   In terms of the damage to the rental unit, the Landlord testified that the contractor stated that due to the extent of the damage to the rental unit, the Tenant would be required to vacate the rental unit to repair/replace the ceilings, floors, walls, plumbing and electrical. The rental unit would also require repairs that would have to shut off the water and hydro to the rental unit, and that the Tenant and all her belongings would have to be out of the rental unit for repairs to commence

 

26.   This position was supported by the Landlord’s photos of the damage to the rental unit, which showed that the rental unit’s ceilings, flooring, and walls had experienced extensive water damage.

 

27.   The Landlord also provided the Board with a copy of a letter from his insurance company, Winmar, dated May 2021. This letter, addressed to the Landlord, informed him that in order for his insurance company to proceed with the required repairs on the rental unit, Winmar would require that the Tenant and all contents belonging to her be removed from the home. Winmar estimated that it would take approximately 6-8 weeks to make the required repairs, and that the rental unit would not be accessible by anyone except Winmar employees and the property owner.

 

28.   The Landlord was asked about whom would be paying for the repairs to the rental unit. The Landlord testified that his insurance company would be paying to repair the damages. He also submitted that he is responsible for paying the $1,000.00 deductible for Winmar to begin the repairs to the rental unit.

 

29.   The Landlord submitted that this was not the first time the Tenant had caused water damage to the rental unit by overflowing the toilet; as a few months earlier, the Tenant’s daughter blocked the toilet by accident when she flushed a sponge down the drain. As a result of this, the residential complex faced extensive flooding on the upper level, with the Tenant spending all night cleaning up the water.

 

30.   The Landlord reminded the Board that this also resulted in flooding to the lower levels of the residential complex, which substantially interfered with the Landlord’s and the other tenants reasonable enjoyment of the residential complex and his lawful rights, privileges and interests by causing significant damage to the residential complex.

 

31.   The Tenant did not deny that the rental unit flooded and experience severe damage as a result of the toilet overflowing on March 19, 2021. However, she took the position that the damage to the rental unit was not caused wilfully or negligently. She testified that on March 19, 2021 around 11:00PM, she heard her a “boom”; she found her adult, special- needs son had fallen next to the toilet. She also that the toilet was blocked and overflowing.

 

32.   Her son informed her that he used the toilet, without toilet paper but it overflowed. The Tenant called her older son and neighbour to address the overflowing toilet. The Tenant, her older son and neighbour stayed up all night and morning on March 19 and 20, 2021, trying to clean up the mess.

 

33.   The Tenant continued by stating that her neighbour suggested they call a plumber due to the possibility of water damage. As a result, the Tenant shut off the water. However, to her surprise, the Tenant said the toilet overflowed, while the Tenant was away from the rental unit. As a result, the Tenant called her own plumber.

 

34.   The Tenant stated that the plumber came to the rental unit, and suggested that the Tenant call the Landlord to have his own plumber, who is hopefully familiar with the residential complex’s plumbing system, assess the issue, since the Tenant’s plumber determine the issue to be systemic within the residential complex. When the Tenant allegedly called the Landlord, the Landlord sent her a list of plumbers, to which she replied that she had already done so. The Tenant stated that the Landlord brought a plumber in later on, but the Landlord allegedly told this plumber that the water in the residential unit’s plumbing system often overflowed and that he was aware of this issue. The Tenant said after the plumber visited, the issue was addressed, and she made repairs to the rental unit and continued to live there.

 

35.   The Tenant denied that the damage to the rental unit’s door was caused wilful or negligently by her and instead submitted that this issue with the door damage pre-dates her occupancy of the rental unit. The Tenant's Legal Representative reminded the Board that the Landlord had not provided photos of before the Tenant rented the rental unit, meaning that the Landlord had not met his burden of proof as the moving party to this application.

 

36.   The Tenant did not deny that she had her adult children and eventually, another family, residing in the rental unit. However, the Tenant's Legal Representative challenged the Landlord to identify how exactly this substantially interfered with his or another tenant’s rights.

 

37.   In terms of the substantial interference alleged by the Landlord in the N5s served on the Tenant related to the Tenant having unauthorized occupants or an unauthorized sublet of the rental unit, normally this kind of application would be dealt with via an Application about a Sublet or an Assignment (an ‘A2 Application’). In this case, the Landlord has chosen to proceed via N5, a voidable Notice. In this case, the N5 was not voided. The Landlord also served an N7 in relation to this.

 

38.   However, after considering the submissions and evidence, based on a balance of probabilities, I was not persuaded that the Tenant’s actions, namely having others move into the rental unit, substantially interfered with the Landlord or another tenant in the residential complex. The Landlord did not present substantial evidence in relation to this, namely what kind of impact this was having on the Landlord and/or other tenants in the residential complex. Without this information, the Landlord has not met his burden of proof as the applicant to this matter.

 

39.   In terms of the wilful or negligent damage to the rental, allegedly caused by the Tenant as described in the N5 and N7 notices, after considering the evidence and submissions of the parties, based on balance of probabilities, I was not persuaded that the damage caused by the Tenant was wilful or negligent, or wilfully causes undue damage to the

 

rental unit. Though it is not denied that the Tenant’s rental unit overflowed and caused extensive damage to both the rental unit and residential complex, the Landlord, who is the applicant in this matter, has not convinced me that the actions of the Tenant leads up to and after the toilet overflowing incidents of March 2021 constitutes wilful or negligent damage to the rental unit.

 

40.   Though it is unfortunate that the Tenant did not contact the Landlord immediately following the initial incident with the toilet on March 19, 2021, the Tenant offered what I would characterize as a reasonable explanation for her actions. I believed her testimony related to the water damage caused to the rental unit, and that this occurred by accident and not a result of wilful or negligent damage. Furthermore, the fact that the Tenant, using her own time and finances, tried to address the situation herself hired her own plumber motivated me to make such a finding.

 

41.   I was also not persuaded that the costs of repairing the rental unit’s door issues and the Landlord having to pay for the plumber to come to the rental unit despite the toilet’s clog being addressed qualified as wilfuly or negligently damaged in the sense that the Act intended. Though I believe that the rental unit’s door is damaged, the Landlord was not able to demonstrate on a balance of probabilities that this damage was caused by the Tenant’s wilful, negligent or wilfully caused undue damage.

 

42.   In terms of the Tenant substantially interfering with the Landlord and other tenants in the residential complex by causing substantial damage to the rental unit by overflowing the toilet on March 19, 2021, after considering the submissions and evidence of the parties, based on a balance of probabilities, I find that the Tenant did substantially interfere with the Landlord’s reasonable enjoyment of the residential complex for all usual purposes.

 

43.   I was persuaded that the Tenant’s actions, namely the fact that the Tenant’s occupant, her son, allowed the toilet to overflow while the Tenant was in the possession of the rental unit and failed to inform the Landlord substantially interfered with the Landlord’s rights, namely by causing significant damage to the residential complex and rental unit which requires the Tenant to vacate the rental unit.

 

44.   In this case, after hearing testimony from the Landlord and Tenant, viewing the extensive damage to the rental unit, and reading the correspondences from the Landlord’s insurer regarding the rental unit, I was persuaded that the rental unit will require ample repairs in order for it to be suitable for renting purposes as a result of the Tenant’s actions.

 

45.   Furthermore, the Tenant does not deny that the incident occurred, and was fully understanding that the Landlord may require her to vacate the rental unit as a result. Based on this, the Tenant’s tenancy is terminated.

 

Section 83 of the Act

 

14.   The Tenant submitted that the Board ought to consider the fact that her Landlord assaulted her and because of the issues from her T2 and T6 applications. Since those matters are already scheduled to the heard by the Board, I will put less weight on them.

 

15.   The Tenant submitted that she understood she was legally obligated to pay her rent.                                                                                                                                    As stated above, based on the significant arrears accrued, she wished to engage in a payment plan with her Landlord.

 

16.   In the event she was evicted, the Tenant submitted she was looking to leave the rental unit.

 

17.   She was invited to make submissions regarding who lived in the rental unit, where she revealed she lives there with her children, ages twenty-two, twenty and eighteen. She also submitted that one of her children has special needs. Since his school was next to the rental unit, she had concerns about moving.

 

18.   As stated above, the Landlord had serious concerns about the on-going financial prejudice caused by the Tenant occupying the rental unit and failing to pay rent. As a result of her actions, the Landlord submitted that he was obliged to pay the mortgage for the rental unit on credit as well as taken on a second job. He also submitted that as a result of having to take on a second job, he was unable to spend time with his family. Since the Tenant had previously failed to engage with a payment plan, the Landlord requested that the Board issue a standard eviction.

 

19.   I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would not be unfair to postpone the eviction until March 13, 2022 pursuant to subsection 83(1)(b) of the Act.

 

20.   I find that this delay takes into consideration the significant financial prejudice incurred by the Landlord over the course of 2021 while also taking into account the Tenant’s family situation, specifically involving her special needs son and his schooling. This delay will afford the Tenant time to find suitable alternative accommodations.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.      The tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated, as of March 13, 2022. The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before March 13, 2022.

2.      The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $23,710.16*, which represents compensation for the use of the unit from May 4, 2021 to March 13, 2022, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlord owes on the rent deposit.

3.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $175.00 for the cost of filing the L1 application.

 

4.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $201.00 for the cost of filing the L2 application.

 

 

5.      If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing* on or before April 13, 2022, the Tenant will start to owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from April 14, 2022 at 2.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

 

6.      If the unit is not vacated on or before March 13, 2022, then starting March 14, 2022, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

 

7.      Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord, on or after March 14, 2022.
