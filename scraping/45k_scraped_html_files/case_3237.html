Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TNL-27687-20

 

 

In the matter of:

40 CRAIGMORE CRESCENT NORTH YORK ON M2N2Y6

 

Between:

Bai Sun

Feng Qiang Sun

Landlords

 

 

and

 

 

Allen Bargman Bessie Mintz Ida Bargman

Tenants

 

 

Bai Sun and Feng Qiang Sun (the 'Landlords') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict Ida Bargman, Bessie Mintz and Allen Bargman (the 'Tenants') because the Tenants did not pay the rent that the Tenants owe. The Landlords also claimed administration charges related to NSF cheques.

 

This application was heard by videoconference on November 19, 2021 at 9:00am. The Landlords and the Tenants attended the hearing.

Determinations:

 

ADJOURNMENT REQUESTS

 

1.   At the hearing on February 8, 2021, the Tenants sought and adjournment on the basis that they did not see the attachment and did not receive the application. The Tenants testified that they had receipts needed time to gather them. The Tenants further indicated that they did not recall receiving the N4 notice of termination but later indicated that they may have gotten it.

 

2.   The Landlord testified that disclosure was sent to the Tenant in advance of the hearing; the Landlord believed this was a delay tactic on the Tenants’ part and opposed the Tenants’ adjournment request as the arrears stood over $25,000.00. The Landlord presented a text message from the Tenant where the Tenant confirms received of the N4, the notice of hearing and states in part “I have your rent and I am not going to transfer it to you until you have cancelled the meeting taking place on the 8th.”

3.   The Board’s records show the notice of hearing was mailed to the parties on January 21, 2021.

 

4.   Based on the evidence before the Board, I denied the Tenants’ adjournment request as I was not satisfied that the Tenant was unaware of the hearing date, did not receive the N4 notice of termination or the application. In fact, the text message presented directly contradicts the Tenants’ testimony which seriously undermines the Tenants’ credibility. I find that the Tenants were aware of the hearing date, the application and had sufficient opportunity to gather their evidence but they failed to do so. I proceeded to hear the parties evidence on the merits of the application.

 

5.   At the hearing on November 19, 2021, the Tenants, once again, sought an adjournment as they required time to gather their receipts in support of their assertion that they had a last month’s rent deposit.

 

6.   The Landlord opposed this request as the arrears were still significant high; further, the interim order indicated what the hearing was going to focus on and the Tenants had since February 2021 to gather this evidence.

 

7.   The Board’s records show the notice of hearing was served to the parties on October 22, 2021. Further, an interim order was issued by the Board on February 22, 2021 instructing the parties to be prepared to deal with the specific issue of the status of the last month's rent deposit.

 

8.   Based on the evidence before the Board, I denied the Tenants’ adjournment request as I found that the Tenants had sufficient opportunity to gather their evidence on this issue but they failed to do so. I proceeded to hear the parties submissions on the last month's rent deposit.

 

L1 APPLICATION

 

1.     The Tenants have not paid the total rent the Tenants were required to pay for the period from August 1, 2019 to November 30, 2021. Because of the arrears, the Landlords served a Notice of Termination effective August 19, 2020.

 

Landlord’s Evidence

 

2.     The Tenants are in possession of the rental unit.

 

3.     The lawful monthly rent is $2,800.00.

 

4.     The Tenants paid $33,000.00 after the application was filed.

 

5.     The Landlords incurred charges of $20.00 for administration charges related to cheques tendered by or on behalf of the Tenants, which were returned NSF.

 

6.     The Landlords are not holding a last month rent deposit and seek an amendment to the application to reflect this.

 

7.     The arrears and costs owing to November 30, 2021 total $29,706.00.

 

8.     The Landlord seeks a standard voidable order with expedited eviction as the quantum of arrears has caused a huge financial distress on the Landlord since she has been using her home line of credit and getting personal loans to ensure mortgage on the rental property is paid on time.

 

Tenants’ Evidence

 

9.     The Tenants dispute the arrears as claimed by the Landlord and testified the rent has been paid for the period June 2020 to December 2020. The Tenants do not recall whether the rent for January 2020 to May 2020 was paid. They agree they owe the Landlord money but not the amount being claimed by the Landlord. No further evidence was submitted in support of this assertion such as bank statements or receipts showing proof of payment.

 

10.  They testified that they were awaiting funds from a motor vehicle accident settlement that has been ongoing for 6 years and for which a hearing is scheduled to take place in March 2021 at which point they would have additional funds to put towards the arrears.

 

11.  The Tenants seek to preserve their tenancy and propose a repayment plan whereby they would pay the rent for February on February 9, 2021 and $5,600.00 on March 31, 2021. No further payments were proposed to pay the balance of the arrears.

 

12.  In the alternative, the Tenants seek a delay in eviction to April 30, 2022 as they will be moving to a retirement home. The Tenants are currently on fixed income and their motor vehicle accident case has not yet resolved, which is why they are in rent arrears.

 

13.  The Tenants also dispute that they moved into the rental unit in August 2019 and believe their tenancy began on August 1, 2017 which was a fixed term lease for two years with an option to extend for a third year. The Tenants did not have their original lease agreement at the hearing, so I permitted them to make a post hearing submission for the lease only on or before November 22, 2021. I note that the Tenants failed to file the lease agreement with the Board as permitted.

 

14.  On cross-examination, the Tenants confirmed that they did not have any proof of payment for rent for 2017. Further, the lease agreement submitted by the Landlord had their initials and their names were printed; however, the writing on the lease agreement was not theirs. The Tenants further confirmed that the payments reflected on the update sheet were correct; that the rent was always $2,800.00; and that they had no evidence that they would move out in April 2022.

 

15.  The Tenants further dispute that there is no last month's rent deposit. The Tenants testified that when they moved into the rental unit on August 1, 2017, they provided the Landlord with a first and last month’s rent via cheque. They had to borrow money from their brother in Florida to be able to do so.

 

16.  The second-named Tenant testified that she was not aware if the deposit had been paid because she was sick in the hospital at the time; she recalls they moved into the unit in the spring and that it was a few years ago.

 

17.  The third-named Tenant testified that she recalls moving into the unit 2-3 years ago but could not recall whether the rent was $1,800.00 or $1,700.00. She recalls giving the Landlord $1,800.00 in first and last month’s rent; she also recalls the lease agreement was signed by herself, her sister, her son and her granddaughter and that they got money from the bank to do so as well as her other son in Florida.

 

ANALYSIS

 

18.  With respect to the quantum of rent arrears, I find, the arrears and costs owing to November 30, 2021 to be aligned with the Landlord’s evidence of $29,706.00. I say this based on the Tenant’s own evidence that the rent for June 2020 – December 2020 was paid, which I find is reflected in the payments totalling $19,600.00. I also say this based on the Tenants’ evidence that the rent for January 2020 – May 2020 may not have been paid.

 

19.  I also say this based on the text message conversation presented by the Landlord at the hearing on February 8, 2021 wherein the Landlord states “according my calculation, you owing rent in the amount of $26,306” and the Tenants’ response to that was “No I wont be able to pay you the $26306 as of now, my accident court case is being heard in March.” At no point is the quantum of arrears contested by the Tenants, but for at the hearing.

 

20.  Finally, I say this as the Tenants failed to support their assertion of owing less than what was claimed by the Landlord by presenting bank statements, withdrawals, e-transfers, etc. of these payments whereas the Landlord submitted the full payment history since the beginning of this tenancy.

 

21.  Without positive evidence, it is very difficult to establish a negative claim. Thus, in order for the Tenants’ claim to be successful, they must provide some positive proof of the disputed payment, which, I find, they did not.

 

22.  At paragraph 26 of F. H. v. McDougall, 2008 SCC 53 (CanLII), the Court found that the civil standard of proof requires that evidence “must always be sufficiently clear, convincing and cogent to satisfy the balance of probabilities test.” Here I find the Tenants have failed to meet the required standard.

 

23.  With respect to the issue of when the tenancy began, as I did not receive a lease agreement from the Tenants by November 22, 2021 and no submissions from the Landlord in response to the Tenants’ submission by November 24, 2021, the lease agreement submitted by the Landlord shows the tenants as the three named on the application and Allen’s daughter – consistent with IB’s testimony. The term of the lease was on year between August 2019 and July 2020. The rent is listed as $2,800.00 and under the deposit section it stipulates that there was none being held. The pages of the lease were initialed by all three Tenants and the first-named Landlord. Furthermore, the lease appears to be signed by the Tenants on August 23, 2019.

 

24.  Based on the evidence before the Board, I find that the best evidence before me is that of the Landlord’s which indicated this lease began on August 1, 2019. This is consistent with

 

the period of rent arrears claimed on the N4 notice of termination and the payment history presented by the Landlord and the lease agreement.

 

25.  I also find that the Landlord is not holding a last month's rent deposit as clearly stipulated on the lease agreement and initialed and signed by the Tenants. I also say this as the Tenants failed to produce receipts showing payments towards the deposit.

 

26.  With respect to the Tenants’ request for relief, I do not find it fair under the circumstances to award the repayment plan as requested by the Tenant. I say this for the following reasons: first, it is unclear what caused the Tenants to be in arrears of rent – on the one hand the Tenants stated it was due to their motor vehicle settlement which has been pending for six years but this occurred prior to their move into the rental unit; and finally, the quantum of arrears outstanding as of the hearing date are significantly large. Absent any other evidence it appears that the Tenants are unwilling to pay the rent to the Landlord.

 

27.  Therefore, I cannot grant the Tenants’ request for a payment plan. I can however consider whether to postpone the eviction pursuant to subsection 83(1)(b) of the Act. I find the Tenants’ request to delay eviction to April 2022 to be excessive given the quantum of arrears outstanding that are beyond the Board’s jurisdiction as of the date of this order.

 

28.  Given the Tenant’s personal circumstances, I find that some delay in eviction is warranted; however, given that the hearing was a month ago, I find that a further delay to January 31, 2022 to be appropriate.

 

29.  Finally, with respect to the Landlord’s request for expedited eviction, Paragraph 74(3)(b) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act') states:

 

An order of the Board terminating a tenancy and evicting the tenant in an application under section 69 based on a notice of termination under section 59 shall,

 

…

 

(b) inform the tenant and the landlord that the order will become void if, before the order becomes enforceable, the tenant pays to the landlord or to the Board the amount required under subsection (4) and specify that amount;

 

30.  This means the Board is mandatorily required to provide the Tenant with another opportunity to avoid eviction by paying the Landlord the arrears of rent and costs outstanding.

 

31.  Orders are mailed to all parties. Pursuant to subsection 191(3) documents that are mailed are deemed to be received five days after mailing.

 

32.  So if the Board issues an order saying the Tenant has less than the standard eleven days the Board usually allows, the maximum time period the Tenant will have to void is less than the standard six days if the mail arrives on time.

 

33.  What this means is that the Landlord’s request for an expedited order runs a very high risk of the Tenant being effectively deprived of the right to void that the Board is mandatorily required to give under the Act. Although the Board certainly has the discretion to do as the Landlord requests it seems to me that doing so in the absence of very exceptional circumstances and expedited service on the Tenant, would be contrary to the spirit of the Act.

 

34.  As a result, while I am mindful of the Landlord’s concern with respect to the quantum of rent arrears, I am not prepared to exercise my discretion and shorten the voiding period from the standard eleven days to anything less as sought by the Landlord.

 

35.  I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (RTA), including the impact of COVID-19 on the parties and whether the Landlord attempted to negotiate a repayment agreement with the Tenant, and find that it would not be unfair to postpone the eviction until January 31, 2022 pursuant to subsection 83(1)(b) of the Act.

 

36.  This order contains all of the reasons for the decision within it. No further reasons shall be issued.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     Unless the Tenant voids the order as set out below, the tenancy between the Landlords and the Tenants is terminated. The Tenants must move out of the rental unit on or before January 31, 2022.

 

2.     The Tenants shall pay to the Landlords $33,322.49 (less any amount paid by the Tenants to the Landlord after the application was filed with the Board)*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to January 10, 2022 and the total charges related to a NSF cheque tendered to the Landlords by or on behalf of the Tenants, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlords owe on the rent deposit.

 

3.     The Tenants shall also pay to the Landlords $92.05 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting January 11, 2022 to the date the Tenants move out of the unit.

 

4.     The Tenants shall also pay to the Landlords $186.00 for the cost of filing the application.

 

5.     If the Tenants do not pay the Landlords the full amount owing* on or before January 31, 2022, the Tenants will start to owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from February 1, 2022 at 2.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

 

6.     If the unit is not vacated on or before January 31, 2022, then starting February 1, 2022, the Landlords may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

 

7.     Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlords, on or after February 1, 2022.

 

8.     Pursuant to subsection 207(1) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’) the Board may not order payment of an amount more than the monetary jurisdiction of the Small Claims Court, which is $35,000.00. I do not believe this applies to the amount to be paid if the Tenants wish to void the order, as this is an optional payment.

 

9.     As such, if, on or before January 31, 2022, the Tenants pay the amount of

$35,306.00** to the Landlords or to the Board in trust, this order for eviction will be void. This means that the tenancy would not be terminated, and the Tenants could remain in the unit. If this payment is not made in full and on time, the Landlords may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

 

10.  The Tenants may make a motion to the Board under subsection 74(11) of the Act to set aside this order if they pay the amount required under that subsection on or after January 22, 2022 but before the Sheriff gives vacant possession to the Landlords. The Tenants are only entitled to make this motion once during the period of the tenancy agreement with the Landlords.
