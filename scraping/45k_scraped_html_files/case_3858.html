Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TSL-18207-20

 

 

In the matter of:

3, 1549A EGLINTON AVENUE W TORONTO ON M6E2G9

 

Between:

Kianseng Enterprises Ltd

Landlord

 

 

and

 

 

Donna Hills

Tenant

 

 

 

Kianseng Enterprises Ltd (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict Donna Hills (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or someone the Tenant permitted in the residential complex has wilfully or negligently caused undue damage to the premises. The Landlord has also applied for an order requiring the Tenant to compensate the Landlord for the damage; because the Tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or someone the Tenant permitted in the residential complex has substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment or lawful right, privilege or interest of the Landlord or another tenant; and because the number of persons living in the unit on a continuing basis is more than permitted by health, safety or housing standards. The Landlord also claimed compensation for each day the Tenant remained in the unit after the termination date.

 

This application was heard by videoconference on October 12, 2021. Only the Landlord’s Agent, Kwok Ching Chan, attended the hearing. As of 11:35 a.m. the Tenant was not present or represented at the hearing although properly served with notice of this hearing as it appears from the Certificate of Service.

 

Determinations:

 

1.     The application is based on a voidable Notice of Termination (N5) served on the Tenant on September 22, 2020, with a termination date of October 15, 2020 because the Tenant blocked the common space with personal items, was aggressive and loud, and because the Tenant permitted too many people to reside in the residential unit, contrary to s. 64 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’). The Landlord also made a claim for damages to cover the cost of cleaning and disposal of refuse.

 

2.     The Landlord’s agent appeared at the hearing and tendered four letters of complaint from other tenants, as well as three photographs of the common area of the residential complex.

 

3.     The Landlord complained that the Tenant permitted too many people to reside in the unit contrary to the City of Toronto Property Standards By-Law 629-25 (c) which states, “The maximum number of persons living in a habitable room shall not exceed one person for each nine square metres of habitable room floor area.” (the Landlord referenced bylaw 639 in error).

 

4.     The Landlord testified the unit occupied by the Tenant is a one bedroom, approximately 380 sq feet (35.03 sq metres). It is undisputed that the Landlord believes nine to ten people are residing in the unit, based on his own observations and the complaints received from other tenants. The other occupants include the Tenant’s sister, two other adults and six children ranging in age from toddlers to teenagers. The Landlord attempted to bring this to the Tenant’s attention, however, she was dismissive and suggested that it was all family and she had a right to have everyone living with her.

 

5.     Based on the size of the unit as evidenced by the Landlord, the maximum occupancy of the unit is three people. The Tenant is permitting the overcrowding of the unit by permitting nine people to live there.

 

6.     The Landlord submitted five photographs of the common space of the residential complex, three of the roof which is accessible to the tenants, and two of the hallway outside the Tenant’s unit. The hallway photos show up to seven full garbage bags, a wheeled cart, a stroller and shoes, all partially blocking the hallway. The other photos show garbage, cases of empty beer bottles, toys, bicycles, laundry and plastic totes on the roof terrace. It is the undisputed evidence of the Landlord these items belong to the Tenant.

 

7.     The Landlord submitted four letters of complaint regarding the Tenant. The letters complain the Tenant’s personal items block the safe egress of other tenants may require in the event of an emergency, as well as representing a tripping hazard. When a neighbour attempted to address this with the Tenant, the Tenant and/or her sister told the complainant to move the stuff herself, and suggested they were hoping the neighbour “would have an accident” by tripping over the items. Another tenant complained about the narrowness of the hallways and the difficulty in passing while carrying groceries, laundry or recycling.

 

8.     Three letters of complaint included generalized references to loud parties, specifically music and talking, happening every weekend well into the early hours of the morning. The Notice of Termination references a noise complaint of September 5, 2020, however the only evidence of a specific noise complaint is found in one of the complaint letters dated September 22, 2020, about a loud party on September 12, 2020. At this time a party took place with loud music until approximately 2 am, making the “unbearable evening” for the complainant.

 

9.     The complaint letter of September 19, 2020 described a confrontation between the Tenant and the author. At this time, according to the letter, the complainant attempted to discuss the content of the hallways, expressing her concern the items represented a tripping hazard. The comments of the Tenant were rude, but, without context or further details I am not satisfied the confrontation was aggressive as described in the notice.

 

 

 

10.  The voiding period for the Notice of Termination was September 23, 2020 to September 29, 2020. It is the Landlord’s evidence that the Tenant has not voided the notice, that she continues to have at least nine people live in the unit, has not removed the personal items from the hallway, and continues to have parties on the weekends disturbing the other neighbours.

 

11.  I am not satisfied that the Landlord has provided sufficient evidence that the Tenant did not void the notice with respect to the noise complaints. He referenced a noise complaint of September 5, 2020, and the letter of complaint referred to a loud party on September 12, 2020. Both of these complaints occurred before the notice of termination was served on the Tenant and the only evidence tendered on the noise is not specific enough for me to determine that it continued during or after the voiding period.

 

12.  I am satisfied the Tenant has interfered with the lawful rights, privileges or interests of the Landlord as she continues to have more people residing in the unit than permitted by the City of Toronto Property Standards Code 629-25, and did not void the notice with respect to this allegation.

 

13.  I am also satisfied that the Tenant has interfered with the Landlord or another tenant’s reasonable enjoyment of the residential complex and their lawful rights, privileges or interests because the Tenant ’s personal items block the common space of the residential complex, which interferes with other tenants safe passage to and from their units, and that the Tenant has not voided the notice with respect to this allegation.

 

14.  The request for damages in the amount of $600.00 is denied. The Landlord is seeking

$600.00 to cover the costs to clean the common area and dispose of the items blocking the hallways and littering the roof.

 

15.  S. 89 of the Act says that a landlord may apply to the Board for reasonable costs for the repair or replacement of damaged property where the Tenant wilfully or negligently causes undue damage to the rental unit or the residential complex. Cleaning and garbage disposal are not damage. There is nothing to be repaired or replaced.

 

16.  I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would not be unfair to grant relief from eviction subject to the condition(s) set out in this order pursuant to subsection 83(1)(a) and 204(1) of the Act providing the Tenant reduce the number of people living in the unit to three, and remove all personal items from the common areas of the residential complex.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     The Landlord’s application for eviction is denied on the condition that, on or before January 15, 2022, the Tenant reduce the number of occupants of the rental unit to three people and remove all personal items from the common areas (hallways and roof top).

 

2.     The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $186.00 for the cost of filing the application.

 

3.     If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing on or before January 15, 2022, the Tenant will start to owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from January 16, 2022 at 2.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

 

4.    

 

 

King
 
If the Tenant does not comply with the conditions set out in paragraph 1 by the dates required, then the Landlord may apply under section 78 of the Act for an order terminating the tenancy and evicting the Tenant.
