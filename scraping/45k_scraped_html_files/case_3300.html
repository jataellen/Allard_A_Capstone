Order under Section 21.2 of the

Statutory Powers Procedure Act

and the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TSL-12939-20-RV

 

 

In the matter of:

25 MOSSOM ROAD TORONTO ON M6S1L9

 

Between:

Gregory Gaglione Norma Gaglione

Landlords

 

 

and

 

 

Refer to attached Schedule 2

Tenants

 

 

Review Order

 

Gregory Gaglione and Norma Gaglione (the 'Landlords') applied for an order permitting the rent charged to be increased by more than the guideline for one or more of the rental units in the residential complex because of municipal taxes and charges.

 

This application was resolved by order TSL-12939-20 issued on October 4, 2021.

 

On October 12, 2021, the Landlords requested that order TSL-12939-20 issued on October 4, 2021, be reviewed.

 

Determinations:

 

1.     Pursuant to Rule 7.1 this application was heard by way of a written hearing. The request for review is similarly being dealt with as a written hearing (pursuant to Rule 26.12). None of the Tenants participated in the original hearing of this application. Pursuant to s. 7(2) of the Statutory Powers Procedure Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. S.22, the Tenants are not entitled to any further notice of hearing for this proceeding. It is not necessary to invite further submissions or evidence on the review from the Landlords.

 

2.     For the reasons that follow the Landlords’ request for review is granted and order TSL- 12939-20 issued on October 4, 2021 is replaced with the order that follows.

 

3.     This application was denied essentially because the Landlords failed to properly complete the application form. More particularly:

 

•         At the time the application was filed one of the rental units in the residential complex was vacant.

 

•         On Schedule 1, Part D of the application the Landlords stated that the total rents for the units covered by the application is $7,352.00 (Column 2).

 

•         Under Column 3 the Landlords were asked to give an amount representing the “total rent charged for all rental units in the complex affected” by the increase in municipal taxes. In that column the Landlords put the same amount as in Column

C. That was actually a mistake on the part of the Landlords.

 

•         Page 7 of the Instructions booklet for L5 applications sets out detailed instructions as to how to complete Part D when one or more of the units in the residential complex is vacant or not subject to the application.

 

•         Essentially, what the Landlords should have done is to assign an average value for the rent for the vacant unit, and added that to the actual rent received to get an amount for the total rent charged for all units affected by the municipal tax increase. Although vacant, the sixth unit is nonetheless affected by the increase.

 

•         In this instance the math looks like this: [$7,352.00 (the total rent for the five covered units) ÷ 5] + $7,352.00 = $8,822.40. That is the amount that should have appeared in Column 3 of Part D of the Schedule to the application.

 

4.     On October 7, 2020 the Board sent the Landlords a standard deficiency letter. With respect to the specific problem with the application it states:

 

You have identified the same rent charged on Schedule 1, Part D, Column 2 and 3 however, you have not applied for an increase above the guideline for all of the units in the residential complex. Please clarify the rent charged in Columns 2 and 3. The rent identified in Column 3 should reflect the total rent for all of the units in the residential complex.

 

[Emphasis added.]

 

5.     The letter then went on to instruct the Landlords that If they chose to file a written submission or amendment in response to the noted deficiency, it had to be done by October 22, 2020. It also noted that the Landlords would be responsible for serving the Tenants with any amendments the Landlords wished to make to the application.

 

6.     On October 9, 2020 the Landlords replied to the deficiency letter explaining that the sixth unit was vacant at the time the application was filed and that is why the rent charged set out in Columns 2 and 3 is the same. Their letter then says “The property tax increase applies to all 6 units but there is to be an order for only units 1 to 5 inclusive”.

 

7.     The Board then proceeded with the written hearing and dismissed the application on the basis it was “deficient”. In explaining why it was doing so the Board stated:

 

3.      The Landlord is required to include rent for vacant units in Column 3 of Schedule 1, Part D, by applying the average rent for all the rental units in the

 

residential complex. This information is used to calculate the Above Guideline Increase.

 

4.      As the Landlords have failed to amend the rent claimed in Schedule 1, Part D, Column 3, for the residential complex, the Board is unable to calculate the appropriate Above Guideline Increase.

 

[Emphasis added.]

 

8.     With respect to the request for review, as a general rule the Board will only exercise its discretion to grant a review when it is satisfied the order contains a serious error, a serious error occurred in the proceeding or the requestor was not reasonably able to participate in the proceeding.

 

9.     The review is granted for the following reasons:

 

•         The deficiency letter was less than clear in terms of explaining to the Landlords what the problem was. For example, it failed to inform the Landlords what was meant by “total rents”, and failed to direct the Landlords to page 7 of the Instructions booklet for more information.

 

•         That lack of clarity is reflected in the Landlords’ common-sense response. The Landlords clearly did not understand they were being asked to amend the application so that Column 3 reflected the math the Board wanted the Landlords to do as explained on page 7 of the Instructions booklet.

 

•         The Landlords’ response to the deficiency letter should have made it clear to the Board that the Landlords did not understand what was being asked of them, but no further information was provided.

 

•         It is actually not true as stated in the order that the Board was unable to calculate the rent increase. The information the Landlords provided may not have been in the right place on the form, but it was sufficient to calculate the rent increase.

 

10.  Given all of the above, the review is granted. The previous order is cancelled and replaced with the following:

 

11.  The Landlords justified a rent increase above the guideline because of an extraordinary increase in the cost for municipal taxes and charges.

 

12.  The municipal taxes and charges claimed by the Landlords in the application have been accepted.

 

13.  I have accepted the Landlords’ correspondence of October 9, 2020 as a request to amend and granted the request.

 

14.  On October 2, 2020, the Helping Tenants and Small Businesses Act, 2020 received Royal Assent. As a result, the following provision was added to the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’):

 

Rent freeze period Definition

 

136.1 (1) In this section,

 

“rent freeze period” means the period that begins on January 1, 2021 and ends on December 31, 2021.

 

Non-application, certain rent increases

 

(2)   This section does not apply with respect to,

 

. . .

 

(c) an increase in rent for a rental unit permitted under subsection 126

(10)   or section 127 for,

 

(i)  an extraordinary increase in the cost for municipal taxes and charges as described in paragraph 1 of subsection 126 (1), if the increase in rent is permitted by an order of the Board that was issued before the day the Helping Tenants and Small Businesses Act, 2020 receives Royal Assent,

 

. . .

 

No rent increase during rent freeze period

 

(3)   No landlord shall increase the rent charged to a tenant during the rent freeze period, even if notice of the increase was given before the day the Helping Tenants and Small Businesses Act, 2020 receives Royal Assent.

 

[Emphasis added.]

 

15.  This means that effective October 2, 2020, the Board cannot issue an order increasing the rent due to an extraordinary increase in the cost for municipal taxes and charges as described in paragraph 1 of subsection 126(1) where the increase would take effect during the period January 1, 2021, to December 31, 2021. Therefore, that portion of the Landlords’ application seeking a rent increase that would take effect during the period January 1, 2021 to December 31, 2021, must be dismissed. An order can be issued for the remaining portion of the application as the rent increase will not take effect during the period January 1, 2021, to December 31, 2021.

 

16.  This order contains all of the reasons for the decision within it. No further reasons shall be issued.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     The Landlords’ request for review is granted. Order TSL-12939-20 issued on October 4, 2021 is cancelled and replaced with the following:

 

2.     The Landlords may increase the rents charged by 0.44% for the units set out in Schedule 1.

 

3.     The Landlords may increase the rents charged within the time period of April 1, 2020 to December 31, 2020.

 

4.     The portion of the Landlords’ application seeking an above-guideline rent increase due to an extraordinary increase in municipal taxes and charges to take effect during the period January 1, 2021 to March 31, 2021, is dismissed.

 

5.     The percentage increase set out in paragraph 2 may be taken in addition to the annual guideline in effect on the increase date for the unit.

 

6.     The Landlords or the Tenant shall pay to the other any sum of money that is owed as a result of this order within 60 days.
