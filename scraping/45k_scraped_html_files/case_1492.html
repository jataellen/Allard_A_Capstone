Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TEL-19188-21

 

 

In the matter of:

UPPER, 461 EDISON AVENUE S PETERBOROUGH ON K9J4G4

 

Between:

Kelly Mahoney

Landlord

 

 

and

 

 

Brittney Beaumont

Tenant

 

 

 

Kelly Mahoney (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict Brittney Beaumont (the 'Tenant') because the Landlord requires possession of the rental unit for the purpose of residential occupation.

 

This application was heard by video conference on January 11, 2022.

 

The Landlord, Kelly Mahoney, and the Landlord’s Legal Representative, Crystal Francey, attended the hearing. As of 11:30 a.m., the Tenant was not present nor represented at the hearing although properly served with notice of this hearing by the Board.

 

 

Determinations:

 

1.     For the reasons that follow, I find that the Landlord, in good faith, requires possession of the rental unit for the purpose of residential occupation. The tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant will be terminated as of February 13, 2022.

 

2.     The Landlord and Tenant entered into a tenancy agreement in September 2019 with a monthly rent of $1200.00. A deposit for last month’s rent was received on September 1, 2019.

 

3.     On August 31, 2021, the Landlord served the Tenant with an N12 Notice of Termination (‘N12 notice’). The N12 notice stated that the Landlord required the rental unit for residential occupation for her child. The N12 notice sought possession of the rental unit for October 31, 2021.

 

The Landlord’s “good faith” intention

 

4.     The N12 notice was served pursuant to section 48 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006, S.O. 2006, c.17 (the Act). The issue to be determined by the Board is whether the Landlords have satisfied the “good faith” requirement set out in subsection 48(1):

 

A landlord may, by notice, terminate a tenancy if the landlord in good faith requires possession of the rental unit for the purpose of residential occupation for a period of at least one year by,

 

(a)   the landlord;

 

5.     The onus is on the Landlord to establish that the Landlord, in good faith, has a genuine intention to occupy the premises. In Feeney v. Noble, 1994 CanLII 10538 (ON SC), the Court held that the test of good faith is genuine intention to occupy the premises and not the reasonableness of the Landlord’s proposal.

 

6.     In Salter v. Beljinac, 2001 CanLII 40231, the Divisional Court reaffirmed the decision in Feeney, noting that the Landlord’s motives are ‘largely irrelevant’. In Fava v. Harrison, 2014 ONSC 3352 (Div. Ct.) the Court held that while the motives of the landlord are, per Salter, “largely irrelevant”, the Board can consider the conduct and motives of the Landlord to draw inferences as to whether the Landlord desires, in good faith, to occupy the property.

 

7.     The Landlord submitted a Declaration signed by her daughter, Alana Mahoney, which stated that, in good faith, she requires the rental unit for her own personal use for a period of at least one year.

 

8.     The Landlord, in her uncontested testimony, stated that her daughter is currently attending Trent University in Peterborough and requires a place to reside while she continues her studies. She further testified that because the Tenant did not vacate the rental unit by October 31, 2022, her daughter has been staying with friends and “couch surfing”. This living arrangement has been very disruptive to her daughter’s university studies and has caused her undue stress.

 

9.     I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would be unfair to grant relief from eviction pursuant to subsection 83(1) of the Act. The Tenant did not attend the hearing, and thus I did not have the opportunity to hear any evidence or submission from them relevant to my consideration under s. 83.

Compensation

 

10.  Section 48.1 of the Act requires a landlord seeking a “personal use” eviction to provide compensation to their tenant in an amount equal to one month’s rent. Section 55.1 of the Act requires that the compensation be paid by the landlord’s proposed termination date.

 

11.  The Landlord testified that the Tenant is in arrears of rent and was served an N4 Notice of Termination on October 5, 2021. She further testified that it was clearly communicated to the Tenant that the rent arrears for October were waived and served as the required compensation. I accept the Landlord’s undisputed evidence on this point.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     The tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated, as of February 13, 2022. The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before February 13, 2022.

 

2.     The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $1,252.53, which represents compensation for the use of the unit from November 1, 2021 to February 2, 2022, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlord owes on the rent deposit.

 

3.     The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $39.45 per day for compensation for the use of the unit from February 3, 2022 to the date the Tenant moves out of the unit.

 

4.     If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing on or before February 13, 2022, the Tenant will start to owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from February 14, 2022 at 2.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

 

5.     If the unit is not vacated on or before February 13, 2022, then starting February 14, 2022, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

 

6.     Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord on or after February 14, 2022.
