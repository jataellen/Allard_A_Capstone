Order under Section 21.2 of the

Statutory Powers Procedure Act

and the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: SWL-46356-20-RV

 

 

In the matter of:

UPPER, 88 GLADSTONE AVENUE ST. THOMAS ON N5R2M1

 

Between:

John Kittelberger

Landlord

 

 

and

 

 

Lorri Havell

Tenant

 

 

 

Review Order

 

John Kittelberger (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict Lorri Havell (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or someone the Tenant permitted in the residential complex has wilfully caused undue damage to the premises; and because the Tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or a person the Tenant permitted in the residential complex has seriously impaired the safety of any person. The Landlord has also applied for an order requiring the Tenant to compensate the Landlord for the damage.

 

This application was resolved by order SWL-46356-20 issued on July 2, 2021. On July 13, 2021, the Tenant requested a review of the order.

The Tenant’s request to review was granted and the application ordered to be heard de novo by order SWL-46356-20-RV-IN1 issued on October 21, 2021.

 

This application was heard de novo via videoconference on January 10, 2022. The Landlord, the Landlord’s legal representative, S. Pereira, the Tenant, and the Tenant’s legal representative, N. Evans, attended the hearing. The Landlord’s witnesses, A. Tedall and J. Sawkins also attended the hearing.

 

Determinations:

 

1.     The Landlord served an N7 Notice of Termination (the ‘Notice’) on the Tenant on October 8, 2020. The termination date on the Notice was October 18, 2020.

 

2.     The Notice alleges that the Tenant pulled a tap from a pipe in the unit and failed to inform the Landlord, removed batteries from smoke detectors and CO2 detectors, flooded the

 

lower level tenants’ unit and the Tenant’s guests broke into the lower tenants’ unit and peered into their window.

 

3.     On a balance of probabilities, I find that the Tenant seriously impaired the safety of other tenants in the residential complex by the floods on November 2, 2019 and September 13, 2020.

 

4.     I find that the Tenant caused the flood on November 2, 2019 by pulling a tap off the wall and caused a flood in the unit below. A. Tedall, a downstairs tenant, testified that on this date she woke up to water pouring through her ceiling in the kitchen and spare room. J. Sawkins, the other downstairs tenant, testified that he awoke to the water pouring in like a fire hydrant. As soon as he saw the water, he informed the Landlord. The Tenant took no reasonable steps to inform the Landlord and/or prevent damage. A. Tedall testified being concerned about an electrical fire as the water was pouring through the light fixture. A photograph of the light fixture was submitted into evidence.

 

5.     I find that the Tenant caused the flood on September 13, 2020 based on the correspondence of Cam Stacey, a licensed plumber. The correspondence explores the cause of the flood. He states that “there was large evidence that water had been leaking from the unit upstairs.” He found that the water lines and drains for the kitchen above the water damage were both dry and had no signs of leaking that day or in the past. The plumber found some water on the kitchen countertop. He further states:

 

In my professional opinion I believe the water could have been left on or not shut off all the way and left dripping at the kitchen tap either pushed to the side so the spout of the tap was dripping onto the countertop, or the sink was plugged or left full of dishes with the drain plugged. Causing the sink to overflow and follow the countertop down behind the stove to the lower unit and into the basement.

 

6.     While the Tenant submitted that she did not leave her sink plugged, filled with dishes and she did not note any leaking on September 13, 2020, she testified that the area behind the stove is “always wet” and the Landlord was aware of this since she moved in. J. Sawkins testified that on September 13, 2020 he saw that the ceiling in the bedroom was collapsing and water was pouring in. He went to the Tenant’s unit to inform her to which she replied, “I know.” In my view, the Tenant was aware of a water leak but failed to report it to the Landlord. Submitted into evidence was photographs of the sunken ceiling.

 

7.     Both incidents seriously impaired safety as the evidence of the downstairs tenants and the Landlord was that they presented concerns of an electrical fire and collapsed ceiling in the lower tenants’ bedroom.

 

8.     While I find that the Tenant negligently caused the floods, the Tenant did not wilfully cause damage. There was insufficient evidence to suggest the Tenant intended to cause the flooding and the subsequent damage to the lower tenants. As such, the Landlord’s claim for wilful damage is dismissed.

 

9.     I do not find that the remaining issues in the N7 notice constitute serious impairments of safety. The Landlord did not lead sufficient evidence that the individual alleged to have

 

broken into the lower tenants’ unit and peered into their unit was invited by the Tenant. Further, I do not find that the Tenant removing batteries from smoke detectors and CO2 detectors seriously impaired safety as I am satisfied by her testimony that she only removed them in advance of the Landlord’s inspection, so it was more convenient for him.

 

DAMAGES

 

10.  The Landlord’s application sought compensation for damages in the amount of $7.146.65, representing repairs for the water damage. I am satisfied that the damage was negligently caused, as discussed above. The Landlord submitted into evidence a quote from Shawn Home Improvements for this amount. The Landlord submitted that he paid $1,000.00 for the deductible and insurance covered the remainder of the cost. The Landlord was therefore seeking $1,000.00.

 

11.  The Tenant submitted that the Landlord should not be entitled to any amount because he gave misleading evidence regarding the amount claimed in his application.

 

12.  I find that the Landlord is entitled to $1,000.00. I am satisfied that the cost of the deductible was incurred to pay for the damage to the residential complex caused by the Tenant. I am satisfied that at the time the Landlord filed this application, he based the amount on the quote from Shawn Home Improvements prior to his insurance claim and he did not intend to mislead.

 

SECTION 83 CONSIDERATIONS

 

13.  I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would not be unfair to postpone the eviction until February 28, 2022 pursuant to subsection 83(1)(b) of the Act.

 

14.  The Landlord submitted that the Tenant had broken a window and put cardboard in its place, changed the locks to the unit, removed the dryer vent, and scratched the kitchen countertops. J. Sawkins and A. Tedall testified that there is a new leak immediately above the stove after the Landlord just repaired the ceiling. Further, on November 4, 2021, he was awoken by excessive noise at 2:00 am. The fire department has attended approximately three times for the Tenant burning items in the backyard. J. Sawkins and A. Tedall testified to having their sleep disturbed by the Tenant and are worried that another flood will occur.

 

15.  The Tenant has resided in the unit for seven years and has a seven-year old child residing with her. The Tenant submitted that some of the damage was from reasonable wear and tear, she ceased burning fires after the fire department came, and she is not aware of any new leaks.

 

16.  The evidence suggests that that since the service of the N7 notice, the impact of the Tenant, her guest or her occupant’s conduct on the lower level tenants is such that the tenancy cannot continue. However, in consideration of the length of the tenancy and her

 

child, I find it appropriate to postpone termination. This delayed termination will provide the Tenant with some time to organize her move.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     Order SWL-46356-20 issued on July 2, 2021 is cancelled and replaced with the following:

 

2.     The tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated, as of February 28, 2022. The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before February 28, 2022.

 

3.     The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $1,000.00, which represents the reasonable costs of repairing the damage.

 

4.     If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing on or before February 28, 2022, the Tenant will start to owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from March 1, 2022 at 2.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

 

5.     If the unit is not vacated on or before February 28, 2022, then starting March 1, 2022, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

 

6.     Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord on or after March 1, 2022. The Sheriff is requested to expedite the enforcement of this order.
