Order under Section 21.2 of the

Statutory Powers Procedure Act

and the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: SOL-19625-21-RV

 

 

In the matter of:

1215, 1260 MARLBOROUGH COURT OAKVILLE ON L6H3H5

 

Between:

1260 Marlborough Court Holdings Limited

Landlord

 

 

and

 

 

Richard Dimock

Tenant

 

 

Review Order

 

1260 Marlborough Court Holdings Limited (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict Richard Dimock (the 'Tenant') because he, another occupant of the rental unit or someone he permitted in the residential complex has wilfully caused undue damage to the premises; because he, another occupant of the rental unit or someone he permitted in the residential complex has substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment or lawful right, privilege or interest of the Landlord or another tenant; and because he, another occupant of the rental unit or a person the Tenant permitted in the residential complex has seriously impaired the safety of any person. The Landlord also claimed compensation for each day the Tenant remained in the unit after the termination date.

 

This application was resolved by order SOL-19625-21 issued on August 24, 2021. On September 23, 2021, the Landlord requested a review of the order.

On September 28, 2021 interim order SOL-19625-21-RV-IN was issued.

 

The request was heard in Passcode: 927 9580 4323# on December 17, 2021.

 

The Landlord’s Representative, Sam Ursino and the Tenant attended the hearing. The Landlord’s Agent Kim Harrop attended the hearing along with Richard Dimock, the Tenant’s father. Matilda Noni attended as witness for the Landlord.

 

My oral decision rendered at the review hearing is replaced in part by my final decision as allowed under the Statutory Powers Procedure Act (see paragraph 9).

 

Determinations:

 

Review:

 

 

 

1.     The Member dismissed the N7 Notice of Termination (non-voidable) because it was served within the seven day voiding period of the N5 Notice of Termination given under section 64 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act') which allows the Tenant to void the N5 Notice by correcting the problem. The Landlord argued the Member errored because the Tenant didn’t raise the argument and there was no evidence on the record that the Tenant led about confusion caused.

 

2.     On the basis of the submissions made in the request, I am satisfied that there is a serious error in the order or that a serious error occurred in the proceedings

 

3.     A Member can ask a Tenant during the hearing if they were confused by the multiple

notices if the Tenant doesn’t raise this issue on their own. If a Member is satisfied having heard from the tenant that the landlord caused confusion by giving multiple notices, then having dismissed the application would be considered a reasonable and acceptable outcome.

4.     In this case, the Tenant did not challenge the submissions made by the Landlord and made no argument on point or suggest that he did not understand the case required to be met. I listened to the recording on July 28, 2021 and there was no evidence from the Tenant that the multiple notices caused confusion which led the member to dismiss the application. The recording confirmed that only the Landlord was asked whether the multiple notices caused the Tenant confusion.     At no time was the question posed to the Tenant during the hearing and when the Tenant attempted to talk on point, the Tenant was interrupted and prevented from doing so.

5.     The Tenant did not challenge the validity of the Notices or claim that he didn’t know the case to be met at the review hearing. I find there was a serious error in the proceeding because that evidence could have changed the outcome of the decision. The Landlord’s request to review order SOL-19625-21-RV is granted and the application is heard de novo.

 

Determinations:

 

6.     The Tenant did not challenge the Landlord’s evidence that he wilfully or negligently caused undue damage to the rental unit as supported by photographs. The Tenant removed the slider doors of the bathroom and entrance closet, broke the glass of the stove oven door, and cut a hole in the bathroom drywall. The Tenant also installed light fixtures and left wires hanging. The Tenant did not pay the amount required to void the N5 Notice of Termination.

 

7.     The Landlord referred to other property damage, related to medicine cabinet, lock on storage room, paint and floors which were not properly before me and was not considered.

 

8.     There’s no dispute the Tenant’s father paid the costs for the entrance door that was damaged when police forced entry because the Tenant barricaded himself inside.

 

9.     The rental unit was in a poor state of cleanliness and the unit was inadequately prepared to be treated for bedbugs. The witness referred to dates when the Tenant failed to prepare for bedbug treatment or refused entry on September 22, 2020, October 13, 2020 and October 27, 2020, February 2, 2021, and February 16, 2021. The Landlord did not present evidence to establish the Tenant’s actions continued during the seven-day voiding period from January 12, 2021 to January 18, 2021. This portion of the N5 Notice is void as such my oral ruling made at the hearing is replaced. The Tenant is cautioned to be mindful of maintaining reasonable cleanliness in the rental unit and cautioned against interfering or refusing the Landlord entry to eradicate bedbugs.

 

10.  The Landlord received complaints from other tenant the Tenant was not wearing a mask in the common areas. The Landlord requires all tenants to wear face masks in indoor public spaces in response to Covid-19 as is mandatory in Ontario. The Tenant provided no medical evidence for not wearing a mask and his behaviour has the potential to seriously impair the safety of another person in the complex.

 

11.  I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would not be unfair to grant relief from eviction subject to the condition(s) set out in this order pursuant to subsection 83(1)(a) and 204(1) of the Act.

 

12.  It’s appropriate to allow the Tenant an opportunity to preserve his tenancy as a means of accommodation given, he was hospitalized for 21 days because of mental health impairments on the December 29, 2020 incident. The Landlord knew or ought to have known that the Tenant was not well, and his impairments was connected to the Tenant’s behaviour. The Landlord has a duty to accommodate to the point of undue hardship. Although the Landlord claims there was no medical evidence disclosed, the Landlord’s staff was informed through the Tenant’s father.

 

13.  There’s no dispute the Tenant’s father paid for the repair of the entrance door that was broken on December 29, 2020 and damage related to flood that occurred in 2020 when pipes froze. The Tenant’s father who attend the hearing commits to assist the Tenant to repair the damage in the rental unit. The Tenant understand and agrees to wear his mask in the common area of the residential complex until mandatory mask rule is lifted by Ontario Government.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     The request to review order SOL-19625-21 issued on August 24, 2021 is granted. The order is cancelled and replaced as follows:

 

2.     Until the mandatory mask rule is lifted by the Ontario government, the Tenant shall stop posing a serious risk to another person’s safety by wearing a face mask in the common areas of the residential complex at all times.

 

3.     On or before March 31, 2022, the Tenant shall restore/repair/and or replace the following property damage:

 

a)      The door of the bathroom;

 

b)     the sliders doors in the bedroom sliders;

 

c)      the glass oven door of the stove;

 

d)     the hole in drywall of the bathroom wall; and

 

e)     the Landlord’s light fixtures.

 

4.     If the Tenant fails to comply with paragraph 1 of this order the Landlord may, without notice to the Tenant apply to the Board pursuant to section 78 of the Act for an order terminating the tenancy and evicting the Tenant. The Landlord must apply within 30 days from the date of the breach.
