Order under Section 68

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: SOL-21457-21

 

 

In the matter of:

1, 519 MARY STREET HAMILTON ON L8L4X6

 

Between:

Joanne Sanson

8914273 Canada Limited

Landlords

 

 

and

 

 

Pauline Pullyblank

Tenant

 

 

Joanne Sanson and 8914273 Canada Limited (the 'Landlords') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict Pauline Pullyblank (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or someone the Tenant permitted in the residential complex has substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment or lawful right, privilege or interest of the Landlords or another tenant.

 

This application was heard by videoconference on September 29, 2021.

 

The Landlord Ms. Sanson, the Landlord’s legal representative Steven Smith and the Tenant attended the hearing.

 

Reasons:

 

Background

 

1.     The Landlord’s application relies on a second non-voidable N5 Notice of Termination with a termination date of April 15, 2021 given to the Tenant on March 18, 2021 under subsections 64(1) and 68(1) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’), which provide:

 

64 (1) A landlord may give a tenant notice of termination of the tenancy if the conduct of the tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or a person permitted in the residential complex by the tenant is such that it substantially interferes with the reasonable enjoyment of the residential complex for all usual purposes by the landlord or another tenant or substantially interferes with another lawful right, privilege or interest of the landlord or another tenant.

 

68 (1) A landlord may give a tenant notice of termination of the tenancy if,

 

(a)     a notice of termination was given to the tenant under section 62, 64 or 67; and

 

(b)     more than seven days but less than six months after the notice mentioned in clause (a) was given to the tenant, an activity takes place, conduct occurs or a situation arises that constitutes grounds for a notice of termination under section 60, 61, 62, 64 or 67, other than an activity, conduct or a situation that is described in subsection 61 (1) and that involves an illegal act, trade, business or occupation described in clause 61 (2) (a).

 

2.     Schedule A to the first voidable N5 details allegations of cigarette smoking in the rental unit, disturbing other tenants of the residential complex, between January 4, 2021 and February 18, 2021. The second N5 asserts that another tenant of the residential complex reported that their unit was very smoky and smelled of cigarette smoke on March 17, 2021 at 5:53 p.m.

 

Evidence

 

3.     The Tenant testified that she has lived in the rental unit for 10-years, that there were never any problems before the current Landlords took over the residential complex and that she rented a smoking unit to avoid this issue.

 

4.     The Tenant did not dispute that there was “smoking” in the rental unit on all of the days specified in the Landlords’ notices of termination or that the smoke can travel through the air vents to neighbouring units. Rather, the Tenant testified that “smoking” is another term for “smudging” and that all of the instances reported on the Landlord’s N5 notices of termination were ceremonies conducted by her daughter, who is a member of the Old Crow First Nations community in the Yukon.

 

5.     The Tenant testified that she informed the Landlord of this practice, which the Landlord Ms. Sanson denied.

 

6.     The Tenant denied on cross-examination that there is any cigarette smoking in the rental unit and that all smoking takes place outside of the residential complex. Ms. Sanson confirmed in direct examination that the Tenant has told her that her daughter smokes for non-ceremonial purposes outside.

 

Analysis

 

7.     The Tenant’s position is that the Landlord is required to accommodate the cultural practice of “smoking” or smudging. On this point, the Tenant cites the Ontario Human Rights Commission’s Policy on Preventing Discrimination Based on Creed, especially Part II, 11, which addresses indigenous spiritual practices, including the following:

 

The smudging ceremony is a common purification rite performed in Ontario that

 

involves burning one or more sacred medicines, such as sweetgrass, sage, cedar and tobacco. There are many variations on how a smudge is done. The OHRC

 

heard that people often faced barriers when seeking to smudge. This was often due to a lack of clear protocols and inclusive design measures to facilitate the practice in a timely and appropriate way.

 

Smudging may sometimes be required at unpredictable times – for example, at a time of death in a hospital, or a moment of crisis during the school or work day.

Where smudging accommodation needs are known to potentially exist, organizations should take proactive steps to facilitate the practice in a dignified and timely way.

 

Failure to accommodate a person’s Indigenous spiritual belief or practice in a timely and appropriate way may be discriminatory under the Code.

 

The Tenant also argued that there are no harmful effects from the natural tobacco and that the Landlords’ concerns about adverse health effects are incorrect.

 

8.     The Landlord’s position was that the Tenant did not deny the conduct and that the Tenant’s evidence about the use of ceremonial tobacco should not be believed.

 

9.     I disagree with the Landlord that the Tenant admitted to the conduct specified in the Landlord’s N5 notices. The Tenant did not admit to smoking cigarettes and she in fact denied that there was any cigarette smoking in the rental unit during the relevant period. What the Tenant did admit to was that her daughter only smokes outside and that her daughter engaged in the burning of tobacco for ceremonial purposes for the purpose of addressing negative feelings, including from the neighbours. In these circumstances, the key questions for me are whether I accept the Tenant’s evidence and, if I do, the impact of the Human Rights Code.

 

10.  These reasons explain why I reject the Tenant’s testimony to the effect that the source of the smoke on the dates particularized on the Landlord’s notices of termination was the ceremonial use of tobacco.

 

11.  First, the Tenant raised the issue of the ceremonial use of tobacco just prior to the hearing. This was an entirely different from the Tenant’s previous submissions and the evidence attached to those submissions.

 

12.  The Tenant’s July 21, 2021 submission reproduces a copy of a July 16, 2019 blog-post entitled “Cannabis Smoke Bothering Other Tenants”. This blog-post provides legal information to landlords about how to address the concerns of tenants in their buildings who are complaining about cannabis smoke, the smell of smoke infiltrating other units, smoke drifting through open windows and smoke coming through shared ventilation systems. This submission also reproduces the following text from the Board’s frequently asked questions page, in part: “Your neighbours can smoke in their private homes, unless they signed a lease or there are condominium bylaws that say they can't. However, if second-hand smoke (tobacco or marijuana) from another unit is interfering with the reasonable enjoyment of your home, speak to your landlord.” This submission also attaches information from the Landlord’s Self-Help Centre:

 

The first thing you should try is writing a letter to the tenants advising them that the smoke is bothering you and request that they smoke outside. If they do not comply you could then serve them with the legal notice (Form N5) that states that they are interfering with your reasonable enjoyment of the premises. When they are served with this notice they have seven days to correct the problem and if not corrected then you can file an application with the Landlord and Tenant Board to terminate the tenancy. It is uncertain that you would be successful in this case because the tenants could argue that their tenancy agreement did not include a non-smoking clause.

 

13.  Apart from the Tenant’s submissions just prior to the hearing, the bulk of the Tenant’s evidence, including the passages quoted above, does not refer in any way to the cultural practice of smudging. It strikes me as odd that the Tenant would provide information about her right to smoke non-ceremonial tobacco in the rental unit if the ceremonial use of the tobacco was the true source of the smoke other tenants were complaining about.

 

14.  Second, although the Tenant testified that she informed the Landlord that the smoke emanating from the unit was tied to the cultural practice of smoking or smudging, the Tenant did not produce any corroborating evidence to this effect. However, the Landlord did introduce a January 14, 2021 letter from the Tenant to the Landlords. In that letter, the Tenant asserts that she did not sign a tenancy agreement that prohibits smoking and asserts that “myself + friends or relatives are also free to smoke in this unit”. The Tenant’s testimony to the effect that the word “smoke” should be read as “smudge” is inconsistent with the Tenant’s testimony to the effect that only her daughter engages in this cultural practice. In other words, in the absence of evidence to the effect that friends and other relatives also engage in this practice, it is clear to me that the Tenant responded to the Landlord’s claim about smoking by asserting a general right to permit non-ceremonial tobacco use in the rental unit.

 

15.  In these circumstances, I do not have trouble concluding that the Tenant behaved in accordance with her stated intention to permit others to use non-ceremonial tobacco in the rental unit. As it was uncontested that this smoke was disturbing other tenants of the residential complex, my finding is that the Landlord has proven this ground of termination on the balance of probabilities.

 

Relief from Eviction

 

16.  Subsection 83(2) of the Act requires me to consider the circumstances of both parties and to consider whether or not to refuse eviction or order that the enforcement of the eviction order be postponed for a period of time.

 

17.  I accept that both parties are in a difficult position.

 

18.  The Tenant’s uncontested testimony, which I accept, is that she has been a tenant of the residential complex for 10-years, that she rented a unit where smoking was permitted at the outset of her tenancy, that she has a fixed income and that she is willing to explore options to reduce the impact of smoke on other tenants of the residential complex, such as by opening the windows and using an air purifier.

 

19.  As the Tenant has been permitted to smoke in the rental unit since the outset of her tenancy, the Landlord cannot unilaterally remove this right, for example by promulgating a no smoking policy. As a result, the Landlord’s first N5 notice of termination, which demands that all smoking inside the Tenant’s unit stop “at any time by anyone”, may go too far.

 

20.  That being said, the Tenant’s exercise of her right to smoke places the Tenant at risk of eviction if smoking substantially interferes with the reasonable enjoyment of the residential complex for all usual purposes by the landlord or another tenant or substantially interferes with another lawful right, privilege or interest of the landlord or another tenant: North Avenue Road Corporation v Travares, 2015 ONSC 6986 (CanLII); TEL-74017-16-RV (Re), 2016 CanLII 100370 (ON LTB).

 

21.  The Landlord is in a difficult position because the Landlord has a responsibility to provide the other residents of the residential complex with quiet enjoyment of their rental units and the residential complex. A failure by the Landlord to address the issue on application could result in legal jeopardy for the Landlord.

 

22.  In balancing the interests of both parties, it is in my view relevant to note that the landlord in Traveres, above, took active steps to address the issue of smoking emanating from the unit in question before seeking eviction. As the Court noted, the landlord blocked the air vents, sealed the baseboards, installed larger fans on the roof, sealed the thresholds under the doors and checked the plumbing and closet areas. The tenant in that case had purchased air purifiers, placed a fan in his window and smoked on the balcony.

 

23.  In the present case, the Landlord is aware that smoke is travelling through shared doorways and through heating and air vents because this is what other tenants are complaining about. However, unlike the situation in Traveres, the Landlord did not lead any evidence of any active steps taken by the Landlord to find a solution that would balance the Tenant’s right to smoke in the rental unit with the rights of other tenants of the residential complex. In these circumstances, including where the Tenant testified that she is willing to explore options to address the impact of smoke on other tenants of the residential complex, my view is that it would be premature to terminate the tenancy or provide conditional relief from eviction until the parties have had an opportunity to explore potential solutions.

 

24.  For these reasons, my conclusion is that it would not be unfair to refuse eviction under paragraph 83(1)(a) of the Act.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     The Landlord’s application is dismissed.
