Order under Section 30

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TST-17370-20

 

 

In the matter of:

403, 3725 DUNDAS STREET WEST TORONTO ON M6S2T5

 

Between:

Anastasia Davis

Tenant

 

 

and

 

 

Toronto Community Housing Corp.

Landlord

 

 

 

 

T6

Anastasia Davis (the 'Tenant') applied for an order determining that Toronto Community Housing Corp. (TCHC) (the 'Landlord') failed to meet the Landlord's maintenance obligations under the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act') or failed to comply with health, safety, housing or maintenance standards.

 

T2

Anastasia Davis (the 'Tenant') applied for an order determining that Toronto Community Housing Corp. (the 'Landlord') substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or residential complex by the Tenant or by a member of the Tenant's household.

 

 

This application was heard via videoconference on November 23, 2021.

 

The Tenant and the Landlord’s Legal Representative Kamran Baradargohari attended the hearing. The Tenant spoke with Duty Counsel prior to the hearing.

 

At the outset of considering the T6 application the parties consented to the amended application filed November 18, 2021, as these amendments addressed remedies.

 

Determinations:

 

 

1.     The Tenant filed a T6 application on August 19, 2020 about maintenance, alleging the Landlord failed to adequately treat the rental unit for bedbugs.

 

2.     The Tenant vacated the rental unit on December 31, 2020, although it was uncontested that rent was paid till end March 2021.

 

3.     The Tenant testified that the bedbugs were first discovered on May 2, 2020, and that the Landlord was immediately contacted.

 

4.     It was uncontested that there was bedbug treatment in May 2020, and in July 2020, as well as treatment for cockroaches in October 2020.

 

5.     The Tenant testified that notwithstanding these treatments she was still suffering from bedbug bites into the fall. This forced her to vacate and move into a homeless shelter until she could find another place to live.

 

6.     The Tenant testified that as a result of the bedbugs she was forced to throw out her bedframe, sofa, pillows, bedding and other items. She had also tried washing many items that were destroyed in the wash or still had to be thrown out. She also had a biopsy of a bite completed in October 2020, and was prescribed a cream to treat for bites.

 

7.     The Tenant testified that the ineffective treatment interfered with her reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit. She indicated that she did not think that the superintendent was taking her concerns seriously, as she was still being bitten.

 

8.     The Landlord’s witness Thiago Dasilva testified. He is the senior superintendent technician since May 2018. He reviewed the Landlord documents, in which there were portions redacted to protect the privacy of others. The unredacted portions show that other units in the block were also being treated for bedbugs. The reports from the post treatment bedbug inspection not only in this rental unit, but those in the block were presented. These showed no live evidence of live bedbugs. The witness confirmed that the Tenant always kept a very clean apartment.

 

9.     The witness Dasilva testified that in his experience bedbugs will follow heat, and in a cluttered apartment they can infest. He confirmed that in a clean apartment that it is possible that there is no where to hide and thus it is easier to find an infestation. He could not confirm nor deny that bedbugs would hide in baseboards and cracks beyond the comment that they follow heat.

 

10.  The witness confirmed that when treatments were undertaken that TCHC staff were not present when this work was done. Further that the witness only escorted personal who were inspecting for bedbugs but that he himself did not conduct the inspections, as not a specialist in that regard.

 

11.  The Landlord witness Simon Burnell testified. He is employed by TCHC as part of the environmental health team since March 2020. His responsibilities include providing pest control advice when escalated by staff or tenants that are not resolved by treatments. He confirmed that previously was employed by Orkin Pest Controls where he was a licensed technician since 2010 where he had received his credentials for pest treatment.

 

12.  Witness Burnell confirmed that he did find a “nymph” in the rental unit, which is a baby bedbug and that this suggests that there are other bedbugs in the unit. However, he could not recall when this was, but suggested it might have been on one of the follow-up inspections. He did confirm that on the block inspection he did find bedbugs that led to further treatments.

 

13.  To the best of witness Burnell memory he believed that the block inspection in summer 2020, did lead to discovery of bedbugs in block units. As a result, he did ensure another round of treatment.

 

14.  The witness testified about having recommended other work be done in the rental unit, however he did not follow up to see if it was completed as he did not think that this was a cause of the bedbug infestation. He could not say for sure that bedbugs could come not from baseboards, cracks or a gap in the ceiling, but indicated it did not make sense for them to come from a fixture in the ceiling.

 

15.  The Landlords witnesses confirmed that the Tenant had not been advised to dispose of any of her possessions as a result of the bedbugs.

 

16.  I have considered the testimony and the evidence and I am persuaded that there were bedbugs in the rental unit, and likely in the block and that although there had been treatments the evidence did not show that it was wholly effective. The Landlord’s witness did acknowledge that there are other treatment regimes, such as extreme heat, but that these are cost prohibitive for the Landlord. It was uncontested that the Tenant continued to show signs of bedbug bites, even if the Landlord could not find evidence of “live activity”. Therefore, I find that the Landlord failed to meet the Landlord’s obligations under subsection 20(1) of the Act.

 

17.  The Tenant took steps on her own, and likely on advice from her health care advisers to dispose of items, and worked diligently to comply with the Landlord’s requirement to prepare her unit for treatment, and to keep it in a very clean that state, such that I am satisfied that the failure to adequately address the bedbugs also substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit..

 

18.  The Tenant provided letters to support her mental health claims, and how the presence of bedbugs has negatively affected her mental health. The Tenant testified about having to take a year off school as a result of the stress and trauma this caused her, having to live and deal with bedbugs for eight months. The Tenant relies on ODSP for income and this has also affected her because she could not afford market rents and ended up in a homeless shelter for a time. She testified, about how her mental health was negatively affected and that this was a “really big hardship” that as a university student she ended up taking a year off.

 

19.  It was undisputed that the tenant voluntarily disposed of some of her possessions. I note that this would be a reasonable expectation when treating for bedbugs. If bedbugs follow heat, then logically, they would be in the sofa and bed, where the source of heat is present, and for which it is difficult to treat. The disposal of pillows, and other things also follows this rationale action. That it was voluntary does not mean it was not necessary. I am satisfied that the Tenant is entitled to costs to replace property in the amount of

$750.00. This recognizes that not everything was replaced and that some replacement furniture was used, given the Tenant relies on ODSP for her income.

 

20.  The Tenant did incur out of pocket expenses to treat for bedbugs, to attempt to prevent them and to clean items possibly infected. I am satisfied that an amount of $375.00 is reasonable in all the circumstances, given the Tenant relies on ODSP for her income. although not all that the Tenant had requested,

 

21.  The Tenant requested a rent abatement for the months of May to December 2020 when she vacated in an amount equal to 100%. I do not find this to be reasonable. I am cognizant that the Landlord made efforts to treat for bedbugs, albeit however unsuccessful it was for this Tenant. As such an abatement of 25% is more reasonable particularly given the impact to the Tenant. Therefore, an amount for May to December 2020 shall be ordered (8 months X 139 X 25% = $278.00.)

 

22.  It was uncontested that the Tenant vacated as of December 31, 2020, even though she paid rent till March 2021. It was also uncontested that the Tenant finally removed all her possessions by March 9, 2021 when she turned over the keys. During this period, it was uncontested that the Tenant was living in a homeless shelter. As a result, I am satisfied that it is reasonable to order a 50% rent rebate for this period. I note that the Tenant may have left voluntarily, in that there was no notice of termination, however the conditions and the impact on the Tenant were significant and although not fully vacated till early March this is reasonable in all the circumstances. Therefore, an amount for January to March 2021 shall be ordered (3 months X 139 X 50% = $208.50)

 

23.  As noted, the Tenant did voluntarily vacate the rental unit, and remained in a shelter until a permanent accommodation was found. In light of these circumstances, it would be unreasonable to direct any moving and storage costs.

 

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     The Landlord shall pay to the Tenant a rent abatement of $486.50.

 

2.     The Landlord shall also pay to the Tenant $750.00. This represents the costs that the Tenant has incurred in replacing property that was disposed of as a result of the Landlord's failure to maintain the rental unit.

 

3.     The Landlord shall also pay to the Tenant $325.00 which is the reasonable out-of-pocket expenses the Tenant has incurred for her own treatment and prevention measures.

 

4.      The Landlord shall also pay the Tenant $53.00 for the cost of filing the application.

 

5.      The total amount the Landlord owes the Tenant is $1,614.50.

 

6.     The Landlord shall pay the Tenant the full amount owing by January 16, 2022.

 

7.     If the Landlord does not pay the Tenant the full amount owing by January 16, 2022 the Landlord will owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from January 17, 2022 at 2.00% annually on the outstanding balance.
