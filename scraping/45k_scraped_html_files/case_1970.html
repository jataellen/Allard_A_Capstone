Order under Section 31

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TET-15325-21

 

 

In the matter of:

6 OBRIEN STREET MARMORA ON K0K2M0

 

Between:

Jason Dafoe Jeanine Harrison

Tenants

 

 

and

 

 

Chatebadhur Balchand

Landlord

 

 

Jason Dafoe and Jeanine Harrison (the 'Tenants') applied for an order determining that Chatebadhur Balchand (the 'Landlord') harassed, obstructed, coerced, threatened or interfered with them, substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or residential complex by the Tenants or by a member of their household and withheld or deliberately interfered with the reasonable supply of a vital service, care service, or food that the Landlord is obligated to supply under the tenancy agreement.

 

This application was heard by videoconference on November 2, 2021. Only the Tenants Jason Dafoe and Jeanine Harrison attended the hearing. As of 9:45 a.m., the Landlord was not present or represented at the hearing although properly served with notice of this hearing by the Board.

 

Determinations:

 

T2 Application

 

1.     The Tenants applied to the Board with an Application regarding Tenant’ Rights (‘T2’) regarding the following issues:

a)      The Landlord substantially interfered with their reasonable enjoyment;

b)      The Landlord harassed them; and

 

c)        The Landlord withheld the vital service of heat.

 

2.     On any application before the Board the person making an allegation bears the burden of proof. This means the Tenants must lead sufficient evidence to establish that it is more likely than not that the Landlord did what they allege he did. In this case, I am not satisfied that Landlord substantially interfered with the Tenants’ reasonable enjoyment or harassed the Tenants. I am also not satisfied based on evidence presented at the hearing that the Landlord deliberately interfered with or withheld a vital service.

 

3.     Since the Tenants T2 application raised allegations under multiple grounds. For the sake of clarity, I will address them separately.

 

Substantial Interference

 

4.     The Tenant listed issues such as: unsafe front stairs in disrepair, missing indoor and outdoor lights, holes and cracks in the front door and rodents.

 

5.     These issues are not properly before me and relate to maintenance and repair or T6 Application issues. If the Tenants wish to pursue these issues they would need to bring a T6 matter to the Board under s. 30 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’).

 

Harassment:

 

6.     The Tenants raised the issue of harassment in relation to;

 

a)      The Landlord threatening to provide an N12 and use property for his own use once the Tenants complained about furnace oil being spilled on their belongings; and

 

b)      Tenants allegations that the Landlord on a few different occasions would sit in the driveway, “drink beer and ask Tenants’ kids personal questions about Tenants”.

 

7.     The lack of evidence presented by the Tenants to substantiate their claims of Landlord harassment through the threat of providing the Tenants with an N12 confirmed there is no merit to this claim.

 

8.     As per the Act, the Landlord has a right to bring an N12 for own use of property.

 

9.     I find that there was insufficient evidence before me to demonstrate that any conversations had with the children constituted harassment or was inappropriate. I find that speaking to the Tenants’ children as alleged by the Tenants does not in and of itself constitute harassment alleged by the Tenants.

 

10.  The Tenants did not provide enough evidence to demonstrate on a balance of probabilities that the Landlord harassed the Tenants and as a result the claim of harassment is denied.

 

 

 

Interference with Vital Services

 

 

 

11.  The main thrust of the Tenant’s claims in this T2 Application relates to interference with vital services, namely lack of heat.

 

12.  Tenant’s evidence was presented by Tenant Jason Dafoe (‘JD’):

 

•         JD confirmed moving into the rental unit of June 24, 2020. He testified there has been no heat since that time.

 

•         JD presented evidence (Tenant Exhibit #1) text messages with the Landlord Chatebadhur Balchand (‘CB’) regarding lack of heat, installation of furnace and consistent no-shows by CB to address the heat issue.

 

•         The Tenant testified the Tenants used space heaters provided by CB for heat given the broken furnace. The Tenant these were then the main source of heat in the rental unit.

 

•         JD testified that CB has a new furnace installed, however the new furnace was never “hooked up” or operational as it was installed by an unlicensed technician.

 

•         JD alleged that his hydro bills increased because of use of space heaters for heating in the house.

 

•         The Tenants submitted post hearing hydro bill submissions, however, they did not lead sufficient evidence to appropriately explain or confirm that any alleged hydro increase is specifically a direct result of using space heaters for heat.

 

 

13.    With respect to the allegations concerning the withholding of vital services, the Tenant’s evidence was with respect to a lack of sufficient heat in the rental property.

14.   Subsection 21(1) says:

 

A landlord shall not at any time during a tenant’s occupancy of a rental unit and before the day on which an order evicting the tenant is executed, withhold the reasonable supply of any vital service, care service or food that it is the landlord’s obligation to supply under the tenancy agreement or deliberately interfere with the reasonable supply of any vital service, care service or food.

 

15.  Pursuant to s. 2(1) heat is a vital service “during the part of each year prescribed by the regulations”. Subsection 4(1) of Ontario Regulation 516/06 says that the prescribed period for heat is September 1 to June 15. This means that during the period the Tenant is talking about here, heat was a vital service under the Act.

 

16.  The difficulty with the Tenant’s application and submissions concerning the heat is that the evidence is insufficient to establish the Landlord deliberately or intentionally withheld heat. Rather the evidence supports the conclusion that there may have been a maintenance problem with the rental property’s furnace as it was not appropriately installed. The issue of maintenance and whether an abatement would result from a failure to maintain is simply not before me on this application.

 

 

 

 

17.  Subsection 21(1) does not apply to a disrepair or maintenance problem. The wording of the provision shows it is intended to address the situation where a landlord deliberately interferes with or withholds a vital service. That is not what happened here, so this part of the Tenant’s application must be dismissed.

 

18.  In any event, even if I were to accept that the failure to maintain could somehow constitute a deliberate withholding of a vital service, in this case the Tenants failed to take any temperature readings in the rental unit to demonstrate the lack of heat. JD provided vague testimony that between September 1, 2020– June 15th, 2021 the temperature was “as low as 58-62 degrees most mornings.” He did not provide any specific evidence regarding temperature readings post June 2021. What this means is that the Tenant failed to lead any evidence of the exact temperature readings which would establish the temperature inside the rental unit. The taking of temperature readings was required because pursuant to section 4 of Ontario Regulation 516/06 heat is a vital service between September 1 and June 15 and the heating system must be capable of warming the rental unit to a minimum of 20 degrees Celsius during that period.

 

19.  In short, given the Landlord provided space heaters, I simply cannot find a deliberate withholding of a vital service and, in any event, there is no precise evidence of temperature readings to support the Tenants application. and claim that there was insufficient heat in the Tenants’ rental unit sometime during the winters of 2020/2021.

20.  As a result, I am not satisfied that the Landlord’s breached section 21 the Act. No remedy results from this claim.

 

 

Damage To Property

 

 

1.     I am satisfied from the uncontested text message evidence (Tenant Exhibit #1) Text Messages with the Landlord that there was an oil leak as a result of CB’s actions that damaged JD’s property. The Tenants application requests a 100% rent abatement for 6 months, property damage, out of pocket expenses, current and future hydro payment differential expenses.

 

2.     I will allow reimbursement of $375.00 for damaged goods and property as outlined in the T2 application and as supported by receipts provided. The other remedies claimed are dismissed since the allegations have not been proved.

 

3.     Although some receipts were provided in the Tenants’ application. It is unclear what these expenses relate to. Therefore, I will not grant the remedy of out-of-pocket expenses. Some of the items requested on the application relate to issues that are not

 

properly before me as part of a T2 Application and relate more to a T6 Application as explained above.

 

4.     Regarding any hydro payment differential as alleged by JD, the Tenants failed to lead any evidence to appropriately explain or confirm that any alleged hydro increase is specifically a direct result of using space heaters for heat. The same is true for any future Hydro differential requested by the Tenants.

 

5.     The Tenant has incurred the cost of $53.00 for filing the application and is entitled to reimbursement of those costs given the partial granting of the T2.

 

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.      The Tenant’s claims with respect to substantial interference with reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit, harassment and vital services are dismissed.

 

2.      The Landlord shall pay to the Tenants $375.00. This represents the reasonable costs that the Tenants has incurred in replacing property that was damaged as a result of an oil spill caused by the Landlord's actions.

 

3.      The Landlord shall pay to the Tenants $53.00 for the cost of filing the applicati on.

 

4.      The Landlord shall pay the Tenants the full amount owing by February 9, 2022.

 

5.      If the Landlord does not pay the Tenants the full amount owing by February 9, 2022, the Landlord will owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from February 9, 2022 at 2.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

 

6.      The Tenants have the right, at any time, to collect the full amount owing or any balance outstanding under this order.
