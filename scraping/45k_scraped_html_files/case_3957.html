Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: EAL-92480-20

 

 

In the matter of:

2, 122 BROCK STREET BROCKVILLE ON K6V4G1

 

Between:

Jeanine Hunter

Landlord

 

 

and

 

 

Shane Walsh

Tenant

 

 

 

Jeanine Hunter (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict Shane Walsh (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes.

 

This application was heard by way of video conference at 9:00 am on December 14, 2021. The Landlord and the Tenant attended the hearing. The Tenant declined to speak to Tenant Duty counsel prior to the hearing.

 

 

Determinations:

 

1.     The Tenant has not paid the total rent the Tenant was required to pay for the period from January 1, 2020 to June 30, 2021. Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination effective September 24, 2020.

 

2.     The current awful monthly rent is $675.00 per month.

 

3.     The Landlord collected a rent deposit of $675.00 from the Tenant and this deposit is still being held by the Landlord.

 

4.     Interest on the rent deposit is owing to the Tenant for the period from May 1, 2020 to September 24, 2020.

 

5.     The Tenant has made rent payments for July 2021 through to December 2021 in full. The Landlord has received the rent payments directly from ODSP.

 

6.     The Tenant states that he has not paid his lawful rent due to the ongoing maintenance issues.

 

7.     The Tenant states that he plans to pay his arrears of rent after the maintenance issues have been rectified.

 

8.     The Landlord states that the Tenant has had no loss of income due to COVID 19 as he is on ODSP. This was undisputed by the Tenant.

 

Section 82 Issues

 

 

9.     The Tenants raised the following issues pursuant to section 82 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’):

 

a)   Oven in unit not working

b)   Leak in unit coming from unit above

c)   Heat in unit is not sufficient

d)   Loud noise and disturbances from another tenant’s in the complex

e)   Continuous dog barking by another tenant’s dog

 

10.  The Tenant is seeking the following remedies:

 

a)   $300.00 being the amount the Tenant incurred to replace the stove

b)   $1200.00 to replace a bed that was damaged from the leak and $1000.00 to replace the laptop that was damaged due to the leak for a total cost of

$2200.00

c)   $300.00 being the amount the Tenant incurred to purchase two space heaters for the unit and for the Landlord to replace all three heaters in the rental unit with new heaters

d)   A rent abatement in the amount of $5000.00 because the Landlord substantially interfered with the Tenant’s reasonable enjoyment of the unit.

 

11.  Section 20 of the Act holds the landlord responsible for providing and maintaining a residential complex, including the rental units in it, in a good state of repair and fit for habitation and for complying with health, safety, housing and maintenance standards.

 

12.  Section 22 of the Act states a landlord shall not at any time during a tenant’s occupancy of a rental unit and before the day on which an order evicting the tenant is executed substantially interfere with the reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or the residential complex in which it is located for all usual purposes by a tenant or members of his or her household.

 

13.  In Onyskiw v. CJM Property Management, 2016 ONCA 477 (CanLII), the Court of Appeal determined that a contextual approach should be adopted when considering a landlord’s potential breach of subsection 20(1) of the Act and a breach will not be found if the landlord’s response to a maintenance issue was reasonable in the circumstances.

 

14.  I must also apply subsection 30(2) of the Act and consider whether the Tenant advised the Landlord of the alleged breaches before applying to the Board, and the parties’ duty to mitigate found in section 16 of the Act.

 

15.  Additionally, in accordance with section 16 of the Act, when a landlord becomes liable to pay any amount as a result of a breach of subsection 20(1), the tenant has a duty to take reasonable steps to minimize the loss.

 

16.  In determining whether the Landlords have breached their obligation to repair and maintain, the Board must first consider whether a maintenance or repair issue existed. The Board must then consider when the Landlords were informed of the issue or should reasonably have been aware of the issue.

 

Oven

 

Tenant’s Evidence- Oven

 

17.  The Tenant testified that approximately two months after he moved into the rental unit in May 2019 that the oven stopped working properly, and he noticed that the element in the oven appeared to be the wrong element. He notified the Landlord sometime in either June or July of 2019 of the issue.

 

18.  The Landlord instructed the Tenant to continue to use the oven as it worked and there was nothing wrong with it.

 

19.  The Tenant was using the oven on August 30, 2019 when he heard a loud bang. The Tenant provided a copy of a text message that he sent to the Landlord letting her know that this had just happened. The Landlord replied and made sure that the Tenant was not hurt and advised that she would have someone come to the property the next day to look at the oven.

 

20.  The Landlord visited the rental unit the next day and removed the element.

 

21.  The Tenant testified that the Landlord brought an element back the day after the Landlord removed the element and the Tenant put the element back into the oven. The element did not work when the Tenant put it back in the oven and it was inoperable.

 

22.  The Tenant then contacted the Landlord the next day to inform the Landlord that the oven was still not operatable, and the Landlord informed the Tenant that she would come by to take another look at the oven.

 

23.  The Tenant testified that the Landlord never came to the property to look at the oven or follow up with the Tenant. The Tenant is unsure as to whether he contacted the Landlord again regarding this issue.

 

24.  In July or August of 2021, the Tenant purchased an oven for the unit as it was still not operable. The Tenant did not notify the Landlord that he had purchased a new stove. The Tenant purchased the stove for $300.00.

 

Landlord’s Evidence- Oven

 

25.  The Landlord testified that the Tenant texted her to let her know that the oven was not working. However, when the Landlord arrived at the rental unit sometime to take a look the Tenant advised her that it was fixed and working fine.

 

26.  The Landlord advised the Tenant that if the problem persisted that they would replace the oven and for the Tenant to let her know if there were further issues. The Landlord did not hear from the Tenant again regarding the issue.

 

27.  The Landlord testified that she received a text message from the Tenant on September 12, 2019 letting her know that the oven was working fine. The Landlord did not enter a copy of this text message into evidence.

 

Analysis - Oven

 

28.  I accept that the Landlord informed the Tenant after the initial inspection that if the problem persisted that she would replace the oven. However, I find that the Tenant notified the Landlord that the problem was persisting, but the Landlord failed to address the issue before the Tenant replaced the stove on his own. Given these circumstances, I find it would be fair to order the Landlord to reimburse the Tenant the amount of $300.00 for the cost he incurred to replace the oven.

 

 

Leak

 

Tenant’s Evidence- Leak

 

29.  The Tenant testified that water began leaking into the bedroom from the bathroom in the rental unit above starting in November 2019.

 

30.  The Tenant notified the Landlord right away.

 

31.  The Tenant testified that the Landlord did come to the property a few days after he notified her, and the ceiling was opened up and the leak was repaired.

 

32.  The Tenant testified that on December 23, 2019 the ceiling started to leak again, and he contacted the Landlord immediately by text message.

 

33.  The following day the Landlord dropped off drywall to an adjacent rental unit and asked the Tenant if he would be able to do the patch work in his rental unit himself.

 

34.  The Tenant advised the Landlord that he could not do the repairs, or the drywall work due to issues with his back. The Tenant did move the necessary drywall required for the patch work from the adjacent unit to his unit.

 

35.  The Tenant testified that he put up a piece of drywall over the hole in the ceiling as he was having issues with bats and squirrels.

 

36.  The Landlord testified that the leak damaged a bed and his daughter’s laptop. The Tenant has not replaced the bed as he cannot afford to do so. The Tenant stated that his children will not sleep on the bed due to the staining from the water damage. The Tenant stated that his daughter’s laptop would not turn on due to the water damage and it needed to be replaced. The Tenant testified that his daughter’s mother paid to replace her laptop.

 

37.  The Tenant testified that he had an agreement with the Landlord that he would be responsible for grass cutting and snow shovelling and this agreement commenced in May 2019. At that time the Tenant’s rent was reduced from $725.00 per month to $675.00 per month for the new responsibilities of the Tenant. He stated that the agreement never included minor maintenance repairs.

 

Landlord’s Evidence- Leak

 

38.  The Landlord testified that in November 2019, the Tenant let her know that there was a leak in the rental unit.

 

39.  The Landlord sent a plumber to the property to take a look at the leak and the plumber rectified the issue and recommended that the hole not be patched for a while to ensure that the leak was fixed and was not going to persist.

 

40.  The Landlord testified that several months later the Tenant reached out to her and advised that the leak was persisting. The Landlord’s husband attended the property the day after the Tenant advised her of the issue and fixed the leak. The Landlord stated that her husband is not a licensed plumber, however, he has 50 years of construction experience that includes experience with plumbing.

 

41.  The Landlord’s husband dropped of the drywall to the Tenant so that he could repair the hole in the ceiling at a later date once it was determined the leak had stopped. The Landlord did not find this to be an unfair request as the Landlord and Tenant had an agreement that the Tenant would perform minor maintenance duties.

 

42.  The Landlord testified that she and the Tenant had an arrangement that the Tenant was going to be responsible for minor repairs to the unit, snow shovelling and grass cutting. She stated that the Tenant’s rent was reduced in May 2019 from $725.00 to $675.00 to reflect the new responsibilities of the Tenant.

 

Analysis- Leak

 

43.  I am satisfied, on a balance of probabilities, that the Landlord responded to the Tenant’s complaints regarding the leak in a reasonable and timely manner. The Landlord sent a plumber to the property the following day after being notified of the leak and believed the issue to be rectified. After the Tenant notified the Landlord that the problem was persisting the Landlord sent her husband that has experience with plumbing, and he corrected the issue. I find that the Landlord repaired the leak in a reasonable and timely manner.

 

44.  However, while I am satisfied that the actual leak has been repaired in a timely manner, I find that the Landlord has breached section 20 of the Act by failing to take necessary action to patch the resulting hole in the ceiling.

 

45.  It is the Landlord’s obligation under section 20 of the Act to perform all necessary repairs, not the Tenants. In Montgomery v. Van, 2009 ONC 808, the Court of Appeal found that the landlord is not entitled to transfer its maintenance obligation under section 20 of the Act requiring the tenant to be responsible for the repairs, even if the parties have signed a lease purporting to make the tenant responsible for the repairs The court further held that the only permissible way this can occur is if the parties enter into a separate severable contract whereby the Tenant agrees to perform the repairs. Therefore, the Landlord shall be ordered to perform the necessary repair to the ceiling.

 

46.  The Tenant has not provided any evidence with respect to the damage of the bed or replacement costs if necessary. The Tenant states that the bed has not been replaced and is still at the rental unit, however, his children will not sleep on it due to the water stains

 

47.  The Landlord stated that she is willing to pay to have the bed steam cleaned for any water damage that the bed may have incurred due to the leak. I find this to be fair in these circumstances. I find that on a balance of probabilities that the bed could still be used after it has been professionally steam cleaned.

 

48.  The Tenant has not provided any evidence that his daughters laptop was ruined due to a result of the water leak. The Tenant states that his daughter’s mother paid to replace the laptop as he could not afford to do so. The Tenant failed to provide proof that the laptop was damaged due to water or proof that a new laptop was purchased for his daughter.

 

Insufficient Heat

 

Tenant’s Evidence- Insufficient Heat

 

49.  The Tenant testified that the lack of heat became an issue at the beginning of his first winter in the rental unit. The Tenant contacted the Landlord regarding the issue sometime in December 2019, and at that time the Landlord was away in Florida.

 

50.  The Tenant stated that the heat did not seem to be reaching his unit and that the tenant that resided in the rental unit below him had control of the thermostat.

 

 

 

51.  The Tenant stated that the Landlord was going to reach out to the lower tenant and advise them to not tamper with the thermostat.

 

52.  The Tenant testified that the heat continued to be an issue and that the lower tenant would constantly tamper with the thermostat making his rental unit very cold.

 

53.  The Tenant provided a photograph that was sent to the Landlord on November 17, 2020, of a meat thermometer that had been sitting on the counter in the kitchen and it was showing a reading of 4 degrees Celsius. The text message from the Tenant to the Landlord stated that there was no heat in the unit and that it was very cold.

 

54.  The Tenant contacted the Landlord again sometime in December 2020 and advised the Landlord that his unit was still cold, and the heat did not appear to be working properly.

 

55.  The Tenant provided evidence of a text message from the Landlord that he sent her on December 7, 2020 advising her that his unit was still cold. He asked the Landlord when she was expected to rectify the issue. The Landlord’s response was that she could not afford the repairs at that time as the Tenant was not paying his rent.

 

56.  The Tenant stated that the tenant of the lower rental unit is no longer in possession of the rental unit and that he is currently receiving more heat to the unit. However, he has had to use portable electric heaters in the kitchen, bathroom and living room, two of which he had to purchase. The Tenant could not recall how much he paid for each heater, but he believed that they were approximately $150.00 per heater

 

57.  The Tenant testified that he has had to use portable electric heaters in the kitchen, bathroom and living room. The Tenant had one heater in his possession that he is using but had to go out and purchase two more heaters. The Tenant could not recall how much he paid for each heater, but he believed that they were approximately $150.00 per heater.

 

58.  The Tenant testified that as of the day of the hearing the current temperature in the unit was reading 15 degrees Celsius with the electric heaters.

 

Landlord’s Evidence- Heat

 

59.  The Landlord testified that prior to the Tenant moving into the rental unit there was never complaints from the previous tenant regarding heat.

 

60.  The Landlord testified that she had someone come to look at the furnace after it was determined that it was not the tenant in the lower rental unit tampering with the thermostat as the problem persisted after that tenant vacated the unit.

 

61.  The Landlord stated that the HVAC technician inspected the furnace sometime in December 2020 and instructed the Landlord that a duct fan would increase the air flow to the Tenants unit.

 

62.  The Landlord has attempted to order the duct fan, and have it installed but this has proven to be very challenging due to COVID.

 

63.  The Landlord testified that she is aware that the Tenant is using portable electric heaters and that the she pays for the hydro at the rental unit and therefore this does not present any financial implications to him.

 

64.  The Landlord testified that she has had the HVAC technician at the rental complex four times and that the furnace is only 15 years old. She stated that the rental complex is a very old building and that the furnace is operating.

 

Analysis- Insufficient Heat

 

65.  I find that the Landlord did breach their obligation under s. 20(1) of the Act by failing to ensure that the furnace consistently provides sufficient heat to the rental unit. I accept that the Landlord had an HVAC technician to the property on four separate occasions and it was determined that the furnace is operating. However, at one of the inspections the HVAC technician recommended that a duct fan be installed to help push air through the ducts to the Tenant’s unit. I appreciate that the Landlord may have been experiencing some delays with the part and installation due to COVID 19, however, almost one year has passed since the recommendation from the HVAC Technician to install a duct fan and the work has yet to be completed. Further, the parties agree that the Tenant has been regularly paying his rent for the last 6 months. I find that the Landlord’s failure to arrange for the installation of the duct fan in a timely manner is a breach of section 20.

 

66.  I find that the Landlord is in breach of Section 21 of the Act which states:

 

A landlord shall not at any time during a tenant’s occupancy of a rental unit and before the day on which an order evicting the tenant is executed, withhold the reasonable supply of any vital service.

 

67.  Pursuant to section 4 of the O.Reg 516/06 under the Act, the Landlord is legally required to ensure that the unit is consistently heated to at least 20 degrees during the period from September 1 to June 15. I am not satisfied that the rental unit was heated to 20 degrees during this period. The Tenant provided a photograph that was sent to the Landlord on November 17, 2020, of a meat thermometer that had been sitting on the counter in the kitchen and it was showing a reading of 4 degrees Celsius. I appreciate that this photograph was taken prior to the lower level tenant vacating that was tampering with the thermostat, however this does not take away the obligation of the Landlord to ensure the unit is heated to 20 degrees. The Tenant testified that as of December 14, 2021 the temperature in the rental unit was 15 degrees with the assistance of three electric portable space heaters. This temperature is still 5 degrees below the minimum required temperature. It is likely that once the duct fan is installed this could substantially increase the amount of heat flow to the rental unit.

 

68.  The Landlord stated that she is willing to pay the Tenant $300.00 for costs incurred by the Tenant to purchase two electric portable heaters. The Landlord also agreed that she

 

would replace all three electric portable heaters currently in the rental unit with new electric portable heaters. I find this to be fair in these circumstances.

 

 

Reasonable Enjoyment

Tenant’s Evidence- Noise Disturbances

69.  The Tenant testified that the tenants that live in the rental unit above him fight constantly. At times it sounds like bodies are being slammed against the wall. The Tenant stated that this behaviour frightens his children.

 

70.  On Jan 10, 2019 the Tenant testified that a fight broke out in the upper unit and that the fight was so bad and loud that the Tenant’s daughter left the rental unit as she was scared. The Tenant called the Landlord to advise of the issue and was instructed by the Landlord that there wasn’t anything that she could do and that the Tenant needed to call the Police. The Tenant contacted the Police and they came to the rental complex and spoke to the tenants in the upper unit. The Tenant provided a Police Incident Number of BR20000304.

 

71.  The Tenant testified that he called the Police and the Landlord on the following dates as the tenants in the upper unit were fighting and it was very loud. The dates these occurrences took place were as follows:

a)   March 9, 2019- Police Incident Number BR20002096

b)   May 19, 2019- Police Incident Number BR20002121

c)   September 9, 2019- Police Incident Number BR20004001

d)   September 10, 2019- Police Incident Number BR20008102

 

72.  The Tenant testified that each time he called the Landlord she instructed him to call the Police and that there was nothing that she could do. He testified that the Police came to the property and each time they spoke to the tenants in the upper unit.

 

73.  The Tenant testified that after the incident on September 10, 2019, that there have been approximately thirty more occurrences but the Tenant did not reach out to the Landlord as the Landlord refused to assist with the prior occurrences and just kept instructing the Tenant to call the police.

 

74.  The Tenant stated that the Landlord should have contacted the Police to obtain the Occurrence Report Numbers so that she could file an application with the Board against the tenants living in the upper unit.

 

75.  The Tenant also testified that other tenants have constantly let their dogs bark for days on end. The Tenant contacted the Landlord in December 2020 to advise her of the dogs barking and the Landlord stated that she would speak to the tenants regarding the dogs barking. The Tenant never heard back from the Landlord regarding the barking dogs.

 

76.  The Tenant testified that one of the tenants whose dog was constantly barking had been arrested and was incarcerated. He testified that the Landlord did call the Police to have

 

the dog removed from the property in December 2020 as the tenant had been incarcerated.

 

Landlord’s Evidence- Noise Disturbances

 

77.  The Landlord testified that each time the Tenant contacted her that she contacted the tenants and asked them to please refrain from fighting in the unit and being loud and to be respectful of other tenants.

 

78.  The Landlord testified that each time the Tenant let her know about the barking dogs that she reached out to the tenants to ask them to please keep the dogs quiet and to be respectful of other tenants.

 

79.  The Landlord stated that she has not filed an application with the Board against the tenants as she was not present at the rental complex when these occurrences happened. She stated that she did reach out to the Police Department and she was instructed that they could not provide her any information or the occurrences numbers as she was not a party to the occurrence.

 

Analysis- Noise Disturbances

 

80.  In this case, the behaviour of the Landlord, which the Tenant claim caused a substantial interference with his reasonable enjoyment of the unit, is the Landlord’s failure to take reasonable and effective steps to address the Tenant’s complaints about other tenants in the building making excessive noise.

 

81.  A landlord’s duty to address substantial interference with the reasonable enjoyment of a tenant by another tenant was affirmed by the Divisional Court in Hassan v. Niagara Housing Authority, [2000] O.J. No. 5650. The Court held as follows:

 

It is not that the other tenant's actions are imputed to the landlord, but, rather, the landlord's legal responsibility to provide the tenant with quiet enjoyment that gives rise to the responsibility on the landlord to take reasonable steps to correct the intrusion of the neighbouring tenant on the tenant's right to quiet enjoyment.

 

As under the old Landlord and Tenant Act so under the new Tenant Protection Act, the landlord has the positive obligation to provide the tenant with quiet enjoyment and take the reasonably necessary action against any tenant that denies a neighbouring tenant quiet enjoyment of his premises.

 

On the evidence before it, there was no evidence that justifies the finding of the tribunal that the landlord took reasonable steps within a reasonable length of time to restore to the tenant the quiet enjoyment to which he was entitled.

 

Writing threatening letters to the offending tenant, holding meetings with the offenders and offended, is not enough if the harassment continues and, indeed, increases. The landlord had to do something effective, if necessary, make application to terminate the tenancy of the offender.

 

 

82.  I find that the Landlord breached their obligation under s. 22 of the Act by failing to take reasonable steps to address the disturbing noise created by the tenants in the residential complex.

 

83.  While I accept that the Landlord did contact the tenants causing a noise disturbance, after receiving noise complaints from the Tenant, this proved to be ineffective. The Landlord should have taken further action such as writing warning letters to the tenants that were causing the disturbance, serving Notice of Termination to the tenants and filing eviction applications with the LTB. I appreciate that the Landlord did not personally witness the disrupting conduct, however, the Landlord does not need to personally witness the conduct in order to take these steps. The Landlord could have served Notice of Termination paperwork to the tenants and filed an application with the Board on the basis of the Tenant’s complaints. The Landlord could call the Tenant as a witness at a hearing with the LTB.

 

84.  The Tenant testified that he stopped notifying the Landlord after September 10, 2019 that the noise disturbances continued. I can appreciate that the Tenant stopped informing the Landlord as he was told consistently that there was nothing she could do. Since the Landlord was aware that the issue was ongoing, she could have followed up with the Tenant to see if the issue had improved.

 

85.  In respect to the noise disturbances the Tenant is seeking a rent abatement of $5000.00. I find that the amount of the abatement that the Tenants are requesting is unreasonable, I appreciate that there has been an impact on the Tenant’s reasonable enjoyment, but I also find that the Landlord did start to take necessary steps by reaching out to the tenants that were causing the disturbances, however they did not follow through. The Tenant also stopped notifying the Landlord of further incidents.

 

86.  Considering these circumstances, I find it reasonable to award a rent abatement of

$1500.00.

 

 

Damaged Property

 

Tenant’s Evidence- Damaged Property

 

87.  The Tenant testified that sometime in the Spring of 2019 another tenant in the rental complex damaged his lawnmowers by slashing the tires and putting water in the gas tank of the lawnmower. The Tenant contacted the Landlord to let her know of the issue and the Landlord instructed the Tenant to contact the Police.

 

88.  The Tenant contacted the Police and the Police informed the Tenant that they had no way of proving that the other tenant did damage the lawnmowers. The other Tenant no longer resides at the residential complex.

 

Landlord’s Evidence- Damaged Property

 

89.  The Landlord testified that she had no knowledge of any damage to the Tenant’s lawnmowers and that she has no way of controlling any damage to the Tenant’s belongings that were left in the yard of the rental complex.

 

Analysis- Damaged Property

 

90.  I find that the Landlord did not damage the Tenant’s lawnmowers. The Landlord has no personal knowledge of any damage done to the Tenant’s lawnmowers by another tenant. It would be unreasonable to hold the Landlord responsible for damage incurred to the Tenant’s lawnmower while it was being stored in the yard of the residential complex. Further, the Tenant’s evidence is insufficient to establish on a balance of probabilities that the damage was even caused by the former tenant.

 

 

Relief from Eviction

 

 

91.  I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would not be unfair to postpone the eviction until February 28, 2022, pursuant to subsection 83(1)(b) of the Act.

 

92.  In making this finding I considered the evidence of the Tenant that since July 1, 2021 to the date of the hearing his rent has been paid directly to the Landlord by ODSP. These payments demonstrate the Tenant’s intention to preserve the tenancy. The Tenant has also indicated that he has plans to pay his arrears once the maintenance issues are dealt with. I have also considered that the Landlord did reach out to the Tenant on several occasions to encourage a payment plan, however, the Tenant advised the Landlord that he would pay the rent arrears after she fixed the maintenance issues.

 

93.  The Tenant provided a letter dated May 11, 2021 from the Children’s Aid Society confirming that his children have been in his care since December 14, 2020. The Tenant is currently on ODSP and is finding it financially difficult to support his family. The Tenant has been trying to apply for the Child Tax Benefit but there have been some challenges as he needs to prove that the children are now in is care. The Tenant has been searching for a larger apartment that could accommodate his needs but due to COVID 19 this been very difficult as the inventory is low, and the rental prices are very high.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.            Unless the Tenant voids the order as set out below, the tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated. The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before February 28, 2022.

 

2.            The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $6,364.35*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to January 4, 2022, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlord owes on the rent deposit and less a rent abatement of $2,100.00.

 

3.            The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $22.19 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting January 5, 2022 to the date the Tenant moves out of the unit.

 

4.            The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $186.00 for the cost of filing the application.

 

5.            If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing* on or before January 15, 2022, the Tenant will start to owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from January 16, 2022 at .00% annually on the balance outstanding.

 

6.            If the unit is not vacated on or before February 28, 2022, then starting March 1, 2022, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

 

7.            Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord, on or after March 1, 2022.

 

8.            If the Tenant wishes to void this order and continue the tenancy, the Tenant must pay to the Landlord or to the Board in trust:

i)            $7,811.00 if the payment is made on or before January 31, 2022, or

ii)            $8,486.00 if the payment is made on or before February 28, 2022**.

 

9.            If the Tenant does not make full payment in accordance with this paragraph and by the appropriate deadline, then the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

 

10.         The Tenant may make a motion to the Board under subsection 74(11) of the Act to set aside this order if they pay the amount required under that subsection on or after March 1, 2022 but before the Sheriff gives vacant possession to the Landlord. The Tenant is only entitled to make this motion once during the period of the tenancy agreement with the Landlord.
