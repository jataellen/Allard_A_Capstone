Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: SOL-22808-21

 

 

In the matter of:

8249 MUNDARE CRESCENT NIAGARA FALLS ON L2G7M6

 

Between:

Rahim Lila

Landlord

 

 

and

 

 

Jacklyn Miller Joseph Steele

Tenants

 

 

 

Rahim Lila (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict Jacklyn Miller and Joseph Steele (the 'Tenants') because the Tenants did not pay the rent that the Tenants owe.

 

This application was heard via videoconference on December 20, 2021. The Landlord and the Tenants attended the hearing. The Tenant, Jacklyn Miller (JM), had an opportunity to speak with Tenant Duty Counsel before the hearing began.

 

Determinations:

 

1.     The Tenants have not paid the total rent the Tenants were required to pay for the period from May 1, 2021 to December 31, 2021. Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination effective June 25, 2021.

 

2.     The Tenants are in possession of the rental unit.

 

3.     The lawful monthly rent is $1,790.00.

 

4.     The Landlord collected a rent deposit of $1,790.00 from the Tenants and this deposit is still being held by the Landlord. Interest on the rent deposit is owing to the Tenants for the period from May 31, 2020 to June 25, 2021.

 

5.     The Tenants paid $2,600.00 after the application was filed.

 

6.     At the hearing the Tenants raised the following maintenance issues pursuant to section 82 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'):

 

•         Exterior Drain/Catch Basin Flooding

•         Bathtub

 

•         Front Door

•         Animals in the Chimney

 

Exterior Drain/Catch Basin Flooding

 

7.     The Tenants submitted that the driveway floods when it rains. JM testified that the driveway is on a slope and when it rains, water pools into the catch basin. The catch basin overflows, resulting in her driveway flooding. This occurred on May 29, 2020, August 15, 2020, June 13, 2021, July 6, 2021 and July 8, 2021. The Tenants submitted that the Landlord’s response to the flooding has been inadequate. Specifically, on August 15, 2020 the Landlord had Roto Rooter attend the unit to snake the drain. On this date, the employee recommended to the Tenant that they break through the concrete to access the roots to prevent further damage. She submitted this was told to her and to the Landlord verbally. After, three more floods occurred. On July 8, 2021, the flood was such that the Tenants had to bail out water from the garage.

 

8.     The Landlord submitted that he was responsive to the flooding issues at the Tenants’ unit. The Landlord testified that the May 29, 2020 flood was reported to him on June 2, 2022. This was not a flood, but the catch basin was draining very slowly. Enercare was contacted to address the draining. They attended two weeks later but they said they could not drain it. The maintenance person attended June 23, 2020 and the Tenant confirmed on June 28, 2020 there were no further issues.

 

9.     Regarding the August 15, 2020 incident, the Landlord made an emergency call to Roto Rooters and they came within hours. They identified and cut out roots and the blockage was cleared. The conversation had with the Roto Rooter employee was that the Landlord should schedule a clearing of the pipes every year. As such, the Landlord booked an appointment one year into the future.

 

10.  On June 12, 2021, the Tenants reported the drain was slow to drain. The Landlord contacted Roto Rooter, who scheduled the Landlord for their next available appointment of July 12, 2021.

 

11.  Prior to July 6, 2021, the City of Niagara ripped up the sidewalks so any water coming in would affect the draining. On July 6, 2021, the Tenants reported to the Landlord that water was slow to clear. On July 8, 2021, the Tenants reported heavy flooding. The Landlord contacted Roto Rooter, who said they were unavailable and could not attend that day. The Landlord told the Tenants to purchase a submersible pump and sandbags, for which he reimbursed them. On July 12, 2021, Roto Rooter attended, and they did a full review, cleaned the drain lines and diverted water. Since that point, the Tenants have not reported further issues. The Landlord submitted into evidence the receipts for each Roto Rooter attendance.

 

Analysis

 

12.  In the case Onyskiw v. CJM Property Management, 2016 ONCA 477 (Onyskiw) (CanLII), the Court of Appeal for Ontario determined that a contextual approach should be adopted when considering a landlord’s potential breach of subsection 20(1) of the Act and a breach will not be found if the landlord’s response to a maintenance issue was reasonable in the circumstances. Therefore, simply because there had been flooding at the rental unit, does not automatically amount to a breach of subsection 20(1) of the Act by the Landlord.

 

13.  The evidence before the Board was that the Landlord had a timely response to the Tenants’ complaints of each flood. While the Tenants may dispute the Landlord’s response was timely between the June 12, 2021 and July 8, 2021 flood, the credible and believable evidence of the Landlord was that Roto Rooter was contacted immediately but no one was unavailable for treatment until July 12, 2021.

 

14.  Further, while the Tenants dispute the adequacy of the Landlord’s response because they did not break through the concrete to access the roots as recommended by Roto Rooter, I am not persuaded this would have prevented further floods. As the Tenants are raising this issue pursuant to section 82 of the Act, the Tenants bear the burden of proving the merits of their claim on a balance of probabilities (i.e., at least 51% of the way). The Tenants did not prove, by calling the employee from Roto Rooter to testify at the hearing, for example, that this was required to prevent any further flooding. Further, it was uncontested that there have been no further floods reported since July 12, 2021.

 

15.  On the evidence before me, I find that the Landlord’s responses to address the flooding were reasonable in these circumstances. Consequently, in accordance with Onyskiw, I am not satisfied that the Landlord breached subsection 20(1) of the Act by failing to maintain the rental unit with respect to the flooding as the Landlord acted reasonably and responded when advised that there was a problem.

 

Bathtub

 

16.  The Tenants submitted that there was water dripping from the bathtub to the furnace room onto the water heater. JM testified that on July 8, 2020, she heard dripping into the furnace room while she was in the basement. She reported this to the Landlord on July 9, 2020. Submitted into evidence were two photos dated July 9, 2020, showing water on top of the water heater. The Landlord recommended that the Tenants caulk around the bathtub. She did as such on July 10, 2020. There was further dripping on September 4 and 21, 2020. The Landlord was advised of the issues. The maintenance person attended and said he could not locate the issue. On August 3, 2021, during a routine maintenance inspection, JM reported the issue again to the maintenance person, for which he said he did not think it was an issue. JM testified this is an ongoing issue and the Tenants have been hesitant to use the bathtub for fear of further leaks.

 

17.  The Landlord acknowledged the Tenants reported the leaking issue on June 9, 2020 but assumed the issue was resolved after they caulked the tub. When the maintenance person attended the unit for the inspection, he did not witness any leaking and therefore, did not repair a leak. The Landlord submitted that if there was a leak, the Tenant should contact the Landlord’s maintenance person immediately so he can observe the leak and know how to fix it.

 

 

 

Analysis

 

18.  On a balance of probabilities, I find the Landlord was in breach of his maintenance obligations. JM’s credible and believable evidence was that there is dripping from the tub into the furnace room and onto the water heater. JM reported the issue of the leak on four occasions. The Landlord’s response was for the Tenant to caulk the tub and thereafter, denied the existence of any issues. While the Landlord submitted the Tenant should have contacted the maintenance person immediately so he could witness the leak, the Landlord should undertake to investigate potential causes of the leak rather than dismiss its existence entirely.

 

19.  Having found the Landlord is in breach of his obligations, the Tenants are entitled to a remedy. JM requested that the Landlord repair the leak. I find this remedy to be reasonable in the circumstances.

 

Front Door

 

20.  The Tenants submitted that the front door has had issues with sticking and latching. JM testified that on July 5, 2020, she first noticed she could not use the front door because it was stuck. She reported it to the Landlord and a contractor came on September 26, 2020 to remove the edging around the door, relevel the door and put back the edging. On December 4, 2020, the Tenants contacted the Landlord regarding further issues with the door, and requested a door installer to attend, but instead the Landlord’s maintenance person came. On February 4, 2021, the door was stuck again, and the doorknob broke off. On March 15, 2021, the maintenance person attended and replaced the doorknob. On June 11, 2021, the Tenants reported the door sticking. On August 9, 2021, someone attended the unit to repair it.

 

21.  The issue to date is that the door no longer sticks but cannot latch. The door swings open if it is not locked shut. JM stated that she informed the maintenance person of the front door not latching in December 2021. However, as of the date of the hearing, the maintenance person had yet to look at the issue. The Tenants are worried that the door will begin sticking again with the winter months because they believe the weight of the snow weighs down the roof, causing the door to stick shut.

 

22.  The Landlord submitted that he responded to all issues regarding the door. When the Tenant first reported the sticky door, the Landlord contacted the door contractor immediately. However, they could not attend until September 2020 because of COVID-19. Thereafter, the Landlord submitted his response was timely. After June 11, 2021, he did not hear about any issues about the door and was first informed of the latching issue at the hearing.

 

Analysis

 

23.  On a balance of probabilities, I find the Landlord was not in breach of his maintenance obligations pursuant to section 20(1) of the Act. Although there is no dispute that the door

 

sticking was a disrepair issue, as per Onyskiw, I find that the Landlords’ response upon being notified of the sticky door to be reasonable. After each time the Tenants reported the sticking door, the Landlord either contacted the contractor to attend or sent the maintenance person immediately. I find that the delay in the door contractor attending the unit was not unreasonable. It is undisputed that the door sticking is presently not an issue and the Tenants’ concern regarding the door sticking in the future is speculative, only.

 

24.  Regarding the latching issue, Section 30(2) of the Act requires me to consider whether the Tenant had advised the Landlords of the issue before applying to the Board. The evidence before me was that Tenants did not make the Landlord’s agent aware of the issue until December 2021, after they filed their list of section 82 issues. It would be unreasonable to suggest that the Landlord should be found as being in breach of a maintenance obligation when they were not aware that a maintenance issue existed until a few weeks before this hearing.

 

Animals in the Chimney

 

25.  The Tenants submitted that there were animals in their chimney beginning March 2, 2021. Pest control was contacted by the Landlord the same day. JM testified that pest control recommended that the chimney be capped off, which the Tenants relayed to the Landlord. To date, the chimney is not capped off. The Tenants did not make any submissions regarding ongoing issues with animals in the chimney.

 

26.  The Landlord submitted that pest control attended as soon as he was notified. The Landlord disputed he was told that the chimney should be capped off. The Tenants did not comment on this issue since March 9, 2021 when JM informed him that she no longer heard animals. A roofing inspector came to the unit on July 13, 2021, and no recommendations were made to cap off the chimney.

 

Analysis

 

27.  On a balance of probabilities, I do not find that the Landlord was in breach of his maintenance obligations. While the Tenants dispute the adequacy of the Landlord’s response because he did not cap off the chimney, I am not persuaded this is a maintenance issue. As the Tenants are raising this issue pursuant to section 82 of the Act, the Tenants bear the burden of proving the merits of their claim. The Tenants did not prove, by calling a person from pest control to testify, for example, that this is required to prevent animals. Further, it was uncontested that there have been no animals in the chimney since March 2, 2021.

 

 

Relief from Eviction

 

28.  I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), including the impact of COVID-19 on the parties and whether the Landlord attempted to negotiate a repayment agreement with the

 

Tenants and find that it would not be unfair to postpone the eviction until January 31, 2022 pursuant to subsection 83(1)(b) of the Act.

 

29.  Pursuant to section 83(6), I am required to consider whether the Landlord made attempts to negotiate a payment agreement with the Tenants. The Landlord testified that numerous payment plans were presented until July 25, 2021. However, the parties did not reach an agreement. I am satisfied that the Landlord attempted to negotiate a payment agreement with the Tenants.

 

30.  With respect to section 83(2), JM testified that her and Joseph Steele’s employment has suffered due to COVID-19 and they have been relying on government assistance. If the tenancy was terminated, this would leave the Tenants and three kids homeless. They are currently quarantining because of COVID in their family. She presented a payment plan of paying $300.00 a month in additional to the lawful monthly rent until the arrears are paid in full.

 

31.  The Landlord submitted it would be prejudicial to continue the tenancy. The property is owned by the Landlord’s senior parents who rely on the income from the unit as part of their retirement. The Landlord’s parents have minimal available credit against their line of credit due the arrears. He further objected to the Tenants’ proposed payment plan.

 

32.  I find it unfair to impose the Tenants’ proposed payment plan on the Landlord as it would take 34 months to pay off the outstanding arrears. While I find the Landlord’s parents’ circumstances to be worrying, in consideration of the Tenants’ children and difficulties with COVID-19, I find it appropriate to postpone eviction. This postponed termination will give the Tenants an opportunity to organize their move.

 

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     Unless the Tenant voids the order as set out below, the tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated. The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before January 31, 2022.

 

2.     The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $8,496.21*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to January 5, 2022, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlord owes on the rent deposit.

 

3.     The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $58.85 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting January 6, 2022 to the date the Tenant moves out of the unit.

 

4.     The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $186.00 for the cost of filing the application.

 

5.     If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing* on or before January 31, 2022, the Tenant will start to owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from February 1, 2022 at 2.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

 

6.     If the unit is not vacated on or before January 31, 2022, then starting February 1, 2022, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

 

7.     Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord, on or after February 1, 2022.

 

8.     If, on or before January 31, 2022, the Tenant pays the amount of $11,906.00** to the Landlord or to the Board in trust, this order for eviction will be void. This means that the tenancy would not be terminated and the Tenant could remain in the unit. If this payment is not made in full and on time, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

 

9.     The Tenant may make a motion to the Board under subsection 74(11) of the Act to set aside this order if they pay the amount required under that subsection on or after February 1, 2022 but before the Sheriff gives vacant possession to the Landlord. The Tenant is only entitled to make this motion once during the period of the tenancy agreement with the Landlord.

 

Regarding the Tenants’ Section 82 issues

 

10.  On or before January 31, 2022, the Landlord is required to repair the leak from the bathtub to the furnace room.
