Order under Section 21.2 of the

Statutory Powers Procedure Act

and the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TEL-17359-21-RV

 

 

In the matter of:      MAIN FLOOR, 86 SEWARD CRESCENT

AJAX ON L1Z1Y9

 

Between:                  Adenike Adengbenro                                                                                   Landlord

 

 

and

 

Edward Etafo                                                                                                                  Tenant

 

 

Review Order

 

 

Edward Etafo (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict Adenike Adengbenro (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or someone the Tenant permitted in the residential complex has substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment or lawful right, privilege or interest of the Landlord or another tenant; because the Landlord requires possession of the rental unit for the purpose of residential occupation. The Landlord also claimed compensation for each day the Tenant remained in the unit after the termination date.

 

This application was resolved by order TEL-17359-21 issued on August 4, 2021.

 

On October 7, 2021, the Tenant requested a review of the order and that the order be stayed until the request to review the order is resolved.

 

On October 8, 2021, interim order TEL-17359-21 -IN was issued, staying the order issued on August 4, 2021.

 

The review request was heard by video conference on December 21, 2021. However, on further deliberation after the hearing I decided to reconvene the hearing for the below reasons.

 

The Landlord, the Landlord’s legal representative, Jeff Shabes, the Tenant, and the Tenant’s legal representative, Rajan Mahavalirajan, attended the review hearing on December 21, 2021 and the reconvened review hearing on January 25, 2022.

 

Determinations:

 

1.     Paragraphs 2 to 6, below, set out the procedural history in the December 21, 2022 hearing.

 

 

 

2.     It is undisputed that the rental unit is a house and that the application and the termination notice (the N12) that gave rise to the application characterized the rental unit as the “main floor” of the house.

 

3.     Mr, Mahavalirajan testified that this mischaracterization constitutes a fatal error in the originating documents, that the review request should be granted and that the application should be dismissed for this reason. Besides identifying the mischaracterization, he did not explain why, in the Tenant’s view, the mischaracterization constitutes a fatal error.

 

4.     Mr. Shabes opposed the review request, testifying that the unit was correctly identified as the “main floor” for the purpose of the application because the Tenant receives her mail on the main floor. I do not accept this argument given Mr. Shabes’ admission that the unit is, in fact, the entire house.

 

5.     However, it is undisputed that the Tenant was present at the original hearing in response to the Notice of Hearing. As such, I am satisfied that the mischaracterization of her address in the originating documents did not interfere with her opportunity to attend that hearing. It is also uncontested that the Tenant did not raise this issue at that hearing. While she testified that she did not do so because she was self-represented at the time, she did not explain whether or how her ability to raise this very basic and strictly factual concern may have been negatively impacted by the absence of counsel. As well, though she also testified to having been “confused” during the original hearing, other than by stating that at that time she was focused on finding alternate housing, she did not explain the basis or the extent of this confusion or how, if at all, it specifically impacted her ability to raise this issue.

 

6.     At the review hearing I orally ruled that while it is clear that the originating documents erred by wrongly styling the rental unit as consisting of the main floor of the house, only, there is an insufficient basis to conclude that this inaccuracy constitutes a serous error as this term is generally applied in the relevant Board jurisprudence. Specifically, I orally ruled that the Tenant did not demonstrate that she was deprived of the opportunity to raise this issue at the original hearing or that she could not have become aware of this issue in time for that hearing by the exercise of reasonable diligence. Moreover, the Board record does not show the absence of a rational connection between the evidence at the original hearing and the presiding adjudicator’s finding of fact with respect to the unit’s address. Especially given that the Board does not intend for parties to advance

their case in stages, in the circumstances of this case, I contemplated that the Tenant’s possible regret that she did not raise an issue at first instance that could reasonably have been advanced at that time might generally be an insufficient foundation for a review request. As such, I orally ruled against the review request.

 

7.     However, as set out in my interim order, on further deliberation after the hearing I also considered the negative implications of an order that wrongly identifies the address of the subject unit. For example, the Landlord’s ability to enforce eviction for the rental unit (i.e., the entire house) may be negatively impacted. As such, out of an abundance of caution I decided to reconvene the review hearing to continue the exploration of possible serious error or to otherwise address the issue of the wrongly named rental unit.

 

8.     At the reconvened review hearing, the parties mutually agreed that the Tenant vacated the unit on September 9, 2021, which obviated possible concerns with respect to the enforceability of the order. Nether party made submissions concerning their respective positions on the merits of the review request beyond those raised at the December 21, 2021, hearing. As such, as a practical matter, a determined that there was an insufficient basis to resile from my oral ruling and reasoning at the December 21, 2021 hearing, which is confirmed in this order.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     The request to review order TEL-17359-21 issued on August 4, 2021 is denied. The order is confirmed and remains unchanged.

 

2.     The interim order issued on October 8, 2021 is cancelled. The stay of order TEL-17359- 21 is lifted.
