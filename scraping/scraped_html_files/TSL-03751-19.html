Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TSL-03751-19

 

 

 


D.L.H.I. (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict C.D and W.E (the 'Tenants') because the Tenants did not pay the rent that the Tenants owe.

 

This application was heard in Toronto on May 6, 2019.

 

The Landlordâs legal representative, B.R, and one of the Tenants, CD, attended the hearing.

 

Determinations:

 

1.      The Tenants owe the Landlord $5,178.60. The Tenants have until May 24, 2019 to pay the Landlord the full amount owing to avoid eviction.

The Tenants owe the Landlord $5,178.60

2.      The Tenants have not paid the total rent the Tenants were required to pay for the period from February 1, 2019 to May 31, 2019.  Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination effective February 20, 2019.

3.      The Tenants paid $1,000.00 after the application was filed.

4.      The Tenant, CD, did not dispute that he owes the amount the Landlord is claiming. CD testified that he made one payment that is not reflected in the Landlordâs calculations - $900.00 paid by e-transfer on May 1, 2019. However, the Landlordâs legal representative advised that as of the hearing date this amount had not been received by the Landlord. The Landlordâs legal representative advised that if/when the Landlord receives this payment, the Landlord will adjust the amount the Tenant owes accordingly. Therefore, I am satisfied on a balance of probabilities, that the Tenants owe the Landlord $5,178.60, including arrears of rent to May 31, 2019 and the application filing fee.

5.      CD testified that the other named Tenant, WE, no longer lives in the rental unit and he moved out in December, 2017. However, WE did not present any evidence to corroborate his claim that either he or WE gave the Landlord notice that WE was moving out. Further, the Tenants are both listed on the tenancy agreement. Therefore, I find that they are jointly and severally liable for the rent. Accordingly, WE remains a party to this application.

6.      The Landlord collected a rent deposit of $1,350.00 from the Tenants and this deposit is still being held by the Landlord.

7.      Interest on the rent deposit is owing to the Tenants for the period from June 1, 2014 to February 20, 2019.

Section 82 issue

8.      CD testified that he has had problems with the heat during the tenancy. I asked CD if he was ready to proceed with a hearing of that issue and, specifically, I asked CD if he had proof of the temperature in the rental unit on any particular days. CD testified that he did not come to the hearing with proof of any temperature readings from the unit.

9.      I told CD that he did not have sufficient evidence to prove that the Landlord breached its maintenance obligations under section 20(1) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the âActâ). Specifically, without evidence of the temperature in the unit, I cannot determine that the Landlord violated Chapter 497 of the Municipal Code by failing to maintain a minimum temperature of 21 degrees Celsius in the unit between September 15 and June 1 of the following year. I note that this does not prevent the Tenant from filing his own application in respect of this issue.

Section 83 considerations

10.   CD asked for conditional relief from eviction in the form of a payment plan. However, I do not think that this relief is appropriate because I did not get a true sense of the Tenantâs financial circumstances such that I am confident that the Tenant can adhere to his proposed plan and/or afford to pay the rent going forward.

11.   Initially, the Tenant testified that he was living with the mother of his child and splitting the rent with her. The Tenant subsequently testified that he was living with a co-worker and splitting the rent with him. Neither of these individuals attended the hearing to attest to their living situation, whether they are contributing to the rent, their income and/or their other monthly expenses. Due to the contradictions in the Tenantâs evidence, I find that the information he provided about his own financial situation unreliable. Therefore, I am not convinced that the Tenant can afford the rent and/or the arrears.

12.   The Tenant asked that if I grant the application and order termination of the tenancy, that I postpone eviction for a month to give him time to find another place to live. However, the Tenant also testified that as of the hearing date he had made no efforts to find a new home. Therefore, I do not think that it would be appropriate to postpone eviction since the reason for the extension he was requesting was his own lack of diligence and his failure to make reasonable efforts to find a new home, despite having ample notice of the application and hearing.

13.   Further, without reliable evidence regarding his financial circumstances, I am not convinced that postponing eviction will not result in an increase in the arrears owing. Accordingly, I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would be unfair to grant relief from eviction pursuant to subsection 83(1) of the Act.

It is ordered that:

 

1.      Unless the Tenant voids the order as set out below, the tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenants is terminated.  The Tenants must move out of the rental unit on or before May 24, 2019.

2.      The Tenants shall pay to the Landlord $2,424.04*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to May 10, 2019, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlord owes on the rent deposit.

3.      The Tenants shall also pay to the Landlord $49.37 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting May 11, 2019 to the date the Tenants move out of the unit.

4.      The Tenants shall also pay to the Landlord $175.00 for the cost of filing the application.

5.      If the Tenants do not pay the Landlord the full amount owing* on or before May 24, 2019, the Tenants will start to owe interest.  This will be simple interest calculated from May 25, 2019 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

6.      If the unit is not vacated on or before May 24, 2019, then starting May 25, 2019, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

7.      Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord, on or after May 25, 2019.

8.      If, on or before May 24, 2019, the Tenants pay the amount of $5,178.60** to the Landlord or to the Board in trust, this order for eviction will be void.  This means that the tenancy would not be terminated and the Tenants could remain in the unit.  If this payment is not made in full and on time, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

9.      The Tenants may make a motion to the Board under subsection 74(11) of the Act to set aside this order if they pay the amount required under that subsection on or after May 25, 2019 but before the Sheriff gives vacant possession to the Landlord. The Tenants are only entitled to make this motion once during the period of the tenancy agreement with the Landlord.
