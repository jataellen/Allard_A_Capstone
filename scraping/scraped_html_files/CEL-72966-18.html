File Number: CEL-72966-18

 

Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

 

 

 


YKC (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict VT (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes.

 

This application was heard in Mississauga on February 21, 2018.

 

The Landlord together with her Paralegal, IB, and the Tenant attended the hearing.

 

Determinations and Reasons:

 

1.      The residential tenancy agreement for the rental unit, which is a condominium, was entered into on October 1, 2011 at a monthly rent of $1,290 payable by first day of each month. There is no dispute that the Landlordâs daughter is the person to whom all rent cheques were made payable by the Tenant. Nevertheless, the Landlord would appear to have agreed to assume the obligation of landlord under the tenancy agreement as she and her daughter were named without objection as Landlords in an application by the Tenant and she has filed this application as the sole landlord of the rental unit

2.      The Tenant has not paid the total rent the Tenant was required to pay for the period from January 1, 2017 to February 28, 2018.  Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination effective January 18, 2018.

3.      There is no dispute the arrears of rent as at the end of February 2018 are $2,631.60 plus the application fee of $175.00.

4.      The Landlord collected a rent deposit of $1,340 and also a key deposit of $150.00. It was not disputed that the latter was obtained contrary to subparagraph 134(1)(a) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the âRTAâ). These deposits totalling $1,440.00 from the Tenant are still being held by the Landlord.

5.      In addition, interest on the rent deposit and key deposit are owing to the Tenant for the period from August 19, 2011 to January 18, 2018.

 Missing rent cheques

6.      I find the Tenant has not established that she affixed an envelope containing two cheques on her door which the Landlord has collected but failed to deposit. Similarly, I am not satisfied the Tenant affixed her rent cheque for February to her door as she claimed.

7.      The Tenant stated she issued her cheque number 88 dated December 27, 2017 for $2 representing a shortfall with respect to the December rent and another cheque number 89 for the January rent in the amount of $1,315.80. She said she put both of them in an envelope which she affixed to the door of the rental unit, as was her practice from time to time. She said she was out of town from December 27, 2017 to January 3, 2018. When she returned, the envelope had been removed. She also said never in the six and one-half years she has lived in her building have her envelopes with rent cheques in them been removed from the door by an unauthorized person in this secure building. Indeed, she stated she left pay cheques for her bookkeeping clients on her door and never did they go missing.

8.      The Landlord claimed she did not see an envelope when she went to the rental unit on January 2, 2018. She said she sent a text message to the Tenant regarding the whereabouts of the December rent cheque and in that message she told the Tenant she would come by on January 4th at 7:00 p.m. to obtain it. She said she sent her husband to the rental unit on January 4th or 5th to retrieve the rent cheque but it was not on the door. He knocked on the door but there was no response. The Landlord said she received a text message from the Tenant stating her husband was never to knock on her door or else she would call the police or words to that effect.

9.      The Tenant explained the reason she sent the text message was because she is a single mother and she was away from the rental unit when the Landlordâs husband repeatedly knocked on her door for what her children, who were alone inside, estimated was for five minutes, and he tried to enter the locked door. Although such behaviour is not to be condoned, I am satisfied that it is more consistent with the Landlord not having obtained the rent cheque for December 2017 than it is that she retrieved it from the door of the rental unit but refused to cash it as the Tenant alleges.

10.   The Tenant is not willing to provide the Landlord with post-dated cheques for her rent and the concierge at her building is unwilling to hold her rent cheques for her Landlord.

Relief from eviction

11.   I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the RTA and find that it would be unfair to grant relief from eviction pursuant to subsection 83(1) of the Act. The Tenant has engaged in a pattern of failing to pay rent and is unwilling to provide the Landlord with post-dated cheques for future rent that comes due and owing.

12.   Section 83(3) of the Act requires the Landlord and Tenant Board (LTB) to deny an eviction application if the landlord is in serious breach of the landlordâs obligations under the Act, As discussed below, the Tenant asserts that the Landlord has been doing everything in her power to terminate the tenancy after the Tenant launched complaints against the Landlord, including not addressing repairs ordered by the Board and serving her with multiple notices of termination.  I find that the Landlord is not in serious breach of her obligations under the Act.

Failure to complete requires repairs

13.   The Tenantâs application against the Landlord resulted in an Order CET-68675-17 being issued by the LTB on October 19, 2017 requiring the Landlord and her daughter on or before November 30, 2017 to (a) repair or replace the dryer venting duct system, the kitchen cabinets and the areas of the hardwood floor that are curling; and (b) inspect and complete any necessary repairs/replacements/treatments for carpeting, kitchen faucet, mould/residue in the bathroom, laundry closet door, and address an insect infestation. The Tenant stated that other than repairing the laundry door, Order CET-68675-17 has been ignored by the Landlord and her daughter.

14.   With respect to non-compliance with Order CET-68675-17, the Landlordâs evidence was that because the dryer venting system is part of the common area, by virtue of one of the condominium by-laws by which the rental unit owner and occupants are bound, repairs to it have to be done by the condominium association and (or) the manager of the building. However, neither of those entities would accept responsibility. According to the Landlords, both of them are defendants in a Small Claims Court action to sort out responsibility for those repairs by which I took them to mean they are litigating this issue in a court of competent jurisdiction. According to the Landlords, as all of the other repairs and replacements relate to the dryer venting system, only the laundry closet door could be repaired, which was done.

15.   By letter dated January 1, 2018, the Tenant wrote to the Landlordâs daughter asking for receipts for all of her rent payments from the beginning of her tenancy; demanding that she be given a rent receipt monthly; requiring interest be paid on her last monthâs rent deposit; and asked for repayment of money paid as a key deposit. Although she noted that the picture window in the living room leaks when it rains, there was no mention of non-compliance with Order CET-68675-17 , though one would reasonably expect if the Tenant was questioning the capacity of the Landlord to comply, it would have featured in that demand letter containing complaints regarding other Landlord infractions of the tenancy relationship.

16.   It was also stated that on two occasions the Tenant refused to let a representative of the manager of the condominium building and the Landlord inspect her rental unit. The Tenant admitted to having received a text message that someone would be coming to inspect but she claimed she did not get the message regarding what I took to be the exact time they would be there and therefore she was out when they came.

17.   Without limiting the Landlordâs obligation to comply with Order CET-68675-17, I am satisfied the Landlord was unable to timely effect the outstanding repairs through no fault of her own and that the laundry closet door was timely repaired. Therefore, I do not find that the Landlord is in serious breach of her maintenance obligations.

Service of multiple N12 notices

18.   While the Tenant was pursuing her application at the Board, the Landlord served her with an N12 notice of termination for Landlordâs own use with a termination date of September 30, 2017. Subsequently, she was given another N12 calling for her tenancy to terminate on October 1, 2017. These two notices were served prior to the Order CET-68675-17.  .

19.   The Tenant continued to receive N12 notices for termination of her tenancy. One N12 was for termination effective on October 31, 2017 and then another for termination on January 18, 2018 and yet another was for termination to take effect on March 31, 2018 â in all, a total of three N12s were given to the Tenant after the Order CET-68675-17. In each instance, the stated purpose was that the Landlord requires the residence for her own use.

20.   While there is no definition of âharassmentâ in the RTA, it is generally held that âharassmentâ is a course of conduct that a reasonable person knows or ought to know would be unwelcome.

21.   At the hearing the Tenant relied on the series of N12 notices to show the Landlord was doing all she could to remove her from her rental unit. Although the Landlords did not provide any evidence of their intended personal use of the rental unit neither did the Tenant cross-examine the Landlords regarding the purpose for which the N12 notices were delivered. Although the series of N12 notices would appear to be excessive in that serving so many were probably not necessary, the evidence is insufficient to establish that these notices were improperly served on the Tenant to harass her.

It is ordered that:

 

1.      Unless the Tenant voids the order as set out below, the tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated.  The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before March 10, 2018.

2.      The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $892.41*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to February 27, 2018, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlord owes on the rent deposit.

3.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $43.26 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting February 28, 2018 to the date the Tenant moves out of the unit.

4.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $175.00 for the cost of filing the application.

5.      If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing* on or before March 10, 2018, the Tenant will start to owe interest.  This will be simple interest calculated from March 11, 2018 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

6.      If the unit is not vacated on or before March 10, 2018, then starting March 11, 2018, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

7.      Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord, on or after March 11, 2018.

8.      If the Tenant wishes to void this order and continue the tenancy, the Tenant must pay to the Landlord or to the Board in trust:
i)        $2,806.60 if the payment is made on or before February 28, 2018, or
ii)      $4,122.40 if the payment is made on or before March 10, 2018**.
If the Tenant does not make full payment in accordance with this paragraph and by the appropriate deadline, then the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

9.      The Tenant may make a motion to the Board under subsection 74(11) of the Act to set aside this order if they pay the amount required under that subsection on or after March 11, 2018 but before the Sheriff gives vacant possession to the Landlord. The Tenant is only entitled to make this motion once during the period of the tenancy agreement with the Landlord.
