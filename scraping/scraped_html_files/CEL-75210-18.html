File Number: CEL-75210-18

Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 


WK (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict PM (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes.

 

This application was heard in Mississauga on May 23, 2018.

 

The Landlord's Agent, WK and the Tenant attended the hearing.

 

Preliminary Issue:

 

The application was amended on consent to correct the name of the Landlord to CCC and LS who are the named Landlords on the written tenancy agreement. 

 

At the hearing the Tenant raised the following issues pursuant to section 82 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'):

a)   Light fixtures;

b)   Weak water pressure in shower;

c)   Floors unfinished;

d)   Entry door lock

e)   Basement entry hatch.

 

Determinations:

 

1.      The Tenant has not paid the total rent the Tenant was required to pay for the period from November 1, 2017 to May 31, 2018.  Because of the arrears, the Landlords served a Notice of Termination effective April 11, 2018.

2.      The monthly rent is $1,200.00.  The tenancy commenced as of October 1, 2017.

Readiness for occupancy clause

3.      The Tenant drafted the tenancy agreement which was entered into on September 28, 2017.

4.      The Tenant relies on a clause in the tenancy agreement subtitled âreadiness for occupancyâ for the position that his obligation to pay rent is fully discounted because the Landlords have failed to comply with all nine conditions in this clause.

5.      It is an established principle in our common law that ambiguities in a contract that affect the substantial rights and obligations of a party will be construed in favour of the party who did not draft the document (contra proferentem).  This general principle of construing ambiguities in a document against its drafter reflects the notion of comparative responsibility. Because the drafter could have avoided the ambiguous language, of the two parties, the drafter should bear the adverse construction (Conroy v. Jalaie 2010 ONSC 3626 at para. 19).

6.      An apparent ambiguity in a word in a contract does not require immediate resort to the contra proferentem rule. It is necessary to examine the contract in its entirety considering the context and purpose (Can. (A.G.) v. Chomcy 2002 CanLII 21639 ONCA (paras. 13 and 14).

7.      Section 4 of the Act states that a provision in a tenancy agreement that is inconsistent with this Act is void.

8.      I am not satisfied that the readiness for occupancy clause in the tenancy agreement is enforceable.

9.      The rental unit is a log cabin.

10.   Some of the clauses in the readiness for occupancy are ambiguous and unclear.  The clause requiring âall rooms to have minimal light fixturesâ is vague because minimal light fixtures is not clearly defined. The clause regarding the basement entrance is contradictory.  It states that this entrance is to both be replaced and repaired. Some of the clauses are statements and do not indicate that any work is required.  Specifically, âall plumbing and septic to be in full working orderâ, chimney and flu pipe to be in full working orderâ, âall exterior doors to be in full working order with locks and keysâ and âroof and shingles to be in good condition and not in need of repair or replacementâ.

11.   The occupancy clause is incomplete because it does not set out any method by which the completion of the listed conditions will be determined. 

12.   As a consequence and after examining the contract as a whole including the context, the Tenant bears responsibility for ambiguities and vagueness in the readiness for occupancy clause.

13.   The Tenant has been able to live in the unit since the tenancy commenced.  To accept the Tenantâs position that he is entitled to live in the unit rent free unless all of the conditions listed in the readiness for occupancy clause are complete leads to an unfair outcome.  Some of the clauses are merely statements which do not specify work required and some of the clauses are ambiguous.

14.   Furthermore, the Act sets out the responsibilities and obligations for both landlords and tenants.  Section 20 requires a landlord to provide a unit in a good state of repair and in compliance with health, safety, housing and maintenance standards.  Interpreting the readiness for occupancy clause as proposed by the Tenant is inconsistent with the Act and disregards the purpose of the Act which includes âto balance the rights and responsibilities of residential landlords and tenants and to provide for the adjudication of disputes for other process to informally resolve disputesâ. 

 

Section 82 issues

15.   The Tenantâs claim regarding repair required to light fixtures is dismissed.  The Tenant testified that the wires connecting the light fixtures in the unit are not placed behind the walls.  He did not provide any photos or any evidence of housing or maintenance standards that were contravened given that the unit is a log cabin. The Landlord's Agent testified that wiring is only placed underneath the log structure when the cabin is constructed because tampering with the logs post-construction affects the structural integrity.

16.   The Tenant testified that there is insufficient water pressure in the shower tap. He did not provide evidence that he reported this issue to the Landlord. The Landlord's Agent said he was willing to check the water pressure.  The Landlord breached its obligation under section 20 by failing to provide adequate water pressure in the bathroom shower.  As I am not satisfied the Tenant reported this issue to the Landlord, it is not appropriate to order an abatement.

17.   The Tenantâs claim that the floors in the unit require repair is dismissed. The Tenantâs evidence does not demonstrate that the flooring is âsub-floorâ.  The video displayed by the Tenant displayed plank wood flooring which appeared to be painted.  I did not observe any repair required although some of the video was quite dark.  The Tenantâs evidence is consistent with the testimony from the Landlord's Agent that the floor has been repainted.

18.   I am not satisfied that any repair is required to the entry door.  The Tenant said he could lock the door but it is difficult to open.  The Landlord's Agent said he is willing to inspect the door.

19.   The Landlord breached section 20 of the Act by failing to provide the basement hatch in a good state of repair.  It is undisputed that the hinge to the hatch requires repair.  The impact to the Tenant is minimal and it is not appropriate to order an abatement.

20.   The Tenantâs request for compensation regarding an agreement he had with the Landlord to do work in the unit is not considered in this order. Section 30 of the Act requires a finding that the Landlord breached section 20 of the Act to authorize any costs for repair to be paid to the Tenant.

Relief from eviction

21.   The Landlord collected a rent deposit of $1,200.00 from the Tenant and this deposit is still being held by the Landlord.

22.   Interest on the rent deposit is owing to the Tenant for the period from September 28, 2017 to April 11, 2018.

23.   The Tenant requested six months to repay any arrears owed.  The Landlord's Agent said the Landlord was willing to delay termination for 90 days.

24.   I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Act, and find that it would not be unfair to postpone the eviction until August 31, 2018 pursuant to subsection 83(1)(b) of the Act.  There are substantial arrears owed.  However, the delay allows the Tenant time to arrange to repay the arrears or to make arrangements to move.

It is ordered that:

 

1.      Unless the Tenant voids the order as set out below, the tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated.  The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before August 31, 2018.

2.      The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $7,431.57*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to June 6, 2018, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlord owes on the rent deposit.

3.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $39.45 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting June 5, 2018 to the date the Tenant moves out of the unit.

4.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $175.00 for the cost of filing the application.

5.      If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing* on or before June 15, 2018, the Tenant will start to owe interest.  This will be simple interest calculated from June 16, 2018 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

6.      If the unit is not vacated on or before August 31, 2018, then starting September 1, 2018, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

7.      Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord, on or after September 1, 2018.

8.      If the Tenant wishes to void this order and continue the tenancy, the Tenant must pay to the Landlord or to the Board in trust:
i)        $9,775.00 if the payment is made on or before June 30, 2018, or
ii)      $12,175.00 if the payment is made on or before August 31, 2018**.
If the Tenant does not make full payment in accordance with this paragraph and by the appropriate deadline, then the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

9.      The Tenant may make a motion to the Board under subsection 74(11) of the Act to set aside this order if they pay the amount required under that subsection on or after September 1, 2018 but before the Sheriff gives vacant possession to the Landlord. The Tenant is only entitled to make this motion once during the period of the tenancy agreement with the Landlord.

 

Section 82

10.  The Landlord shall inspect and provide any repair required to the water pressure in the shower tap and the basement hatch on or before June 30, 2018.
