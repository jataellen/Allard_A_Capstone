Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: SWL-11849-17

JP (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict RS (the 'Tenant') because the Landlord intends to demolish the rental unit.

 

This application was heard in Windsor on March 29, 2018.

 

MH, the Landlord’s Legal Representative, attended the hearing. The Tenant attended the hearing and declined the opportunity to speak with Duty Counsel prior to the hearing as he had previously been represented by counsel.

Determinations:

 

1.      The Landlord requires the rental unit to be vacated in order to demolish it. I am satisfied that the Landlord has taken all reasonable steps to obtain the necessary permits for this work.

 

2.      As set out in the attached reasons, I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would not be unfair to postpone the eviction until May 31, 2018, pursuant to subsection 83(1)(b) of the Act.

It is ordered that:

 

1.      The tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated, as of May 31, 2018. The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before May 31, 2018.

 

2.      If the unit is not vacated on or before May 31, 2018, then starting June 1, 2018, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

 

3.      Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord on or after June 1, 2018.

REASONS

Reasons to Order SWL-11849-17 issued on April 10, 2018 by Carol McDermott.

 

1.   The Tenant testified that he accepts that the Landlord does intend to demolish the premises but that he believes that that pursuant to section 83 the eviction should be delayed for a period of time.  He could not identify what an appropriate length of time would be.

 

2.   The Tenant testified that he has disorders and disabilities that make it difficult for him, in his own words, to fit into societal norms. 

 

3.   He said that when the Landlord gave given him the Notice to Terminate at End of the Term for Conversion, Demolition, or Repairs (N13) on September 13, 2017, with a termination date of January 31, 2018, the Landlord suggested that he start looking for new accommodation immediately because he was hoping to start demolition January 1, 2018.  However, he had been unable to focus on looking for alternate accommodation during the 6 month period between then and the hearing because he had been completely focused on preparing for the hearing.

 

4.   The Tenant produced letters that his former legal representative had obtained from his psychiatrist and the Learning Disability Association that express the opinion that the Tenant has multiple mental health disabilities which will be exacerbated by being evicted. 

 

5.   The Tenant testified that the four month notice period required for this ground of eviction might be adequate for other people but is not adequate for him because four months is like four days to him.

 

6.   The Tenant testified that the Landlord has a duty to accommodate him because of his disabilities and that the Landlord took no steps to fulfil that duty.  He said that he told the Landlord that is disabled and wanted the Landlord to work with him to negotiate a termination but the Landlord just told him to work it out for himself. 

 

7.   The Tenant testified that on December 14, 2017, the furnace stopped working and it took the Landlord 40 hours, during which time he texted him 15 times, to get it fixed.

 

8.   The Landlord’s Legal Representative testified that the letters the Tenant produced do not identify any time frame or any manner in which the Tenant’s transition could be carried out in a manner which would be less detrimental to the Tenant.  He said that the Tenant has had 6 months’ notice but has taken no steps to find alternate accommodation and the Landlord will be financially prejudiced if eviction is delayed because he cannot start the demolition until the premises are vacant.

 

RELIEF FROM EVICTION

 

9.   Section 83(2) of the Act provides that the Board must review the circumstances and consider whether or not it should exercise its powers to grant relief from eviction

 

Mandatory Relief from Eviction

 

10. Section 83(3) of the Act sets out specific circumstances where the Board must deny eviction, including where “the landlord is in serious breach of the landlord’s responsibilities under this Act or of any material covenant in the tenancy agreement”.

 

11. Although the Tenant did say that his furnace did not work for 40 hours, I am not satisfied that the Landlord was in serious breach of the Landlord’s obligations for the relatively short delay in making the repairs. Furthermore, any breach that may have existed was remedied almost four months ago.

 

12.  The Tenant also said that the Landlord made some threats to sue him or charge him a daily rate if he failed to vacate on the termination date.  I am not satisfied that a threat to take legal action or charge a per diem rent is a breach of any obligation of the Landlord.

 

Discretionary Relief from Eviction

.

13. The Board also has the discretion to grant relief from eviction in certain circumstances:

 

83.  (1)  Upon an application for an order evicting a tenant, the Board may, despite any other provision of this Act or the tenancy agreement,

 

(a)  refuse to grant the application unless satisfied, having regard to all the circumstances, that it would be unfair to refuse; or

 

(b) order that the enforcement of the eviction order be postponed for a period of time.

 

14. The Tenant asserted that he has a disability of which the Landlord was aware, and the Landlord took no steps to accommodate his disability. I will first consider the Landlord’s duties under the Ontario Human Rights Code (‘Code’) and then consider the appropriate remedy within the context of all the circumstances.

 

Landlord’s duty not to discriminate

 

15. Subsection 2(1) of the Code provides that everyone has the right to equal treatment with respect to housing, without discrimination because of race, ancestry, place of origin, colour, ethnic origin, citizenship, creed (religion), sex (including pregnancy, gender identity), sexual orientation, age, marital status, family status, disability, or the receipt of public assistance. "Disability" is defined by subsection 10(1) to include both physical conditions and mental disorders.

 

16. The Board’s Interpretation Guideline 17 addresses Code issues raised during the course of an eviction proceeding. It is not binding upon me but it provides a useful framework for analyzing this issue:

 

a.   Is the Tenant protected under section 2(1) of the Code?

b.   Has the tenant been discriminated against contrary to the Code?

c.   Has the Landlord accommodated the Tenant to the point of undue hardship?

 

a).Is the Tenant protected under section 2(1) of the Code?

 

17. The letters from the Tenant’s psychiatrist and the Learning Disability Association unequivocally describe the Tenant as significantly disabled and the Landlord’s Legal Representative did not deny that the Landlord was aware of the Tenant’s disability.

 

18. I am satisfied therefore that the Tenant has a disability within the definition of s.10 of the Code which includes a condition of mental impairment or developmental disability. Therefore, he is protected by the Code.

 

b.) Has the Tenant been discriminated against contrary to the Code?

 

19. There was no allegation that the Landlord directly discriminated against the Tenant because of his disability.  However, the Tenant argued that expecting him to vacate with only four months’ notice was tantamount to adverse effect discrimination because it would take him much longer than the four months’ in which an average tenant might be expected to find new accommodation.

 

20. In determining whether the Landlord has discriminated against the Tenant contrary to the Code because he wants to demolish the rental unit I am guided by the following excerpt from Guideline 17:

 

………..the discrimination may be indirect, such as a rule or standard applied by the landlord that appears neutral but has the effect of discriminating against the tenant because the tenant belongs to one or more of the categories contained in subsection 2(1) of the Code. For example, a requirement that all tenants remove their belongings from cupboards and drawers to enable the landlord to fumigate all of the rental units may discriminate against tenants who are unable to perform such tasks because of a physical disability. If the landlord is seeking to evict a disabled tenant for failing to comply with this requirement, the Member would take such discrimination into account in applying the Code.

The Divisional Court in Connelly v. Mary Lambert Swale Non-Profit Homes6 suggested that where the landlord's application for eviction is based on the tenant's conduct, the Member must also consider whether the conduct has been directly caused by the tenant's disability.

If the grounds for eviction contained in the landlord's application are unrelated to the tenant's membership within one or more of the categories contained in subsection 2(1) of the Code, the Member cannot find that the tenant has been discriminated against by the landlord. For example, if the landlord has filed an application for termination of the tenancy based on arrears of rent, the fact that the tenant has a hearing impairment is likely not relevant to the Member's determination of the merits of the landlord's application. However, even if the Member finds that the landlord's application is unrelated to the tenant's membership within one or more of the categories contained in subsection 2(1) of the Code, the Member may still take all of the tenant's circumstances into account when considering relief from eviction pursuant to section 83 of the RTA. Both scenarios are discussed below. [emphasis added]

21. In this case, the ground of termination, demolition of rental unit, is unrelated to the Tenant’s Code ground. Therefore, I am not satisfied that the Landlord is discriminating against the Tenant by pursing his statutory right to seek termination of the tenancy in order to demolish the rental unit. As a result, it is unnecessary for me to consider the issue of accommodation up to the point of undue hardship.

 

22. However, this does not mean that the Tenant’s disability is not relevant or cannot be considered by the Board. On the contrary, the Board must still consider all the circumstances in determining whether to exercise the discretion to delay or deny the eviction under section 83. The relevant circumstances here include the fact that it may take this Tenant longer than other tenants to secure alternate accommodation due to his disability.

 

23. The Tenant did not ask that the eviction be denied; he recognized that the Landlord intends on demolishing the premises.  Instead, he asked for an extension of time, but could not identify an appropriate amount of time.

 

24. The Landlord’s Legal Representative agreed to delay the eviction until May 31, 2018, the date that the Landlord had already negotiated with the Tenant’s grandmother who lives in another unit in the building. The Tenant said that May 31, 2018, was much too soon.

 

25. The letters from the Tenant’s psychiatrist and Learning Disability Association did not identify a time frame and suggested that no amount of delay would prevent the Tenant from suffering significant exacerbation of his symptoms.

 

26. The Landlord’s Legal Representative stated that the Landlord will suffer significant financial consequences if the demolition is delayed.

 

27. I am satisfied that delaying the eviction until May 31, 2018, will give the Tenant some time to find new accommodation without prejudicing the Landlord.  This further delay will result in the Tenant receiving almost 7 months’ notice, which is about three months longer than the notice period required by section 50(2) of the Act.  I understand that the Tenant was not able to focus on finding new accommodation before this time because he believed that he needed to focus on preparing for the hearing. My hope is that once the Tenant receives this order, he will have the certainty he requires because he will have the specific date by which he must vacate.  Although there is no doubt that moving will be difficult for the Tenant, he has demonstrated an ability to engage the assistance of support services when necessary.  Now that there is a fixed termination date, support services may be more able to assist him in making plans to relocate.
