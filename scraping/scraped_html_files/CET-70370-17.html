CET-70509-17

Order under Sections 30, 31 and 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

AM (the 'Tenant') applied for an order determining that BA (the 'Landlord') failed to meet the Landlord's maintenance obligations under the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act') or failed to comply with health, safety, housing or maintenance standards (CET-70370-17).

 

The Tenant also applied for an order determining that the Landlord or the Landlord's agent harassed, obstructed, coerced, threatened or interfered with the Tenant, substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or residential complex by the Tenant or by a member of the Tenant's household and withheld or deliberately interfered with the reasonable supply of a vital service that the Landlords are obligated to supply under the tenancy agreement (CET-70509-17).

 

The Landlord applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict the Tenant because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes (CEL-71395-17).

 

These applications were heard together in Barrie on February 9, 2018.

 

The Tenant and the Landlordâs Agent, AB, attended the hearing.

 

The following witnesses testified at the hearing:

BB (former Landlordâs assistant) on behalf of the Tenant

LM (upper tenant) on behalf of the Tenant

RH on behalf of the Landlord

LD (Landlordâs Service Manager) on behalf of the Landlord

 

 

Determinations and reasons:

 

1.      The Landlord is amended as stated in the written tenancy agreement.

Regarding CET-70370-17

 

2.      The issues in this application are whether the Landlord has maintained the rental unit in a good state of repair and fit for habitation in accordance with section 20 of the Act.  The Tenant stated that there were numerous on-going issues, however only the issues in the application can be considered at this hearing.   In the application, the Tenant alleged the following:

a)   Unfinished renovations including: missing drywall, incomplete flooring, bathroom plumbing not completed;

b)   No heat;

c)   Water leak/penetration; and

d)   Mold.

3.      Subsection 20(1) of the Act states the following:

A landlord is responsible for providing and maintaining a residential complex, including the rental units in it, in a good state of repair and fit for habitation and for complying with health, safety, housing and maintenance standards.

4.      In the recent decision in Onyskiw v. CJM Property Management, 2016 ONCA 477 (Onyskiw), the Court of Appeal for Ontario determined that a contextual approach should be adopted when considering a landlordâs potential breach of subsection 20(1) of the Act and a breach will not be found if the landlordâs response to a maintenance issue was reasonable in the circumstances.

5.      The Tenant viewed the rental unit which was under renovation on August 17, 2017.  At that time the parties entered into a tenancy agreement for occupation as of September 1, 2017.  The monthly rent for the unit is $1,500.00.  This application was filed on October 3, 2017.

6.      The Tenant stated that on September 1, 2017, she was asked to move in later in the day to allow the workers to finish the renovations.  Upon moving into the rental unit, the Tenant asserted that the unit was not usable.  The drywall was incomplete; mudding, sanding and painting was still to be done.  There was missing flooring in the hallway and kitchen.  There was no working toilet.  There were issues with both water and hot water in the bathroom.  According to the Tenant, the unit was not livable for approximately three weeks.   The Tenant submitted and relied on photographs taken by herself regarding the condition of the unit in the month of September 2017.

7.      According to the Tenant, shortly after the completion of the unit, on September 26, 2017, she discovered a water under one of her daughterâs beds.  She had removed a suitcase being stored under the bed and it was wet and moldy.  She then also discovered a puddle of water on the floor in the master bedroom.  The Tenant submitted that three mattresses which were on the floor in the bedrooms were damaged as a result of exposure to water and mold.

8.      There was no dispute that the Tenant contacted the Landlordâs office regarding the issue of the water pooling.  According to the Tenant, once the sub-pump was turned on there were no further issues of water pooling.  Although, there was evidence of water damage in the corner of her childrenâs bedroom.  Someone from the Landlordâs office attended and pulled away the trim and cut out drywall from this area.  The Tenant stated that this area was not repaired until just before Christmas.

9.      According to the Tenant, the heat at the rental unit was non-existent until October 16, 2017 when a working thermostat was installed.     However, after this time she continued to have periodic issues with insufficient heat.  Each time there was an issue with the furnace, the Tenant reported the issue and the Landlordâs contractor attended.  The Tenant also confirmed that she was given space heaters to use from the Landlordâs contractor.

10.   The Tenantâs witness and the Landlordâs former assistant, BB, testified that she was present on the Tenantâs move in date of September 1, 2017.  She confirmed that the renovations at the rental unit were not completed at that time.  She stated that drywall was still being installed and the bathroom was not finished.  She also testified that she was the one who removed the trim in the corner of the bedroom after the Tenant reported a leak.  She said the area was mushy and just fell apart when touched. 

11.   The Tenantâs witness, the upper unit tenant, LM, testified that there were issues with heat at the residential complex and it was constantly having to be turned up or down during this period of time. 

12.   AB acknowledged that the premises were under renovation prior to the Tenant taking possession.  He stated that the unit was available for occupancy as of September 1, 2017.  In support of this assertion, a copy of the occupancy permit and inspection report provided by the Town of Midland issued September 1, 2017 was submitted into evidence.  In response to the Tenantâs photographs, AB submitted that the Tenant had access to the rental unit prior to taking occupancy and believed that some of the pictures submitted by the Tenant were taken prior to September 1, 2017. 

13.   AB also acknowledged that there was an issue with water penetration into the unit.   He attended the unit to view the Tenantâs alleged damage to property.  During his visit, the Tenant did not show him the damaged mattresses or any other items.  He did see the area of the bedroom where some drywall and trim was removed.  He confirmed that area would be fixed once the exterior work regarding the water penetration was completed.

14.   The Landlordâs property manager as of mid-October 2017, RH, testified that he also attended the rental unit to view the Tenantâs damaged property.  He confirmed that there were three mattresses stacked in one of the rooms, he could not determine their age however did notice that there was some pilling on the side of one of the mattresses.  He further testified that there was no evidence of water damage/staining in the master bedroom. He confirmed that the corner area of the other bedroom still had exposed drywall. 

15.   RH acknowledge an issue with lack of heat reported by the Tenant.  He recalled at least two other service requests regarding this issue.   He had a heating company attend and address this issue each time service was requested.

16.   The Landlordâs current service manager, LD, testified that he attended the rental unit approximately 6-7 times and had discussions with the Tenant regarding her issues.  He testified that there are currently no outstanding maintenance issues at the rental unit.  He submitted that the drywall and trim in the corner of the bedroom was addressed within one to two weeks of the repairs to the exterior being completed.  He could not state a date for sure.  He also acknowledged that there were additional drywall repairs at the rental unit related to a water leak from the furnace line.  He believed the area of the living room was affected for this repair.  In any event, he stated that both repairs were just cosmetic, nothing that would prevent the Tenant from living in the rental unit.

17.   LD further acknowledged there were heating issues at the rental unit. He was not aware of any current heating issue.  At the hearing, the Tenant stated that there was an issue the week prior, however LD confirmed under cross-examination that he was not made aware of that issue.   LD testified that they responded to the Tenantâs reported issues in a reasonable time frame.  LD further confirmed that the Tenant was supplied with secondary heating units.

18.   Based on the evidence before me, I am satisfied that the Landlord breach section 20 of the Act as the rental unit was in disrepair during the month of September 2017.  In this regard, I preferred the testimony of the Tenantâs witness, BB, who was on site on the Tenantâs move in date.  While although, I am satisfied that the witness, BB, is emotional towards her former employer, I found her testimony regarding this issue consistent, credible and corroborated the Tenantâs evidence.  In any event, other than the inspection reports and occupancy permit submitted, AB and the Landlordâs other witnesses did not provide direct testimony as to the condition of the rental unit in September 2017. 

19.   As a result of the disrepair in the rental unit at this time the Tenantâs belongings were piled in the middle of the rooms covered in plastic awaiting the completion of drywall and painting.  The Tenantâs evidence was also uncontested as to the condition of the bathroom in September 2017, specifically having no working toilet for a few days, no hot water for a number of days, and no use of the shower for approximately two weeks. 

20.   The Tenant requested an abatement of rent totalling $4,500.00 equal to three monthâs rent for all the issues in both applications.  With respect to this issue, I find a 50% abatement for three weeks, being $562.50 ($1,500.00 x 0.75 x 0.50) to be more appropriate in these circumstances.

21.   The Tenantâs uncontested evidence that all the repair issues related to this disrepair were completed within three weeks of the Tenant moving into the unit.  A specific timeline for the completion of each of the repairs was not provided, however, the Tenant confirmed that some issues were completed prior to three weeks such as the toilet and shower.  Therefore, I am satisfied that 50% abatement for this period is appropriate given that only the Tenant was able to occupy the unit during this time, as her children were sent to live with their father due to the condition of the unit.   However, since the Tenant continued to live at the unit during this time, a full abatement of rent as requested was not warranted.

22.   Based on the evidence before me I am not satisfied that the Landlord breached section 20 with respect to the issues related to heating.  There was no dispute that the Landlord responded immediately to each of the Tenantâs complaints.  As well, the Tenant was able to contact the Landlordâs contractor directly after hours to report any problems with the heat, which the Tenant had done on at least one occasion.  She also confirmed that the contractor regularly attended the unit after she reported an issue.  In accordance with Onyskiw, a breach will not be found if the Landlordâs response was reasonable in the circumstances.  Not only did the Landlordâs contractor address the heating issues consistently and promptly, they also provided the Tenant with a secondary heating source, namely space heaters.

23.   Based on the evidence before me I am satisfied the Landlord breached section 20 of the Act due to the disrepair of the drywall and trim in one area of the bedroom related to the water penetration.  This area was not repaired by the Landlord for approximately two months. 

24.   The Tenant requested an abatement of rent totalling $4,500.00 equal to three monthâs rent for all the issues in both applications.  With respect to this issue, I find a 5% abatement for two months, being $150.00 ($1,500.00 x 0.05 x 2) to be more appropriate in these circumstances.  The Tenant stated that this area was not repaired for approximately two months. The Landlordâs service manager disagreed and found that timeframe to be too lengthy but could not confirm a completion date.  Therefore, I preferred the evidence of the Tenant. I find a 5% abatement to be appropriate as I am satisfied that having this area in disrepair had a minimal impact on the Tenantâs ability to occupy the premises.  This was a small area in the corner of one of the bedrooms.  A full abatement of rent for two months is not warranted.

25.   With respect to the water penetration and mold allegations.  It was uncontested that the Landlord acted immediately and effectively to address the water penetration issue.  Other than this one time of water pooling in the bedrooms, there was no evidence provided by the Tenant that the issue was on-going.  Therefore, in accordance with Onyskiw, I am satisfied that the Landlord did not breach section 20 of the Act as he acted reasonably with respect to the water penetration at the rental unit. 

26.   There was no evidence provided that the Landlord was aware of an issue with water penetration prior to the Tenant moving into the rental unit.  The Landlord had recently purchased this residential complex and renovated the interior.  This issue of water penetration was unknown until it occurred.   Since, I am not satisfied the Landlord breached section 20 of the Act, consequently, I am not considering the Tenantâs request for costs to replace property that was damaged because the Landlord did not maintain the rental unit or residential complex.

27.   In any event, the Tenant did not substantiate the replacement costs requested.  She did not provide any receipts for the items claimed.  She simply estimated their costs based on similar items found on the internet.  As well, the Tenant confirmed that she received replacement mattresses at no cost.  The amount for replacing three mattresses representing the majority of the total amount requested by the Tenant.  

28.   The evidence before me was insufficient to find that there is a mold issue in the rental unit.  None of the witnesses who attended the rental unit testified to the presence of mold.  And the Tenant only referred to mold regarding her property damage, which she confirmed she discarded.

29.   The total abatement of rent with respect to this application is $712.50 for the period of September 1, 2017 to December 31, 2017 as stated above.

Regarding CET-70509-17

30.   The issue in this application is whether the Landlord harassed and/or threatened the Tenant, substantially interfered with the Tenantâs reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit and withheld or deliberately interfered with the reasonable supply of a vital service, namely heat, water and hot water that the Landlords are obligated to supply under the tenancy agreement.  Some of the allegations regarding substantial interference with the Tenantâs reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit related to the Landlordâs failure to maintain the rental unit.  Those issues were addressed in application CET-70370-17 above and any remedy given considered whether or not substantial interference with the reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit was affected.

31.   Subsection 21(1) of the Act states the following:

A landlord shall not at any time during a tenantâs occupancy of a rental unit and before the day on which an order evicting the tenant is executed, withhold the reasonable supply of any vital service, care service or food that it is the landlordâs obligation to supply under the tenancy agreement or deliberately interfere with the reasonable supply of any vital service, care service or food.

32.   Subsection 2(1) of the Act describes a vital service:

means hot or cold water, fuel, electricity, gas or, during the part of each year prescribed by the regulations, heat.

33.   Section 22 of the Act states the following:

A landlord shall not at any time during a tenantâs occupancy of a rental unit and before the day on which an order evicting the tenant is executed substantially interfere with the reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or the residential complex in which it is located for all usual purposes by a tenant or members of his or her household. 

 

34.   Section 23 of the Act states the following:

A landlord shall not harass, obstruct, coerce, threaten or interfere with a tenant. 

35.   There was no dispute that the Tenantâs issues with water/hot water at the commencement of the tenancy and heating during the tenancy were the result of disrepairs as pleaded and found in application CET-70370-17.  Given this, as required by subsection 21(1) of the Act, I am not satisfied that the Landlord withheld or deliberately interfered with these vital services.

36.   The Tenant alleged that the Landlord was responsible for a break-in at her unit on September 24, 2017 as the key to the Landlordâs lock box had gone missing.  The Tenant also alleged that the person who broke into the unit was a former contractor for the Landlord.  The Tenantâs witness, LM, testified she heard banging in the Tenantâs unit and the Tenantâs dog barking for long period of time on September 23, 2017.

37.   The Tenant stated that she came home late on September 23, 2017 to find her animals uneasy and the lights on in her rental unit.  She contacted the police to report a possible break-in according to the police report submitted into evidence.  The Tenant confirmed that nothing was missing or disturbed. 

38.   The evidence before me was insufficient to find that the Landlord or a contractor/ employee of the Landlord was responsible for an alleged break in at the rental unit.  The evidence regarding this incident was inconsistent.  At the hearing, the Tenant testified that she came home to find her lights on and her animals uneasy.  The police report stated that the Tenant came home to also find her door unlocked.  This was not stated at the hearing.  As well, the Tenantâs witness, BB, testified that the Tenant had provided her with a description of a person leaving the premises that night.   The Tenant did not testify that she had seen a person leaving her unit nor was this reported to the police.

39.   The Tenant also contacted the police on October 3, 2017 to report an unwanted person at her rental unit.  According to the Tenant, she discovered âB,â who she believed was not supposed to be on the property, working on the exterior of the premises.  She stated that she received an email from the Landlord advising of a property management change and that the old property management company personnel should not be at the premises. The Tenantâs witnesses, BB and LM, also confirmed the existence of this email.  However, a copy of the email was not submitted into evidence.

40.   The Landlordâs company divided the duties of construction and property maintenance to two different companies, one of which is owned and operated directly by the Landlordâs company.  I find it more likely than not that the Tenant and her witnesses confused the message in the email. 

41.   In any event, I am not satisfied that this person working on the exterior of the rental unit substantially interfered with the Tenantâs reasonable enjoyment of the premises.  The police confirmed with âBâ that he was hired by the Landlord to work on the siding, yet the Tenant insisted that he leave the premises which he did.  I find it more likely than not that the Tenant interfered with the Landlordâs ability to complete repairs to the exterior of the unit by forcing this authorized person to leave.  The Tenant confirmed that the person was not in her rental unit. 

42.   The Tenant alleged that when AB attended her rental unit on September 29, 2017, he was unprofessional and threatened her with an eviction.  She stated that AB clearly just wanted to get this meeting at the rental unit âdone and over with.â  According to her, he kept stating âwhatâs the issueâ even though she directed him to the issue right in front of him.  Then he left abruptly and told her to deal with the property manager.  She also stated that he threatened to evict her.  

43.   The Tenantâs witness, BB, testified that AB had asked if she would attend with him on this date, when she responded âno,â he screamed at her to âget the Tenant out.â  BB also confirmed that during her employment with the Landlord that AB had only attended one other rental unit. 

44.   AB contended that he doesnât attend rental units, this is the property managerâs role.  However, since the Tenant was so unhappy, he felt he should attend personally to inspect the unit and review the Tenantâs issues.  While at the unit, AB stated that the Tenant maintained there was water damage in the other bedroom (master), he asked her to show him and she never did. 

45.   According to AB, the Tenant was having trouble speaking as she was very emotional.  When she started texting on her phone, he decided to leave.  She came out in tears asking him to come back into the unit.  He returned and viewed the corner area of the one bedroom that had some drywall and trim removed because of the water penetration.  He confirmed with her that they are completing a foundation repair and then would repair this area.  He will let the property management know to follow up. 

46.   AB denied that he threatened eviction.  He stated that he told her to pay her rent into the Board in trust and she should call property standards.  The Landlordâs witness, RH, also testified the Tenant regularly challenged him on everything he was saying or doing during their interactions. 

47.   The evidence before me was insufficient to find that the Landlord harassed or threatened the Tenant with an eviction on September 29, 2017.  I find it more likely than not that the parties were simply at odds with respect to the issues at the rental unit. I also find it more likely than not that the Tenant advised AB in this encounter that she would be filing an application with the Board given the time of her file date which would lead AB to advise her to pay her rent into the Board to avoid an eviction.

48.   The Tenant has failed to prove on a balance of probabilities regarding the issues in this application that the Landlord or the Landlordâs agent harassed and/or threatened her or substantially interfered with her reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or that of another family member.  Consequently, the application is dismissed.

Regarding CEL-71395-17

49.   The Tenant did not dispute the rent arrears of $7,500.00 owing to February 28, 2018 as claimed by the Landlord.

50.   The Tenant has not paid the total rent the Tenant was required to pay for the period from October 1, 2017 to February 28, 2018.  Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination effective November 14, 2017.

51.   The Landlord collected a rent deposit of $1,500.00 from the Tenant and this deposit is still being held by the Landlord.

52.   Interest on the rent deposit is owing to the Tenant for the period from September 1, 2017 to November 14, 2017.

53.   The Tenant would like to continue the tenancy.  She believed that there would be no rent arrears owing given the issues in her application.  However, if rent arrears are found to be owing she stated that she would need approximately one year to pay off the outstanding amount.   She is on ODSP and receives CTC for her three children.  She currently does not receive any support payments for the children.

54.   AB submitted that the rent arrears are substantial.  The Tenant has not made any payments towards the rent since paying first and last monthâs rent at the commencement of this tenancy.   He submitted that he has previously attempted to negotiate rental payments with her to no avail. 

55.   Having considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Act, I find that it would be unfair to grant relief from eviction pursuant to subsection 83(1) of the Act. 

56.   This is a young tenancy, the Tenant only moved into the rental unit as of September 1, 2017 and did not pay any rent since that time.  The rent arrears are substantial, I am not satisfied that there is a reasonable expectation that the Tenant can pay the rent arrears in a reasonable time frame.  A one year repayment plan is not reasonable given that the Tenant has only lived in the rental unit for six months.

It is ordered that:

 

1.      Application CET-70509-17 is dismissed.

2.      Unless the Tenant voids the order as set out below, the tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated.  The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before March 4, 2018.

3.      The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $4,855.97*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to February 21, 2018, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlord owes on the rent deposit and less the amount of the abatement of $712.50, the Landlord owes the Tenant.

4.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $49.32 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting February 22, 2018 to the date the Tenant moves out of the unit.

5.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $175.00 for the cost of filing the application.

6.      If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing* on or before March 4, 2018, the Tenant will start to owe interest.  This will be simple interest calculated from March 5, 2018 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

7.      If the unit is not vacated on or before March 4, 2018, then starting March 5, 2018, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

8.      Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord, on or after March 5, 2018.

9.      If the Tenant wishes to void this order and continue the tenancy, the Tenant must pay to the Landlord or to the Board in trust:
i)        $6,962.50 if the payment is made on or before February 28, 2018, or
ii)      $8,462.50 if the payment is made on or before March 4, 2018**.
If the Tenant does not make full payment in accordance with this paragraph and by the appropriate deadline, then the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

10.   The Tenant may make a motion to the Board under subsection 74(11) of the Act to set aside this order if they pay the amount required under that subsection on or after March 5, 2018 but before the Sheriff gives vacant possession to the Landlord. The Tenant is only entitled to make this motion once during the period of the tenancy agreement with the Landlord.
