Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: CEL-63931-17

 


In the matter of:

 

 

 

 

 

Between:

M S

Landlord

 

 

 

 

and

 

 

 

 

 

M F

Tenant

 

 

 

 

M S (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict M F (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes (L1 Application).

 

The Landlord also applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict the Tenant because the Tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or someone the Tenant permitted in the residential complex has wilfully or negligently caused undue damage to the premises; because the Tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or someone the Tenant permitted in the residential complex has substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment or lawful right, privilege or interest of the Landlord or another tenant; and because the Landlord requires possession of the rental unit for the purpose of residential occupation. The Landlord has also applied for an order requiring the Tenant to compensate the Landlord for the damage. The Landlord also claimed compensation for each day the Tenant remained in the unit after the termination date (L2 Application).

 

This application was heard in Port Elgin on February 28, 2017.

 

The Landlord and the Tenant attended the hearing.  The Tenant was assisted by a friend, A. P.

 

Determinations and Reasons:

 

Regarding L1 Application (rent arrears)

 

1.      The monthly rent is $1,000.00.

2.      The Tenant has not paid the total rent the Tenant was required to pay for the period from December 1, 2016 to February 28, 2017.  Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination effective December 16, 2016.

Regarding L2 Application (N5 Notice)

 

3.      On December 2, 2016, the Landlord served the Tenant with an N5 Notice in accordance with sub sections 64(1) and 62(1) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’). The N5 Notice has a termination date of December 22, 2016 and alleges that:

a)   The Tenant is harassing and substantially interfering with the reasonable enjoyment of the Landlord through numerous phone calls, voicemails and unannounced visits to the Landlord’s home;

b)   The Tenant damaged the front room ceiling, walls and floor due to “brewing kegs of homemade wine” which exploded all over the room. The Landlord claimed  $320.00 to repair the alleged property damage.

4.      The N5 Notice sets out the dates, times and specific allegations against the Tenant.

5.      Because this is a first N5 Notice, the Tenant has an opportunity to void the notice in accordance with sub section 64(3) of the Act by correcting the identified problems within seven days of the date the notice was served. In this case, the seven day period begins the day following service of the N5 Notice, from December 3, 2016 to December 9, 2016. The Landlord confirmed that the N5 Notice was not voided and that the Tenant did not pay $320.00 for the alleged damages claimed in the N5 notice.

6.      The Landlord testified:

a)   The Tenant is her biological father;

b)   The Tenant was brewing wine in the front room of the home and the red wine “exploded” and stained the ceiling, walls and floor. The Landlord submitted photos of the front room;

c)   The Landlord gave notice to enter to replace the battery for the furnace and the Tenant denied entry and police were called;

d)   The Tenant is not co-operative and verbally abusive such that the Landlord went to the police.  He withheld rent in return for personal favours, forced his way in and used the Landlord’s cottage, requested help in paying bills and constantly calls the Landlord with unreasonable requests.  He does not respect boundaries. He caused a “scene” when the roof was re-shingled and damaged the workers’ truck. When he comes to her house, he throws temper tantrums.

7.      The Tenant admitted responsibility for the red wine stains on the ceiling of the front room. He testified that the container of red wine was corked and the cork popped causing wine to spray on the ceiling.  He said he paid his grandson to clean the stains.

Wilful or negligent damage – front room

 

8.      Section 62(1) of the Act states that a landlord may give a tenant notice of termination if the tenant wilfully or negligently causes undue damage to the rental unit.

9.      I am not satisfied that the conduct of the Tenant was negligent or that the wine stains on the ceiling is representative of undue damage. 

10.   “Conduct is negligent if there is a risk of damage which a reasonable person would take reasonable precautions against” (Bolton v. Stone [1951] A.C. 850).  Further, “If a person reasonably, albeit mistakenly, believes that there is no risk then the conduct is not negligent” (Beutler v. Beutler [1983] O.J. No. 505 at para. 148).

11.   Wilful conduct means with intent or deliberate.

12.   The Tenant placed a cork in the container of wine which shows that he took reasonable precautions against spillage.  The fact that the cork popped and caused the wine to spray the ceiling and spill onto the floor was not reasonably foreseeable.  As a consequence, the Tenant’s conduct was not negligent.  The facts do not demonstrate any intent by the Tenant to spray wine on the ceiling and floor.

13.   I am not satisfied that the stains on the ceiling and the faint spot on the floor are representative of undue damage.  Undue suggests some damage is acceptable.  The stains on the ceiling and floor are akin to normal wear and tear. 

14.   As the Landlord has not met the test under section 62(1), this claim is dismissed. In any event, the Landlord did not substantiate the remedy claimed of $320.00 with a quote or other similar evidence.   

15.   The Landlord’s claim regarding damage caused by hooks on a ceiling beam is dismissed because the Landlord did not explain how the hooks caused undue damage.

Substantial interference

 

16.   Asking someone to read your mail to them, show them how to pay bills, smoke fish for them and not respecting boundaries indicates that the Landlord’s allegations are representative of personal issues between members of a family rather than those between a Landlord and Tenant.  This does not represent substantial interference of reasonable enjoyment of the residential complex by the Landlord or harassment and this claim is dismissed.

17.   The issues claimed by the Landlord that occurred at her home did not occur at the residential complex and therefore do not substantially interfere with her reasonable enjoyment of the residential complex. This claim is dismissed.

18.   The Landlord’s claim that the Tenant damaged the roofing worker’s truck is dismissed. The Landlord did not provide sufficient details regarding this claim or any supporting evidence regarding this alleged damage.

19.   The Landlord did not provide sufficient details such as dates and times, to support a finding of harassment or substantial interference regarding her complaints that the Tenant demanded she come over to change a light bulb, wanted a new kitchen tap and made numerous calls regarding the thermostat “flashing”.  These claims are dismissed.

20.   The Landlord failed to substantiate her claim regarding denial of entry to the rental unit to replace the battery for the furnace on December 1, 2016.  A notice to enter was not provided and no details regarding police involvement were submitted.  In any event, a single event is not indicative of substantial interference. This claim is dismissed. 

21.   The Landlord led evidence that the Tenant failed to maintain the unit in a state of ordinary cleanliness.  However, these issues were not raised in the N5 Notices which formed the basis of the L2 Application and are not relevant to a finding of a substantial interference or wilful or negligent damage.

Regarding L2 Application (N12 Notice – Landlord own use)

 

22.   The Landlord served the Tenant with an N12 notice of termination effective January 31, 2017 claiming the Landlord intended to move into the rental unit.

23.   The Landlord testified that she intends to move into the rental unit to use the unit for her home based business and for other business purposes.

24.   Subsection 48(1) of the Act states:

A landlord may, by notice, terminate a tenancy if the landlord in good faith requires possession of the rental unit for the purpose of residential occupation by,

(a) the landlord;

(b) the landlord’s spouse;

(c) a child or parent of the landlord or the landlord’s spouse; or

(d) a person who provides or will provide care services to the landlord, the landlord’s spouse, or a child or parent of the landlord or the landlord’s spouse, if the person receiving the care services resides or will reside in the building, related group of buildings, mobile home park or land lease community in which the rental unit is located. 

 

25.   The term “residential occupation” is not defined in the Act. However, the Court has stated in various decisions that residential occupation is generally understood to mean the property will be used as the person’s main residence and requires more than occasional or temporary use of the unit.

26.   Based on the Landlord’s evidence, the test of residential occupation has not been met. It is clear from the Landlord’s own evidence that the property will not be the main residence of the Landlord and she intends to use the unit for business purposes, not residential occupation.  This claim is dismissed.

Relief from eviction (L1 Application)

27.   The Tenant testified that his source of income is retirement benefits of about $1,800 per month.  He has a hearing impairment and relies on oxygen for breathing purposes.  If evicted, he requested until April to find another home.

28.   The Landlord said that she will lose her house if the arrears are not paid because she is using credit to pay bills and the mortgage.

29.   I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Act, and find that it would not be unfair to grant relief from eviction subject to  the conditions set out in this order pursuant to subsection 83(1)(a) and 204(1) of the Act.

30.   In granting relief, I am taking into account the submissions of the Landlord and her financial obligations, and balancing that against the Tenant’s disclosed circumstances. Therefore the relief from eviction is subject to conditions set out in this order which, if not met, would allow the Landlord to apply to the Board, without further notice to the Tenant, for an order terminating the tenancy and evicting the Tenant pursuant to section 78 of the Act.

It is ordered that:

 

L2 Application

 

1.      The Landlord’s L2 application is dismissed.

L1 Application (rent owing)

 

2.      The Tenant shall pay the Landlord $3,500.00 which represents the amount of rent arrears owing up to March 31, 2017.

3.      The Tenant shall also pay the Landlord $190.00 for the cost of filing the application.

4.      The Tenant shall pay the Landlord the full amount owing of $3,690.00, and the additional rent that comes due, as follows:

a.   On or before April 1, 2017                  April rent

b.   On or before April 30, 2017                 $3,690.00 (arrears and filing fee)

5.      If the Tenant fails to make any one of the payments ordered in paragraph 4, in full and on time, the Landlord may apply under section 78 of the Act, without notice to the Tenant for an order terminating the tenancy and evicting the Tenant. The Landlord must make this application no later than 30 days after the Tenant’s failure to make a payment.
