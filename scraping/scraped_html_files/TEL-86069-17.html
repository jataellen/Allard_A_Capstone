Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

D.L. and G.R. (the 'Landlords') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict A.J. (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes.

 

At the hearing the Tenant applied pursuant to section 82 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’) for an order determining that the Landlord failed to meet the Landlord’s maintenance obligations or failed to comply with health, safety, housing or maintenance standards and that the Landlord entered her rental unit illegally and the Landlord or the Landlord's superintendent or the Landlord's agent substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or residential complex by the Tenant or by a member of the Tenant's household and withheld or deliberately interfered with the reasonable supply of a vital service.

 

This application was heard in Whitby on January 3, 2018.

 

The second-named Landlord and the Tenant attended the hearing.  S.A. (the ‘Witness’) also attended the hearing on behalf of the Tenant.

 

Determinations:

 

THE APPLICATION FOR NON-PAYMENT OF RENT

1.      The Tenant has not paid the total rent the Tenant was required to pay for the period from September 1, 2017 to January 31, 2018.  Because of the arrears, the Landlords served a Notice of Termination effective November 16, 2017.

2.      The Landlords collected a rent deposit of $1,765.00 from the Tenant and this deposit is still being held by the Landlords.

3.      Interest on the rent deposit is owing to the Tenant for the period from June 30, 2017 to November 16, 2017.

4.      The Tenant disputed the amount of the monthly rent stating she believes it to be $1,515.00 and not $1,765.00 as listed by the Landlords.  The Tenant further stated that she believes the initial next to the rental amount on the least has been forged by the Landlords. 

5.      The Landlord in attendance said that the lease has not been tampered with and the amount was always $1,765.00. 

6.      The Witness said she was present during the Tenant’s move in and the rent was $1,515.00.  She had a conversation with the second-named Landlord at the time about how high the rent was and the payment the Tenant made was counted in front of her.

7.      The Tenant provided a text message exchange between herself and the Witness about the rental amount being $1,515.00 but I find the question in the text to be leading as opposed to having the Witness state what she actually heard the rent to be.  For this reason, I am not satisfied the rent is $1,515.00. 

8.      Furthermore, the text exchange with the first-named Landlord and the Tenant indicates the Tenant withheld $500.00 in rent for September, 2017.  The Landlord clearly states the remaining amount left from the $1,200.00 payment made by the Tenant to be $565.00, which supports the monthly rent the Landlords have claimed in their application.

9.      The monthly rent for the rental unit is $1,765.00.

THE TENANT’S SECTION 82 CLAIMS

10.   When the Tenant notified the Board she had issues under section 82 of the Act the application was held down to allow the Tenant to tell the second-named Landlord of her list of issues and provide the Landlord with disclosure. 

11.   When the parties came before the Board the Landlord was asked whether she was ready to proceed with the hearing and she indicated she was ready to proceed.  The hearing went ahead.

12.   The Tenant brought forward a number of issues, which include the following:

a)   Roaches;

b)   Rats;

c)   Porch disrepair;

d)   Lack of gas in the rental unit;

e)   Items disposed of by the Landlord;

f)     Harassment from the basement tenant; and

g)   Illegal entry.

13.   The Tenant requested a remedy in the amount of $18,180.00 for her section 82 issues.

Roaches

14.   The Tenant noticed roaches in her rental unit on July 1, 2017 and verbally notified the first-named Landlord on July 20, 2017.  The Tenant testified that when she told the first-named Landlord he responded with ‘oh no’, but no action was taken at that time. 

15.   The Tenant sent a text to the first-named Landlord on August 10, 2017.  She also had a conversation over text with the Landlord on September 13, 2017, of which she submitted a copy of to the Board.  Since the first-named Landlord was not in attendance, I find the Tenant’s testimony to be reliable.

16.   The pictures provided by the Tenant show swarms of roaches in numerous areas of her rental unit.  The roaches are small and large.  It is evident there is a serious roach problem within the unit.  The Tenant had the City of Oshawa (the ‘City’) attend the rental unit and the City issued an order with a list of infractions; one being the “massive cockroach and mouse/rat infestation” in the rental unit.

17.   The Landlord submitted a copy of notices to enter the rental unit and the Tenant did not allow entry during those times.  More specifically for September 5, 2017 and December 11, 2017.  On November 09, 2017 the pest control company did perform cockroach removal.  The Landlord also stated she believes the reason the roaches were in the unit was due to the Tenant’s lack of cleaning; however, provided no evidence to support this statement.

18.   When asked how the pest control company was not allowed entry into the rental unit on September 5 and December 11 the Landlord in attendance said the Tenant did not let the pest control company in.  The Tenant testified that she was not at home during the visits.

19.   A tenant does not have to be present to allow an agent of a landlord into the rental unit.  It is a landlord’s responsibility to let in any agents attending the unit to perform work within the rental unit after 24 hours’ notice is given by the landlord.

20.   The fact that the Landlords did not attend with the pest control company to allow them in is not the fault of the Tenant.  The lack of entry on September 5 and December 11 falls under the Landlords’ responsibility and I will not penalize the Tenant for these times.  Rather, I view them as inaction by the Landlords.

21.   For this reason, I am satisfied the Tenant notified the Landlords of the roach issue on July 20, 2017 and the Landlords did not take action until November 9, 2017.  The Tenant testified to the roaches being throughout her rental unit and that she had a young child living under these circumstances. 

22.   The Tenant notified the Landlords of the roach issue July 20, 2017 and the Landlords had a pest control treat once on November 9, 2017.  The Landlords did not deal with the issue appropriately after that date.  Another inspection and/or a follow up would be expected but did not happen. 

23.   Remedy in the form of abatement is addressed further on in this order under the heading of remedies.

Rats

24.   The Tenant testified that there was a rat in her son’s crib.  She has cleaned rat feces from the crib and believes the rats are getting in through a large hole in the wall. 

25.   The Tenant submitted a picture of a large rodent, which she says is a rat, in her cupboard.  She also provided pictures of items, such as a vinegar jug, with what looks to be scratched holes in the side. 

26.   The Tenant texted the first-named Landlord on August 13, 2017 about the rats in the unit.  The rats are also mentioned in the text to the Landlord on September 13, 2017, and based on the statement from the Tenant in the text message; it seems that this text was the first communication regarding the rats.

27.   The second-named Landlord said the pest control company that was hired to attend the unit does extermination of all pests, including rodents.  The invoice the Landlord submitted; however, shows that the pest control company only treated for roaches.

28.   The Tenant said there was steel wool stuffed into the hole in her son’s room to prevent them from re-entering the room.  She also found a nest in a shirt with dead baby rats inside.  The City’s report notes this as an issue as stated above in paragraph 16.

29.   Remedy in the form of abatement is addressed further on in this order under the heading of remedies.

Porch disrepair

30.   The Tenant said her foot went through the porch and requested maintenance on the porch.  On October 10, 2017, she talked to the Landlords’ maintenance man about the porch.  This item is also on the City report.

31.   The second-named Landlord submitted a copy of a renovation contract signed January 28, 2017 claiming there was no disrepair to the porch because it was addressed by the contractor.  The agreement talks about ‘steps outside’ but does not state any work to be completed regarding the porch itself.

32.   I am satisfied there was disrepair to the porch and the remedy for same is addressed below.

No Gas in the Rental Unit

33.   The Tenant claims there was no gas in the rental unit for the first 6 weeks of her tenancy.  She would go to a friend’s home to bath her son and herself. 

34.   The gas was not on at the commencement of the tenancy and was turned on August 12, 2017.  The Landlord did not dispute this claim.

35.   The Witness testified that she had cleaned the rental unit for the Landlords prior to the Tenant moving in.  There was no hot water when she was cleaning.

36.   Gas is considered a vital service under subsection 2(1) of the Act.  Without gas going to the rental unit the Tenant would have no hot water.

37.   Pursuant to subsection 21(1),

A landlord shall not at any time during a tenant’s occupancy of a rental unit and before the day on which an order evicting the tenant is executed, withhold the reasonable supply of any vital service, care service or food that it is the landlord’s obligation to supply under the tenancy agreement or deliberately interfere with the reasonable supply of any vital service, care service or food.

[Emphasis added]

38.   The Landlords knew the gas was not on in the rental unit at the onset of the tenancy.  To have this vital service off for over 6 weeks is unreasonable.  The second-named Landlord provided no evidence to explain why the gas was off other than to say the first-named Landlord dealt with the issue.

39.   Based on the testimony of both parties I am satisfied the Tenant had no gas in her rental unit for the first 43 days of the tenancy.  Furthermore, I view the unreasonable amount of time to have it turned on, and the lack of evidence provided by the Landlord to explain why, constitutes withholding the vital service and/or deliberately interfering with the vital service.  The remedy for this issue is discussed below.

Items Disposed of by the Landlords

40.   The Tenant claims the second-named Landlord attended the rental unit and took her items from the lawn and other items that went missing.  The items included a large cat scratch post, a wheelchair, and her son’s toys. 

41.   The second-named Landlord said she had a company called East-West Services Company to go to the unit and remove the items from the lawn due to a complaint received from the Tenant.  She also provided a copy of the invoice from this company regarding the pickup. 

42.   The Tenant said she watched the Landlord dispose of the items.  I find it difficult to believe that the Tenant would not vehemently object if she saw her items being thrown out.  For this reason, no remedy is awarded for this item.

 

Harassment from Basement Tenant

43.   The Tenant said the basement tenant has been harassing her and even left a notice to enter saying he was doing it on behalf of the Landlords.  The Landlords were not notified of the issue prior to the hearing.  For this reason, this issue is dismissed.

Illegal Entry

44.   Lastly, the Tenant said the second-named Landlord entered the rental unit. 

45.   On one occasion the maintenance man told the Tenant to move boxes from where the electrical panel was and the only way the Landlord would have known it was there was if she entered the rental unit. 

46.   The second incident was when she entered the rental unit on August 10, 2017 and the Tenant’s son saw her there.

47.   The Landlord testified that she did not enter the rental unit illegally at any time. 

48.   On any application before the Board the person making an allegation bears the burden of proof. This means the Tenant must lead sufficient evidence to establish that it is more likely than not that the Landlord entered the rental unit illegally as she alleges. Here, the evidence is essentially equal but opposite which means the Tenant has failed to meet her burden of proof.

Remedies

49.   With respect to quantum of the abatement to be awarded the Board takes into account criteria that include things like the impact on the Tenant, the length of time the problem persisted, the nature of the problem and the behaviour complained of, and the Landlord’s efforts to mitigate the tenant’s damages.

50.   Given the lack of action taken by the Landlords regarding the cockroaches and rats, and the Tenant’s evidence as to how upsetting it was, and my knowledge of previous like cases before the Board, I believe an appropriate abatement for the period of July 20, 2017 to the date of the hearing is 30% of the rent charged or $2,924.58.  This 30% abatement of rent shall continue until a pest control company reports the rental unit is free of pests and vermin.  The rent less 30% is $1,235.50.

51.   Regarding the lack of the vital service of gas for 43 days and the absence of evidence from the Landlords to mitigate the situation, I believe an appropriate abatement of the rent would be about 35% of the rent charged for the period July 1, 2017 to August 12, 2017 or $873.31. An order shall issue accordingly.

52.   Other than falling through the porch, the Tenant did not provide any other impact the disrepair caused on her day to day life.  For this reason a flat abatement amount in the amount of $100.00 as a percentage would not reflect an appropriate remedy for this item.

53.   The Tenant also requested the Landlords be ordered to fix the issues mentioned in the Tenant’s claim, but also mentioned in the City report.  An order shall issue directing the Landlord to fix the issues. 

Relief from Eviction

54.   The Landlord requested an order with standard terms to pay the amount owed to the Landlords or to vacate the rental unit.  The Tenant asked for a repayment plan that lasts a year. 

55.   Based on the ongoing breach of the Landlords’ obligations under the Act and the second-named Landlord’s action only after being given a report from the City and now responding to the Tenant’s section 82 claims at the Board, relief from eviction is warranted in the way of a repayment plan.

56.   The Tenant did not give the Board dates she would be able to pay the amounts.  The Tenant only told the Board she wanted a year to pay the amount outstanding.  The Tenant is on Ontario Works which is paid out once a month.  The household income is $2,700.00.  To simply allow the Tenant to pay the outstanding amount all at once would not be reasonable.  Keeping this in mind, the payments ordered shall be in tandem with the monthly rent payments.

57.   I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would not be unfair to grant relief from eviction subject to  the condition(s) set out in this order pursuant to subsection 83(1)(a) and 204(1) of the Act.

58.   This order contains all of the reasons for my decision within it. No further reasons shall be issued. 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     The Landlords shall have a pest control company attend the rental unit to treat for roaches and rats on or before February 26, 2018.

2.     The Landlords shall continue to have a pest control company attend the rental unit as the pest company prescribes in order to rid the rental unit of roaches and rats. 

3.     The monthly rent shall be $1,765.00 less 30% or $529.50 for a total of $1,235.50, until a pest control company reports the rental unit is free of roaches and rats.

4.     Once the rental unit has been deemed free of roaches and rats, the Landlords shall provide a copy of this report to the Tenant and the monthly rent shall return to $1,765.00 the month following the date of the pest control report.

5.     The Landlords shall repair the porch on or before February 26, 2018.

6.     The Tenant shall pay to the Landlords $3,917.11 for arrears of rent to January 31, 2018, plus costs, less abatement of rent.

7.     The Tenant shall pay to the Landlords the amount set out above in paragraph 1 in accordance with the following schedule:

a)   $326.43 on or before February 23, 2018;

b)   $326.43 on or before March 1, 2018;

c)   $326.43 on or before April 1, 2018;

d)   $326.43 on or before May 1, 2018;

e)   $326.43 on or before June 1, 2018;

f)     $326.43 on or before July 1, 2018;

g)   $326.43 on or before August 1, 2018;

h)   $326.43 on or before September 1, 2018;

i)     $326.43 on or before October 1, 2018;

j)      $326.43 on or before November 1, 2018;

k)   $326.43 on or before December 1, 2018; and

l)      $326.38 on or before January 1, 2019.

8.     The Tenant shall also pay the rent for February, 2018 on or before February 23, 2018.

9.     The Tenant shall also pay the monthly rent on or before the first day of every month beginning March 1, 2018 until January 1, 2019.

10.  If the Tenant fails to make any one of the payments in accordance with this order, the outstanding balance of any arrears of rent and costs to be paid by the Tenant to the Landlord pursuant to paragraph 6 of this order shall become immediately due and owing and the Landlord may, without notice to the Tenant, apply to the Board pursuant to section 78 of the Act for an order terminating the tenancy and evicting the Tenant and requiring that the Tenant pay any new arrears, NSF fees and related charges that became owing after January 31, 2018.
