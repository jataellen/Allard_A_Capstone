Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TSL-07620-19

TST-05964-19

 

 

 

 


Landlord’s L1 application

 

A B (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict C F (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes.

 

Tenant’s T2 application:

 

C F (the 'Tenant') applied for an order determining that A B (the 'Landlord') failed to meet the Landlord's maintenance obligations under the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act') or failed to comply with health, safety, housing or maintenance standards.

 

Tenant’s T6 application:

 

C F (the 'Tenant') applied for an order determining that A B (the 'Landlord') the Landlord's agent, his son T, entered the rental unit illegally, altered the locking system on a door giving entry to the rental unit or residential complex without giving the Tenant replacement keys and substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or residential complex by the Tenant or by a member of the Tenant's household.

 

These applications were heard in Toronto on July 30, 2019.

 

The Tenant and the Landlord’s son T B (TB) and the Landlord’s Legal Representative L W attended the hearing.

 

Determinations:

 

Preliminary issue regarding bias

 

1.   These parties had previously appeared before me and I issued an order terminating the tenancy. The Tenant informed me today that he has appealed that decision to the Divisional Court.  The Tenant asked me to recuse myself because he felt that I would likely be biased against him now that I realized that he had appealed my earlier decision. 

2.   There is presumption of judicial impartiality and the threshold to establish bias is high. [R. v. S. (R.D.) 1997 CanLII 324 (SCC), 1997 3 S.C.R. 484]

3.   The legal test of a reasonable apprehension of bias is “Whether an informed, reasonable and right-minded person, viewing the matter realistically and practically, and having thought the matter through, would conclude that it was more likely than not that the trial judge would not decide fairly.” [Bailey v. Barbour 2012 ONCA 325].

4.   The Landlord did not object to my presiding over the hearings.

5.   The fact that the Tenant has appealed an earlier decision that I issued does not satisfy this test. I did not recall the parties or the previous case until I heard from the Tenant that he appealed my decision.  I had no doubt of my ability to determine the issues in this case in an impartial manner, and I do not believe that an informed, reasonable and right minded person would conclude differently.  I proceeded accordingly.

 

Determinations on the L1:

 

6.   The Tenant has not paid the total rent the Tenant was required to pay for the period from May 1, 2019 to September 30, 2019.  Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination effective July 4, 2019.

7.   The Tenant is in possession of the rental unit.

8.   The monthly rent is $1800.00.

9.   The Landlord is not holding a last month's rent deposit.

10. The Tenant has made no payments since the application was filed.

11. The Tenant does not dispute the arrears claimed.  He explained that he stopped paying rent to pending the outcome of his two applications. 

12. I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), additionally, the Tenant filed a T2 and T6 and as result a determination must be made into whether relief from the eviction is appropriate because the Landlord is in serious breach od his obligations under the Act as set out in s. 83(3)(a).  For the reasons that follow below, I am satisfied that the Landlord is not in serious breach of the Landlord’s obligations under this Act, therefore I find that it would be unfair to grant relief from eviction pursuant to subsection 83(1) of the Act. The Tenant is not entitled to withhold rent once he has filed applications at the Board.

Tenant’s T2 application

 

13. The Tenant alleges that the landlord substantially interfered with his reasonable use and enjoyment of the rental  because:

14.  TB who is the Landlord’s son and also acts the property manager, attended the residential complex for unknown reasons on the following dates: April, 17, 2018, April 29, 2018, May 3, 2018, May 15, 2018, May 31, 2018, June 4, 2018, June 5, 2018, June 19, 2018, September 6, 2018;

15. TB attended on the grounds as late as 8:00 p.m. to mow the lawn;

16. TB unlawfully entered his rental unit to apply a pest control treatment;

17. TB changed the locks to residential complex without providing the Tenants with notice and in doing so failed to accommodate the Tenant’s injury as he required to under the AODA and other comparable legislation.

18. For the reasons that follow; the Tenant’s T2 application is dismissed;

Unnecessary visits to the residential complex:

 

19. The Tenant testified that he had logged the number of times when he observed the Landlord on the premises between April 17, 2019 and September 6, 2019  The Tenant viewed the TB’s presence on the property as a form of harassment because the Tenant complained to the Electrical Safety Authority (ESA) about the rental unit. 

20. TB agreed that he was at the residential complex on the dates noted.  TB explained that he was at property, though not in or near the Tenant’s unit, for the following reasons:

(a)  To respond to maintenance requests made by another tenant in the residential complex;

(b)  To cut the grass and prepare the garbage bin for pick up;

(c)  To meet with a new tenant in the property and exchange keys;

(d)  To change the locking mechanism for the front door; and

(e)  To respond to a complaint about a possible trespass and install security cameras;

21. The rental unit is one of three in the complex.  The building primarily houses students who are transitory.  The Tenant admitted that he was unaware that other residents required TB to attend to their needs.  

22. Based on the testimony of the parties I am not satisfied that the Tenant has established that the Landlord substantially interfered with his use and enjoyment of his rental unit by attending at the residential complex during the period April 1, 2018 and September 6, 2018. The Landlord was the residential complex for legitimate purposes including addressing the maintenance needs of the other tenants of the residential complex.

 

Locks changed

23. Section 24 of the Act states:

24 A landlord shall not alter the locking system on a door giving entry to a rental unit or residential complex or cause the locking system to be altered during the tenant’s occupancy of the rental unit without giving the tenant replacement keys. 

 

24. TB did not dispute the Tenant’s evidence that on or about July 18, 2018 he replaced the existing lock system that required the residents to lock the main entrance door with key when they exited with a self-locking system.

25. The parties agree that entry key remained the same only the locking system changed. 

26. TB explained that he wrote the residents of the property on July 18, 2018 and explained that he switched the door knob and lock because of a safety situation brought to his attention by the parents of the two tenants who reside in the middle floor unit.  he was notified that one of the female students arrived home late on night and found the main entrance door ajar and identified it as a safety concern.    

27. TB testified that when he investigated the complaint, he discovered that the main door to the property was often left unlocked and he felt to an automatic locking device would be safer, especially for the units occupied by the female students.

28. TB testified that the property is near Humber College in Toronto and to make his units appealing to students and their parents he takes security seriously which includes installing security systems and making sure that the doors lock properly.

29. At no time was the Tenant unable to enter the residential complex due to the Landlord’s installation of a new locking device. TB produced a copy of his notice to the Tenants of the property letting the them that he had changed the knob and installed an auto lock system.  His message also confirmed that the keys remained unchanged.  The Tenant did not dispute being able o use his main entrance key to gain access after the new door knob and auto lock system were installed.

30. Based on the testimony of the parties, I am not satisfied that the Tenant has proven that Landlord changed the locks to the rental unit illegally and failed to provide the Tenant with a key. 

Failure to Accommodate injured wrist:

 

31. The Tenant alleged that Landlord breached his obligation to accommodate a disability/ injury to the Tenant’s right arm.  The Tenant testified that he preferred the old door knob and locking system to the new one due to his wrist injury. 

32. The Tenant asked to have the old locking system returned to the property. However, it was unclear how the new locking system and door knob would affect his injury.  The Tenant provided a medical note from his specialist which explained the nature of his physical issue.  The medical note suggests that the Tenant can manage his discomfort by modifying his activities. 

33. The Tenant acknowledged that he had not notified either the Landlord or TB that he required accommodation prior to filing his application. 

34. It is settled that law that accommodation is a collaborative process.  A tenant is required to notify a landlord of any issue requiring accommodation. In this case, the Tenant did not notify the Landlord that he required accommodation.  Therefore, I cannot find that the Landlord breached its obligation to accommodate the Tenant prior to hearing date.

35. Furthermore, the evidence does not establish that the Tenant continues to require the requested accommodation TB relied on video evidence which he submitted to show that the Tenant has full range of motion and use of the wrist he alleged is injured.   The video shows the Tenant using a variety of tools to remove his tire, lug nuts and alternator. The images also depict the Tenant using his injured hand to open the door with the new door knob.  Therefore, based on the evidence of the parties, I am unable to determine that the Tenant suffers from disability that requires the accommodation he has requested.

Unauthorized entry to spray for ants:

36. The Tenant also alleged that the Landlord substantially interfered with use and enjoyment of the rental unit by entering the unit illegally on May 22, 2018 to spray it for ants. 

37. TB’s submitted an invoice given by Orkin the company hired to spray for ants, which confirms that it was basement apartment that was sprayed for ants and not the Tenant’s unit.  Any odours the Tenant smelt in his unit likely travelled up from the basement. 

38. Therefore, the Tenant’s T2 application shall be dismissed as I am not satisfied that Landlord, TB or Orkin accessed the Tenant’s unit on May 22, 2019 to spray for ants.

 

T6 application

Tenant’s evidence:

39. The Tenant claims that the Landlord breached his maintenance and repair obligations under s. 20 of the Act because the rental unit is not air conditioned and the Tenant is unable to operate a large portable air conditioner in his bedroom. 

40. The Tenant explained that when he attempted to operate the air conditioner in June 2018 his lights flickered and eventually shut off. 

41. The Tenant notified TB who responded immediately advising the Tenant to operate the air conditioning unit from the larger bedroom at the back of the rental unit and open the doors  allowing the cool air to circulate. 

42. When the problem persisted between June 1, 2018 and August 8, 2018, the Tenant contacted the Electrical Safety Authority (ESA) and arranged to have one of its investigators look into the wiring. 

43. The investigation by the ESA concluded that air conditioner was overloading the outlet the Tenant plugged it into and that unless the Landlord added a new fuse/amp to the circuit breaker or the Tenant used different plug, the problem would persist. 

44. The ESA investigator did not issue a work order or provide a written report citing any ESA violations against the Landlord.  Instead they explained that the solution could be a few hundred dollars and a new fuse added to fuse box.  

Landlord’s Response:

 

45. TB agreed that the electrical wiring in the residential complex could be updated if they chose but they have never been ordered to do so by the ESA.  TB explained that he and his father own several small residential complexes and they allocate capital improvements/expenditures annually based on the immediate need of each property. 

46. TB explained given the age of the property, they do not offer air conditioning in the property that houses the rental unit because they know that the wiring is older.  They have never objected to Tenants using their own air conditioning unit provide that they did so within safe parameters.  

47. TB argued that previous tenants have used portable air conditioning units in the rental unit without incident because they operated the portable units safely.

48. TB explained that he sent his own electrician out in August when the Tenant complained that the problem persisted.  TB testified that his electrician inspected the electrical outlet in the rental unit and found that they were functioning appropriately. 

Analysis:

 

49. Section 20 of the Act states:

20 (1)  A landlord is responsible for providing and maintaining a residential complex, including the rental units in it, in a good state of repair and fit for habitation and for complying with health, safety, housing and maintenance standards.  [emphasis added]

50. There is no dispute between the parties that TB responded within hours to the first compliant regarding the power outage.  There is also no dispute that he responded quickly each time the Tenant complained. 

51. The key issue raised in this application is whether the Tenant is entitled to operate any appliance from anywhere within the rental unit that he choses.  In other words, is it a breach of the Landlord’s obligation under section 20 to limit the Tenant operation of the air conditioning unit to the spare bedroom at the back of the rental unit.

52. The ESA investigated the rental unit and did not find that the Landlord was in breach of any standard relating to the electrical wiring. Further, it cannot be said that the Tenant’s inability to place his air conditioner in the room of his choosing makes the rental unit unfit for habitation.

53. Thus, in order for the Tenant’s claim to be successful he must establish that the fact that the existing wiring in the residential complex is insufficient for the Tenant’s air conditioner other than in one of the bedrooms means that the rental unit is not in a good state of repair

54. Under contract law a landlord is obliged to ensure that the tenant receives the entire bundle of rights he contracted for.  The Tenant’s interpretation that he enjoys unfettered use and access to the rental unit is in correct, however, he should receive exactly what he contracted for.  Here the lease did not include an air conditioning unit. Therefore, the Tenant does not have an automatic entitlement to be able to operate his air conditioner anywhere in the rental unit.

55. Further, the electrical wiring does operate as intended – it provides a supply of electricity so long as the circuits are overloaded. What the Tenant wants the Landlord to do is improve or upgrade the electrical system so that it is capable of running an air conditioner anywhere in the rental unit

56. Therefore, I find that the Landlord did not fail to meet the Landlord's obligations under subsection 20(1) of the Act.  An important distinction must be made between a landlord’s maintenance and repair obligations under section 20(1) that would trigger an automatic inquiry into whether a tenant has been deprived of something he or she contracted for, and the situation we have here, where two separate qualified electricians investigated the Tenant’s complaint and concluded that the wiring did not need repair.  The Tenant’s claim is based on his view that he is entitled to operate the air conditioning from any room in the unit so chooses. 

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.      Unless the Tenant voids the order as set out below, the tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated.  The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before November 11, 2019.

2.      The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $10,642.42*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to October 31, 2019.

3.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $59.18 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting November 1, 2019 to the date the Tenant moves out of the unit.

4.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $175.00 for the cost of filing the application.

5.      If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing* on or before November 11, 2019, the Tenant will start to owe interest.  This will be simple interest calculated from November 12, 2019 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

6.      If the unit is not vacated on or before November 11, 2019, then starting November 12, 2019, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

7.      Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord, on or after November 12, 2019.

8.      If the Tenant wishes to void this order and continue the tenancy, the Tenant must pay to the Landlord or to the Board in trust:
i)        $10,975.00 if the payment is made on or before October 31, 2019, or
ii)      $12,775.00 if the payment is made on or before November 11, 2019**.
If the Tenant does not make full payment in accordance with this paragraph and by the appropriate deadline, then the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

9.      The Tenant may make a motion to the Board under subsection 74(11) of the Act to set aside this order if they pay the amount required under that subsection on or after November 12, 2019 but before the Sheriff gives vacant possession to the Landlord. The Tenant is only entitled to make this motion once during the period of the tenancy agreement with the Landlord.

10.   The Tenants T2 and T6 application are denied.
