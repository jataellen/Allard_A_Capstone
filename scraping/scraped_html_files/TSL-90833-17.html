Expanded Collapsed

Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TSL-90833-17

 

 

 

 


#.G (Toronto) Limited (the 'Landlord') applied, to the Landlord and Tenant Board (the “Board”), for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict A.T and J.B (the 'Tenants') because the Tenants did not pay the rent that the Tenants owe.

 

This application was heard in Toronto on January 29, 2018 and February 22, 2018.

 

The Landlord was represented by S.K, licensed paralegal. The Tenant, A.T (“AT”) attended the hearing on January 29, 2018, she did not attend on February 22nd. The Tenant J.B (“JB”) did not attend the hearing on either date set.

 

The Tenants did not attend the hearing on February 22nd. I had no record before me of a request to adjourn and I was satisfied the Tenants received proper notice of the time, place and location of the hearing. Nonetheless, I waited past 2:00 p.m. When the Tenants still did not arrive, I proceeded to hear the Landlord’s application.

 

Determinations:

 

Preliminary Matters

 

Tenant JB

 

1.      On January 26, 2018, the Board received a lengthy email from Tenant JB explaining why he was not planning to attend the hearing scheduled for January 29th. In summary, JB writes about alleged abusive conduct, directed towards him, by Tenant AT and her boyfriend, since approximately June, 2017. JB goes on to explain he would not attend the January 29th hearing out of fear of AT and her boyfriend.

2.      JB also provided a Tenant’s Statement About Sexual or Domestic Violence and Abuse (the “Form N15”), which JB states he served to the Landlord on or about October 31, 2017, giving the Landlord notice he would be vacating the unit on November 30, 2017. From the documents provided by JB, it appears he purportedly vacated the unit on December 1, 2018. However, the Landlord’s legal representative stated, as at February 22, 2017, JB remained in possession of one or more keys to the unit and some of his belongings remained in the unit. On January 29th, AT testified JB’s room in the unit is locked as JB placed a lock on the door to his room and, further, some of JB’s belongings remain in his room.

3.      In some circumstances Tenants are entitled to terminate a tenancy by serving a notice to terminate in Form N15 on the Landlord—here JB was no so entitled. Pursuant to s. 47.2(1) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act') a joint tenant may terminate his or her interest in a tenancy by giving notice of termination to the landlord if the tenant is deemed under subsection 47.3 (1) to have experienced violence or another form of abuse. As is clearly stated on the Form N15, the tenant serving the Form N15, or his or her child, must be at risk of harm or injury at the hands of a spouse or former spouse, someone the tenant lives with, or lived with in a conjugal relationship, a person the tenant dated or used to date or a person related by blood to the tenant or the tenant’s child. The very lengthy email and other documents submitted by JB to the Board on January 26th fail to disclose any such required relationship between JB and AT or JB and AT’s boyfriend and, as such, JB was not permitted to serve the Form N15 to the Landlord.  

4.      Given all of the above, I am satisfied that JB was in possession of the unit for the purposes of the Act on the date the Landlord’s application was filed on November 28, 2017 and, as such, JB will remain a named respondent Tenant in the style of cause.

5.      I should also state the documents provided by JB clearly indicate he was well aware of the January 29th hearing date—indeed, JB included a copy of the notice of hearing in his documentation submitted to the Board. The documents provided do not, in my view, provide an adequate reason for JB’s non-attendance. In the event JB’s fear of attending the hearing was genuine, it was open to him to request accommodation in the form of additional security in the hearing room. Such requests are regularly received by the Board and the requesting party is much more often than not accommodated accordingly.

Costs

6.      On January 29, 2018, and approximately five hours after the conclusion of the hearing, Tenant AT sent an email to the Board stating, in part, two payments made by her in December, 2017 and January, 2018, each in the sum of $664.51, were not reflected in the Landlord’s L1/L9 update form submitted during the hearing; consequently the arrears amount claimed by the Landlord was in error.

7.      AT’s email appeared somewhat curious to me as the Landlord’s L1/L9 update form submitted during the hearing, and provided to her, clearly reflects the two payments referred to in the email. However, not knowing if AT may have, perhaps, been referring to additional payments made in the same amount, and as an accommodation to her, as a self-represented Tenant, I decided to reconvene the hearing as my order had not yet been written. The matter was scheduled to reconvene on February 22nd and the Board sent out notices of hearing to the parties at their respective addresses and nothing was returned as undelivered. On February 22nd there was nothing in the file indicating AT had not received the notice of hearing, was unable to attend or required an adjournment. Yet, AT failed to attend, so I proceeded to hear the Landlord’s evidence and submissions.

8.      The Landlord’s legal representative requested an order for costs against Tenant AT for preparation and attendance. The Landlord’s representative stated AT’s email of January 29th led to the Board reconvening the hearing and, on the scheduled date, AT did not bother to attend. The Landlord’s representative requested an order for costs in the sum of $450.00 and that I add that amount to the quantum required to void my order, below.

9.      The Board’s Interpretation Guideline 3, entitled Costs, says, in part:

The Board should not use its power to order costs in a way which would discourage landlords and tenants from exercising their statutory rights.

Generally, costs may be ordered where a party's conduct in the proceeding was unreasonable…

Before ordering a party, a party's agent or a party's legal representative to pay costs, a Member should ensure that the person who will be affected by the order for costs has an opportunity to make submissions on the matter.

However, if a party has received notice of a hearing and does not attend a hearing, or if a party's agent or legal representative is on the record as representing a party and does not attend a hearing, a Member may proceed to make an order for costs without notifying the person affected of the intention to do so, provided that the failure to attend the hearing delayed the process unnecessarily or caused unnecessary expense to the other party…

A Member has the discretion to require a party, a party's agent or a party's legal representative to pay, as costs, any representation or preparation expenses of another party where the conduct of the party, a party's agent or a party's legal representative was unreasonable. Conduct is unreasonable if it causes undue expense or delay… [Emphasis added]

10.   Unaware AT would not attend on February 22nd, the Landlord’s representative prepared for, and attended, the hearing. AT had ample time, prior to the day of the hearing, to communicate her decision not to attend the hearing to the Landlord, the Landlord’s legal representative and the Board. Yet, she did nothing and simply did not show up. Given all of the circumstances an adverse costs award is clearly warranted as against AT as her failure to notify anyone of his decision not to attend the hearing was unreasonable and caused undue expense to the Landlord. An order will issue accordingly. While the Landlord’s legal representative requested I attach the costs award to the amount required to void the order, I will not do so as, in my view, and notwithstanding this may be permissible by the statute, to do so is too prejudicial to the remaining Tenant, AT,—especially given the quantum of the costs award. Moreover, adding the costs award to the amount required to void the order would unfairly bind Tenant JB, who, in all likelihood, had nothing to do with AT’s non-attendance on February 22nd.

The February 14th Cheque

11.   Lastly, prior to the February 22nd sitting of the hearing, Tenant AT delivered a personal cheque, in the sum of $3,987.00 and dated February 14, 2018, to the Landlord. As at February 22nd sitting, the Landlord’s representative was unable to state whether the cheque had cleared. In the event the said cheque clears, the Tenants are permitted to deduct the amount of $3,987.00 from the amount required to void this order as outline below.

The Landlord’s Application

12.   The Landlord collected a rent deposit of $1,993.51 from the Tenants and this deposit is still being held by the Landlord.

13.   Interest on the rent deposit is owing to the Tenants for the period from May 1, 2017 to November 23, 2017.

14.   The Tenants paid $1,329.02 after the application was filed.

15.   The Landlord’s legal representative requested a standard order.

16.   I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would be unfair to grant relief from eviction pursuant to subsection 83(1) of the Act. In particular, on January 29, 2018, AT stated she is willing and able to pay the total amount required to void the order within the eleven days normally required by a standard order. Indeed, and as stated above, AT provided the Landlord with a cheque dated February 14, 2018 in the sum of $3,987.00.

It is ordered that:

 

1.      Unless the Tenants void the order as set out below, the tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenants is terminated.  The Tenants must move out of the rental unit on or before March 9, 2018.

2.      The Tenants shall pay to the Landlord $3,729.73*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to February 26, 2018, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlord owes on the rent deposit.

3.      The Tenants shall also pay to the Landlord $65.54 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting February 27, 2018 to the date they move out of the unit.

4.      The Tenants shall also pay to the Landlord $190.00 for the cost of filing the application.

5.      If the Tenants do not pay the Landlord the full amount owing* on or before March 9, 2018, the Tenants will start to owe interest.  This will be simple interest calculated from March 10, 2018 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

6.      If the unit is not vacated on or before March 9, 2018, then starting March 10, 2018, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

7.      Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord, on or after March 10, 2018.

8.      If the Tenants wish to void this order and continue the tenancy, they must pay to the Landlord or to the Board in trust:
i)        $6,170.51 if the payment is made on or before February 28, 2018, or
ii)      $8,164.02 if the payment is made on or before March 9, 2018**.
If the Tenants do not make full payment in accordance with this paragraph and by the appropriate deadline, then the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

9.      The Tenants may make a motion to the Board under subsection 74(11) of the Act to set aside this order if they pay the amount required under that subsection on or after March 10, 2018 but before the Sheriff gives vacant possession to the Landlord. The Tenants are only entitled to make this motion once during the period of the tenancy agreement with the Landlord.

10.   Tenant Annie Turcotte shall pay to the Landlord costs in the amount of $450.00 by March 15, 2018.

11.   If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing on or before March 15, 2018, she will start to owe interest.  This will be simple interest calculated from March 16, 2018 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.
