Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TEL-05656-19

 

 

 

 


(SG) (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict (LA)ham (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes (the ‘L1 application’) and because the Tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or someone the Tenant permitted in the residential complex has substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment or lawful right, privilege or interest of the Landlord or another tenant (the ‘L2 application’).

 

This application was heard in Toronto on November 27, 2019.

 

The Landlord and the Tenant attended the hearing.

 

It is determined that:

1.      The Landlord’s L2 application is dismissed because it is based on a notice of termination that is invalid on its face.

2.      The notice in question is in the Board’s Form N5. None of the boxes have been checked off which means there is no indication as to whether the N5 is a notice issued pursuant to ss. 62, 64, 67 or 68 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’). Although it contains particulars (which means the Tenant would have known that it was about unpaid monies related to utilities), it does not give any information to the Tenant about voiding as required by s. 64(2)(c), nor does it indicate in any way which section of the Act the Landlord is relying on.

3.      Pursuant to s. 69 of the Act, an application like this one is based on a notice of termination served in accordance with the Act. As the N5 here was not in accordance with the requirements of the Act, the application must be dismissed. The Board does not have the jurisdiction to evict a tenant based on an invalid notice.

4.      With respect to the L1 application, the parties dispute the amount of rent owing. In particular, they dispute how much the Tenant paid to the Landlord in the month of July, 2019.

5.      The Tenant says that on July 3, 2019 the Landlord and her spouse were at the residential complex cutting the grass and she paid to the Landlord $1,835.00 in cash. She says that payment represents $1,035.00 she withdrew from her bank account, $700.00 paid to her by her roommate, and $100.00 the Tenant borrowed. According to the Tenant, both her roommate and her father were present when this occurred and her roommate counted out the money for the Landlord.

6.      The Tenant offers in corroboration: a copy of her bank records showing withdrawals on July 2, 2019 totaling $1,035.00: and a letter from her roommate that says the roommate gave $700.00 to the Tenant on July 3, 2019 and she saw the Tenant count all of the money before handing it to the Landlord.

7.      The Landlord says the Tenant did not give her any cash on July 3, 2019.

8.      There is no dispute that on July 5, 2019 the Landlord told the Tenant that July's rent was unpaid. When the Tenant replied that she paid it, the Landlord told her she must have memory problems and to look for it.

9.      On July 6, 2019 the Tenant texted the Landlord saying that she was looking for the missing cash and could not find it in the house or in her dad's car. The Tenant says she did this because she did not know what else to do or say.

10.   The next thing that happened was the Tenant paid $500.00 to the Landlord via e-transfer on July 13, 2019.

11.   Then August rolled around. On August 4, 2019 the Tenant sent the Landlord a text which explains the payment she was making that day. That explanation includes July’s rent and deducts the $500.00 e-transfer but not the $1,835.00 allegedly paid in cash on July 3, 2019. The Tenant says she did this because she could not prove she paid the $1,835.00.

12.   So the question here is whether or not I believe the Tenant when she says she gave the Landlord $1,835.00 in cash on July 3, 2019.

13.   Given all of the evidence before the Board, I am not satisfied that amount was paid. I say this because the texts of July 6 and August 4, 2019 both contain statements by the Tenant that contradict the assertion that money was paid. If the money had been paid there was no reason for her to state she was looking for it; and there was no logical reason why the Tenant would not have deducted it from the balance owing on August 4, 2019. Although the Tenant offers explanations for these texts, in the face of the Landlord’s denial and the absence of corroborating testimony from the Tenant’s father or roommate, I am not prepared to make a finding that the cash was paid.

14.   I would also observe that the bank records may show a withdrawal from the Tenant’s account, but they do nothing to prove the amount was given to the Landlord.

15.   Further, the letter from the Tenant’s roommate is hearsay so its contents must be given little to no weight. If the Tenant wished to rely on her roommate’s evidence she should have summonsed her to testify. I would also observe that the letter actually contradicts the Tenant’s own oral testimony. The letter says the Tenant counted out all of the money whereas the Tenant says her roommate did.

16.   Given all of the above I find that the rent arrears owing for the period ending November 30, 2019 total $1,588.79 as of the date of hearing before the Board.

17.   The Landlord incurred costs of $190.00 for filing the application and is entitled to reimbursement of those costs.

18.   The total amount owing under this order for the period ending November 30, 2019 is $1,778.79.

19.   The lawful monthly rent is $1,800.00.

20.   I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’), and find that it would not be unfair to grant relief from eviction in the form of the repayment plan requested by the Tenant. I say this because the Landlord was content to accept a payment plan whereby rent is paid on the first and arrears are to be paid at the rate of $400.00 per month on or before the 20th of the month until the balance owing is paid.

21.   Please note that the order below includes rent charged for the period ending July 31, 2020 due to the delay in issuance of this order. Any payments made after the hearing held on November 27, 2019 must be deducted from the amount outstanding in paragraph 1 below.

22.   This order contains all of the reasons for the decision within it. No further reasons shall be issued.

It is ordered that:

1.      The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $16,178.79 LESS ANY PAYMENT MADE TO THE LANDLORD BY THE TENANT FOR RENT AFTER THE HEARING HELD ON NOVEMBER 27, 2019.

2.      The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord the amount set out in paragraph 1 in accordance with the following schedule:

(1)  $400.00 on or before the 20th day of each month starting July 20, 2020 and continuing until the balance outstanding is paid.

3.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord new rent on time and in full for the period commencing December 1, 2019 and continuing until the balance owing under paragraph 1 is paid.

4.      If the Tenant fails to make any one of the payments in accordance with this order, the outstanding balance of any arrears of rent and costs to be paid by the Tenant to the Landlord pursuant to paragraph 1 of this order shall become immediately due and owing and the Landlord may, without notice to the Tenant, apply to the Board within 30 days* of the Tenant’s breach pursuant to section 78 of the Act for an order terminating the tenancy and evicting the Tenant and requiring that the Tenant pay any new arrears, NSF fees and related charges that became owing after November 30, 2019.
