1273599 OL. o/a APM. (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict ML (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes (L1 Application).

 

The Landlord also applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict the Tenant because the Tenant has been persistently late in paying the Tenant's rent. The Landlord also claimed compensation for each day the Tenant remained in the unit after the termination date (L2 application).

 

Pursuant to s. 82 of the Act the Tenant raised issues regarding repair and maintenance of the rental unit and issues relating to tenant’s rights.

 

This combined application was heard in Toronto on May 28, 2019.

 

The Landlord and the Tenant attended the hearing.

 

Determinations:

 

For the reasons set out below it is determined that:

 

1.      The Tenant owes the Landlord $4,496.38. The tenancy terminates unless the Tenant pays this amount by July 15, 2019.

2.      The Landlord’s harassment of the Tenant and the Landlord’s breach of the Tenant’s privacy rights constitute a breach of the Landlord’s responsibilities under the Act.

3.      The Landlord has failed to maintain the rental unit in relation to the kitchen ceiling light fixture cover and a broken window locking system.

 

L1 Application – Arrears

4.      The Tenant has not paid the total rent the Tenant was required to pay for the period from January 1, 2019 to May 31, 2019.  Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination (the ‘N4 notice) effective February 18, 2019.

5.      The Landlord collected a rent deposit of $1,200.00 from the Tenant and this deposit is still being held by the Landlord.

6.      Interest on the rent deposit is owing to the Tenant for the period from April 15, 2017 to February 18, 2019.

7.      We have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would be unfair to grant relief from eviction pursuant to subsection 83(1) of the Act.

L2 Application – Persistent Late Payment

8.      On December 28, 2018, the Landlord served the Tenant a Notice to Terminate at End of the Term for persistent late payment of rent (the ‘N8 notice’) with a termination date of February 28, 2019.

9.      The Tenant testified that he did not disagree with the Landlord’s schedule of late payments in the N8 notice. The Tenant testified that due to his pay schedule, he pays his monthly rent in 2 to 3 installments each month. As a result, his rent is not paid in full on the first of each month. Therefore, we find that the Tenant has persistently paid his rent late.

10.   We have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would be unfair to grant relief from eviction subject to the condition(s) set out in this order pursuant to subsection 83(1)(a) of the Act.

Section 82 - Tenant Issues in Non-Payment of Rent Application

11.   Pursuant to s. 82 of the Act, the Tenant raised both repair and maintenance issues in the rental unit and tenant rights issues. These issues may lead to an abatement of rent which would be set off against any arrears of rent owing to the Landlord.

12.   The Tenant alleged that the Landlord harassed and interfered with his rights by: (1) illegal entries; (2) withholding vital services; (3) sending offending emails; and (4) the unauthorized use of his personal information.

13.   The Tenant also alleged that he Landlord failed to maintain the residential unit in a good state of repair and fit for habitation and failed to comply with maintenance standards in relation to: (1) mold in the bathroom fan; (2) stove exhaust; (3) kitchen light fixture cover; (4) broken window lock; and (5) bathroom and kitchen plumbing/pipe flooding.

T2 – Tenant Rights

Illegal Entry

14.   This claim arises as a result of the Landlord, or their agents, entering the boiler room of the residential complex, without prior notice to the Tenant. Specifically, the Tenant states that the Landlord entered the boiler room on October 19, 2018 and on February 25, 2019. The question before the Board is whether the Landlord was required to provide notice of entry pursuant to s. 27 of the Act, when entering or accessing the boiler room.

15.   The Landlord and the Tenant dispute what areas are contained within, and form a part of, the Tenant’s rental unit. The Tenant stated that the boiler room is part of his rental unit. The Landlord disputes this assertion and states that the boiler room is part of the common arears of the complex and does not form part of the Tenant’s renal unit. Neither party submitted the rental agreement to clarify the boundaries of the Tenant’s rental unit.

16.   The evidence before the Board was that the boiler room contains the residential complex’s boiler and other equipment. It is located between the Tenant’s rental unit and the shared laundry room and can be accessed through locked doors at either end, leading into the Tenant’s kitchen and on the opposite end into the shared laundry room. There are also shelves within the boiler room that both the Landlord and the Tenant store their property on. The Tenant testified that as his kitchen door provides entry into the boiler room it must constitute part of his unit. The Landlord testified that he maintains access to the boiler room as this is necessary for maintenance and repair issues for the boiler/equipment. Having considered the evidence of the parties we find the evidence of the Landlord to be more reasonable as the boiler room contains equipment that would require access by the Landlord on a regular basis.

17.   We are satisfied that the boiler room does not form part of the Tenant’s rental unit for their exclusive use and is in fact a common area of the residential complex. The Landlord’s entry into the boiler room therefore does not constitute illegal entry into the Tenant’s rental unit. This claim is dismissed.

Withholding of Vital Services

18.   Pursuant to s. 21 of the Act, a Landlord is prohibited from withholding or deliberately interfering with the supply of vital services that it is obligated to supply under the tenancy agreement. Subsection 2(1) of the Act provides the definition of vital services, which includes the provision of heat to a rental unit between the months of September 1 to June 15 (section 4 of O.Reg. 516/06). The Tenant gave evidence that he had concerns regarding the temperature regulation in his unit, however, the complaint arose as a result of the rental unit being too hot. The Tenant did not provide evidence that his tenancy agreement contracted for the provision of air conditioning for his unit. As there are no provisions under the Act that require the Landlord to maintain the residential unit below a certain temperature or a provision requiring air conditioning, this claim is dismissed.

 

Harassment and Substantial Interference with Reasonable Enjoyment

19.   In the hearing, the Tenant stated that the Landlord harassed and interfered with the Tenant by sending offending emails and improperly disclosing his personal information. Section 23 of the Act states that the Landlord shall not harass, obstruct, coerce, threaten or interfere with a tenant. There is no definition of “harassment” in the Act, however prior Board decisions have defined it as follows: “harassment is usually considered to be a course of conduct that a reasonable person knows or ought to know would be unwelcome” (TST-62367-15).

Offending Emails

20.   The Tenant submitted several emails from the Landlord into evidence that the Tenant submitted were harassing and offending. The Landlord confirmed in the hearing that he had sent the emails and had done so out of frustration that the rent had not been paid.

The Landlord sent the following emails to the Tenant: 

                             i.         January 29, 2019 stating, “You really need to get a job”. 

                           ii.         February 8, 2019, calling the Tenant a thief.

                           iii.         March 22, 2019, stating “I knew you were a criminal from the moment you cut those bushes and lied about it…”

                           iv.         April 3, 2019, stating “All of Canada is ashamed of your behavior”.

                           v.         May 4, 2019, stating “6 months now $7,200. You low life crook!!!”

                           vi.         May 18, 2019, stating “… You’re a nasty person”

21.   The Tenant advised that he was upset by the tone and content of the emails, and that they were very disrespectful and hurtful. He advised that the allegations were untrue and offending as he was not a thief, a criminal nor a crook.

22.   The Tenant requested a rent abatement as a result of the alleged harassment by the Landlord and that the Board order the Landlord stop the harassing behavior.

23.   We find that the average and reasonable person would consider the comments made in the Landlord’s emails to be offending, demeaning and inappropriate and we accept the Tenant’s evidence that they had a negative impact on him. We are therefore satisfied that the Landlord breached s. 23 of the Act through these emails and that they amount to harassment of the Tenant.

Use of Personal Information

24.   The Tenant stated that the Landlord had recently contacted the individuals who the Tenant had used as references in his original tenancy application with the Landlord in 2017. The Tenant advised that on April 3, 2019, his references were contacted by the Landlord and they were informed that the Tenant was in arrears of rent and the amount of arrears owing. The Landlord confirmed that he had contacted the Tenant’s references as he felt that as references, they should assist him in convincing the Tenant to pay the rent owing.

25.   The Landlord should not have disclosed information about the Tenant’s rental account to third parties. The Tenant’s payment history was personal information and pursuant to subsection 5(3) of the Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act, SC 2000, c. 5, should only be disclosed for purposes that a reasonable person would consider appropriate in the circumstances.

26.   The Landlord breached the Tenant’s privacy rights by sharing information about his payment history with third parties and he ought to have known that this was unreasonable as the Tenant’s references would not have a right to know of the Tenant’s debts or credit history. We find that the privacy breach interfered with the Tenant’s reasonable enjoyment.

Conclusion and Remedy

27.   The Tenant is entitled to an abatement of rent for the harassment and also for substantial interference. Based on the nature of these problems and the impact on the Tenant, we find that an appropriate amount for the harassing emails would be $250.00 and an appropriate amount for the substantial interference would be $500.00.

Repair and Maintenance Claims

28.   The Tenant alleged that the Landlord failed to conduct repairs in a timely manner and failed to maintain the unit in relation to: (1) mold in the bathroom fan; (2) stove exhaust vent; (3) kitchen light fixture cover; (4) broken window lock; and (5) bathroom and kitchen plumbing/pipe flooding.

29.   Pursuant to section 20(1) of the Act a “landlord is responsible for providing and maintaining a residential complex, including the rental units in it, in a good state of repair and fit for habitation and for complying with health, safety, housing and maintenance standards.”

Bathroom fan mold.

30.   The Tenant alleged that there was black mold in the bathroom fan fixture and submitted photographs at the hearing in support of this claim. The photographs were unclear and did not show evidence of mold. The Landlord contested this claim and advised that he had attended the unit to inspect the fixture and there was no evidence of mold. Based on the photographs provided, we did not see any discoloration of any sort in the fan or evidence of mold and we find that the Tenant was unable to prove his claim for mold on the balance of probabilities. This claim is dismissed.

Stove exhaust vent.

31.   The Tenant alleged that there was an issue with the stove exhaust vent being open to the exterior of the unit, such that a baby bird was able to enter into his unit. The Tenant could not explain where, or if, there was an actual opening in the exhaust vent, but only that there would have been no other way for a bird to enter into his unit. The Landlord testified that there was a screen over the exhaust vent and as a result it would be impossible for a bird to enter through the venting system and into the unit. When asked if there was a screen over the exhaust vent, the Tenant advised that he did not know, that it could be possible. As the Tenant is uncertain how the bird entered his unit and whether or not the exhaust vent is secured, we find that he was unable to prove his claim on a balance of probabilities and this claim is therefore dismissed.

Kitchen light fixture cover.

32.   The Tenant alleged that the ceiling light fixture in the kitchen had a missing light fixture cover. He advised that the cover was not in place as it was broken and he had advised the Landlord and had asked for it to be repaired. The Landlord testified that he was aware that the light fixture cover was broken and had not repaired or replaced it. Based on the evidence before the Board, we find that the light fixture cover is broken. The Landlord will be ordered to replace the light fixture cover.

Broken window lock.

33.   The Tenant alleged that the unit’s window lock is either broken or is incapable of securing the window. The Tenant and the Landlord both agreed that in order to “better” secure the window, a piece of wood has been installed to hold the window shut. The Tenant advised that the insertion of the wood has not fully secured the window. Based on the evidence before the Board we are not satisfied on a balance of probabilities that the window locking system is sufficient to secure the window. The Landlord will be ordered to install a window locking system that is sufficient to secure the window and if that is not possible with the current window system, the Landlord will be ordered to provide a secure window.

Bathroom and kitchen plumbing/pipes flooding.

34.   The Tenant alleged that there was a “pin hole” leak in the plumbing fixtures that run from the kitchen to the washroom. This leak caused water to flow within the ceiling and down a wall. The Tenant provided photographs that showed the leakage caused damage to paint on one wall and this leak was the cause of the damage to the light fixture cover in paragraph 34. The Landlord acknowledges that there was a leak that caused the issues listed above. The Landlord advised that he attended the unit and repaired the leak issues, with only the paint and light fixture as outstanding repair issues. Based on the information before the Board we find on a balance of probabilities that the leak caused a small area of paint to peel and that this took several months for the Landlord to repair. However, having viewed photographs of the affected area, we cannot find that this issue was of a sufficiently serious nature to warrant a remedy.

 

 

 

It is ordered that:

 

            L1 Application – Arrears

 

1.      Unless the Tenant voids the order as set out below, the tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated.  The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before July 15, 2019.

2.      The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $4,496.38*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to June 14, 2019, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlord owes on the rent deposit. The amount of $750.00 payable by the Landlord to the Tenant pursuant to paragraph 12 of this order below, is set off against the arrears of rent, leaving a net balance payable by the Tenant to the Landlord of $4,496.38

3.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $39.45 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting June 15, 2019 to the date the Tenant moves out of the unit.

4.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $190.00 for the cost of filing the application.

5.      If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing* on or before July 15, 2019, the Tenant will start to owe interest.  This will be simple interest calculated from July 16, 2019 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

6.      If the unit is not vacated on or before July 15, 2019, then starting July 16, 2019, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

7.      Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord, on or after July 16, 2019.

8.      If the Tenant wishes to void this order and continue the tenancy, the Tenant must pay to the Landlord or to the Board in trust:
i)        $6,640.00 if the payment is made on or before June 30, 2019, or
ii)      $7,840.00 if the payment is made on or before July 15, 2019**.
If the Tenant does not make full payment in accordance with this paragraph and by the appropriate deadline, then the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

9.      The Tenant may make a motion to the Board under subsection 74(11) of the Act to set aside this order if they pay the amount required under that subsection on or after July 16, 2019 but before the Sheriff gives vacant possession to the Landlord. The Tenant is only entitled to make this motion once during the period of the tenancy agreement with the Landlord.

 

 

L2 Application – Persistent Late Payment

10.   If the Tenant voids the L1 application above, the Tenant shall also pay the Landlord monthly rent owing on or before the first day of each month commencing July 1, 2018 and continuing thereafter until and including June 1, 2020.

11.   If the Tenant fails to make any of the payments in paragraph 10, above, the Landlord may apply under section 78 of the Act, within 30 days of the breach and without notice to the Tenant, for an order terminating the tenancy and evicting the Tenant.

Tenant’s s. 82 Claims

12.   We grant an abatement of rent for harassment and substantial interference in the amount of $750.00. It is appropriate that the amount owing by the Landlord for the abatement of rent should be set off against the arrears of rent that the Tenant owes.

13.   The Landlord must refrain from harassing the Tenant including but not limited to: sending offending emails to him and improperly disclosing his personal information

14.   The Landlord is ordered to do the following repairs within 30 days of the date of this order:

a.   Replace the ceiling light fixture in the kitchen;

b.   Repair or replace the window locking system to ensure security of the window.

15.   If the Landlord does not complete the repairs as outlined in paragraph 14, by July 15, 2019, the Tenant is authorized to arrange for the repairs and may recover the cost of the repairs by deducting the amount from the rent paid in the month after the repairs are completed.
