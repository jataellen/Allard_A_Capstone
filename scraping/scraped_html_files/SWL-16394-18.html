Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: SWL-16394-18

 


NR (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict KW and AB (or collectively the 'Tenants') because the Tenants did not pay the rent that the Tenants owe.

 

This application was heard in [CITY] on June 25, 2018.  The Landlord’s Legal Representative, SA, attended the hearing.  The Tenant’s Legal Representative, SH, and the Tenants also attended the hearing.  All my reasons are contained in this Order.  No further reasons will be issued.

 

Preliminary Issue: Credibility

 

I misspoke in this hearing noting at the end that I considered the Tenant’s evidence credible.  I ought to have said that the Tenant’s evidence was, in my view, genuine and honest.  The difference being that I do not consider the Tenant’s evidence sufficient to meet the onus and demonstrate the allegations she made are accurate, despite my conclusion that the Tenant believes this to be true. 

 

The Issues in Dispute

 

The Tenants, after reviewing the Landlord’s documentation at the outset of this hearing, accepted that the amount of arrears claimed to June 30, 2018 is accurate and correct.  There were therefore only two issues before me. 

 

First, the Tenants requested a repayment plan lasting eight months as a result of one Tenant’s status as a person with a disability and reliance on income supports to that end.  The Tenants would find prompt repayment financially burdensome, whereas the arrears owed to date are not very large and the Tenants have shown good faith in making significant payments to the date of this hearing.

 

Second, pursuant to section 82 of the Act the Tenant, KW, requested compensation in the amount of three months’ rent ($3,036.30) on the basis of the Landlord’s alleged conduct which KW felt substantially interfered with her enjoyment of the rental unit and constituted harassment.  KW also argued the Landlord illegally entered the rental unit on several unspecified occasions.  AB elected not to participate in this aspect of the hearing and did not wish to pursue remedies under section 82 of the Act. 

 

The Tenants were cautioned that if the allegations were not proven on a balance of probabilities, these matters would be considered res judicata going forward, meaning that a separate Tenants’ application could not be filed with respect to the same grounds.  The Tenants (who were represented) expressed that this was understood and KW elected to proceed in any event despite not having any documentary evidence or additional witnesses, including AB who refused to testify on these matters.

 

KW testified that the Landlord entered the rental unit without providing notice on several occasions.  KW was not able to provide any dates of entry.  Thus, lacking any specifics, I cannot determine that the Landlord was responsible for entry without notice. 

 

KW also testified that the Landlord moved her glasses, took her laundry – though it was returned at a later date, and put a hole in one of her tomatoes.  KW felt that the Landlord was responsible for a campaign of aggravation designed to frustrate and upset KW.  This included refusing to help KW move her furniture on the date she took possession of the rental unit and making various noises on unspecified dates.  I was not presented with any evidence that the Landlord agreed to move KW’s furniture or in any way was responsible for the same.  I was not presented with dates or times of any complaint raised by KW or any corroborative evidence from any external source. 

 

In Ball v. Metro Capital Property, [2002] O.J. No. 5931 (Divisional Court), the Court held that to succeed in raising an allegation, specific information must be provided so that the responding party is able to meaningfully reply to the allegations.  The Tenants did not provide sufficient details to support these allegations or to conclude that the Landlord is responsible for behaviour that amounts to substantial interference with their reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or harassment in this instance. 

 

The Landlord’s Legal Representative argued that there are many other, equally plausible, explanations for the Tenant’s experience and I agree.  It is as likely as not that the items “moved” by the Landlord were simply mislaid by the Tenant, or a guest of the rental unit.  There is no reliable basis on which I am able to conclude that the Landlord is responsible for the actions the Tenant described.  The details provided by the Tenant, and the supporting evidence, were simply too scant to withstand any scrutiny. 

 

This is not to suggest that I considered the Tenant’s evidence dishonest or even deliberately vague.  I found the Tenant genuine in her belief, but her assumptions are unlikely to be accurate no matter how earnestly she considers this to be so.  As a result, I find on a balance of probabilities that the Tenants did not meet the onus in demonstrating that the Landlord harassed or substantially interfered with their reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit up to the date of this hearing.  

 

Determinations:

 

1.      The Tenants have not paid the total rent they were required to pay for the period from August 1, 2017 to June 30, 2018.  Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination effective April 25, 2018.

2.      The Tenants are in possession of the rental unit.

3.      The monthly rent is $1,012.10.

4.      The Landlord collected a rent deposit of $950.00 from the Tenants and this deposit is still being held by the Landlord.

5.      Interest on the rent deposit is owing to the Tenants for the period from February 1, 2017 to April 25, 2018.

6.      The Tenants paid $1,945.00 after the application was filed.

7.      The parties agree that the amount outstanding to June 30, 2018, inclusive of rent arrears ($648.00), and costs ($190.00), is $838.00.

8.      I am not satisfied that the Landlord is responsible for the behaviour that KW alleged.  The Landlord’s Legal Representative argued that the Tenant’s complaints might just as easily have been attributed to another tenant in the residential complex, or to the other Tenant in this rental unit, AB.  I agree that the Tenant failed to demonstrate on a balance of probabilities that the Landlord is responsible for the behaviour the Tenant described. 

9.      I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would not be unfair to grant relief from eviction subject to  the condition(s) set out in this order pursuant to subsection 83(1)(a) and 204(1) of the Act.  I accepted the Tenants’ repayment plan as this appeared likely to be followed by the Tenants, likely to preserve the tenancy which is in keeping with the purposes of the Act, and unlikely to cause significant prejudice to the Landlord given the good faith payments made recently and the relatively minimal arrears owed on the date of the hearing.
