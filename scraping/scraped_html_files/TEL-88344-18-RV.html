Review Order

 

P.J. (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict M.H. and K.H. (the 'Tenants') because the Tenants did not pay the rent that the Tenants owe.

 

This application was resolved by order TEL-88344-18 issued on March 12, 2018.

 

On March 21, 2018, the Tenants requested a review of the order.

 

The request was heard in Toronto on April 5, 2018.

At the hearing the Tenants applied pursuant to section 82 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’) for an order determining that the Landlord or the Landlord's superintendent or the Landlord's agent substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or residential complex by the Tenant or by a member of the Tenant's household; withheld or deliberately interfered with the reasonable supply of a vital service that the Landlord is obligated to supply under the tenancy agreement; and failed to meet the Landlord's maintenance obligations under the Act or failed to comply with health, safety, housing or maintenance standards.

The Landlord and the first-named Tenant attended the hearing.  The Landlord was represented by P.M..  R.C. (the ‘Property Manager’) attended the hearing as a witness for the Landlord.

 

 

 

 

 

Determinations:

 

Request for Review

1.     The first-named Tenant testified that she has brain cancer and on the date of the hearing she was in hospital undergoing treatment.  There was also a note in the file from the Board staff stating the Tenant had called the Board to state she is in hospital and unable to attend the hearing scheduled for the next day.  The first-named Tenant also mentioned that she has trouble remembering things.

2.     The Tenant provided a copy of her hospital wrist band and a picture of her prescription she received on that day.

3.     The Tenant stated that the second-named Tenant, her daughter, was also unable to attend because she was out of the country.  The second-named Tenant is very ill and is currently suffering from mental illness is unable to attend this hearing.

4.     While the Landlord did not agree to the review, I am satisfied based on the evidence before the Board that the Tenants genuinely intended to participate in the hearing.  For this reason, the review is granted.

APPLICATION FOR NON-PAYMENT OF RENT

5.     The Tenants have not paid the total rent they were required to pay for the period from December 1, 2017 to April 30, 2018.  Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination effective January 30, 2018.

6.     The Landlord no longer holds a last month’s rent deposit as it was given back to the Tenants in December 2017.  The first-named Tenant agrees that the Landlord has given this amount back to the Tenants.

7.     The Landlord incurred costs of $190.00 for filing the application and entitled to reimbursement of those costs.

SECTION 82 ISSUES

8.     At the hearing, the Tenants brought forward claims under section 82.  Prior to proceeding with the Tenants’ claims I inquired as to whether the Tenants were ready to present their case and if they had all of their evidence ready to go.  The first-named Tenant stated that she was ready to proceed.

9.     At this time, I told the first-named Tenant to give the Landlord a copy of all of her evidence.  I also asked for a list of issues the Tenants wish to bring forward.  The Tenants’ list consists of the following issues:

a)   Roach infestation;

b)   Bedbug infestation;

c)   Rats the size of cats;

d)   Mice;

e)   Flooding;

f)     Disturbances from the upstairs unit; and

g)   Lack of heat.

10.  The Landlord stated he was ready to continue with the application based on the Tenants’ allegations. 

Infestation of Roaches, Bedbugs, Rats and Mice

11.  The first-named Tenant testified that she told the Landlord of the issue regarding the roaches and bedbugs in a conversation over the phone on November 15, 2017.

12.  The Tenant also said that she had notified the Landlord’s Property Manager about the issue via text message and/or email.  When asked to present the correspondence the Tenant searched her phone and then said she could not find the text messages.  Then the Tenant said it was sent by email.  She seemed to search her phone again and then stated she deletes all of her sent emails. 

13.  The Landlord provided a copy of a letter from S.Q.P.C. A.W.L.S. (‘S.Q.’) which notes they attended the basement unit three times prior to the Tenant moving in to the rental unit.  Each time S.Q. sprayed the rental unit. 

14.  The Tenant claims that S.Q. is not a real company.  She said that someone from Ontario Disability Support Program (‘ODSP’) told her that they looked up the company to find that it did not exist.  As this was a curious statement, I did a search on Google using the phone number provided on the invoice.  The Google search provided me with the company’s website, information on Canada411, and the company’s Facebook profile.  As I did this search the very next day after the hearing, I do not find it to be plausible that the company creating all of the internet data within one night. 

15.  The Property Manager testified that she viewed the rental unit when she first met the first-named Tenant on December 2, 2017.  She did not find any pests as described by the Tenant.  She also did an inspection with a pest control company a few weeks ago and neither the pest control company nor she found any pests as described by the Tenant.

16.  The Property Manager further stated that when she first met the first-named Tenant and the Tenant told her about bugs the Property Manager asked if she wanted a pest control company to attend the unit.  The Tenant refused the offer.

17.   The first-named Tenant said she did not tell the Landlord about the rats or mice that she saw approximately four weeks ago.  She said she did tell the Property Manager about them via text but, again, could not produce the text.  Then she stated it was via email and again said she does not keep her emails.

18.  Lastly, the Tenants presented a ‘Schedule A’ of an order dated November 16. 2017 from the City of Toronto (‘City’).  This schedule states that, “all properties shall at all times be kept free of roaches and other pests from condition which may encourage infestation by pests.”

19.  While I find the first-named Tenant’s testimony to be less than credible due to the inconsistency, I find the order from the City pivotal to the question of whether or not there were pests in the rental unit previously or during the tenancy.  I do not believe the pest level was at an infestation as the Tenant describes; however, the lack of response from the Landlord to continue to deal with the pest issue is questionable. 

20.  Having noted that, I believe the Property Manager when she stated that treatment was offered and the Tenants rejected the offer.  In this case, the Tenants are also culpable in this situation. 

21.  Given all of the above, I am satisfied the Landlord did not act in a timely and effective manner regarding the pests and for that reason remedy will granted below.

Flooding

22.  The first-named Tenant stated that there was a flood in the rental unit within the first week of her tenancy.  She then proceeded to say it was the first day of her tenancy on November 15, 2017.  The Tenant said she told the Landlord on this same day and the Landlord did not deal with the issue until November 29, 2017.  She submitted copies of Plumbing Field Invoices from Enercare who attended the unit on November 29 to assess and fix the problem.  The Tenant submitted pictures, but it is difficult to determine what is going on in each picture other than an unclean floor in one.

23.  The parties agree that the issue was from the upstairs rental unit and the plumbing which created the leak into the basement unit. 

24.  Representatives from Enercare attended twice on November 29, 2017.  The first representative noted that they snaked pipes and was sending another representative later on in the day.  The second representative notes that the drain was snaked again.  The furnace area was very wet upon arrival and they were unsure of the source of the water. 

25.  The first-named Tenant provided additional testify that there were other leaks on December 5, 2017 and January 23, 2018; however, provided no further supporting evidence of these leaks.

26.  The Landlord testified that the issue had been fixed with the snaking of the drains and that there were no other leaks.  The Property Manager testified that she received no correspondence from the Tenants about the leaks as it happened prior to her being hired in December 2017.

27.  Based on the evidence before the Board, I am satisfied there was only one leak and the Landlord dealt with the leak in a timely and effective manner.  No remedy is granted for this issue.

Disturbances from Upstairs Tenants

28.  The first-named Tenant claimed the police have attended the residential complex 108 times since she moved in November 15, 2017.  She stated that the police told her she could get a copy of the police occurrences, but the Tenant failed to provide this information saying that it would have taken her a month to get the report.  As I told the Tenant at the hearing, the Tenant was asked whether she was ready to proceed with her claim and whether she had all of her evidence.  She confirmed she did and we began the hearing. 

29.  The first-named Tenant said she spoke with the Property Manager about the disturbances and the Property Manager confirmed this conversation.  She also confirmed that the Landlord gave the Tenants back their last month’s rent deposit, in addition to paying the Tenants money for storage and moving so the Tenants could find a new location due to the fact that the Tenants were unhappy with the upstairs tenants. 

30.  Furthermore, the Property Manager attempted to find the Tenants a new temporary location in order to appease the Tenants and the Tenants refused the offer because it would mean they would be in a house with a man. 

31.  The first-named Tenant went on to say that her daughter attempted suicide because of the upstairs tenants; however, provided no evidence to support her extremely serious declaration.  This does not mean I do not believe the second-named Tenant may have attempted to take her own life.  I believe such an issue is of the utmost import and must be dealt with appropriately.  What the first-named Tenant failed to determine is that it is as a result of the upstairs tenants as opposed to possible mental illness that she stated her daughter suffers from.

32.  The Landlord stated that he has taken the necessary steps for eviction regarding the upstairs tenants due to the complaints received.  He currently has a date of Sheriff enforcement of April 16, 2018 for these tenants.

33.  Here, the Landlord took appropriate action to rectify the issue of the upstairs tenants and the disturbances by the tenants.  The Landlord said he had no knowledge of 108 police visits, but he was aware of a few visits. 

34.  I am satisfied the Landlord did as he was supposed to in this instance.  For this reason, no remedy will be awarded for this situation.

Lack of Heat

35.  The first-named Tenant stated there was no heat at all in the basement unit since November 2017.  There is only one air vent located in the kitchen of the unit.  She also declared that she was unable to buy a portable heater because her daughter has ‘winter skin’ and the heat source must be a gas furnace. 

36.  The Property Manager admittedly saw the first-named Tenant dressed in two robes and boots when she first met the Tenant; however, she did not mention feeling cold in the unit.  While the Property Manager was in the unit she asked the upstairs tenant to turn on the furnace and when he did, the air vent was blowing hot air. 

37.  The first-named Tenant asked for an extra vent to be put in the basement in the hallway.  The Landlord stated he would have reimbursed her for work done by her husband as the Tenant said her husband could put the extra vent in.  The Tenant said her husband said that the work could not be done due to the state of the wall.  Her husband did not attend the hearing to testify.

38.  In this case, the Tenants failed to provide sufficient evidence to prove their allegation of no heat in the rental unit.   

39.  This claim is dismissed.

Section 82 Remedies Requested

40.  The Tenants requested the following remedies: abatement of rent; out-of-pocket expenses; and damages arising from the breach in the nature of pain and suffering.

41.  Abatement of the rent is the most common remedy awarded to tenants in these kinds of situations. It is intended to reflect the idea that if a tenant is paying rent for a bundle of goods and services and not receiving everything being paid for, then the tenant is entitled to abatement of the rent proportional to the difference between what is being paid for and what is being received.

42.  Here, the Tenants had a concern about a pest issue, the Landlord offered treatment, and the Tenants refused the offer.  The Landlord did give back the Tenants’ last month’s rent deposit, and paid for storage and moving costs with the understanding the Tenants were moving.  Instead, the Tenants stayed on and continued the tenancy.

43.  Given the above, I believe the abatement of 50% of rent charged from November 15, 2017 to February 28, 2018 as requested by the Tenants is unreasonable.  Given my knowledge of like applications, a reasonable abatement for this period is 10% of the rent charged or $331.07.

44.  The Tenants led insufficient evidence to support their claim for out-of-pocket expenses and for damages arising from the breach in the nature of pain and suffering in the amount of $4,000.00.

Relief from Eviction

45.  The first-named Tenant testified that she is partially moved out and she will be vacating the rental unit on or before April 30, 2018.  She also stated that she has withheld $3,000.00 in rent and she has this money available at this time. 

46.  Taking into consideration the reimbursement of the Tenants’ last month’s rent deposit already to aid in the Tenants’ next rental unit and the money given for storage and moving, I believe the Tenants’ request to prolong payment for 45 days is unreasonable.

47.  Also, the Tenants are already partially moved out.  To delay termination at this point does not seem reasonable.

48.  I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would be unfair to grant relief from eviction pursuant to subsection 83(1) of the Act.

49.  An order with standard terms for payment shall issue.

50.  This order contains all of the reasons for my decision within it. No further reasons shall be issued. 

It is ordered that:

 

1.     Order TEL-88344-18 issued on March 12, 2018 is cancelled and replaced by the following:

2.     Unless the Tenant voids the order as set out below, the tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenants is terminated.  The Tenants must move out of the rental unit on or before April 17, 2018.

3.     The Tenants shall pay to the Landlord $3,617.10*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to April 6, 2018 and less the amount of the abatement the Landlord owes the Tenants.

4.     The Tenants shall also pay to the Landlord $31.23 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting April 7, 2018 to the date they move out of the unit.

5.     The Tenants shall also pay to the Landlord $190.00 for the cost of filing the application.

6.     If the unit is not vacated on or before April 17, 2018, then starting April 18, 2018, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

7.     Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord, on or after April 18, 2018.

8.     If, on or before April 17, 2018, the Tenants pay the amount of $4,840.00** to the Landlord or to the Board in trust, this order for eviction will be void.  This means that the tenancy would not be terminated and the Tenants could remain in the unit.  If this payment is not made in full and on time, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

9.     The Tenants may make a motion to the Board under subsection 74(11) of the Act to set aside this order if they pay the amount required under that subsection on or after April 18, 2018 but before the Sheriff gives vacant possession to the Landlord. The Tenants are only entitled to make this motion once during the period of the tenancy agreement with the Landlord.
