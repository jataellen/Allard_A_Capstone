Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

 

 

 

 


The Tenant, MFA, applied for an order determining that the Landlord, TB, deliberately interfered with the reasonable supply of a vital service that the Landlord is obligated to supply under the tenancy agreement and harassed, obstructed, coerced, threatened or interfered with the Tenant (T2 application- CET-72638-18).

The day prior to the commencement of the hearing, the Landlord filed an L1 application to terminate the tenancy and to evict the Tenant because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes (L1 application- CEL-73985-18).

 

The Tenant acknowledged that he did not pay the rent he was required to pay for the period from January 1, 2018 to February 28, 2018. Accordingly and with the consent of the parties, these applications were heard together in Mississauga on March 1, 2018.

 

The Tenant also acknowledged that he incorrectly included TCH as a Landlord in his application. At the hearing THC was deleted as a Landlord party.

 

The Landlord, the Landlordâs spouse and the Tenant attended the hearing.

 

Determinations and Reasons:

 

1.      The monthly rent is $650.00. The Tenant has not paid the total rent of $1,300.00 he was required to pay for the period January 1, 2018 to February 28, 2018.

2.      The Landlord collected a rent deposit of $500.00 from the Tenant and this deposit is still being held by the Landlord.

3.      Interest on the rent deposit is owing to the Tenant for the period from November 1, 2016 to February 28, 2018.

Inadequate Heat

4.      The rental unit is a studio apartment. The Tenant stated that throughout the winter of 2016-2017 his rental unit was cold. However, he did not dispute that the first complaint he made to the Landlord about the inadequacy of heat was on October 1, 2017 when he sent a text message to the landlord stating: âI spent last winter without heat. I will appreciate if you do something about that this year.â Based on the response from the Landlord, it would appear this complaint came as a surprise. He sent a text message back to the Tenant stating: âWhat do you mean without heat. Thereâs heat in there. I will get the woman upstairs to turn it up.â Indeed, the Tenant acknowledged to the Landlord in one of his text messages on December 15, 2017 that he never complained about the heat not working during the winter of 2016-2017, adding that he could not live with inadequate heat again this year.

5.      The Landlord said he contacted the person who lived above the Tenant and she reported to the Landlord that she was not experiencing a problem with the heat in her unit.

6.      On November 10, 2017 the Tenant followed up with the same complaint about inadequate heat. He sent a text message to the Landlord asking the Landlord to do something about the heat and complained that it is ridiculous that he has to wear a jacket to go to the bathroom which is accessed through the hall entrance to the Tenantâs rental unit. The Landlord promised to get O, the handyman who occupies a back apartment in the premises, to investigate. The Landlord said that O checked the rental unit in November and found no problem with the heat. However, the Tenant said that all O did was to change the filters on the furnace, but that had made no difference.

7.      On December 15, 2017 the Tenant repeated by text message to the Landlord that he still did not have enough heat and that he was fed up. The Tenant also demanded that the Landlord not send O to his door again because he does not know anything about heating, ventilation and air conditioning (HVAC). He warned the Landlord that his next step would be a complaint to the Landlord and Tenant Board. On December 16, 2017 the Tenant repeated his complaint by text message to the Landlord.

8.      Initially in his testimony, the Landlord insisted there was no problem with the heat. He said O checked the rental unit in November and December and found nothing to complain about. He also said O had lived in the rental unit before the Tenant took occupancy and had no problem. He claimed O told him he had to open a window in his back apartment presumably because he found it too warm. O was not a witness at the hearing. The Landlord suggested that the Tenant and O had a falling out that resulted in the Tenant refusing to allow O to look at the rental unit. The written evidence, however, does not support these assertions.

9.      On the morning of December 18, 2017, the Tenant sent a text message to the Landlord stating that he got home in time to meet the guy the Landlord sent to look into the heat issue. He went on to write that that person has no knowledge of HVAC; rather, he does renovations. At the hearing the Tenant said the person who attended then was merely a handyman who talked just about fixing the front door. That day the Tenant sent the Landlord a series of seven unanswered messages complaining about being ignored and how unfairly he was being treated.

10.   On December 21, 2018, the Tenant again warned by text message he would be taking proceedings against the Landlord.

11.   The Landlord stated he told the Tenant he would call Winston to fix the heating problem but the Tenant insisted on 48 hoursâ notice to enter his rental unit. Indeed, he said the Tenant always insisted on 48 hoursâ notice.

12.   At first, the Landlord stated that Winston went to the rental unit three times but he was not allowed in and each time the Landlord was charged $100. On further questioning, the Landlord said that W was allowed to enter on the third occasion that he visited the rental unit and that the Landlord was only charged $100 by W.

13.   The Tenantâs version of what happened at his rental unit differs, however, from the Landlord and has the added advantage that it is memorialized in text messages made concurrently with the events. On December 22, 2017 the Tenant complained by text message that he had made himself available that morning but the Landlord cancelled the visit at the last minute.

14.   On December 23, 2017 the Landlord sent a text message to the Tenant at 7:28 P.M. informing him that O had arranged for a technician to attend the rental unit the next day between 10:00 A.M. and 12:00 P.M. The Tenant immediately responded that he had told O he was not available. After some communication back and forth regarding why, among other things, the Tenant refused to leave the rental unit open to a stranger, the Tenant again agreed to wait for the technician, which he did. However, the technician again failed to attend as arranged with the result that the Landlord received a barrage of unanswered complaints from the Tenant.

15.   On December 26, 2017 the Landlord sent a text message to the Tenant offering to have the technician visit his rental unit on December 30, 2017 at a time convenient to the Tenant between 10:00 A.M. and midnight. They agreed on a time between 10:00 A.M. and 1:00 P.M. The Tenant said the technician attended and told him only one vent is connected to the furnace and that to remedy the problem would be a big job involving breaking through the drywall and removing and installing pipes.

16.   On December 28, 2017 the Landlord sent a text message to the Tenant telling him the problem with the heat cannot be fixed until the weather changes, by which I took him to mean when it became warmer. The Landlord also suggested in that text message that the Tenant move out. At the hearing the Tenant stated that the Landlord had asked the Tenant to let him lock up his place so that he could do the repair in the summer. The Tenant said it was his understanding the Landlord had asked him to find a new place to live.

17.   However, the Landlord stated he asked the tenant to move out for two days and stay in a room at his sisterâs place while the repairs were undertaken but the Tenant had refused. I prefer the Tenantâs version of these communications for reasons I will set out later in this judgment, however, I pause here to say that the Tenantâs alleged refusal of an empty bedroom at the Landlordâs sisterâs residence, I find, would be a reasonable position to take. It would be asking too much of the Tenant to expect him to experience the inconvenience of living in a room at a place with total strangers because of a problem of the landlordâs own making.

18.   The Landlord stated that he did not want to undertake the repair of the heating problem while the Tenant resided in the rental unit because it would not be in the best interest of the Tenant to endure a mess and significant dust. One would expect this reasoning would come from the Tenant rather than the Landlord. More importantly, the Landlord told the Tenant in writing he would not be undertaking the needed repairs until the warmer weather arrived which is consistent with the Landlord intending to do nothing to rectify the inadequate heat in the rental unit in the coming winter months rather than remedying the problem in two days. Accordingly and having regard to the Tenantâs insistence he not have to live without adequate heat, it is likely the Landlord was inviting the Tenant to leave the rental unit permanently.

19.   The undisputed evidence was that at the end of December 2017 the Landlordâs spouse and the Tenant spoke on the telephone and she then dropped off the two portable heaters she had bought. Thereafter the Tenant looked for a new place to rent which he has found and has taken possession of it on March 1, 2018.

20.   The Landlord explained that in September 2015 he had the baseboard heaters removed from the rental unit and had a family member, who had earlier installed the furnace, connect a new vent to the furnace. It is also not disputed that only one vent was actually connected to the furnace and that the repair to connect all of the vents would be a major undertaking.  

21.   Thus, I find the Landlord failed to provide to the rental unit adequate heat, which is an essential service the Landlord is obliged to provide.

22.   Section 21 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006, (the âActâ) provides:

21 (1) A landlord shall not at any time during a tenantâs occupancy of a rental unit and before the day on which an order evicting the tenant is executed, withhold the reasonable supply of any vital service, care service or food that it is the landlordâs obligation to supply under the tenancy agreement or deliberately interfere with the reasonable supply of any vital service, care service or food.

23.   In Onyskiw v CJM Property Management Ltd,[1] the often referred to decision of the Ontario Court of Appeal on the landlordâs duty to maintain rental premises, a six-story building had its single elevator out of service for 96 days over four periods of time. The parties agreed that the landlord acted reasonably throughout to deal with the elevator problems. Nevertheless, the tenants sought an abatement of rent for the periods the elevator was not operational. Applying a contextual analysis, the Ontario Court of Appeal denied the Tenantsâ claims.

24.   The present case, however, is much different from the situation dealt with by the courts in Onyskiw. In this case it cannot be said the Landlord had an effective program of preventative maintenance and addressed the problem in a reasonable and timely manner. The rental unit lacked an adequate heating system when the tenancy began. Judging by later inspection, this deficiency likely would have been known to the Landlord had he engaged in a reasonable inspection before the Tenant took occupancy of the rental unit. Because the Tenant did not complain until October 2017, I find the Tenant was unnecessarily exposed to inadequate heat for a period of approximately four months due to the Landlordâs failure to provide and maintain the heating system in the rental unit in a good state of repair.

25.   The Tenant is entitled to a lump sum abatement of rent equal to 25% of the monthly rent for four months which totals $650.00 for the lack of an adequate heating system, though I recognize there were some warm days in the early part of that period and that eventually heaters were provided, one of which sat on the floor in the hallway. Also, in determining this amount, I have taken into consideration the impact on the Tenant and I have considered that despite the inadequate temperature in the rental unit, the Tenant was still able to live in the unit and he should pay some rent for the services and facilities he continued to receive.

26.   The Tenant is also entitled to $50.00 for the application filing fee the Tenant incurred.

27.   The $650.00 rent abatement and the $50.00 filing fee are deducted from the undisputed rent arrears that the Tenant owes the Landlord.

28.   I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would not be necessary to grant relief from eviction pursuant to subsection 83(1) of the Act. The Tenant has already moved out of the rental unit.

Harassment, Obstruction, Coercion, Threatening or Interference with the Tenant

29.    While there is no definition of âharassmentâ in the RTA, it is generally held that âharassmentâ is a course of conduct that a reasonable person knows or ought to know would be unwelcome.

30.   The Tenantâs text messages to the Landlord were critical of the Landlordâs delay in responding to him and there was an occasion on December 28, 2017 on the telephone when the conversation became heated resulting in the landlord hanging up on the Tenant. The Landlord wanted it understood that delays in responding to the Tenantâs text messages were due to him having gone to Florida from January 3, 2018 to January 6th and from there he went on to Jamaica where, he said, internet service was poor and expensive. He returned to Ontario on January 18, 2018.I note, however, the delays that the Tenant complained about in his text messages occurred before the Landlord left Canada.

31.   I find the gist of the Landlordâs behaviour that the Tenant has complained about is essentially grounded on the Landlordâs lack of professionalism, by which I took the Tenant to mean his unreasonableness. That behaviour is so entwined in the failure to provide adequate heat that granting relief for harassment, I find, would amount to double recovery which I am not prepared to award.

32.   In addition, I am not satisfied the communications or lack thereof on the part of the Landlord rise to the level of constituting harassment or any of the other forms of inappropriate conduct listed in the T2 application. At the hearing which took place on the morning of March 1, 2018, the Tenant returned the keys to the rental unit to the Landlord. The Landlord requested to terminate the tenancy in his L1 application. The tenancy is terminated as of February 28, 2018.

 

It is ordered that:

 

 

1.      The tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated as of February 28, 2018.

2.      The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $89.56*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to February 28, 2018, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlord owes on the rent deposit and less the amount of the abatement the Landlord owes the Tenant.

3.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $175.00 for the cost of filing the application.

4.      If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing* on or before March 16, 2018, the Tenant will start to owe interest. This will be simple interest calculated from March 17, 2018 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

5.      If the unit is not vacated on or before February 28, 2018, then starting March 1, 2018, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

6.      Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord, on or after March 1, 2018.
