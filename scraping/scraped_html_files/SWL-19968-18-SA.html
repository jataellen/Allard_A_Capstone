Order under Sections 69 and 77(8)

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Numbers: SWL-19968-18-SA

SWL-20358-18

 

 

 

 


NW (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict AJY and BM (the 'Tenants') because the Tenants did not pay the rent that the Tenants owe (the ‘L1 Application’).

 

The Landlord also applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict the Tenants because the Tenants entered into an agreement to terminate the tenancy (the ‘L3 Application’).  That application was resolved by order SWL-19958-18 issued on August 8, 2018.  The Tenants filed a motion to set aside order SWL-19968-18.

 

The L1 Application and the Tenants’ motion were heard in Stratford on August 31, 2018.  The Landlord, the Landlord’s Legal Counsel, GKC, the Tenant, BM, and the witnesses, TW and MG, attended the hearing.  While KM initially signed the attendance register, he did not represent the Tenants at the hearing.  BM attended on behalf of both Tenants and spoke with Duty Counsel prior to the hearing.

 

Determinations and Reasons:

The L1 Application

 

1.     The Tenants have not paid the total rent they were required to pay for the period from June 1, 2018 to September 30, 2018.  Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination effective August 15, 2018.

2.     The Tenants are in possession of the rental unit.

3.     The monthly rent is $900.00.

4.     The Tenants have made no payments since the application was filed. 

5.     The Landlord collected a rent deposit of $900.00 from the Tenants and this deposit is still being held by the Landlord.

6.     Interest on the rent deposit is owing to the Tenants for the period from April 28, 2018 to August 15, 2018.

7.     The rental unit is located on the second floor of the residential complex, above T.W.’s commercial office where he operates his business as a chiropractor.  M.G. is T.W.’s receptionist and her desk is located in T.W.’s office.  While the Nora Walden (‘N.W.’) is the named Landlord on the tenancy agreement, T.W. typically attends to the daily management of the residential complex and serves as the Landlord’s primarily agent for interactions with the Tenants.

8.     The parties agreed that the amount outstanding to August 31, 2018, inclusive of rent arrears ($1,800.00) and costs ($175.00), is $1,975.00.  Although B.M. later equivocated on this acknowledgment, he ultimately offered no evidence to contradict the Landlord’s calculations of the balance outstanding.  

9.     B.M. ruminated at length on whether or not to raise other issues with respect to the tenancy at the hearing pursuant to section 82 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’) or to file his own applications regarding these issues.  Although he ultimately never announced any clear decision on this point, he did describe several other incidents of alleged harassment by T.W. beyond the events that provided the basis and context for the set aside motion, 

10.  For example, B.M. testified that when T.W. first showed the rental unit to the Tenants, he remarked that the Tenants would be “shitting in my toilet” and that their feces would be coursing through his pipes, all the while gripping his genitalia and generally intimidating them.  This bizarre incident allegedly occurred even before the tenancy began.  However, despite this ignominious introduction to the residential complex and alleged rumours from disgruntled former tenants that T.W. was insane, B.M. nonetheless concluded that the rental unit represented a good deal and maintained this assessment even at the hearing.    

11.  He also recounted hearsay from the other Tenant, A.Y.J., with respect to an incident on or about May 1, 2018 when A.J.Y. alleged that T.W. entered the rental unit while B.M. was at work.  According to B.M., .J.Y. had claimed that T.W. had menaced him, forcing him to go into the basement and ordering him to do “this and that,” apparently with respect to a malfunction with the plumbing that had resulted in sewage spraying “all over the toilet” in the commercial portion of the building. 

12.  B.M. also stated that after the Tenants signed the N11 Agreement to End the Tenancy (the ‘N11 Agreement’), T.W. felt entitled to enter the rental unit without notice.  He stated that A.J.Y. had informed him that on an unspecified date, he awoke to discover T.W. standing over him naked, ranting that the Tenants were not permitted to store their underwear on the carpet. 

13.  Finally, B.M. stated that T.W. had on an unspecified date forbidden A.J.Y. from cooking curry or any Asian cuisine on the basis that he is of a non-Caucasian ethnicity.

14.   As A.J.Y. did not testify at the hearing, his statements to B.M. were hearsay.  The essential defining features of hearsay are an out‑of‑court statement adduced to prove the truth of its contents in the absence of a contemporaneous opportunity to cross‑examine the declarant.  A.J.Y.’s statements were also not reduced to writing.

15.   The function of the trier of fact is to guard against undue reliance on hearsay evidence which is unnecessary or the reliability of which is neither readily apparent from the trustworthiness of its contents nor capable of being meaningfully tested at the hearing. 

16.   I find that to assign this hearsay statement the same evidentiary value as direct testimony without allowing the Landlord’s Legal Counsel any means of cross examining A.J.Y. on these allegations would be unduly prejudicial under the circumstances.  While subsection 15(1) of the Statutory Powers Procedures Act, 1990 permits admission of unsworn hearsay statements and allows the Board Member to assign it the appropriate probative weight, for the evidentiary concerns I have outlined above, I would assign this evidence virtually no weight at all.

17.   T.W. flatly denied all of the Tenants’ allegations.  He testified that on July 10, 2018, he attended at the rental unit with M.G. to determine whether the Tenants had cleaned the residence sufficiently to allow him to show it to prospective renters.  He had served the Tenants with 24 hours’ notice as well as a letter setting out a number of things he wanted the Tenants to do to render the unit turnover ready.  Since the Tenants had agreed to vacate the rental unit effective July 29, 2018, he was authorized to enter the unit to show it to prospective tenants pursuant to subsection 26(3) of the Act. 

18.   After knocking on the door, he loudly announced his presence, shouting, “Joel, we’re entering; we know you’re home.”  He testified that he observed A.J.Y. sitting in a chair with a blanket pulled up to his chin; B.M. was not present.  A.J.Y. assured T.W. that B.M. would pay the funds owing to the Landlord as soon as possible.  T.W. and M.G. inspected the rental unit and found it in a generally disheveled state, not fit for showing.  As they exited, T.W. encountered A.J.Y. sitting on a stool, wearing only boxer shorts.  Remaining roughly six feet from A.J.Y., T.W. informed him that he would be showing the rental unit as soon as the next day and reminded him that the Tenants were scheduled to move out of the unit on July 29, 2018.  A.J.Y. became irate with him as he believed that T.W. was talking down to him.  M.G. testified that A.J.Y. was outside of her line of sight when he and T.W. exchanged these parting comments, she confirmed T.W.’s account of the content of this conversation.

19.   Later, in light of B.M.’s subsequent allegations of sexual assault, he suspected that the Tenants had set him up.  Specifically, B.M. attended at T.W.’s office on August 14, 2018 and threatened to accuse him publicly of sexual misconduct if he did not negotiate with the Tenants.  He noted that such an accusation could ruin T.W.’s reputation as a chiropractor.  M.G. overheard this conversation from her desk in the next room and confirmed T.W.’s account in her own testimony.  Significantly, B.M. did not challenge any part of the testimony offered by T.W. or M.G., including their evidence that he had attempt to extort more favourable terms for the termination of the tenancy.

20.   Between B.M.’s unsupported and rather fanciful hearsay and T.W.’s direct testimony, the latter corroborated by M.G., on the balance of probabilities, I prefer T.W.’s version of these events as the more persuasive evidence. 

21.   As a result, I find that B.M. failed to demonstrate on the balance of probabilities that the Landlord or her agent, T.W., harassed, obstructed, coerced, threatened the Tenants or substantially interfered with their reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or the residential complex.

22.  Although B.M. repeatedly stated that he required additional time to vacate the rental unit, he offered no details in support of this request.  Despite blaming T.W. for a temporary loss of employment, he confirmed on several occasions that he has a new job and can pay the balance outstanding.  As a result, I do not find that any postponement of eviction is warranted.

23.  I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Act and find that it would be unfair to grant relief from eviction pursuant to subsection 83(1) of the Act.

24.  The Landlord’s Counsel acknowledged that the eviction provisions in the L1 Application would be redundant should the Landlord enforce the eviction ordered by order SWL-19968-18, dated August 8, 2018.  However, he stated a preference to maintain termination provisions in both orders out of an abundance of caution, given the highly contentious relationship between the parties.  It should be noted that once the stay of order SWL-19968-18 has been lifted, the voiding options included in the L1 Application at paragraphs 8 and 9 of the order below would be rendered unenforceable.

The Tenants’ Set Aside Motion

25.  There was no dispute at the hearing that the parties signed the N11 Agreement on July 9, 2018 (the ‘N11 Agreement’) with a termination date of July 29, 2018 and that the Tenants did not vacate the rental unit on or before that date. 

26.  However, B.M. testified that he signed the N11 Agreement under duress and took the position that the Landlord and Tenant Board (the ‘Board’) should therefore set aside order SWL-19968-18, issued on August 8, 2018.  Specifically, his written statement filed in support of the motion read as follows:

The Landlord trapped me in forceable [sic] confinment [sic] and threatened me to sign a termination aggrement; [sic] I went striaght [sic] to the police and made and doccumented [sic] the harrassment [sic] in a police report with charges pending, the officer told him the L2 was null and void I have a police report the order is null and void [sic]

27.  Significantly, the above statement was signed by only B.M. and not A.J.Y.  As noted above, A.J.Y. did not attend the hearing or submit any statement to the Board recounting his own version of events.  However, T.W. and B.M. largely agreed that A.J.Y. is significantly less assertive compared to B.M. and is to some extent subservient to his will.

28.  By way of background, the tenancy commenced on May 1, 2018.  Term 7 of the tenancy agreement, signed by the parties on April 28, 2018, assigns responsibility for payment of utilities to the Tenants:

7.     Utilities to be paid by the Tenant(s) are electricity, water, sewage, and garbage removal.  Any other services/utilities such as cable, telephone, and internet are the responsibility of the Tenant(s) to arrange and pay for.   

29.  B.M. testified that on June 22, 2018, T.W. attended at the rental unit to collect payment for an unpaid hydro bill.  B.M. objected to his presence in the common areas of the residential complex, preferring T.W. to contact him first before approaching the rental unit.  When B.M. answered the door, T.W. showed him a bill in T.W.’s name and demanded payment.  B.M. informed him that he only had funds to pay the rent due in a few days.  He testified that T.W. then reached through the door and tried to grab him by the shoulders, apparently intending to drag him from the unit and down the stairs.  He stated that T.W. said, “I’ll take you to the fucking bank right now to pay this bill” and “blah, blah, blah.”  He emphasized the accuracy of the latter quote later in the hearing.

30.  Under cross-examination, B.M. reluctantly acknowledged that T.W. did not actually make contact with him when he reached through the doorway, but attributed this not to any shortage of violent intent on T.W.’s part, but to his own preternaturally cat-like reflexes as a professional skate boarder that allowed him to move quickly out of T.W.’s reach.

31.  B.M. testified that he informed T.W. that he was assaulting him and stated that he was going to call the police immediately.  He shut the door, but this did not deter T.W., who continued to stalk about outside the door, angrily threatening to evict the Tenants.  B.M. stated that he was “so scared” and said, “Look man, settle down” and slid the requested funds under the door, pleading for T.W. to leave or he would call the police. 

32.  He told T.W. that because he took the hydro money, he was no longer responsible for payment of the rent for July, 2018.  He told T.W. that he would be short on the upcoming rent and T.W. would have to wait until July 3, 2018 when B.M. was next paid.  He took the position that this would be T.W.’s fault since he “stole” the hydro money.  He stated that T.W. told him “that won’t do.”  B.M. then advised T.W. that their relationship was now terminated due to T.W.’s conduct.  B.M. stated that T.W. then presented him with the N11 Agreement.  Needing to get to work and unable to “handle any of that crap,” B.M. initially refused to sign as he was an ideal tenant.  However, according to B.M., since T.W. had B.M. cornered in the rental, he was “forced” to sign the N11 Agreement in order to escape and go to work.  Eventually, T.W. left and B.M. was able to exit the rental unit.

33.  However, he stated that T.W. then chased him while he tried to flee the premises in his car.  He said that T.W. reached into the car, impeding B.M.’s ability to drive as both grappled for control of the vehicle.  As B.M. noted, “This pissed me off” and he attended at the “cop shop” to disclose the T.W.’s “heinous crimes” and “savagery.”  Why T.W. would have chased B.M. after had already obtained his signature on the N11 Agreement was not clarified.

34.  Later, when A.J.Y. alleged that T.W. had been peeping through the window at him, B.M.’s anger built up.  He stated that he has since refused to pay any further rent to the Landlord.  As noted above, he blamed T.W. for the loss of his job, but did not explain how this occurred.  B.M.’s account of these events also does not explain how the N11 Agreement came to be signed and dated by both Tenants on July 9, 2018, not June 22, 2018, despite his testimony that he alone signed the form when T.W. coerced his cooperation during the harrowing encounter on the earlier date.

35.  B.M. testified that the police investigated his allegations by attending at T.W.’s residence and ordered him not to harass the Tenants.  Although T.W. agreed that the police spoke with him, they simply recorded his version of events and did not take either party’s side in the conflict.  Both he and B.M. agreed that B.M. was not privy to this interview.  Instead, B.M. claimed that the officer later reported the substance of this exchange to him.  As well, in his written motion to set aside the prior order, he claimed there were “charges pending” against T.W.

36.  At the hearing, the Landlord’s Counsel submitted an occurrence report obtained by B.M.  The report confirmed that the Stratford Police Service (‘S.P.S.’) investigated B.M.’s allegations that T.W. had assaulted him on June 22, 2018 and invaded the rental unit on July 10, 2018 to sexually harass A.J.Y.  Having interview both B.M. and T.W., the officers concluded the report with the following:

All parties were advised there were no grounds to lay charges at this time and that the matter at hand is a Landlord/Tenant Tribunal matter and not a police matter at this time.

This concludes police involvement in this matter.

37.  When the Landlord’s counsel confronted B.M. on the considerable disparity between the police report and his written claim that the police intended to charge T.W. with a criminal offence, B.M. was initially evasive.  Eventually, he agreed that the police report did not reflect his version of events.  He expressed displeasure with the S.P.S. since having paid to obtain the police report under the Municipal Freedom of Information and Protection of Personal Privacy Act, he expected the police provide a report that reflected his version of events. 

38.  He also attempted to explain the lack of criminal charges against T.W. by stating that the police complied with his instructions not to “press charges” against T.W.  Despite B.M.’s claims of a vast knowledge of the legal process, it would appear this latter story is derived more from American television than reality.  Lastly, he speculated that the report may not correlate with his expectations because the building he entered to provide his complaint on July 10, 2018 may not have been a real police station.

39.  Ultimately, I find that B.M.’s account of his interactions with the police regarding “charges pending” against T.W. is simply a fabrication and that the officers never ordered T.W. to stop harassing the Tenants or that they instructed T.W. not to enter the unit as he claimed at the hearing.  Similarly, I find that his testimony that the officers offered a legal opinion that order SWL-19968-18, issued on August 8, 2018, was “null and void” is also not truthful.  Nonetheless, despite the content of the police report, B.M. insisted that his version of events was accurate, asserting that whatever he says is by definition fact.

40.  By contrast, I find that T.W. and M.G. offered a more persuasive albeit less exciting account of the events of June 22 and July 9, 2018.

41.  T.W. testified that at the beginning of the tenancy, he had told the Tenants that they must set up an account with Festival Hydro to assume payment for the utilities as required by the tenancy agreement. 

42.  As they had not complied by June 2, 2018, the hydro bill for the rental unit came to him.  He sent a letter to B.M., reminding him of his obligations under the tenancy agreement and informed him that the Tenants owed the Landlord $152.00.  He agreed that he attended outside the rental unit on June 22, 2018 and requested payment of the outstanding hydro balance and that B.M. refused to pay on the grounds that he had no money.  He offered to take B.M. to his bank to obtain the necessary funds, but vehemently denied even attempting to grab B.M.  When B.M. announced his intention to delay payment of the upcoming rent to a date of his own convenience, T.W. simply stated that he was not happy with this turn of events.

43.  On July 9, 2018, after the Tenants had failed to pay the monthly rent for June and July, 2018, T.W. invited them to his office and proposed two options to resolve the issue: they could sign the N11 Agreement or he would serve them with an N4 Notice to terminate the tenancy for unpaid rent.  He denied ever blocking their path out of his office or using any threats or profanity in the course of this conversation.  He stated that if the Tenants had wished to leave, they could have simply opened the door behind them.  He agreed that the encounter was confrontational, but attributed the hostility to B.M. who assumed a belligerent attitude throughout the exchange, at one point offering to engage T.W. in a physical fight.

44.  While B.M. incorrectly claimed that he could simply walk away from the tenancy and scoffed at T.W.’s request that they clean the rental unit before vacating it, both Tenants voluntarily chose to sign the N11 Agreement.  

45.  Having little reason to trust the Tenants after their repeated failures to live up to their obligations under the tenancy agreement, he asked M.G. to listen to their conversation from her desk out of view of the Tenants.  While she could not see all parties during their conversations and missed some statements by the Tenants, I find that she substantially confirmed T.W.’s account of the events for both June 22 and July 9, 2018.

46.  As noted above, B.M. chose not to cross-examine either T.W. or M.G. on their evidence, opting instead to berate them with his own version of events at them, maintaining his position that he and A.J.Y. are victims and entitled to unspecified but substantial compensation.

47.  I find that B.M. intentionally conflated the events of June 22 and July 9, 2018 when he testified that T.W. obtained his signature on the N11 Agreement at the door of the rental unit on June 22 in order to falsely portray his acquiescence to T.W.’s demands as the product of duress and intimidation. 

48.  T.W.’s version of events is simply more logical and supported by the other evidence.  I find that he left the rental unit after B.M. announced his intention to withhold the rent and later proposed the two options to both Tenants in his office on July 9, 2018.  He proposed that the Tenants sign the N11 Agreement on that date, not June 22, 2018 as he would have had no reason to bring this form to the rental unit on June 22, 2018 since he only attended at their residence to collect on an unpaid hydro bill, not propose ending the tenancy. 

49.  I find that the Tenants voluntarily attended at T.W.’s office at his invitation on July 9, 2018 after the Tenants followed through with B.M.’s threat to withhold the rent for July, 2018.  Presented with the two options described by T.W., both then signed the N11 Agreement precisely according to the testimony of both T.W. and M.G.  This version of events is consistent with the date on the N11 Agreement as well as A.J.Y.’s signature obtained on the same date.  Both parties agreed that A.J.Y. was absent from the rental unit on June 22, 2018 and B.M. did not challenge the Landlord’s evidence that he was present at the meeting on July 9, 2018.

50.  In light of all of the evidence, I prefer the evidence of T.W. and M.G. to that offered by B.M.  While B.M.’s testimony was inconsistent, unreliable and at times patently untruthful, T.W. offered coherent, detailed and internally consistent descriptions of the relevant events that was supported by M.G. and the documentary evidence submitted at the hearing.  As a result, I find that the Tenants voluntarily agreed to terminate the tenancy on July 29, 2018 and that T.W. exerted no duress or coercion to obtain that agreement.

51.  Having regard to all the circumstances, I therefore do not find that it would be fair to set aside order SWL-19968-18, issued on August 8, 2018.

52.  This order contains all of the reasons in this matter and no further reasons will issue.

It is ordered that:

The L1 Application

 

1.     Unless the Tenants void the order as set out below, the tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenants is terminated.  The Tenants must move out of the rental unit on or before September 21, 2018.

2.     The Tenants shall pay to the Landlord $1,208.30*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to September 10, 2018, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlord owes on the rent deposit.

3.     The Tenants shall also pay to the Landlord $29.59 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting September 11, 2018 to the date they move out of the unit.

4.     The Tenants shall also pay to the Landlord $175.00 for the cost of filing the application.

5.     If the Tenants do not pay the Landlord the full amount owing* on or before September 21, 2018, the Tenants will start to owe interest.  This will be simple interest calculated from September 22, 2018 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

6.     If the unit is not vacated on or before September 21, 2018, then starting September 22, 2018, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

7.     Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord, on or after September 22, 2018.

8.     If, on or before September 21, 2018, the Tenants pay the amount of $2,875.00** to the Landlord or to the Board in trust, this order for eviction will be void.  This means that the tenancy would not be terminated and the Tenants could remain in the unit.  If this payment is not made in full and on time, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

9.     The Tenants may make a motion to the Board under subsection 74(11) of the Act to set aside this order if they pay the amount required under that subsection on or after September 22, 2018, but before the Sheriff gives vacant possession to the Landlord.  The Tenants are only entitled to make this motion once during the period of the tenancy agreement with the Landlord.

The Tenants’ Set Aside Motion

10.  The motion to set aside Order SWL-19968-18, issued on August 8, 2018, is denied.

11.  The stay of order SWL-19968-18 is lifted immediately.

12.  Order SWL-19968-18 is unchanged.
