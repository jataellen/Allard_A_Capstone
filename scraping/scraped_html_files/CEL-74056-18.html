File Number: CEL-74056-18

 

Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

 

 


DJ (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict JR (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes.

 

This application was heard in Mississauga on April 18, 2018.

 

The Landlord and the Tenant attended the hearing.

 

At the hearing the Tenant raised the following issues pursuant to section 82 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'):

a)   Lack of heat;

b)   Mould.

 

Determinations:

 

1.      The Tenant has not paid the total rent the Tenant was required to pay for the period from December 1, 2017 to April 30, 2018.  Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination effective March 2, 2018.

2.      The Landlord collected a rent deposit of $1,750.00 from the Tenant and this deposit is still being held by the Landlord.

3.      Interest on the rent deposit is owing to the Tenant for the period from September 30, 2017 to March 2, 2018.

Section 82 issues

Law

4.      Section 20(1) of the Act obligates a Landlord to maintain a unit in a good state of repair.

5.      In Onyskiw v. CJM Property Management, 2016 ONCA 477, the Court of Appeal determined that a contextual approach should be adopted when considering a landlord’s potential breach of subsection 20(1) of the Act and a breach will not be found if the landlord’s response to a maintenance issue was reasonable in the circumstances.

6.      I must also apply subsection 30(2) of the Act, and consider whether the tenant, or former tenant, advised the landlord of the alleged breaches before applying to the Board, and the parties’ duty to mitigate found in section 16 of the Act.

 

Heat

7.      The tenancy commenced as of October 1, 2017.  The monthly rent is $1,756.50.

8.      The unit is a condominium.  The Landlord is responsible for the provision of heat. There is a thermostat in the unit.

9.      The Tenant testified that there has been no heat in the unit since October 1, 2017.  She said the boiler system in the complex requires repair.

10.   The building management turned the heat on in the complex as of October 23, 2017 and informed residents to contact management if they are unable to control heat in the unit. The Tenant submitted a copy of this notice from the condominium management.

11.   The condominium management became aware of issues with the boiler system in approximately mid-December 2017. The condominium management commenced replacement of the boiler with two boilers although one of the new boilers was capable of providing sufficient heat to the complex.

12.   On or about December 18, 2017 a contractor inspected the heating system in the unit and determined that a new actuator was required as well as replacement of a good portion of the piping. The Tenant submitted a copy of an email from the condominium management containing this information.  The Tenant forwarded this email to the City of Mississauga municipal law enforcement officer (‘City’). 

13.   On December 19, 2017 the condominium manager informed the Tenant that the heat was fully functional in her unit.  The Tenant submitted this email.

14.   The Landlord became aware about the heat issue on or about January 9, 2018 and took steps to follow up with the condominium management in an effort to resolve the heat issue.  The Landlord submitted an email he sent to the condominium management on January 9th to corroborate his testimony.

15.   On January 9, 2018 the Landlord sent a notice to the Tenant regarding the boiler repairs and replacement and offered to cover 50% of the cost to purchase a heater to warm up the unit.

16.   On February 9, 2018 the condominium manager informed the Landlord and Tenant that one boiler was replaced.

17.   On February 13, 2018 the condominium management informed the City that installation of the first boiler was to be completed on February 16, 2018.  This email was submitted by the Landlord.

18.   In an email dated February 24, 2018, the Landlord informed the Tenant that a new boiler was to be installed and operational on February 23rd and heat should be available over the weekend.

19.   The Landlord breached section 20 of the Act by failing to provide a reasonable supply of heat.  It is more probable than not that there was inadequate heat in the unit from mid-December 2017 until March 6, 2018 because the boiler system was not functioning properly.  The condominium management informed the Tenant that heat was fully functional in her unit however the condominium manager also informed the Tenant on January 2, 2018 that the boilers cannot keep up with the demand for heat.  On January 9, 2018 the Landlord acknowledged that the heat issue continued and offered to assist with the purchase of a second heater.  On February 9, 2018 the Landlord followed up with the condominium management inquiring when the heat would be back on in the unit.  On March 6, 2018 the temperature in the unit was 75 degrees Celsius and the condominium management confirmed that no further work is required in the unit.  The Landlord has not received any order from the City regarding inadequate heat and the evidence demonstrates that the City was involved in the resolution of the heat issue.

20.   The Tenant is entitled to an abatement of 50% from January 9, 2018 (the date when she informed the Landlord) to March 6, 2018 when the heat issue was resolved for $1,617.28.  The Tenant was still able to reside in the unit and the Landlord took reasonable steps to respond to the Tenant’s complaints, however the Tenant was impacted with inadequate heat despite the Landlord’s efforts.  She sent her daughter to live elsewhere because of the lack of heat in the unit.

Mould

21.   The Tenant testified that there was mould in the windows in the unit as well as condensation likely because of the inadequate heat.  She submitted photos taken in February 2018 of the mould around the windows.

22.   The Landlord became aware incidentally about the mould in mid-February 2018.  He contacted various mould remediation companies and arranged for a unit inspection with a mould inspector on March 13, 2018.  A quote from GTA Mold Removal dated March 13, 2018 was submitted.

23.   The Landlord used over the counter products to clean up the mould on April 16, 2018 and provided the Tenant with a dehumidifier to prevent reoccurrence of condensation, the likely cause of the mould.

24.   There is no breach of section 20 regarding the mould issue.  The Landlord responded promptly and reasonably upon discovering mould around the windows.  The mould was resolved by cleaning the surfaces which indicates that the impact to the Tenant was minimal.  This claim is dismissed.

Relief from eviction

25.   The Tenant requested a few months to repay the arrears.

26.   The Landlord testified that there have been no payments of rent since December 2017 and he has been carrying the costs of the unit on his own. The substantial arrears are stressing the financial resources of the Landlord and his family.

27.   I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Act, and find that it would be unfair to grant relief from eviction pursuant to subsection 83(1) of the Act. There are substantial arrears ($5,940.22) to April 30, 2018.  The Tenant paid partial rent for December 2017 and January 2018 and has not paid any rent since that time.  The Landlord is financially stressed because of the arrears. The amount of rent owed is reduced and the opportunity remains for the Tenant to pay the amount owing to void the order is she chooses to do so.

It is ordered that:

 

1.      Unless the Tenant voids the order as set out below, the tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated.  The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before May 7, 2018.

2.      The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $3,665.18*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to April 26, 2018, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlord owes on the rent deposit and less the amount of the abatement the Landlord owes the Tenant.

3.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $57.75 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting April 27, 2018 to the date the Tenant moves out of the unit.

4.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $175.00 for the cost of filing the application.

5.      If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing* on or before May 7, 2018, the Tenant will start to owe interest.  This will be simple interest calculated from May 8, 2018 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

6.      If the unit is not vacated on or before May 7, 2018, then starting May 8, 2018, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

7.      Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord, on or after May 8, 2018.

8.      If the Tenant wishes to void this order and continue the tenancy, the Tenant must pay to the Landlord or to the Board in trust:
i)        $5,940.22 if the payment is made on or before April 30, 2018, or
ii)      $7,696.72 if the payment is made on or before May 7, 2018**.
If the Tenant does not make full payment in accordance with this paragraph and by the appropriate deadline, then the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

 

 

9.      The Tenant may make a motion to the Board under subsection 74(11) of the Act to set aside this order if they pay the amount required under that subsection on or after May 8, 2018 but before the Sheriff gives vacant possession to the Landlord. The Tenant is only entitled to make this motion once during the period of the tenancy agreement with the Landlord.
