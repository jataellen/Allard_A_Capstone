Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: TSL-93207-18-RV

 TST-94747-18

TSL-00082-18

 

 

 


A.A.D (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict J.E.S (the 'Tenant') because the Landlord intends to convert the rental unit to a non-residential use. The Landlord also claimed compensation for each day the Tenant remained in the unit after the termination date (TSL-93207-18).

 

The Landlord also applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict the Tenant because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes (TSL-00082-18).

 

The Tenant applied for an order determining that the Landlord harassed, coerced, obstructed, threatened, and substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit or residential complex by the Tenant (TST-94747-18).

 

It was agreed by the parties that the applications would be heard together, and TSL-00082-18 was ordered to be heard with the other applications. 

 

An interim order in relation to TSL-93207-18 was issued on July 31, 2018.  The Tenant requested a review of the order on August 31, 2018 and a hearing for the request for review was granted and it was determined that the review request would be heard along with the other two applications.

 

These applications were heard together in Toronto on June 4, 2018, July 19, 2018, October 4, 2018, and December 4, 2018.    

 

The Landlord’s legal representative, D.C, the Landlord’s representative, P.C, and the Tenant attended the hearing. C.L (CL),  C.P (CP), R.C (RC), and H.B (HB) testified at the hearing.  

 

           

Determinations:

 

Request for Review -TSL-93207-18

 

1.      The Tenant requested a review of the order issued on July 31, 2018.  An interim order was issued TSL-93207-18-RV-IN, staying the order that was issued and directing that at the hearing of the request to review the application, the following issues should be considered:

                 I.        Did the Board seriously err in failing to consider the application of s. 83(3)(c) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’) to the evidence before it with respect to the Landlord’s demands for an illegal rent increase;

               II.        Did the Board seriously err in failing to consider the evidence of the Tenant with respect to his complaints to governmental authorities concerning the commercial unit below and by failing to consider the application of s. 83(3)(a) and (b) of the Act to any findings of fact with respect to those complaints; and

              III.        Did the Board seriously err by providing inadequate reasons with respect to the relevance of the Tenant’s evidence of complaints to governmental authorities?

2.      For the reasons provided at the hearing, the request for review was granted.  It was agreed that all the evidence presented in support of the request for review would be included and considered as evidence at the hearing.  The parties also agreed that evidence would be heard from the Tenant to complete the evidence for his application, as well as the evidence of the Landlord with respect to the new L1 application. 

3.      The Tenant also raised, in the course of the request for review, an application that I recuse myself on the basis of bias, as it was my order that the Tenant had reviewed.  The Tenant confirmed that he had no other issues to raise concerning any bias I might have. 

4.      The test for reasonable apprehension of bias is set out in the dissenting judgment of Mr. Justice de Grandpre in Committee for Justice and Liberty et al. v. National Energy Board et al., 1976 CanLII 2 (SCC), [1978] 1 SCR 369 at 394:

…the apprehension of bias must be a reasonable one, held by reasonable and right minded persons, applying themselves to the question and obtaining thereon the required information. In the words of the Court of Appeal, that test is “what would an informed person, viewing the matter realistically and practically – and having thought the matter through – conclude. Would he think that it is more likely than not that [the decision maker], whether consciously or unconsciously, would not decide fairly.

5.      The onus of demonstrating bias lies with the person who is alleging its existence. The basis for a reasonable apprehension of bias must be substantial. “Mere suspicion” is not enough, and a real likelihood or probability of bias must be demonstrated. (R v S (RD) p. 531.)

6.       I do not believe that an informed and reasonable person, viewing the matter realistically and practically, and having thought the matter through would conclude that there is a reasonable apprehension of bias in this case. Cases are frequently reviewed, and the simple fact that the request to review will be considered is not sufficient to raise a reasonable apprehension of bias.  The other consideration is that it is most expedient for the Board to continue the hearing of these matters together, without the time and effort of rehearing all the evidence before another member.  As a result, I declined to recuse myself.

TSL-93207-18

7.      These applications arise out of a long term tenancy of approximately 20 years in which the Tenant has occupied a rental unit over top of a commercial unit that the non-profit association used for its own cultural activities. In July 2017, the Landlord rented its space to a commercial tenant, who commenced operation of a licenced bar at the beginning of February 2018. 

8.      The Landlord requires the rental unit to be vacated in order to convert it to a non- residential use. I am satisfied that the Landlord has taken all reasonable steps to confirm that the unit is appropriately zoned and that there are no necessary permits to convert the space to non-residential use.

9.      The Association’s President and Directors confirmed in their testimony that due to financial pressures, the Association (by its membership at a duly convened general meeting) passed a resolution to rent the store-front space in the lower floor of the building to a commercial tenant and to take steps to terminate the tenancy in the upper rental unit (occupied by the Tenant) and use the space for their own Association purposes.

10.   The Tenant raised issues at the hearing that he did not believe that the rental unit space would be appropriate for the Association’s needs.  I do not see these concerns as relevant to the issue to be determined, that is whether the Landlord is converting the unit to non-residential use.  It is not necessary for the Landlord to establish that the motives are “reasonable”, but only that the Landlord is not providing the notice of termination in “bad faith”.

11.   The Tenant also testified at the hearing that the Landlord, in a meeting at the rental unit, on February 6, 2017, that the Directors of the Landlord explained that they were experiencing financial pressures and asked him to consider an extraordinary rent increase, which he declined to do.  The Tenant says that the Notice of Termination was made in retaliation for his refusal of the rent increase. 

12.   The Tenant testified that at the meeting he was put under “extreme pressure for 90 minutes to sign a lease”, but the recording of the meeting which the Tenant produced and selected parts to play at the hearing did not support his description.  The meeting sounded in the recording as civil, and there was no lease with a rent increase ever presented to the Tenant to sign. 

13.   I do not see that the evidence supports the Tenant’s position that the application was brought in retaliation.  It was the consistent evidence of directors for the Landlord that upon their appreciation of the serious financial pressures facing the Landlord, they began to look for options to manage the position of the Association to continue its activities but be able to meet its obligations.  One of the options considered was receiving an increase in rent from the rental unit.  When that was refused by the Tenant, they considered receiving rental income from their own space in the building.  This was the option they ultimately took to the Association membership who provided their majority approval for the plan.

14.   The Tenant contends that the proposal from the Landlord concerning the discussion of a rent increase raises the issue of whether the Landlord should be denied the eviction pursuant to section 83(3)(c) of the Act.  This section applies in circumstances where there is a finding that the reason for the eviction is that the tenant has attempted to secure or enforce his legal rights.  The evidence does not support that the purported rent increase was ever more than a suggestion to the Tenant as a way in which the Landlord not for profit organization could address its urgent financial pressures.  The Tenant indicated he refused to consider any proposal from the Landlord and the Landlord took no further steps in relation to imposing any rent increase. 

15.   I find, on a balance of probabilities, considering the evidence presented by both parties, that the application is not being brought by the Landlord as a result of the Tenant attempting to secure or enforce his legal rights, and that section 83(3)(c) does not apply.

16.   The Tenant testified that he complained to government agencies, specifically the City of Toronto Municipal Licensing and Standards By-Law Enforcement Unit concerning noise, and the Healthy Environments Enforcement Coordinator concerning the smells of hookah smoking coming from the commercial unit below. 

17.   The email the Tenant says he received from the Health Environments Enforcement Coordinator states that they became aware of the premises on February 2, 2018.  An excerpt of the email presented by the Tenant says that on February 3 and 10, and March 10 and April 21, 2018 the commercial tenant was “found in violation of the Municipal Code” and was ticketed, convicted and fined. Another excerpt of the email says that on September 9 and October 11, 2018, the commercial tenant was found in violation of the Municipal Code and that summons were issued for “permit person to use hookah upon licensed premises”.  The Tenant did not summons or call any witness from this government department, so there was no direct evidence available on these complaints.

18.   The Tenant also produced an email dated October 11, 2018 apparently from the Bylaws Enforcement in which he was advised that a “noise charge” had been issued as a summons to the commercial tenant, but that when they had visited the bar recently, “the music was not an issue”.  Another email in evidence says the first complaint received from the Tenant concerning noise was made to the department on February 22, 2018.  The Tenant did not summons or call any witness from this government department, so there was no direct evidence available on these complaints.

19.   The Notice of Termination served by the Landlord on the Tenant was dated February 16, 2018, with a termination date of June 30, 2018.  The testimony of the Landlord was that the not for profit association had sought the approval of their membership at a duly convened meeting in the spring of 2017 to rent the lower unit commercially and take steps to take over the upper unit for the association’s use.

20.   I do not doubt that the Tenant is in correspondence with government agencies concerning his complaints that the commercial tenant below is disturbing him with noise and smells, I am troubled that he has not called the agencies as witnesses, and that the hearsay email evidence he presented is not clear and complete, which leads me to give it little weight.

21.   My decision pursuant to section 83(3)(b) of the Act is that the eviction must be denied if I find that “the reason for the application being brought is that the tenant has complained to a governmental authority of the landlord’s violation of a law dealing with health, safety, housing or maintenance standards”.  The evidence does not support that the complaints by the Tenant to the City departments were the reason that the Landlord brought the application.  The Landlord’s purposes in pursuing the eviction were long established as supported by the directions from their membership. It should also be noted that the complaints being pursued were all against the commercial tenant and not against the Landlord.  

22.   I find, on a balance of probabilities, considering the evidence presented by both parties, that the application is not being brought by the Landlord as a result of the Tenant complaining to a government authority, and that section 83(3)(b) does not apply.

23.   I find that the Landlord has established their intention to convert the rental unit to non-residential purposes, for the use of the Association’s membership.  The application to evict will be granted subject to consideration of the circumstances pursuant to section 83(3)(a) of the Act and section 83(2) of the Act.

TST-94747-18

24.   The Tenant’s application is alleging interference with his reasonable enjoyment by the operation of the licenced bar below his unit. The Tenant has taken many complaints against the operator of the business, but this application is alleging that the Landlord has interfered with the Tenant’s reasonable enjoyment of the premises by failing to address substantial interference by the commercial tenant. To be successful in this application, the Tenant must establish that the other tenant substantially interfered with his reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit by making excessive noise and noxious smells. The Tenant must also establish that the Landlord failed to take reasonable steps to address the interference with the Tenant’s reasonable enjoyment.

25.   The Tenant testified that the noise of music from the bar downstairs has severely impacted his reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit and his health.  The Tenant says he is kept awake each night until the bar closes, approximately 3 am, and that it has made him depressed, exhausted and that he has had to stop work since February 2018.

26.   The Tenant played recordings of the noise he said he had taken in his apartment, but he had transferred the recordings from his phone to a computer and then insisted on playing them through an amplified sound system.  I find that it was impossible to estimate the actual sound levels within the rental unit. 

27.   The Tenant provided a letter from his doctor dated April 4, 2018 which repeats the concerns of the Tenant and says that they are contributing to his anxiety and depressed mood. 

28.   The Tenant called a witness, CL, who testified that she has been in the rental unit while the bar is operating, and has observed noxious fumes, and heard loud music, that makes normal conversation difficult.  CL also testified that she has seen significant changes in the Tenant since the bar opened because of his stress and anxiety over the noise and noxious fumes.

29.   CP testified that she is a resident of a building adjacent to the building in which the Tenant has his rental unit.  She further testified that the neighbourhood has changed since the bar opened, in that she hears noisy music from the bar and finds the crowds around the bar doorway intimidating. CP has never attended the Tenant’s rental unit and she was not played any recordings of the noise in the Tenant’s unit so as to corroborate the noise levels. 

30.   RC testified that she is also a resident of a building adjacent to the building in which the Tenant has his rental unit.  She also testified that she finds the noise from the bar disruptive and has complained to the City and her local councillor.  She estimated that the music is disruptive until 10 – 12 pm, 3 – 4 days per week.  She also has never attended the Tenant’s rental unit, nor was played any recordings of the noise so as to corroborate the noise levels. 

31.   As was noted previously, the Tenant did not summons or call any witness from the City who is responding to his complaints of noise and noxious odours. 

32.   HB testified as a reply witness.  She is the owner of the bar/hookah lounge, licenced with a business and liquor licence.  HB admitted that the lounge has received tickets for providing nicotine tobacco, but that she no longer allows any nicotine tobacco in the lounge and is contesting all the tickets she has received.  She says that the bar closes on time and that inspectors have come to observe the noise and found the noise not to be excessive.  She further testified that she did special soundproofing in the roof and an efficient ventilation system that takes the smoke outside. 

33.   The Landlord testified that Directors in the association have spoken with the commercial tenant regarding the noise and noxious fumes complaints and have received explanations from the commercial tenants as to how the issues are being addressed.  The Landlord is aware of the complaints made to the City but have not been contacted by the City to have any substantive role in the proceedings. 

34.   Noise is a highly subjective issue.  The Tenant must establish on a balance of probabilities that the commercial tenant downstairs is making excessive noise and noxious fumes and that the Landlord has not taken reasonable steps to address his complaints. 

35.   All of the evidence concerning the levels of noise and fumes came only from the Tenant.  I found his descriptions to be hyperbolic and melodramatic.  The Tenant also cannot expect the issues he is facing to be compared against what he had when he had only the not for profit utilizing its space for cultural purposes.  It is within the rights of the Landlord to lease its lower unit for a commercial purpose within the allowable zoning. 

36.   I find that the Tenant has established, on a balance of probabilities, that there is excessive noise and fumes coming from the commercial tenant and that he is deserving of a rent abatement to compensate him for some loss of reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit.

37.   An abatement is a contractual remedy. It recognises the idea that a tenant is paying rent for a bundle of goods and services and if the tenant is not receiving everything being paid for then he or she is entitled to abatement proportional to the difference between what is being paid for and what is being received.  It is also necessary, pursuant to section 16 of the Act, for the Tenant to take reasonable steps to minimize any losses arising from the circumstances.

38.   I find that the appropriate abatement in these circumstances, considering that the Tenant remains in the unit and is disrupted in his ordinary use of the rental unit in the evening hours, some days of each week, to be 20% of the monthly rent of $1200.00 per month, or $240.00 per month, from February 2018 through to the end of the tenancy. The Tenant can set off the allowed abatement against the rent found owing on the L1 application.

39.   I also find, on a balance of probabilities, and considering the evidence presented by both parties, that the Landlord’s breach of responsibilities is not a serious breach within the application of section 83(3) (a) and so it does not apply in this situation. 

TSL-00082-18

40.   The Tenant did not pay the total rent the Tenant was required to pay for the period from July 1, 2018 to September 30, 2018.  Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination effective October 9, 2018.

 

41.   The Tenant admits he did not comply with an interim order issued on November 5, 2018 that the sum of $6,000.00 should be paid into the Board in trust on or before November 30, 2018.

Section 83(2) Considerations

42.   The Tenant did testify that he has experienced significant personal loss in the last few years and as a result has experienced severe situational depression for the last eighteen months and is still currently suffering.  He testified he has experienced the stress of having the new commercial tenant below operating since February 1, 2018 as exacerbating all of his symptoms and causing him to be sleepless and anxious, as well as having breathing difficulties that he attributes to the smoke from downstairs. The Tenant presented no medical evidence beyond the doctor’s letter described above.

43.   The Tenant also testified that he has been unable to consider looking for new accommodation due to his depression.  He has lived in the rental unit for approximately 20 years and also works from home, which had required satellite equipment to be installed on the roof.  He also says that he is attached to the neighbourhood and would like to find comparable accommodation, at a comparable rate as he has limited income. The Tenant was unable to respond when asked for an estimate of how long he anticipated he would need if the tenancy was ordered to terminate.

44.   I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would not be unfair to postpone the eviction until April 30, 2019 pursuant to subsection 83(1)(b) of the Act as this will give the Tenant some extra time to look for new accommodation and will not excessively inconvenience the Landlord, having also taken into account the protracted nature of these proceedings and the extra time in the rental unit the Tenant has had as a result.

It is ordered that:

 

1.      The request to review order TSL-93207-18-IN AND TST-94747-18-IN, issued on July 31, 2018, is granted. The order is cancelled and replaced by the following order.

2.      The tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated. The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before April 30, 2019.

3.      The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $10,323.72*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to March 15, 2019, plus the Landlord’s cost of filing the application.

4.      The Landlord owes the Tenant a rent abatement in the amount of $3,360.00, which has been set off against the amount owing to the Landlord. 

5.      The full amount owing is $6,723.72.

6.     If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing* on or before April 30, 2019, the Tenant will start to owe interest.  This will be simple interest calculated from May 1, 2019 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

7.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $39.45 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting March 16, 2019 to the date the Tenant moves out of the unit.

8.      If the unit is not vacated on or before April 30, 2019, then starting May 1, 2019, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

9.      Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord on or after May 1, 2019.
