File Number: CEL-77739-18

 

Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

 

 


TTMI (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict AA (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes.

 

This application was heard in Mississauga on October 2, 2018.

 

The Landlord’s Agent, MNZ, and the Tenant attended the hearing.

 

At the hearing the Tenant raised the following issues pursuant to section 82 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'):  no operational laundry facility, no air conditioning, broken toilet, flood, mold, parking issues, illegal entries, loss of use of yard, threats and harassment.

 

Determinations:

 

1.      The Landlord is amended to reflect the named Landlord in the written Residential Tenancy Agreement (Agreement) signed and dated by the parties on August 30, 2017.

2.      The Tenant did not dispute the rent arrears of $6,750.00 owing to October 31, 2018 as claimed by the Landlord in this application.  The Tenant requested a set off of the rent arrears owing and a further full rent abatement from September 1, 2017 to October 31, 2018 due to her issues raised pursuant to section 82 of the Act.

3.      The Tenant has not paid the total rent the Tenant was required to pay for the period from May 1, 2018 to October 31, 2018.  Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination effective July 17, 2018.

4.      The Landlords collected a rent deposit of $1,350.00 from the Tenant and this deposit is still being held by the Landlords.

5.      Interest on the rent deposit is owing to the Tenant for the period from September 1, 2017 to July 17, 2018.

6.      The Tenant has paid $1,350.00 to the Board in trust since the application was filed. **.

7.      The Tenant raised issues regarding whether or not the Landlord breached section 20 of the Act because of the disrepair of the washer, dryer, air conditioner, and toilet.  Also because there was a flood in April 2018 which was not addressed properly and led to mold growth in the unit. 

8.      Subsection 20(1) of the Act states the following:

 

A landlord is responsible for providing and maintaining a residential complex, including the rental units in it, in a good state of repair and fit for habitation and for complying with health, safety, housing and maintenance standards.

 

9.      For the reasons stated below, I am satisfied that the Landlord breached section 20 of the Act.  There was no dispute that the washer, dryer, air conditioner and toilet were in disrepair at various times during the tenancy and that there was surface mold in the laundry room.  I am also satisfied that the Landlord’s Agent illegally entered the rental unit and substantially interfered with the Tenant’s reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit by preventing access to the back yard.

 

10.   Subsection 20(1) of the Act does not contain an element of fault. Therefore, regardless of the reasons why a landlord is unable to provide a rental unit in a good state of repair and fit for habitation, if they are unable to do so, they will generally be found to be in breach of their obligations under the Act.

 

11.   The Ontario Court of Appeal in Onyskiw v. CJM Property Management Ltd. (2016 ONCA 477) determined that landlord is not automatically in breach of their maintenance obligations as soon as a problem arises and a contextual approach is necessary in determining whether or not a landlord has breached their maintenance obligations under section 20 of the Act. That approach involves a consideration of the “entirety of the factual situation” before determining that a landlord is in breach of their maintenance obligations.

12.   I have taken into account the entirety of the factual situation including the condition of the rental unit, the scope of the work and the time involved in resolving the issue. In this context, I find that the Landlord breached their maintenance obligations under section 20 of the Act.  Despite the fact that the rental unit was newly built prior to the Tenant moving in, there was no dispute that these issues were in disrepair.

13.   The laundry facility in the Tenant’s unit had issues from day one.  The washer flooded on the Tenant’s first use.  According to the Tenant, the washer was not replaced until December 2017.  At that time the Landlord also installed a new dryer.  However, the dryer was not hooked up to an exhaust vent.  When the dryer was finally hooked up to an exhaust vent in April 2018, the exhaust was connected to the stove exhaust fan.  There was no dispute that the City Inspector attended the rental unit and issued an order for the Landlord to comply and properly ensure that the dryer vents to the exterior.  This was completed in August 2018.

14.   The Tenant stated that she was unable to use the laundry facility in her unit because of these issues at various times.  The Landlord’s Agent submitted that prior to December 2017 when he installed new appliances in the Tenant’s unit, the Tenant had access to the upper level laundry facility.   This was not disputed by the Tenant.  However, there was no dispute that as of approximately March 2018, the Tenant did not have access to the upper unit’s laundry room. 

15.   Given that the dryer in the Tenant’s unit was not operational for the period of March 2018 to August 2018 when the Tenant did not have access to another dryer, I find that the Tenant is entitled to a lump sum abatement of rent totalling $120.00, representing $20.00 per month.  The Tenant stated that not having use of a laundry facilities was inconvenient, however she did not provide any other details such as costs or travel incurred as a result.  The rent abatement is appropriate in these circumstances given that this amount is comparable to the reasonable costs of using a dryer at a laundry mat and because the use of a dryer was agreed upon by parties in the tenancy.

16.   Although there was no dispute that the washer was also replaced due to an initial flood from the original machine.  I find it more likely than not that the Tenant had use of the upper laundry facilities while the washer was broken down between September 2017 and December 2017.  The Tenant provided no evidence regarding any inconvenience during this period of time. As well, after the initial flood from the new washer in December 2017, most likely due to poor installation, there were no further reports of the machine not being operational.

17.   The Landlord’s Agent did not ignore the issue with the disrepair of the air conditioning unit when the Tenant reported it in June 2018.  He immediately contacted a technician to address the problem.  However, there were still a number of visits by various technicians until a final repair was completed at the end of August 2018.  The air conditioning unit was only fully operational at that time. 

18.   Therefore, I find that the Tenant is entitled to a lump sum abatement of rent totalling $300.00 is more appropriate in these circumstances representing $100.00 for each month she lived without air conditioning, namely June, July and August 2018.  The months are during the summer season where air conditioning would normally be utilized. 

19.   The Landlord’s Agent was out of the country in May 2018 when the toilet stopped working at the rental unit.  The Tenant first reported the issue on May 2, 2018.  The Landlord’s Agent’s son was able to assist the Tenant three days later and permitted her access to the upper unit for use of a bathroom.  The toilet was repaired after a part was obtained on May 13, 2018. 

20.   There is only one bathroom in the Tenant’s unit.  The Tenant loss use of a toilet for approximately four days, three days awaiting a response from the Landlord’s Agent and one further day when the Landlord’s Agent locked access to the upper unit the day prior to the toilet being repaired.

21.    Therefore, I find that the Tenant is entitled to a lump sum abatement of $200.00, representing $50.00 for each day the Tenant did not have use of a toilet in her unit. 

22.   There was no dispute that a flood occurred in the rental unit in April 2018.  The Landlord’s Agent immediately responded by attending the rental unit. He stated that the sub-pump had been disconnected and once turned on, the water drained.

23.   Since there were no further issues with water flooding in this respect, I am satisfied that the issue with the sub-pump was the cause.  In this circumstance, I am not satisfied that the Landlord breached section 20 of the Act.  The Landlord’s Agent responded immediately and effectively to the issue. 

24.   Other than a baseboard along one wall of the unit affected by this flood being in need of repair, the Tenant provided no photos of repair or damages elsewhere.  The baseboard area was repaired by the Landlord’s Agent but the Tenant disagreed with how the repair was completed.  The Tenant’s issue with the repair was simply cosmetic.  The Landlord’s Agent responded reasonably to the issue and removed and replaced the baseboard. 

25.   While I understand that the Tenant was not pleased with the repair or the fact that Landlord’s Agent’s used her belongings after the flood, such as her mop, towels and/or other linens, these issues are very minor in nature and do not amount to substantial interference.  Parties have a duty to mitigate their losses in accordance with section 16 of the Act.  I find it very unlikely that the Tenant’s linens and mop could not be cleaned and reused.

26.   There was surface mold growth on the wall in the furnace room.  The Tenant stated that water had been pooling in this area which she believed to be the cause of the mold. The Landlord’s Agent acknowledged that the drain for the air conditioning unit had been clogged with dirt and debris, and once cleaned the water drained properly.  The Tenant alleged that the mold on this wall affected the area on the other side of the wall where she stored some personal belongings.  According to her, the items in her storage bins smelled like mold.

27.   The mold in the furnace room was never addressed by the Landlord. In the last few months, the Tenant has advised the Landlord’s Agent via text messages regarding this issue. The Tenant remarked on the look and smell of the area affected. 

28.   The area affected by the surface mold is one wall behind the furnace, in a room which also houses the washer and dryer used by the Tenant. Other than the odour, I am not satisfied that the Tenant’s ability to use the laundry room was impacted.  Therefore, I find that the Tenant is entitled to a lump sum rent abatement of $200.00 for this issue.

29.   The evidence before me was insufficient to find that the Tenant’s storage was affected as a result of the mold issue.  There was no dispute that the Tenant utilized the Landlord’s garage for storage and only had to obtain her own storage facilities when she was instructed to remove her belongings from the garage this past June.  Also, the Tenant had no photos of her storage items allegedly removed from the storage area behind the furnace room because of the mold on the wall.

30.   There was no dispute that the Tenant initially had use of the backyard at the residential complex. When the Landlord’s Agent was fed up with the Tenant failing to clean up after her dogs, as of June 12, 2018, he prohibited the Tenant from using the yard. 

31.   Based on the uncontested evidence before me I am satisfied that the Landlord’s Agent substantially interfered with the Tenant’s reasonable enjoyment of the residential complex by prohibiting use of the yard as of June 12, 2018. 

32.   Therefore, I find that the Tenant is entitled to a lump sum abatement of rent totalling $200.00 for the period of June 12, 2018 to current.  I find this amount to be appropriate in these circumstances given that this period of time was during the summer season when the Tenant would have fully utilized the yard for family gatherings, barbeques and other outdoor activities which normally take place outside.

33.   Based on the evidence before me, I find it more likely than not that the Landlord’s Agent unlawfully entered the rental unit.  I am satisfied that it has only been in recent months where the Landlord’s Agent has provided a written notice stating a date, reasonable timeframe, and reason for entry into the Tenant’s unit.  A number of notices were submitted by the Landlord’s Agent into evidence, however the ones that are dated are between August 17, 2018 and September 14, 2018 only.  Therefore, prior to this timeframe, I find it more likely than not that the Landlord’s Agent would simply text or call the Tenant to advise of any entries into the unit.

34.   The Tenant’s evidence regarding the Landlord’s Agent’s entries was vague, however she did recall that if there was a notice it would be given the day prior and state an entry timeframe of the next day between 8:00 a.m. and 8:00 p.m.  She further recalled at least three occasions where the Landlord’s Agent entered to show the unit with the tenants from the upper unit with proper sufficient notice.

35.   Therefore, I find that the Tenant is entitled to a rent abatement of $150.00, representing $50.00 for each illegal entry.  The Tenant was very upset at the Landlord’s Agent entering her unit and on one occasion her son was sleeping in the living area and woke up to a number of people standing over him.

36.   I am not satisfied that the Tenant’s issues with parking amounted to a substantial interference by the Landlord.  The Tenant acknowledged that she was aware upon renting the unit that parking is available only in tandem in a single driveway.  There were no issues in the first few months of the tenancy simply due to the fact that the upper unit was vacant and the Tenant was the only one parking a car in the driveway.  The Tenant agreed to this parking arrangement knowing that the upper unit would eventually be rented to other tenants who would also park a vehicle in the driveway.

37.   The Tenant and her witness described an incident in the driveway between the Landlord’s Agent and the Tenant’s witness, wherein the Landlord’s Agent yelled at the witness and called him an outsider.   There was no dispute that the Landlord opposed anyone else living with the Tenant in the rental unit and was upset that the witness was regularly there.   I am not satisfied that this one encounter with the Landlord’s Agent rose to the level of substantial interference with the Tenant’s reasonable enjoyment of the rental unit.

38.   For the reasons above the Tenant is entitled to an abatement of rent totaling $1,170.00.

39.   The Tenant is actively seeking out new accommodations for her and her two children.  She hopes to have secured a new rental unit for November 1, 2018. 

40.   The Landlord is requesting payment of the rent arrears or eviction.

41.   I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Act, and find that it would not be unfair to postpone the eviction until October 31, 2018 pursuant to subsection 83(1)(b) of the Act.  The Tenant will not have secured a new place prior to this timeframe, one month’s rent will be paid out from the Board’s trust account and the Landlord is holding a last month’s rent deposit.  This delay is not unreasonable.

It is ordered that:

 

1.      Unless the Tenant voids the order as set out below, the tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated.  The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before October 31, 2018.The amount of $1,350.00 plus accrued interest shall be paid out to the Landlord.

2.      The Tenant shall pay to the Landlords $3,113.61*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to October 5, 2018, less the rent deposit and interest the Landlord owes on the rent deposit and less the amount of the abatement the Landlord owes the Tenant.

3.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $44.38 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting October 6, 2018 to the date the Tenant moves out of the unit.

4.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $190.00 for the cost of filing the application.

5.      If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing* on or before October 31, 2018, the Tenant will start to owe interest.  This will be simple interest calculated from November 1, 2018 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

6.      If the unit is not vacated on or before October 31, 2018, then starting November 1, 2018, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

7.      Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord, on or after November 1, 2018.

8.      If, on or before October 31, 2018, the Tenant pays the amount of $5,770.00** to the Landlord or to the Board in trust, this order for eviction will be void.  This means that the tenancy would not be terminated and the Tenant could remain in the unit.  If this payment is not made in full and on time, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

9.      The Tenant may make a motion to the Board under subsection 74(11) of the Act to set aside this order if they pay the amount required under that subsection on or after November 1, 2018 but before the Sheriff gives vacant possession to the Landlord. The Tenant is only entitled to make this motion once during the period of the tenancy agreement with the Landlord.

 

10.   The Board shall pay to the Landlord the amount of $1,350.00 together with any accrued interest. **
