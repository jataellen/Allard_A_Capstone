Order under Section 69 and Section 30

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Numbers: EAL-60748-16

EAT-60550-16

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 


P. (P.) P. (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict C. L. (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes.

 

The Tenant applied for an order determining that the Landlord failed to meet the Landlord's maintenance obligations under the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act') or failed to comply with health, safety, housing or maintenance standards.

 

These applications were heard in Ottawa on November 15, 2016.

 

The Landlord’s Agent, A. P., and the Tenant attended the hearing.

 

Evidence and Determinations:

 

1.     The Tenant has not paid the total rent she was required to pay for the period from September 1, 2016 to November 30, 2016.  Because of the arrears, the Landlord served a Notice of Termination effective October 21, 2016.

2.      The Tenant was in possession of the rental unit on the date of the hearing. However, she indicated that she was planning on moving out by November 30th  

3.      The monthly rent is $830.00.

4.      The Landlord is not holding a last month's rent deposit.

5.      The Landlord did not receive any payments from the Tenant since the application was filed. However, the Tenant claimed that she paid the Landlord the monthly rent for October 2016 in cash on October 2nd but the Landlord did not provide her with a receipt. The Tenant also claimed that, on September 1st, she paid the Landlord $630.00 in cash towards the September rent but was not provided with a receipt on that occasion either. The Landlord had no record of receiving these payments.

6.      The Tenant indicated that, since moving into the rental unit in July 2016, there was repeated series of occurrences where she would ask the Landlord to undertake certain repairs, the Landlord would tell her that he would do so and provide her with a time when he would come to do the repairs but that he would then either not show up at the time indicated or, if he did show up, only do part of the work and fail to return to complete it.

7.      The nature of the Tenant’s request to the Landlord included fixing smoke detectors that were not working moved in, moving numerous garbage cans that were placed in front of her entrance by the sidewalk, removing litter in the front yard, fixing kitchen shelves that had fallen, putting screens on the kitchen windows that were necessary because of an infestation of fruit flies, fixing an improperly installed pharmacy cabinet in the bathroom, addressing a mice infestation, giving the Tenant the ability to control the thermostat, replacing the mailbox that was rusting and providing her with her own mailbox rather than having to share one with other tenants of the residential complex.

8.      The Tenant provided the Board with an inspection report from the City of Ottawa dated October 5, 2016 which found that the Landlord was in violation of the property standards bylaw and ordered a series of corrective actions, including fixing a broken window, providing a separate mailbox for each Tenant, ensuring that garbage cans are not located in the front yard, repairing the kitchen cupboards, repairing bathroom items including the vanity, vanity top, vanity drawers and vanity door and having an exterminator come into the unit to address the mice infestation. The Landlord was required to complete the corrective action by November 5th .

9.      The Landlord’s agent testified that the work required by the City of Ottawa had been completed. He stated that the unit has a thermostat which the Tenant is able to control.

10.   In her application, the Tenant sought compensation of $508.00 for damaged property. This includes $275.00 for a vase that she claims was broken by the Landlord when he was fixing the cupboard doors and $233.00 for perfume bottles which were broken when the pharmacy cabinet fell on her. The Tenant also sought $240.00 in compensation for the cost to hire individuals to clean up the front yard.

11.  I have considered all of the disclosed circumstances in accordance with subsection 83(2) of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the 'Act'), and find that it would be unfair to grant relief from eviction pursuant to subsection 83(1) of the Act. The Tenant is in a significant amount of arrears and, by her own admission, she is not interested in continuing to live at the residential unit.

12.  With respect to the Tenant’s contention that she paid her rent for the months of September and October 2016, it was her responsibility to either obtain a receipt from the Landlord or provide some other form of proof of payment, such as a bank withdrawal slip.  I cannot accept her word over that of the Landlord in the absence of any supporting documentation.

13.  However, I find that the Tenant is entitled to a rent abatement of $1,000.00 for the difficulties that she experienced during the course of the tenancy due to the Landlord’s failure to complete necessary repairs in a timely fashion. I am satisfied that the evidence establishes that the Landlord was not in substantial compliance with his obligation under s. 20 of the Act to maintain the residential unit in a good state of repair. The Landlord’s contention that it completed all of the repairs required by the Bylaw Enforcement Officer’s report in a timely fashion does not appear to be supported by the evidence.

 

It is ordered that:

 

1.      Unless the Tenant voids the order as set out below, the tenancy between the Landlord and the Tenant is terminated.  The Tenant must move out of the rental unit on or before January 20, 2017.

2.      The Tenant shall pay to the Landlord $2,586.24*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to January 9, 2017 less an amount of $1,000.00 which represents an abatement of rent.

3.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $27.29 per day for compensation for the use of the unit starting January 10, 2017 to the date he moves out of the unit.

4.      The Tenant shall also pay to the Landlord $170.00 for the cost of filing the application.

5.      If the Tenant does not pay the Landlord the full amount owing* on or before January 20, 2017, the Tenant will start to owe interest.  This will be simple interest calculated from January 21, 2017 at 2.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

6.      If the unit is not vacated on or before January 20, 2017, then starting January 21, 2017, the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

7.      Upon receipt of this order, the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) is directed to give vacant possession of the unit to the Landlord, on or after January 21, 2017.

8.      If the Tenant wishes to void this order and continue the tenancy, she must pay to the Landlord or to the Board in trust $3,320.00 if the payment is made on or before      January 20, 2017**. If the Tenant does not make full payment in accordance with this paragraph and by the appropriate deadline, then the Landlord may file this order with the Court Enforcement Office (Sheriff) so that the eviction may be enforced.

 

 

 

9.      The Tenant may make a motion to the Board under subsection 74(11) of the Act to set aside this order if they pay the amount required under that subsection on or after    January 21, 2017 but before the Sheriff gives vacant possession to the Landlord. The Tenant is only entitled to make this motion once during the period of the tenancy agreement with the Landlord.
