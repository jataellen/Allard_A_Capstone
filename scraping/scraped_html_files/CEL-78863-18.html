Order under Section 69

Residential Tenancies Act, 2006

 

File Number: CEL-78863-18

 

 

 

 

 

YKC (the 'Landlord') applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict VT (the 'Tenant') because the Tenant did not pay the rent that the Tenant owes.  (the L1 Application)

 

The Landlord also applied for an order to terminate the tenancy and evict the Tenant because the Tenant, another occupant of the rental unit or someone the Tenant permitted in the residential complex has substantially interfered with the reasonable enjoyment or lawful right, privilege or interest of the Landlord or another tenant (N5 notice of termination); and because the Tenant has been persistently late in paying the Tenant's rent (N8 notice of termination).  (the L2 Application)

 

These applications were heard in Mississauga on October 23, 2018 and on January 18, 2019.

 

One of the Landlords, AL, the Landlords’ Legal Representative, IB  and the Tenant attended both hearings.  The Tenant’s Legal Representative, ‘SS’, only attended the second hearing on January 18, 2019.

 

Subsequent to the first hearing on October 23, 2018, interim order CEL-78863-18-IN was issued on October 26, 2018, which in effect made some preliminary findings on the L1 application, and ordered the disclosure of documents between the parties.

 

Preliminary Issues

 

1.  SS argued for the dismissal of both applications on the basis that the multiplicity of different notices of termination served on the Tenant have, in effect, confused and confounded the Tenant so as to not clearly understand what she had to do, and when, to void those notices that were able to be voided.  SS submitted two prior Board orders (exhibit TT#6- CEL-24830-12 and exhibit TT#7-TSL-93538-18) in support of his arguments. 

 

IB claimed that in respect of the N4 notices, each time the Tenant voided a N4 notice for unpaid rent, she then subsequently became late again in paying another rent.  IB argued that a landlord is in its legal right to serve another N4 on a tenant when a tenant falls into new arrears of rent.  As for the N5 and N8 notices, IB argued that the claims in those notices are for distinctively different bases, and cannot reasonably be confused with one another.

 

I denied the request for the dismissal of the applications.  I believe that on a balance of probabilities, the Tenant here was not confused as to what notice was valid, or what notice had the ability to be voided.  The Landlord used Board approved notices at all times, and those notices each describe in plain language what is at issue, and what can or cannot be done to either void the notice or contest the Landlord and maintain the tenancy.

 

Further, TT#6 dealt with a single N4 notice issue in which the landlord sent multiple N4s and after failing before the Board at an initial hearing based on the second N4 that was served, the landlord decided to file another application based on the first N4 notice (instead of starting anew).   This is not the case here.

 

TT#7 dealt with three N5, N6 and N7 notices at the same time, with the same termination date, and all based on the identical reasons for ending the tenancy.  Again, this is not the case here.

 

2.  SS raised the issue of receiving documents from the Tenant one day later than the deadline specified in interim order CEL-78863-18-IN.   The deadline in the interim order was effectively, January 3, 2019.  Because of this breach, SS requested that the Board not consider any the Tenant’s evidence at this hearing.

 

IB produced evidence that on January 2, 2019, he sent all documents via Canada Post’s overnight courier service, so any delay was not of his doing.  Further, IB objected to this being raised at all since SS never once indicated anything to him that this was somehow a fatal breach of the interim order.  Both IB and SS submitted arguments in respect of “natural justice” being served.

 

Based on the respective submissions, I do not find the Landlord’s technical breach of my interim order to be of the Landlord’s doing, or significant enough for me to take the position that I should disallow everything the Landlord might present at the second hearing.  I rely upon section 202 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006 (the ‘Act’) which directs me to ascertain the real substance of all transactions, and not just the outward appearance of them.  In this case, I believe IB intended fully to comply with my interim order, and made a good faith posting of his evidence to SS on January 2, 2019.   I therefore denied the Landlord’s request on this issue.

 

3.  SS argued that much of the evidence he has seen, and which may be adduced at the second hearing, is about events that post-date the date when the applications were filed by the Landlord on August 29, 2018.  He requested that anything post-filing be disallowed at the second hearing.

 

IB submitted arguments in respect of “natural justice” being served, relying on section 233 of the Act for his arguments.  SS submitted his own arguments in reply about “natural justice” ending with again, the N5 and N8 are for different, contradictory bases.

 

I denied the Landlord’s argument about post-filing evidence being adduced at the hearing.  With regard to section 201 of the Act, Interpretation Guideline 13(d) and Rule 19 of the Board’s Rules, Members have the ability, while respecting procedural fairness, to hear whatever evidence is deemed necessary to properly consider all issues in an application being heard.  There is no limitation set anywhere that requires all evidence to reflect solely pre-filing events.   Board notices of termination often set termination dates well beyond application filing dates, and it would simply render the hearing process useless if some “event date” limitation relative to an application filing date were to be set.  As well, section 83 of the Act requires Members to hear all the circumstances in a landlord’s application that seeks eviction, which in most cases requires Members to hear submissions about events happening after the filing of the application at bar.  Finally, hearings are supposed to be summary in nature, and must dispose of all matters in a hearing as efficiently and expeditiously as possible, all in keeping with section 183 of the Act.

 

Determinations:

 

            October 23, 2018 Hearing

 

1.      From that hearing, and with reference to section 212 of the Act, I find the Notice of Rent Increase that was served is a valid notice as that notice is substantially compliant with respect to the content of that form. 

2.      I also find that the lawful monthly rent for the rental unit as of February 1, 2018 is $1,335.54.

3.      However, with regard to the N4 notice underlying the L1 application at bar, I find the application cannot proceed as it stands, because the N4 notice claims rent for a period of time that had already been ruled upon in order CEL-72966-18.   Order CEL-72966-18 was never reviewed, it became final and has since expired as of September 11, 2018.

            January 18, 2019 Hearing

L1 Amended to L9 Application

4.      The Landlord requested that the L1 application be amended to become an arrears-only application under section 87 of the Act.  After hearing the submissions of the parties, I consent to amending the L1 application so that it effectively becomes an arrears-only application beginning with the March 1, 2018 rental period going forward.

5.      The Tenant has not paid the total rent the Tenant was required to pay for the period from March 1, 2018 to January 31, 2019.  Because of the arrears, the Landlord had originally served a Notice of Termination effective August 2, 2018.

6.      The Landlord collected a rent deposit of $1,290.00 from the Tenant and this deposit is still being held by the Landlord.

7.      Interest on the rent deposit is owing to the Tenant for the period from August 19, 2011 to August 2, 2018.

8.      The Tenant paid $5,263.20 after the application was filed.

L2 Application

9.      The Landlord submitted nine exhibits (LL#3 to LL#11), and with some testimony from AL, claimed VT substantially interfered with the Landlord’s lawful rights and privileges in respect of refusing entry, or interfering with the ability to enter, of the Landlord or the Landlord’s agents on over 40 occasions including on:  February 9, 2018 (LL#3-mechanical contractor), April 13, 2018 (LL#4- pest control), August 14, 2018 (LL#5-inspection by AL), August 17, 2018  August 20, 2018 (LL#6-inspection by AL), and November 9, 2018 (LL#7-Landlords’ real estate agent). 

10.   IB stated that the issue of being refused entry is still outstanding to date.  IB stated that VT went as far as stating in writing that she would not be allowing the Landlords into the unit (LL#8- VT letter dated November 9, 2018), despite being advised that the rental unit was put up for sale by the Landlords (LL#9,  LL#10).  Exhibit LL#11 was submitted to show that for 62 possible showings, only 5 were successful because IB claimed that VT refused the Landlords entry.

11.   IB submitted that the Landlords notified VT in writing that they did not have the key to the rental unit (LL#8, L#9, LL#10). 

12.   SS and the Tenant then proceeded by submitting rebuttals against each claim of refused entry presented on by the Landlords at the hearing. Two additional exhibits were submitted as TT#8 (April 13/18 text message between IB and AL) and TT#9 (copy of purchase agreement which the Tenant received on December 10/18).  Many of the “refused” entries were actually occasions when the Landlords came to the rental unit and no one was there to let them in.

13.   It became very evident to me that there are two central issues surrounding the N5 claims under this application.  The first is language.   I noted at times in the hearing when the Landlords’ limitation of the English language manifested itself.  An example of that is exhibit LL#3, which clearly states that a mechanical contractor needed to enter on February 9, 2018.  That notice makes no mention that the Landlords themselves need any entry, so when AL showed up with the mechanical contractor at the rental unit on February 9th, VT only permitted the mechanical contractor to enter.

14.   The second issue relates to the key which unlocks the entry door to the rental unit.  It was clear to me at the earlier part of the claims, the Tenant was not aware that the Landlords had no key to enter the rental unit. The notices for entry request the Tenant to be present at the unit, but VT pointed out correctly, there is no need for her to be present at the unit for any time that the Landlords had duly notified her of the need to enter.

15.   The door-key issue became understood by the Tenant, as the evidence and testimonies strongly suggest, in November 2018 (most certainly on November 29, 2018-LL#10), when the matter of a security “key code” on the rental unit key was finally expressed in writing. As it turns out, a security “key code” is required so that a duplicate key can be made; and that “key code” appears on the actual key itself, which to date only the Tenant has possession of.  All along, I believe VT’s testimony that she thought the Landlords had their own key, or if not, she believed they could have made their own duplicate key through the condo management company that manages the building.

16.   However, since she has become fully aware of the Landlords’ need for a “key code”, VT has not been forthcoming in giving the much needed “key code” to the Landlords.  I firmly suspect that because of all the happenings under the tenancy, the parties’ relationship has deteriorated to the point of acrimony, so that neither party seems willing to talk to the other, never mind cooperate with one another.

17.   Before making final determinations on the N5 notice, I asked the parties that if the “key code” were passed between the parties, would that not resolve the application at bar.  The parties both agreed that this would be satisfactory, and before me, VT handed over the “key code” to AL.  IB agreed that the L2/N5 application was adequately resolved; there was no objection from SS.

18.   With respect to the N8 notice of termination (claiming the Tenant has been persistently late in paying her rents), I held the matter down for a few minutes so the parties could talk about settling the N8 basis of the L2 application.  The parties returned, requesting a continuation of the tenancy under an order on consent for payment of future rents, on time and in full, subject to section 78 of the Act.

It is ordered that:

 

L1/L9 Application

 

1.      The Tenant shall pay to the Landlords $1,722.94*, which represents the amount of rent owing and compensation up to January 31, 2019 and includes the cost of filing the application.

2.      If the Tenant does not pay the Landlords the full amount owing of $1,722.94 on or before February 12, 2019, the Tenant will start to owe interest.  This will be simple interest calculated from February 13, 2019 at 3.00% annually on the balance outstanding.

3.      To be clear, the rent that is due on the February 1, 2019 for the rental period of February 1-28, 2019 is not included as part of this order.

L2 Application

4.      With respect to the Landlords being able to make a duplicate key to the rental unit at their own expense, if there is additional information or assistance the Landlords need so the rental unit key can be duplicated, the Landlords shall clearly communicate in writing the information or assistance needed, and the Tenant shall continue to cooperate with the Landlords in good faith.

5.      The tenancy between the Landlords and the Tenant shall continue but shall be subject to the following order on consent: 

6.      The Tenant shall pay the Landlords the full monthly rent on or before the first day of each month for a twelve month period commencing February 1, 2019 through to and including January 1, 2020.

7.      If the Tenant fails to make any one of the payments ordered in paragraph 6, in full and on time, the Landlords may apply under section 78 of the Residential Tenancies Act, 2006, without notice to the Tenant for an order terminating the tenancy and evicting the Tenant. The Landlords must make this application no later than 30 days after the Tenant’s failure to make a payment.
